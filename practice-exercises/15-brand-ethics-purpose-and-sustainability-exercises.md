## Worked Example

*Study this example before attempting Tier 1. After reading it, close it and try to recall the key steps from memory before moving on.*

**Problem:** Siosaia is the founder of a B2B AI contract analysis tool. He wants to add an ethics section to his company's about page. He has drafted four sentences: (1) "We build AI that respects human dignity." (2) "Our model is trained on diverse, representative datasets." (3) "We are committed to responsible AI development." (4) "We conduct quarterly bias audits on gender and racial dimensions of contract outcome predictions using [named methodology], and we publish the results on our transparency page."

Apply the claim-proof map discipline to evaluate each sentence and determine which can be published, which needs evidence the company must first obtain, and which should be removed or revised.

**Approach:**
- Step 1: For each sentence, identify whether it is a specific, verifiable claim or a generic values statement.
- Step 2: For each specific claim, identify the evidence required to substantiate it.
- Step 3: Assess whether the company has that evidence, needs to obtain it, or cannot substantiate the claim.
- Step 4: Route each sentence accordingly.

**Answer:**

| Claim | Evidence required | Status | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| "We build AI that respects human dignity." | Undefined — no specific behavior is described | Cannot substantiate (generic values language) | Remove or replace with a specific behavioral claim |
| "Our model is trained on diverse, representative datasets." | Documentation of training data sources, diversity criteria, and how "diverse and representative" is defined and verified | Need — the company must have this documentation; without it, the claim is unsubstantiated | Obtain the documentation or revise to describe what is actually known about training data |
| "We are committed to responsible AI development." | None that could be specified — "responsible AI" is a generic claim | Cannot substantiate (generic AI ethics claim with no methodology behind it) | Remove entirely or replace with a specific practice claim |
| "We conduct quarterly bias audits..." (sentence 4) | Published audit results at a named URL, using the named methodology, on the named protected characteristics | Have — if the quarterly audits are documented and published at the URL | This sentence can be published as written, provided the documentation exists |

**What to notice:** Sentence 4 is the model of what an ethics claim should look like — specific enough to verify or falsify, with a named methodology, named scope, named frequency, and a verifiable output location. Sentences 1–3 are exactly the type of claim AI tools generate fluently and cannot substantiate. The AI+1 discipline here is running every AI-drafted sentence through the claim-proof test before publication.

**Exercise 1**

The chapter offers a behavioral test for distinguishing real purpose from purpose-washing. State the test in your own words — not as a quote — and explain why the test is behavioral rather than linguistic.

*(Tests: purpose vs. purpose-washing — behavioral test)*

**Answer:** The behavioral test asks: is this a commitment the brand maintains when maintaining it costs something real? If the commitment is only expressed when it is costless or profitable, it is purpose-washing. The test is behavioral rather than linguistic because purpose language is easy to produce (and increasingly generated by AI at volume) while behavioral evidence is expensive to manufacture. What the brand does when the commitment is inconvenient or costly is the test of whether the purpose is genuine. A brand that "believes in sustainability" but increases production when demand rises has failed the behavioral test, regardless of how compelling the language is.

**Common error:** Students describe the test as "whether the brand talks about its values" — treating the linguistic statement as the evidence. The chapter is explicit that the test is behavioral: what decisions does the commitment require, and are those decisions actually made?

**Exercise 2**

True / False: A brand that makes no public values claims cannot experience a purpose-washing backlash.

*Explain your reasoning in one sentence.*

*(Tests: backlash asymmetry — the mechanism linking claims to behavioral expectations)*

**Answer:** True — with an important qualification.

**Correct because:** The backlash asymmetry mechanism requires a prior values claim: the claim creates a behavioral expectation against which future behavior is evaluated. A brand that has made no values claims has not created an elevated expectation, so an incident produces a standard crisis rather than the compounded "hypocrisy crisis" the chapter describes. The important qualification: silence is not protection against all reputation damage — it only prevents the specific backlash amplification that occurs when a stated commitment is violated.

**Common error:** Students answer False, arguing that "any brand can face backlash." The chapter's backlash asymmetry is specifically about the compounding effect of hypocrisy — claims without behavior erode trust faster than the equivalent incident would without the prior claim. That mechanism specifically requires a prior claim to operate.

**Exercise 3**

The EU ECGT Directive bans two specific categories of environmental claim after 27 September 2026. Name both banned categories, give one concrete example of a claim that would be banned under each, and give one concrete example of a claim in the same territory that would survive the ban.

*(Tests: EU ECGT Directive — banned claim categories)*

**Answer:**
- Category 1: Generic green claims without substantiation. Banned example: "Our packaging is eco-friendly." Surviving example: "Our packaging is certified compostable by TÜV Austria under OK Compost Home (certificate number: [number]), verified annually." The difference: the surviving claim is specific, names a recognized certification, names the certifying body, and is verifiable.
- Category 2: Product-level "climate neutral" or "net zero" claims based primarily on carbon offsets without actual value-chain emissions reductions. Banned example: "This product is carbon neutral" (where the company purchases carbon offsets equal to estimated product emissions). Surviving example: "This product's manufacturing emissions have been reduced by 42% since 2020 (verified by [auditing body]), with the remaining 8% offset through [named program] — emissions data available at [URL]." The difference: the surviving claim leads with verified reduction, with offsets as a documented minor supplement.

**Common error:** Students describe the ban as "no green claims at all." The ECGT bans unsubstantiated generic claims and offset-only neutrality claims — specific, verifiable claims with recognized certification are permitted. The ban targets vagueness and offset-based laundering, not environmental claims as a category.

**Exercise 4** *(Contrastive Classification)*

Classify each of the following as a signal of *CSR*, *ESG reporting*, or *B Corp certification*. Then rank the three by level of third-party scrutiny (low/medium/high) and explain the key limitation of the highest-scrutiny option.

- (a) A consumer products company publishes an annual report disclosing its Scope 1 and Scope 2 carbon emissions, workforce diversity percentages, and board composition, using GRI Standards framework.
- (b) A tech company announces it will match employee charitable donations up to $5,000 per year and has committed to planting one tree for every product sold.
- (c) A food company has undergone B Lab's verification process, achieved a score qualifying for certification, and agreed to periodic recertification to maintain the credential.

*(Tests: CSR vs. ESG reporting vs. B Corp — distinguishing the signals and their scrutiny levels)*

**Answer:**
- (a) ESG reporting: standardized disclosure framework (GRI Standards), quantified metrics, but note the chapter's caution — ESG reporting is disclosure, not performance verification. A company can report ESG metrics accurately and still perform poorly on those metrics.
- (b) CSR: voluntary initiatives beyond legal compliance (donation matching, tree-planting), not third-party verified, no standardized reporting framework.
- (c) B Corp certification: third-party verified by B Lab, requires meeting performance standards, includes periodic recertification.

Third-party scrutiny ranking: Low = CSR (voluntary, self-reported), Medium = ESG reporting (standardized framework, but company-reported and verification quality varies), High = B Corp (third-party verified by B Lab against defined standards).

Key limitation of B Corp (highest scrutiny): B Corp is a floor, not a ceiling — it sets minimum performance standards rather than optimizing for maximum social and environmental performance. Additionally, some B Corps operate in sectors with inherently high negative externalities, where meeting B Corp standards still leaves significant harm. The certification is meaningfully harder than self-reporting but is not a guarantee of sector-leading performance.

**Common error:** Students rank ESG highest because it involves detailed metrics. ESG reporting can be third-party audited or can be company-self-reported with no external verification — the scrutiny level depends on the specific framework and whether an auditor is engaged. B Corp's external verification by a specific certifying body (B Lab) is structurally more rigorous than ESG disclosure, which can be produced without external audit.

**Exercise 5**

In your own words, explain the backlash asymmetry mechanism described in this chapter. Your explanation should specify what a brand that made no values claims experiences in a scandal versus what a brand that made values claims experiences in the same scandal — and explain the mechanism that produces the difference.

*(Tests: backlash asymmetry — mechanism and asymmetry between silence and claims)*

**Answer:** A strong answer captures: (1) A values claim creates a behavioral expectation encoded in the audience's memory as a standard against which future behavior is evaluated. (2) When the brand subsequently acts inconsistently with that claim, the violation is evaluated against that standard — the inconsistency is not just the behavior itself, but the hypocrisy of having claimed otherwise. (3) A brand that made no values claim has a crisis. A brand that made values claims and failed to act on them has a trust collapse — the claim inflated the expectation, and the violation deflates it by the full inflated amount. (4) The net damage is greater for the brand that made claims because the gap is larger: the audience expected the brand to hold higher standards and specifically did not. The asymmetry is severe: the upside of authentic purpose is cumulative; the downside of performed purpose is faster and deeper.

**What a strong answer includes:**
- The claim creating a behavioral expectation (not just a reputational position)
- The mechanism: violation assessed against the elevated standard the claim created
- The asymmetry: the inflated expectation amplifies the fall

**Exercise 6**

True / False: A company that publishes an ESG report using GRI Standards has demonstrated that it is performing well on the environmental, social, and governance dimensions it reports on.

*Explain your reasoning in one sentence.*

*(Tests: ESG reporting as signal — reporting vs. performing)*

**Answer:** False.

**Correct because:** The chapter explicitly distinguishes ESG reporting (disclosure of metrics) from ESG performance (actually performing well on those metrics) — a company that reports ESG metrics accurately and in standardized form is disclosing, not demonstrating performance. The disclosure makes the numbers available for scrutiny; whether those numbers are good is a separate question.

**Common error:** Students conflate "reporting" with "performing" because "they took the time to report, so they must care about ESG." The chapter's caution is that reading ESG reports as performance evidence rather than disclosure evidence is precisely the error. A company can report its carbon emissions accurately at a very high level without the level being commendable.

**Exercise 7** *(Error Analysis)*

A student has submitted the following claim-proof map for an AI fitness coaching startup:

> | Claim | Evidence we have |
> |---|---|
> | "We are committed to inclusive AI." | Our team has members from 8 countries. |
> | "Our AI is bias-free." | We tested it on 500 users during beta. |
> | "We protect user privacy." | We comply with GDPR. |
> | "We are carbon neutral." | We purchase carbon offsets equal to our office energy use. |

1. Identify the most significant error in this claim-proof map.
2. Explain the misconception that produced it — what did the student misunderstand about what counts as verifiable evidence for an ethics claim?
3. Rewrite one row of the map correctly, using the chapter's claim-proof discipline.

*(Tests: claim-proof map — evidence standards for ethics claims)*

**Answer:**
1. The most significant error is that no row contains verifiable evidence matched to the specific claim made. "We are committed to inclusive AI" with evidence of "team members from 8 countries" confuses organizational diversity with AI inclusivity — the claim is about the AI product, not the team. "Bias-free AI" with "500 beta users" is not evidence — the beta testing design (what protected characteristics were tested, what outcomes were measured, who conducted the assessment) is the evidence, not the sample size alone. "Carbon neutral" based solely on offsets for office energy use would violate ECGT's offset-based neutrality ban if the company operates in the EU.
2. The misconception: the student treated any internal data point as "evidence" rather than asking "what specific evidence would a regulator or journalist need to substantiate this specific claim?" The chapter requires that evidence match the claim's category — AI ethics claims need published methodology and audit results, not team demographics.
3. Correctly rewritten row (using the "bias-free" claim as the example): 

| Claim | Evidence required | Evidence we have | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Our AI outcomes are audited for gender and age bias in fitness recommendations." | Published bias audit report: protected characteristics tested, test set composition, outcomes measured, methodology, and results — produced by or verified by an independent assessor | In progress — we have conducted internal testing; external audit scheduled for Q3; results will be published at [URL] when complete | NEED — do not publish until external audit is complete |

**Common error:** Students correct the map by adding more data points to each row without addressing whether those data points actually substantiate the specific claim. More internal data does not make an unsupported claim supported; the evidence type must match the claim category.

**Exercise 8** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

Fatima is the founder of a sustainable packaging startup that makes compostable mailers for e-commerce. She is preparing her company's about page for launch. She has four candidate claims:

- (a) "Our mailers are eco-friendly."
- (b) "Our mailers are certified home-compostable under TÜV Austria OK Compost Home standards (certificate number: OC19-180)."
- (c) "We are a climate-neutral company."
- (d) "Our mailers decompose fully within 180 days in a home compost environment, as tested by [named independent lab] using [named standard]."

Evaluate each claim against: (1) the EU ECGT Directive's two banned categories, and (2) the chapter's claim-proof map discipline. For each claim, state whether it can be published, should be revised, or should be removed. Explain your reasoning.

*(Tests: EU ECGT Directive — banned claim evaluation + claim-proof map discipline)*

**Answer:**
- (a) "Eco-friendly": banned under ECGT as a generic green claim without substantiation. "Eco-friendly" is exactly the type of unspecific term the directive targets. Cannot publish as written. Should be replaced with a specific, verifiable claim like (b) or (d).
- (b) Certified compostability claim: passes ECGT — it is specific (named certification standard, named certifying body, certificate number). Passes claim-proof discipline — the evidence (the certification) exists and is verifiable by number. Can be published.
- (c) "Climate-neutral company": likely banned under ECGT if it is based on carbon offsets without documented value-chain emissions reductions. A small packaging startup using this language would need to show that (i) they have measured and reduced their own emissions across the value chain, and (ii) any remaining offset represents a minor supplement to documented reductions. A startup whose climate neutrality is based entirely on purchasing offsets would be in violation after 27 September 2026. Should be removed unless Fatima can document a genuine reduction-plus-minor-offset structure.
- (d) Specific decomposition claim: passes ECGT (specific, named lab, named standard) and passes claim-proof discipline. Can be published if the referenced lab test and standard are real and the company possesses the documentation.

**Common error:** Students approve claim (a) as "not explicitly banned." The ECGT's generic claim category explicitly includes "eco-friendly" as an example of a banned term. The ban is on the unsubstantiated generic claim, regardless of whether the company has good intentions.

**Exercise 9** *(Self-Explanation)*

The chapter argues that AI is useful for drafting purpose language but specifically dangerous in the claim-proof discipline. Explain in 2–3 sentences why AI tools are particularly likely to produce ethics and sustainability claims that fail the claim-proof test. Your explanation must use the term "claim-proof map" correctly and must reason from the principle, not just say "AI can be wrong."

*(Tests: AI+1 discipline in ethics claim production — self-explanation)*

**Answer:** A strong answer captures: AI language models generate ethics and sustainability claims by pattern-matching on what purpose language sounds like in their training data — not by reasoning about what a specific brand can actually demonstrate. The output is fluent, specific-sounding, and warmly phrased, but the specificity is mimicry of the form of evidence-backed claims rather than evidence itself. When run through a claim-proof map — which requires matching each claim to the specific evidence required and honestly assessing whether that evidence exists — AI-drafted purpose language consistently produces claims in the "cannot substantiate" column because the model has no access to the company's actual certifications, audit results, or documented practices. The discipline is to treat every AI-drafted ethics sentence as a hypothesis to be verified, not a fact to be published.

**Common error:** Students say "AI can make things up" without explaining the mechanism. A strong answer identifies why the AI's output is structurally likely to fail the claim-proof test: the model pattern-matches on what credible claims look like without having access to whether the evidence exists.

**Exercise 12** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

In 2011, a major outdoor clothing brand ran a full-page ad telling customers not to buy their product. The brand had instituted a repair program, a used-gear marketplace, and a legal structure committing the majority of profits to environmental causes. Compare this to a fast-fashion company that launched a "Conscious Collection" — a product line of marginally lower environmental impact, representing 1% of total production — while increasing overall annual production volume.

Apply the backlash asymmetry framework to evaluate the risk profile of each brand's communications strategy going forward. Which brand faces greater backlash risk on sustainability topics, and under what specific circumstances would the backlash mechanism activate for each?

*(Tests: backlash asymmetry — values claims without behavior and risk calibration)*

**Answer:** The outdoor brand has made costly behavioral commitments that demonstrate the values it claims — repair programs, used-gear sales, and profit-commitment structures all impose real business costs. Its backlash risk is low on sustainability topics because the claims and the behavior are aligned; the bar has been raised but the brand is clearing it. Backlash would activate for this brand only if behavior later contradicts its commitments: if it expanded production rapidly, ended the repair program for cost reasons, or was found to have overstated the environmental benefit of its practices. The fast-fashion brand faces substantially higher backlash risk. The "Conscious Collection" creates a values expectation (sustainability commitment) against which all subsequent behavior is now evaluated. If the brand's overall production volume continues to rise, if a supply chain investigation reveals labor or environmental violations, or if a journalist documents the 1% scope of the "conscious" program, the backlash mechanism activates fully: the inconsistency between the stated commitment and the actual business model makes the violation a hypocrisy crisis rather than a standard business crisis. The mechanism: the fast-fashion brand made a claim it cannot sustain with its business model, elevating the expectation without changing the behavior that would be needed to meet it.

**Common error:** Students evaluate the outdoor brand as "higher risk because it has made bigger claims." The backlash asymmetry is specifically about the gap between claims and behavior — the outdoor brand's costly behavioral commitments mean the claims and behavior are aligned, making the backlash risk lower, not higher.

**Exercise 13** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

In Chapter 13: Measuring Brand Equity and Impact, the chapter argues that brand tracking studies should monitor association strength — the degree to which audiences associate specific attributes with the brand. In this chapter, the backlash asymmetry mechanism argues that values claims without behavioral follow-through erode trust faster than silence.

A brand director argues: "If values claims without behavior erode trust, the measurement strategy should be simple: track whether our stated values appear as associations in brand tracking surveys. If they do, the claim is working. If they don't, the claim has failed."

Evaluate this argument. What does it get right? Where does it misidentify the measurement that actually matters?

*This question connects the backlash asymmetry mechanism (Ch. 15) to brand tracking — association strength monitoring (Ch. 13).*

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Surface: "association tracking is useful but incomplete"
- Strong: identifies the specific measurement gap — association tracking measures what audiences think, not whether behavior is consistent with claims. The backlash risk does not come from weak associations; it comes from behavioral inconsistency that, when revealed, collapses strong associations
- Strong: explains what the measurement strategy should actually monitor in addition to association tracking

*(Tests: backlash asymmetry + brand tracking — association strength — Ch. 15 + Ch. 13)*

**Answer:** The director is right that association strength tracking is relevant — if the brand's stated values are not appearing as audience associations, the claims are not landing, which is a problem. But the measurement logic is inverted. The backlash asymmetry does not activate when associations are weak — it activates when associations are strong and behavior contradicts them. A brand that has successfully built "sustainability leader" as a strong association (association tracking would show this as a win) faces maximum backlash risk at the moment a behavioral inconsistency is revealed, because the gap between the strong expectation and the failing behavior is at its widest. The measurement strategy should monitor two things: (1) association tracking to see what the brand is becoming in the audience's mind (useful for early detection of weak claims); and (2) behavioral compliance — internal monitoring of whether the decisions the claimed values require are actually being made. The second measurement is not an audience-facing metric; it is an internal accountability check. Brand tracking alone tells you how the story is landing; it cannot tell you whether the story will hold when behavior is examined.

**Common error:** A surface answer agrees that association tracking is useful and notes it is incomplete. A strong answer identifies the specific way the director's measurement strategy misses the backlash mechanism: strong associations + behavioral inconsistency = maximum backlash, not weak associations + behavioral inconsistency.

**Exercise 14** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

In Chapter 1: The Creative Engineer, Spence signaling theory established that the value of a signal depends on its cost and verifiability. In this chapter, the claim-proof map requires pairing every ethics or sustainability claim with specific, verifiable evidence.

Apply Spence signaling theory to explain why a B Corp certification functions as a more credible signal of social and environmental commitment than a company's self-published CSR report, even when both describe similar practices. Then apply the same logic to explain why a company's published bias audit (with methodology, named protected characteristics, and a third-party assessor) is a stronger AI ethics signal than a statement that the company "believes in responsible AI."

*This question connects the claim-proof map and CSR/ESG/B Corp signal hierarchy (Ch. 15) to Spence signaling theory — cost and verifiability (Ch. 1).*

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Surface: "B Corp requires external verification, which is more credible"
- Strong: applies the Spence cost-asymmetry condition — B Corp is costly to obtain (application process, performance standards, recertification), which low-commitment organizations would not rationally invest in; the CSR report is cheap to produce and does not carry this cost asymmetry
- Strong: connects this to the AI ethics signal case — a published bias audit is costly (requires an actual audit, a named methodology, public disclosure of results), while "responsible AI" is a zero-cost claim available to any brand

*(Tests: claim-proof map and CSR/ESG/B Corp signals + Spence signaling theory — Ch. 15 + Ch. 1)*

**Answer:** Spence signaling predicts that a signal carries credibility when it is costly enough that a low-commitment organization would not rationally invest in it. A self-published CSR report is cheap: it requires writing, design, and publication — no external verification, no performance standard, no ongoing accountability. A low-commitment company can produce a compelling CSR report at low cost. B Corp certification, by contrast, requires meeting performance standards assessed by an external party (B Lab), an application process with costs, and periodic recertification. The cost asymmetry means that organizations without genuine commitment would find the certification cost disproportionate to its value, creating a partial separating signal. For AI ethics signals, the same logic applies: "We believe in responsible AI" is a zero-cost claim that any brand can produce — it is a pooling signal that does not differentiate. A published bias audit with a named methodology, named protected characteristics, a named third-party assessor, and public disclosure of results is costly to produce (the audit itself is expensive, and public disclosure exposes the results to scrutiny regardless of their content). The cost asymmetry creates a separating signal: a company with genuinely poor bias audit results would not rationally publish them publicly with a named methodology, so the willingness to publish specific results is itself evidence of confidence in those results.

**Common error:** A surface answer says "external verification is more credible." A strong answer applies the cost asymmetry condition: why would a low-commitment organization not invest in this signal? The CSR report cost asymmetry is low (anyone can produce one); the B Corp cost asymmetry is higher; the published bias audit cost asymmetry is specific to the disclosure of results (a company with bad results would not publish).
