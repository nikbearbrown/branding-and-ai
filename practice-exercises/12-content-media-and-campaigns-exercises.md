## Worked Example

*Study this example before attempting Tier 1. After reading it, close it and try to recall the key steps from memory before moving on.*

**Problem:** Amara is launching a new AI-powered project management tool aimed at remote engineering teams. She has a monthly budget of $1,500, eight weeks to run the campaign, and a goal of generating 25 demo requests from engineering team leads. She is deciding how to distribute effort and budget across PESO channels.

**Approach:**
- Step 1: Identify the audience and where they spend professional attention. Engineering team leads read developer newsletters, participate in Slack communities, and use LinkedIn — they rarely respond to broad display advertising.
- Step 2: Determine the objective type. The goal is conversions (demo requests), not initial awareness. This means the reach vs. frequency trade-off favors frequency: concentrated impressions on fewer qualified people over wide reach to many unqualified ones.
- Step 3: Map PESO channels to objective and audience. Owned: write one long-form case study demonstrating the tool with real workflow examples (primary content asset). Earned: pitch two developer-focused newsletters to feature the case study. Shared: post a thread summarizing the key finding in three engineering Slack communities and LinkedIn. Paid: reserve $400 to boost the best-performing LinkedIn post to engineering managers after organic reach is measured.
- Step 4: Check IMC coherence. All four channels carry one message: "Know exactly where each project stands, in real time, without a meeting."
- Step 5: Set measurement. Primary KPI is demo requests attributed to campaign traffic via UTM parameters on the case study landing page.

**Answer:** Amara distributes the campaign with owned content as the anchor (the case study), earned and shared channels as the amplification layer, and paid as a targeted boost applied after organic performance is measured. The channel mix is derived from the audience and the conversion objective — not from which channels are most familiar.

**What to notice:** The channel mix is determined by the audience and the objective, not by habit or channel familiarity. A conversion objective favors frequency over reach, and frequency is delivered most efficiently through the channels where the target audience already lives and trusts content.

**Exercise 1**

For each of the four PESO channel types, write one sentence explaining what makes it economically distinct from the other three — not just the name of the channel, but the relationship between the brand and the audience in that channel.

*(Tests: PESO model — four channel types and their distinct economics)*

**Answer:** Paid: the brand pays for placement, so the audience sees the message because money changed hands, not because they sought it — the relationship is transactional. Earned: third parties publish coverage without payment because they judged the work worth amplifying, making this the most credible channel and the one the brand cannot manufacture. Shared: content reaches audiences through individual acts of forwarding, commenting, and saving on social platforms — the brand can publish but the audience controls whether it spreads. Owned: the brand controls the surface entirely (website, email list, podcast), builds audience slowly without paid distribution, and the asset is durable rather than rented.

**Common error:** Students often describe owned media as "free" because there is no per-impression cost. The chapter's point is that owned media is not free — it costs audience-building effort over time — but it is *durable* in a way that paid and shared channels are not. The economic distinction is durability, not cost.

**Exercise 2**

True / False: A campaign that achieves 10,000 impressions spread across 10,000 distinct people will produce more conversion behavior than a campaign that achieves 10,000 impressions concentrated on 2,000 people — as long as the total impression count is equal.

*Explain your reasoning in one sentence.*

*(Tests: reach vs. frequency trade-off — conversion objective)*

**Answer:** False.

**Correct because:** For a conversion objective, frequency is the relevant variable — marketing research consistently shows a single impression rarely changes behavior, so concentrating five exposures on 2,000 qualified people produces more action than one exposure to 10,000 people, even at identical total impression counts.

**Common error:** Students often assume more reach is always better because it exposes the message to more people. The chapter distinguishes between awareness objectives (where reach dominates) and conversion objectives (where frequency dominates). The question gives a conversion framing, so frequency wins.

**Exercise 3**

In your own words, explain what Integrated Marketing Communications (IMC) requires of a brand, as introduced in this chapter. Do not quote the chapter directly.

*(Tests: Integrated Marketing Communications — organizational discipline)*

**Answer:** A strong answer captures: (1) IMC is the insistence that every channel carries one synchronized underlying message, voice, and archetype — even when the specific format adapts to the channel; (2) the failure it prevents is departments optimizing each channel independently, producing a brand that says different things in different contexts; (3) the campaign is the unit that enforces this coherence.

**What a strong answer includes:**

- The organizational diagnosis — IMC is a response to siloed channel ownership, not just a style preference

**Exercise 4** *(Contrastive Classification)*

Below are four real brand behaviors. Classify each as belonging to paid, earned, shared, or owned media. Then write one sentence explaining the most important distinction between paid and earned in this list.

- (a) A fintech startup's explainer article is linked to by three independent personal finance blogs without any payment or arrangement.
- (b) The same startup purchases a sponsored slot in a widely-read fintech newsletter.
- (c) The startup's founder posts a Twitter/X thread summarizing a key finding from their research, and it is reshared 800 times.
- (d) The startup's own blog publishes a quarterly report on small-business lending trends.

*(Tests: PESO model — paid vs. earned distinction)*

**Answer:** (a) Earned — third parties independently judged the content worth linking to, with no payment. (b) Paid — the startup purchased the placement; the newsletter audience sees it because money changed hands. (c) Shared — the founder published content on a social platform and the audience controlled whether it spread. (d) Owned — the startup controls the blog completely and built the audience through content production.

Key distinction between paid and earned: paid is guaranteed placement purchased with money; earned is credibility granted by an independent third party who cannot be bought. This is why earned media carries more trust weight per impression than paid — the audience knows it was not purchased.

**Common error:** Students sometimes classify sponsored newsletter content as "earned" because it appears alongside editorial content. Disclosure and compensation are the distinguishing markers: if the brand paid for placement, it is paid, regardless of where it appears.

**Exercise 5**

The chapter lists six sections of a campaign brief. Name all six and explain, in one sentence each, what question each section answers.

*(Tests: campaign brief — six sections and their functions)*

**Answer:** (1) Objective: What specific, measurable change will the campaign produce? (2) Audience: Who, specifically, is this campaign trying to reach? (3) Message: What is the one thing the audience should believe or do differently after encountering this campaign? (4) Channel mix: Which PESO channels are used, why each was chosen for this audience and objective, and how they connect? (5) Budget: What will the campaign spend on paid, production, and distribution? (6) Measurement: What is the primary KPI, and how will it be tracked?

**Common error:** Students often list "goals" or "strategy" rather than the six specific sections from the chapter. The brief is precise: the objective is measurable, the message is one claim (not a list), and the measurement structure is set before launch.

**Exercise 6**

True / False: A campaign with a high-reach media mix is the correct choice when the objective is to convert existing free-trial users into paid subscribers.

*Explain your reasoning in one sentence.*

*(Tests: reach vs. frequency trade-off — objective-to-mix alignment)*

**Answer:** False.

**Exercise 7** *(Error Analysis)*

The following campaign brief section was submitted by a student. It contains an error.

> **Objective:** We want to build awareness of our AI writing assistant among freelance writers and content creators. We want to increase our social media presence and get more engagement on our posts. We also want to drive traffic to our website and eventually convert visitors into paying subscribers.

1. Identify exactly where the error occurs.
2. Explain the misconception that caused it — what did the student misunderstand about campaign objective-writing?
3. Write a corrected version of the objective section.

*(Tests: campaign brief — objective-writing discipline vs. publishing impulse)*

**Answer:**
1. The error occurs throughout: the student has listed four separate goals (awareness, social presence, engagement, website traffic, conversions) rather than one specific measurable objective. Each goal also lacks specificity — there is no number, no timeframe, no verification mechanism.
2. The misconception: the student treats the objective as a wish list rather than a constraint document. The chapter is explicit that the objective must be a single, measurable change you can verify after the campaign ends — "more awareness" is a publishing impulse, not an objective. Multiple objectives mean multiple campaigns.
3. Corrected version: "Generate 50 new paid subscriber conversions from freelance writers who arrive via content marketing channels (newsletter, blog, or social) over the next eight weeks. Primary KPI: paid conversions attributed to campaign sources via UTM parameters."

**Common error:** Students conflate the objective with the strategy or the goals. The brief's objective answers "what specific change?" — one thing, measurable, with a deadline.

**Exercise 8** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

Nadia runs a small B2B consulting practice helping supply chain managers reduce procurement costs using AI tools. She has identified her audience precisely: supply chain operations directors at manufacturing companies with 200–2,000 employees. Her budget is $800 per month. Her objective is to generate 8 qualified consultation inquiries per quarter from this audience.

Her current content mix: she posts daily on LinkedIn (high reach, low frequency to any one person), runs occasional paid search ads with broad keywords, and has a website with three service pages but no owned content beyond that.

Apply the chapter's reach vs. frequency reasoning and the PESO framework to recommend what she should change about her channel mix, and explain why.

*(Tests: reach vs. frequency trade-off and PESO channel integration applied to a specific case)*

**Answer:** Nadia's current mix is misaligned with her objective and audience. The objective is consultation inquiries (conversion), which favors frequency over reach. Daily LinkedIn posts spread one impression across a wide audience, but her target (supply chain directors at mid-size manufacturers) is small and specific — they need multiple exposures, not one scroll-past impression.

Recommended changes: (1) Invest the owned channel — produce one substantive long-form piece per month (a case study or decision framework for supply chain cost reduction) that her exact audience would find useful. This builds the high-frequency contact with serious prospects. (2) Shift LinkedIn from daily posts to 1-2 per week amplifying the owned content, with a clear call to action. This reduces volume and increases value. (3) Replace broad paid search with a small retargeting budget targeting visitors who read the owned content but did not inquire. (4) Pursue earned coverage in two trade publications that supply chain directors actually read — a pitch framed as a methodology, not a promotion.

**Common error:** Students recommend adding more channels (including TikTok or Instagram) rather than deepening the channels where the specific audience lives. The PESO choice should follow audience behavior, not channel popularity.

**Exercise 12** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

**Exercise 13** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

In Chapter 6: Brand Strategy and Positioning, the concept of negative space was introduced — the things the brand deliberately will not do, claim, or say, which are as defining as the positive commitments. In this chapter, the campaign brief's message section requires one claim, not a list.

A brand manager argues: "Negative space is a brand strategy concept — it doesn't affect campaign execution. The brief's 'one message' rule is about creative focus, not brand identity."

Evaluate this argument. How does the concept of negative space from Chapter 6 directly determine what goes in and what is excluded from the campaign message section of Chapter 12's brief?

*This question connects campaign brief message discipline (Ch. 12) to negative space as identity constraint (Ch. 6).*

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Surface: restates that both chapters emphasize focus
- Strong: shows the mechanism — negative space in the brand strategy predetermines which claims the brand cannot make in the campaign message, independent of creative focus
- Strong: explains that the "one message" rule is not just about brevity — it must be a claim that falls within the brand's identity, not outside it

*(Tests: campaign brief message discipline + negative space as brand identity constraint — Ch. 12 + Ch. 6)*

**Answer:** The argument is wrong in its premise. Negative space from Chapter 6 directly constrains the message section: a brand with a defined negative space (e.g., a Sage-archetype brand that will not make hype claims or broad benefit statements) cannot fill its campaign message with aspirational language that contradicts that identity, regardless of how focused the message is. The "one message" rule serves both creative focus and brand integrity simultaneously. If a campaign message would require the brand to make a claim that falls into its negative space — a claim it has committed to not making — then that message is unavailable, even if it would be effective with the target audience. The Chapter 6 negative space is the constraint that precedes the Chapter 12 message decision; they are not parallel — one produces the other.

**Common error:** Students agree with the argument that "negative space is strategy, not execution," treating the two concepts as operating in separate domains. A surface answer will note that both are about focus without showing the dependency — the negative space constrains which messages are available, making it directly operational in brief writing.

**Exercise 14** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

In Chapter 1: The Creative Engineer, Spence signaling theory established that the value of a signal depends on its cost to produce and its verifiability by the receiver. In this chapter, AI-assisted content creation is described as collapsing the cost of producing content — the "flood."

Apply Spence signaling logic to the following question: if AI lowers the cost of producing high-volume, fluent brand content, what happens to the signaling value of that content? What does the chapter's flood-and-filter argument imply about which layer of brand content retains its signaling value even as AI content becomes ubiquitous?

*This question connects the PESO/content strategy framework (Ch. 12) to Spence signaling theory (Ch. 1).*

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Surface: "AI content is less valuable because everyone can produce it"
- Strong: applies the cost asymmetry condition specifically — what AI content cannot do is demonstrate the judgment, verification, and editorial decision-making that are costly to produce and verifiable by the audience
- Strong: connects this to the chapter's owned media and editorial layer as the residual high-cost signal

*(Tests: AI-assisted content execution and editorial judgment layer + Spence signaling theory — Ch. 12 + Ch. 1)*

**Answer:** Spence signaling predicts that when the cost of producing a signal falls uniformly, the signal's separating capacity collapses — it becomes a pooling signal available to anyone. If AI can produce fluent marketing copy at near-zero cost, that copy no longer separates a knowledgeable, credible brand from a low-credibility brand. The flood is precisely this: volume at near-zero cost that gives no information about the producer. The filter — the chapter's term for what retains value — is the editorial judgment layer: the decision about what is worth saying, the verification that it is true, the voice that sounds like a specific person with a specific track record. These remain costly because they require genuine expertise to verify and are detectable when absent. Owned content that demonstrates actual expertise — with cited evidence, original analysis, and verifiable claims — functions as a separating signal because a low-quality brand cannot sustain it over time, regardless of how much AI drafting capacity it has. The chapter's AI+1 discipline is the operational description of the costly signal: AI produces the volume, the human produces the judgment that makes the content verifiable.

**Common error:** A surface answer says AI content is lower quality so audiences prefer human content. The Spence framework provides a more precise mechanism: quality alone is not the variable — verifiability and the cost asymmetry between high- and low-judgment producers are the operative factors.

The "one message" rule requires that the message fall within the brand's identity, not outside it. The two chapters are not parallel; the Chapter 6 negative space is a prerequisite constraint that precedes the Chapter 12 message decision.
*What a strong answer includes:* The mechanism (negative space predetermines which claims are unavailable); the dependency direction (Chapter 6 constrains Chapter 12, not vice versa); at least one example of a claim that would be excluded by a specific negative space.
*Common error:* Agreeing with the argument that negative space is "strategy" and brief message is "execution" — treating them as separate domains. A surface answer notes both chapters emphasize focus without showing the constraining relationship.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 12 "The Campaign Brief — Message" + Ch. 6 negative space

**Ex 14 — Synthesis**
*Model answer:* Spence signaling predicts that when content production cost falls uniformly, brand content becomes a pooling signal.
