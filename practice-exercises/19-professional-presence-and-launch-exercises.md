## Worked Example

*Study this example before attempting Tier 1. After reading it, close it and try to recall the key steps from memory before moving on.*

**Problem:** Fatima is building her ten-slide final presentation about an AI tool she has built — a pipeline that automatically generates weekly summaries of academic AI research papers for graduate students who do not have time to read every paper in their field. She is drafting Slide 2 (Problem). Her current draft reads: "Graduate students face information overload with academic research." Apply the Airbnb coherence principle to improve it.

**Approach:**
- Step 1 — Apply the "specific numbers do load-bearing work" principle. "Information overload" is a symptom statement without a user, frequency, task, or cost. The Airbnb deck used real numbers from beta runs. What is the specific user? A PhD student or master's student who needs to stay current in their field. What is the specific frequency? Weekly reading load. What is the specific cost? Time spent, and the consequence of not reading (missing relevant work).
- Step 2 — Apply "every slide answered the same question." Slide 2 should set up the exact problem that Slide 3's solution will solve. If the problem is vague, the solution slide cannot be specific. The problem and solution must form a chain.
- Step 3 — Open in scene. The chapter's guidance is to open in a specific moment — one person, one morning, one pain.

**Answer:** Revised Slide 2: "It is Sunday afternoon. A second-year PhD student in machine learning has 47 new papers in her RSS feed from the past seven days. She needs to be current before her lab meeting Monday morning. Reading all 47 is impossible. Skimming takes three to four hours and still misses half. She is falling behind — not because she is not working hard, but because the field publishes faster than any individual can track." This version has a specific user, a specific situation, a specific time cost, and a specific consequence. The audience can find ten people who fit this description before they finish reading the slide. It sets up Slide 3's solution claim precisely.

**What to notice:** The Airbnb deck's strength was not its visual quality — it was the precision of each slide's problem framing. "Strong early user interest" does not raise money; "87 bookings in the first six weeks" does. The same precision applies to a course final presentation: "information overload" does not earn the audience's attention; a specific student on a specific Sunday afternoon does.

**Exercise 1**

The chapter identifies three coherence properties from the Airbnb deck analysis. Name all three. For each, write one sentence describing what the property looks like when it is *absent* from a presentation.

*(Tests: three coherence properties — Airbnb deck analysis)*

**Answer:** (1) Every slide answered the same question (argument coherence). Absent: the deck makes ten different arguments — technical architecture, brand rationale, career story, funding ask — each slide a separate plea, leaving the audience with no single clear thing to remember or act on. (2) Specific numbers did load-bearing work (specificity as evidence). Absent: the presenter uses vague claims — "strong early interest," "users love it," "the market is large" — that signal no verification has been done and that the presenter has not done the ground work. (3) The team slide carried weight. Absent: the team slide lists names and titles without relevant history, leaving the audience unable to answer the fundamental question: do we believe these people can execute this?

**Common error:** Describing only one or two properties and treating "coherence" generically as "consistency." The three properties are distinct, and a strong answer names all three precisely.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "The Coherence Principle."

**Exercise 2**

True / False: The 30-point font rule in Kawasaki's 10/20/30 framework is primarily a design choice — a visual standard to make slides look cleaner.

*Explain your reasoning in one sentence:*

*(Tests: 10/20/30 pitch structure — Kawasaki's 30-point rule)*

**Answer:** False. The 30-point font rule is an editing discipline, not a design choice. Kawasaki's argument is that a presenter who uses smaller font has too much content per slide and is therefore reading the slides rather than presenting them. Forcing 30-point font forces editing — if the content does not fit, the content is too dense for slides and should move to the speaking script. The rule is about the relationship between content density and presenter behavior, not about aesthetics.

**Common error:** Agreeing with the statement because "30-point is a design standard." The chapter is explicit: the rule's purpose is to force editing by making dense slides impossible to format.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "The Final Presentation."

**Exercise 3**

In your own words, explain the difference between an *ATS-optimized résumé* and a *designer résumé* as the chapter defines them. What does each version optimize for, and who is the primary reader for each?

*(Tests: ATS-optimized vs. designer résumé — two versions, one identity)*

**Answer:** The ATS-optimized résumé optimizes for automated text parsing systems that extract content and score it against keyword criteria before any human reads it. It uses a single column, standard section headers (Experience, Education, Skills, Projects), standard fonts, no graphics or text boxes, and keyword-rich language matching the job description vocabulary. The primary "reader" is the ATS software; the human secondary reader encounters it only after the automated filter is cleared. The designer résumé optimizes for the human reviewer who has already cleared the ATS filter and wants a document that signals craft and intentionality. It uses the brand's visual system — color palette, typography, layout — and signals the archetype within the first five seconds of opening. Both versions contain identical content (same job titles, companies, dates, bullets); only the format differs. The primary reader is a hiring manager or technical reviewer who has specifically requested or received the document.

**Common error:** Treating the designer résumé as a separate document with different content. The chapter is explicit: same content, different format. A student who describes the designer version as having "more personal information" or "a different selection of experience" has missed the one-identity constraint.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "The Resume — Two Versions, One Identity."

**Exercise 4**

Label each of the following résumé bullets as either a *responsibility statement* or an *outcome statement*. For each responsibility statement, identify what specific information is missing that would make it an outcome statement.

(a) "Worked with the data team to build pipelines for processing user activity logs."
(b) "Deployed a multi-agent content pipeline that reduced manual content drafting time by three hours per week for a four-person marketing team, processing 200+ articles daily."
(c) "Assisted in migrating the company's infrastructure to AWS."
(d) "Built and shipped a Streamlit dashboard that surfaces competitor news to marketing managers, reducing research prep time from 90 minutes to 8 minutes per week."

*(Tests: outcome-oriented bullet construction — responsibility vs. outcome distinction)*

**Answer:** (a) Responsibility statement. Missing: what was built (the pipeline's function), at what scale (volume of logs processed), and what changed as a result (latency, accuracy, team time saved). (b) Outcome statement. Contains: what was built, scale (200+ articles daily), result (three hours per week saved), context (four-person marketing team). (c) Responsibility statement. Missing: what specific components were migrated, at what scale (number of services, data volume), and what the migration achieved (cost reduction, latency improvement, reliability metric). (d) Outcome statement. Contains: what was built, what it does for whom, and a specific measurable result (90 minutes → 8 minutes).

**Common error:** Labeling (a) as an outcome statement because it mentions "building pipelines." Naming what was built is not the same as stating what resulted from building it. An outcome statement must include a specific, verifiable result.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "The Resume — Two Versions, One Identity."

**Exercise 5**

The chapter describes the 10-slide pitch structure. For each of the following slide descriptions, identify which slide number it corresponds to and explain in one sentence what work that slide is doing in the argument chain.

(a) "I am looking for a machine-learning engineering role at an early-stage AI startup. If that describes your team, I would like fifteen minutes."
(b) The presenter shows the architecture: "The tool triggers on a daily schedule, ingests RSS feeds, runs them through GPT-4o-mini for sentiment scoring, and writes a structured summary to Google Sheets."
(c) "I build Sage-archetype AI tools for professionals who need intelligence they can trust. The interface is structured rather than chat-style because my audience does not want to explore — they want a verified answer."
(d) "In the first three weeks of use, three of four beta users opened the summary every morning and shared it with at least one colleague. One described it as 'the first thing I actually act on.'"

*(Tests: 10/20/30 pitch structure — slide identification and function)*

**Answer:** (a) Slide 10 — The ask. This slide closes the chain by converting attention and understanding into a specific action; vague asks produce vague responses, so specificity here determines whether the audience can respond. (b) Slide 4 — How it works. This slide provides the architecture overview at a level the audience can follow; it answers the "but how?" question raised by Slide 3's solution claim. (c) Slide 6 — Brand position. This is the slide that distinguishes the presentation from every other technical build in the room; it makes the archetype and design philosophy explicit rather than implicit. (d) Slide 7 — Validation. This slide provides real evidence of the problem-solution fit; even small numbers with specific behavior data (opened every morning, shared with a colleague) are more credible than projected market size.

**Common error:** Labeling (d) as Slide 3 or Slide 5 because "it shows the tool working." Validation (Slide 7) is distinct from the solution (Slide 3) and demo (Slide 5) — it shows user behavior in response to the tool, not the tool itself.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "The Final Presentation."

**Exercise 6** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

Mateus is preparing his ten-slide final presentation. He has drafted Slide 2 (Problem) as follows: "Many professionals spend too much time on manual research tasks. AI can help reduce this burden."

Apply the three coherence properties from the Airbnb deck analysis to diagnose what is wrong with this draft. Then rewrite the slide content (the text that would appear on the slide — not the speaker notes) to demonstrate all three properties.

*(Tests: three coherence properties — applied to pitch deck problem framing)*

**Answer:** Diagnosis: Property 1 (argument coherence) — the slide makes a generic claim that could belong to any AI tool presentation; it does not yet imply a specific solution, so it cannot function as a chain link that the next slide builds on. Property 2 (specific numbers) — "many professionals" and "too much time" are vague claims that signal the presenter has not measured the problem; no number appears. Property 3 (team weight) is not yet at stake on Slide 2, but the slide's vagueness suggests a presenter who has not done the empirical work. Rewrite: "It is Monday morning. A marketing manager at a five-person B2B SaaS team opens her laptop and spends the next 2.5 hours manually pulling competitor updates from 8 sources before she can brief her team. She does this every week. The intelligence is 24 hours stale by the time it is shared. Her team makes decisions based on last week's landscape."

**Common error:** Rewriting the slide with better language but still omitting a specific number. A strong answer includes at least one measurable claim (2.5 hours, 8 sources, 24 hours stale).

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "The Coherence Principle."

**Exercise 7** *(Error Analysis)*

> The following LinkedIn headline and about section opening were submitted by a student.

*Headline: "Machine Learning Engineer | Python | TensorFlow | NLP | Computer Vision | AWS | PyTorch"*

*About section: "I am a passionate machine learning engineer with 3+ years of experience in developing cutting-edge AI solutions. I am skilled in Python, TensorFlow, NLP, Computer Vision, and AWS. I am currently seeking opportunities to leverage my expertise in a dynamic and innovative environment."*

1. Identify the coherence failure in the headline and explain what it signals to a recruiter who reads it.
2. Identify the coherence failure in the about section and explain what is missing.
3. Rewrite both for a Caregiver-archetype AI engineer whose positioning is "AI engineer building tools that help healthcare workers document patient care faster."

*(Tests: coherence principle applied to LinkedIn — two-version résumé + coherence audit)*

**Answer:**
1. Headline failure: it is a list of keywords, not a positioning sentence. It tells the recruiter where the student has been (what tools they know) rather than what they are for (what value they create and for whom). The keyword list is a pooling signal — identical to thousands of other ML engineer LinkedIn headlines, indistinguishable from any other technical candidate with the same stack. It does not appear in search as a distinctive identity.
2. About section failure: it is a template written about no one — "passionate," "cutting-edge," "dynamic and innovative environment" are filler phrases present in millions of profiles. The section contains no positioning claim (who this engineer builds for), no specific work referenced, and no actual voice. The call to action ("seeking opportunities") is passive rather than specific.
3. Rewrite: Headline: "AI engineer building tools that help healthcare workers spend less time documenting and more time with patients." About section opening: "I build AI tools for the people who do the work that keeps others healthy. Right now, that means documentation assistance tools — the kind that let a nurse finish her notes in fifteen minutes instead of ninety, without compromising accuracy or compliance. I care about the workflow more than the model; the best AI tool for a healthcare worker is one they can use without thinking about it."

**Common error:** Rewriting the headline as a job title with one added phrase ("ML Engineer focused on healthcare"). A strong rewrite is a full positioning sentence.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "Social Media as Brand-Coherence Surface."

**Exercise 9** *(Self-Explanation)*

In this chapter, the Airbnb deck is used instead of a more visually polished modern pitch deck as the teaching case for professional presence. Explain in 2–3 sentences why the chapter uses a plain, 2009-era deck rather than a contemporary design-forward example. Your explanation must use the term "coherence" correctly and must reference what the Airbnb deck demonstrates that visual polish does not.

*(Tests: coherence principle — self-explanation of why the teaching case was chosen)*

**Answer:** The Airbnb deck is used precisely because it lacks visual polish — its plain typography and simple bar charts make clear that the argument's persuasive power came from its coherence, not from its design. A visually polished deck would conflate two separate variables (design quality and argument quality), making it harder to identify which produced the result. The chapter uses the Airbnb deck to isolate the coherence principle: that a presentation in which every slide answers the same question, uses specific numbers, and establishes credibility will outperform a beautiful presentation with ten different arguments, regardless of which is better-designed.

**Common error:** Describing the Airbnb deck as successful "despite" its lack of polish, as if polish is normally the key variable. The chapter's argument is stronger: the deck succeeded because coherence was the operative variable, and visual polish was irrelevant to the outcome.

**Exercise 10** *(Cumulative — prior chapter concept)*

In Chapter 18, you learned about the *coherence audit* — the discipline of checking whether the portfolio website, LinkedIn profile, and résumé read as three angles on one person. In this chapter, you are working with *the 10-slide pitch presentation* as a fourth professional surface.

A student has run the coherence audit from Chapter 18 and found that her portfolio, LinkedIn, and résumé are aligned. She now adds a ten-slide presentation. Identify two specific ways the presentation could introduce a new coherence failure that the Chapter 18 audit would not have caught, and explain how to check for each.

**Exercise 11** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

The chapter says: "Open in scene, not in introduction." Joon-ho is opening his ten-slide presentation. His current opening is: "Hi, I am Joon-ho and today I will be presenting my AI tool for competitive intelligence research, which I built for marketing teams."

Rewrite his opening sixty seconds (what he would say aloud, not the slide content) to open in scene. Include: a specific person in a specific moment, a specific pain, and a natural transition to the tool he built.

*(Tests: 10/20/30 pitch structure — scene-opening vs. introduction-opening)*

**Answer:** Sample rewrite: "It is 6:58 on a Tuesday morning. Yuki, a marketing manager at a twelve-person SaaS company, opens her laptop before her team standup. **Exercise 12** *(Application)*

The chapter describes a post-course compounding plan as consisting of three types of commitments: portfolio additions, published pieces, and relationship engagements. The chapter says: "Write the post-course plan before the final presentation, not after."

Explain why the timing matters. What does writing the plan before the presentation — rather than after — accomplish that waiting until after the presentation cannot?

*(Tests: post-launch compounding plan — timing and mechanism)*

**Answer:** Writing the plan before the presentation ensures that the commitments are made while the motivation and momentum are live — while the work is recent, the identity is clear, and the specific opportunities (people who saw the presentation, specific roles mentioned, specific publications referenced) are immediately available. After the course ends, the specific context that makes each commitment legible begins to fade: which three case studies to add becomes a less clear decision when six months have passed and new projects have accumulated; which five people to engage becomes harder when the course connections have dispersed. The chapter's argument is that cadence is installed as a habit during the course's momentum period — a plan made after the presentation is not installed during the momentum period and therefore is less likely to be executed. The plan is also more specific when written before the presentation because the student knows exactly which projects are buildable extensions of the course's work, which topics emerged from the presentation's feedback, and which people in the room are the most relevant relationship targets.

**Common error:** Treating the timing as a productivity tip ("write it while it's fresh") rather than as a compounding mechanism. The chapter's argument is about installing habits during the momentum period, not just about memory.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "Building for After."

**Exercise 13** *(Application)*

The chapter distinguishes between building "a network" and building "relationships." In two sentences each, describe: (a) what the chapter means by a network, (b) what the chapter means by relationships, and (c) the specific action pattern the chapter recommends for converting a network into relationships.

*(Tests: post-launch compounding plan — network vs. relationship distinction)*

**Answer:** (a) A network is a list of people you have met — connections that exist as names in a contact list or LinkedIn connections, without substantive ongoing engagement. Maintaining a network requires no repeated investment. (b) Relationships are the five to ten people whose work you respect, whose archetype is compatible with yours, and with whom you engage substantively over time. Relationships compound: the opportunities that matter most — unposted jobs, partnerships, introductions — come from this small set, not from a large undifferentiated contact list. (c) The specific action pattern: identify five people whose work you have read in the course; engage with one piece of their work each month in a way that shows you read it closely — a specific thought, a question, a counterargument, a data point. Not "great post!" — a substantive, specific response. Do this once a month with each person for a year.

**Common error:** Treating "build relationships" as generic networking advice. The chapter's specific action pattern (five people, once a month, substantive engagement for a year) is the mechanism — not the aspiration.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "Building for After."

**Exercise 14** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

In Chapter 1, you learned about *Spence signaling theory* — the distinction between separating signals (costly, verifiable) and pooling signals (cheap, unverifiable). In this chapter, you are working with the *coherence principle* across the full professional surface stack (résumé, portfolio, LinkedIn, pitch presentation).

The chapter argues that a coherent professional presence — where every surface expresses the same archetype, positioning, and voice — produces better hiring outcomes than an incoherent presence of equivalent technical merit. Evaluate this claim using Spence signaling theory. Is the coherence of a professional surface stack a separating signal? Under what conditions?

*This question connects Spence signaling theory (Chapter 1) to the coherence principle across surfaces (Chapter 19).*

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- A strong answer identifies why coherence is a costly signal — what it requires to produce and why that cost is asymmetric
- A strong answer addresses whether coherence is *verifiable* by the receiver, which is the second Spence condition
- A strong answer identifies the conditions under which coherence might function as a pooling signal (e.g., if AI tools can produce surface-level coherence cheaply)

*(Tests: Spence signaling theory + coherence principle — Ch. 1 + Ch. 19)*

**Answer:** Coherence across a full professional surface stack is a separating signal under the conditions Spence specifies: it must be costly to produce and verifiable by the receiver. Producing genuine coherence — the same archetype expressed authentically through four different surfaces (résumé, portfolio, LinkedIn, pitch), each optimized for a different reading speed and audience — requires sustained identity work. A candidate without a genuinely clear archetype cannot produce coherent surfaces by imitating the form: the voice, the selection of projects, the specific positioning sentence, and the brand-position slide of the presentation all require a stable underlying identity to generate consistently. This makes coherence costly in the relevant sense: it is a signal that a low-identity candidate would struggle to produce at equivalent quality. The verifiability condition also holds: a hiring manager who reviews all four surfaces in sequence can detect incoherence — a different positioning sentence, a different archetype, a different voice — without the candidate present to explain it. The condition under which coherence functions as a pooling signal: if AI tools advance to the point where they can produce surface-level coherence (matching vocabulary and visual style across surfaces) without genuine identity work, then the signal cheapens. The chapter's larger bet — that the identity work underlying coherence is what AI tools cannot do — is the claim that keeps coherence costly.

*What a surface answer looks like:* "Yes, because having a consistent presence takes effort." This mentions both concepts but does not show the cost-asymmetry mechanism or address the verifiability condition.

*Common error:* Treating coherence as a design/aesthetics question rather than a signaling question.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "The Coherence Principle"; Chapter 1, "Spence Signaling Mechanism."

**Exercise 15** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

In Chapter 6, you learned about *brand archetypes as forcing functions* — that a committed archetype makes certain choices obvious and certain others unavailable. In this chapter, you are working with the *two résumé versions* (ATS-optimized and designer) and the principle that both must express one consistent identity.

A student asks: "If the ATS version must be plain — single column, standard fonts, no graphics — how can it express the archetype? Doesn't the ATS format override the archetype?"

Use the archetype-as-forcing-function principle to answer this question. Which elements of the ATS résumé format remain within the archetype's control, and which are format constraints that the archetype cannot override?

*This question connects archetype as forcing function (Chapter 6) to the ATS résumé format (Chapter 19).*

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- A strong answer identifies the specific elements the archetype controls within the ATS format (it is not zero)
- A strong answer explains why this is consistent with the archetype-as-forcing-function concept — the archetype constrains content and voice, not only visual expression
- A strong answer names a specific example of archetype expression in an ATS résumé

*(Tests: archetype as forcing function + ATS résumé format — Ch. 6 + Ch. 19)*

**Answer:** The archetype-as-forcing-function principle holds that an archetype constrains choices across all surfaces — including those with format restrictions. The ATS format constrains visual elements (fonts, colors, layout) but does not constrain: the selection of projects to list (a Sage lists projects that demonstrate insight and verified results; a Creator lists projects that demonstrate original production; a Caregiver lists projects that demonstrate helping others); the specific vocabulary of outcome bullets (a Sage's bullets are precise, data-dense, and use verification language — "processed," "measured," "validated at 90% accuracy"; a Rebel's use disruptive action verbs and frame results in terms of what was challenged or changed); the strategic use of keywords (a Sage's keyword selection emphasizes analytical tools and research methodology; a Creator's emphasizes generative systems and original output); and the structure of the summary or about section (voice remains archetype-consistent even in plain text). The ATS format cannot be overridden, but the archetype does not need to be expressed through visual elements to function as a forcing function — it operates at the level of what is selected, how it is framed, and what vocabulary is used to describe the work.

*What a surface answer looks like:* "The ATS format doesn't allow archetype expression, so both versions express different things." This gives up on the synthesis question rather than finding where the archetype operates within the constraint.

*Common error:* Treating the ATS constraint as eliminating archetype expression rather than redirecting it to content and voice.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 19, "The Resume — Two Versions, One Identity"; Chapter 6, "Archetypes as Forcing Functions."

**Ex 1** — *Three coherence properties*
*Model answer:* (1) Argument coherence — absent: ten different arguments, no single clear takeaway. (2) Specific numbers — absent: vague claims, no measurable evidence. (3) Team weight — absent: names and titles without relevant history.
*Common error:* Naming only one or two properties, or treating "coherence" generically.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Coherence Principle."

---

**Ex 2** — *30-point rule*
*Answer:* False.
*Correct because:* The rule is an editing discipline: forcing 30-point font makes dense slides impossible to format, which forces the presenter to edit content out of slides and into the speaking script.
*Common error:* Treating it as a visual design standard.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Final Presentation."

---

**Ex 3** — *ATS vs. designer résumé*
*Model answer:* ATS: single column, standard headers, keyword-rich, optimized for automated parsing. Designer: uses brand visual system, signals craft to a human reviewer who has cleared the filter. Both have identical content; only format differs.
*Common error:* Describing the designer version as having different or more personal content.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Resume — Two Versions, One Identity."

---

**Ex 4** — *Responsibility vs. outcome bullets*
*Model answer:* (a) Responsibility — missing: what the pipeline did, at what scale, what changed. (b) Outcome. (c) Responsibility — missing: what components, what scale, what result. (d) Outcome.
*Common error:* Labeling (a) as outcome because it mentions "building pipelines."
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Resume — Two Versions, One Identity."

---

**Ex 5** — *Ten-slide structure identification*
*Model answer:* (a) Slide 10 — The ask. (b) Slide 4 — How it works. (c) Slide 6 — Brand position. (d) Slide 7 — Validation.
*Common error:* Labeling (d) as Slide 5 (Demo) rather than Slide 7 (Validation).
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Final Presentation."

---

**Ex 6** — *Coherence properties applied to problem slide*
*Model answer:* Diagnosis: no argument chain link, no specific numbers, no implied solution. Rewrite: specific user, specific situation, specific time cost, specific consequence. At least one measurable claim required.
*Common error:* Rewriting with better language but still without a specific number.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Coherence Principle."

---

**Ex 7** — *LinkedIn error analysis*
*Headline failure:* Keyword list = pooling signal; no positioning sentence. *About section failure:* Template language, no specific work, no voice, passive ask. *Rewrite:* Headline is a positioning sentence; about section opens with what the work is for and who it serves; voice is archetype-consistent.
*Common error:* Adding one phrase to the headline rather than rewriting it as a positioning sentence.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "Social Media as Brand-Coherence Surface."

---

**Ex 8** — *AI Interaction — coherence principle*
*Model answer:* Strongest: "each slide built on the previous one." Imprecise: coherence treated as pitch-deck property rather than universal presentation property. Correction: three coherence properties apply to every professional surface. Verification test: ask one audience member "what is the one thing this presentation is arguing?"
*What a strong answer includes:* The surface-generalizability of the coherence properties; a concrete, specific verification method.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Coherence Principle."

---

**Ex 9** — *Self-explanation: why the Airbnb deck was chosen*
*Model answer:* The Airbnb deck's lack of polish isolates coherence as the operative variable; a visually polished deck would conflate design quality with argument quality. The chapter uses it to demonstrate that coherence produces outcomes independently of visual quality.
*Common error:* Describing the deck as successful "despite" its lack of polish, rather than because polish was irrelevant.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Coherence Principle."

---

**Ex 10** — *Cumulative: coherence audit + ten-slide structure*
*Model answer:* Failure 1 — different positioning sentence on Slide 1 than LinkedIn/portfolio. Check: compare Slide 1 text to LinkedIn headline word-for-word. Failure 2 — Slide 6 brand position contradicts brand strategy archetype. Check: compare Slide 6 copy to brand strategy document's archetype section.
*Common error:* Identifying only surface-level format differences rather than specific content failure modes.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Final Presentation"; Ch. 18, "Deploy and align."

---

**Ex 11** — *Scene opening*
*Model answer:* Specific person + specific time + specific sequence of actions + specific pain + natural transition to the tool. "Hi, I'm presenting..." is disqualifying.
*Common error:* Opening with "Imagine a marketing manager who..." — a generic hypothetical, not a specific scene.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Final Presentation — Opening and Closing."

---

**Ex 12** — *Post-launch plan timing*
*Model answer:* Writing before the presentation installs habits during the momentum period — when the identity is clear, specific opportunities are immediately available, and the plan can be specific to the course's work. After the course, context fades and specificity weakens.
*Common error:* Treating timing as a productivity tip ("fresh memory") rather than a habit-installation mechanism.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "Building for After."

---

**Ex 13** — *Network vs. relationships*
*Model answer:* Network = list of contacts, no repeated investment required. Relationships = small set of people with compatible archetypes and substantive ongoing engagement. Action pattern: five people, once a month, substantive specific response, for a year.
*Common error:* Treating "build relationships" as generic networking advice rather than as a specific action pattern.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "Building for After."

---

**Ex 14** — *Synthesis: Spence signaling + coherence principle*
*Model answer:* Coherence is costly (requires genuine identity work a low-identity candidate cannot replicate at equivalent quality) and verifiable (a hiring manager reviewing all four surfaces can detect incoherence). Potential pooling condition: if AI tools can produce surface-level coherence without identity work.
*What a strong answer includes:* Cost-asymmetry mechanism; verifiability condition; conditions under which coherence could become a pooling signal.
*Common error:* Treating coherence as a design question rather than a signaling question.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Coherence Principle"; Ch. 1, "Spence Signaling Mechanism."

---

**Ex 15** — *Synthesis: archetype as forcing function + ATS format*
*Model answer:* The ATS format constrains visual elements but not: project selection, outcome bullet vocabulary, keyword strategy, summary voice. The archetype operates at the level of what is selected and how it is framed, not only through visual expression.
*What a strong answer includes:* Specific elements the archetype controls within ATS format; example of archetype expression in plain text.
*Common error:* Treating the ATS constraint as eliminating archetype expression entirely.
*Chapter reference:* Ch. 19, "The Resume — Two Versions, One Identity"; Ch. 6, "Archetypes as Forcing Functions."

**Error analysis (Ex 7):** The deliberate errors target the two most common LinkedIn failures — keyword-list headlines and template about sections. In student work, watch for any headline that is a job title or a skill list rather than a positioning sentence.

**AI interaction (Ex 8):** The AI response contains a real gap: treating the coherence properties as pitch-deck-specific rather than generalizable to all professional surfaces. Students who accept the response without noting this miss the chapter's central extension of the Airbnb analysis.

**Cumulative exercise (Ex 10):** Requires Chapter 18's coherence audit. The synthesis asks students to identify failure modes the Ch. 18 audit would not catch — which requires understanding what the audit checks and what it does not.

**Common errors to watch for:**
- Treating the 30-point rule as a design preference rather than an editing discipline (Ex 2)
- Labeling responsibility statements as outcomes because they mention building things (Ex 4)
- Treating network and relationship-building as equivalent terms (Ex 13)
