## Worked Example

**Problem:** A graduate student is building a personal brand as an AI engineer. She has written the following positioning statement:

> "I help technology teams use AI effectively."

Her advisor reviews it and says: "This is a pooling statement, not a positioning statement. Rewrite it." 

Using the chapter's four criteria for a negative-space list (specific, archetype-consistent, costly, testable), explain why the original statement fails each criterion and produce a revised statement that meets all four.

**Approach:** Apply each of the four criteria from Chapter 6's negative-space list to the original statement, diagnosing the failure. Then rewrite using the same four criteria as a construction guide.

**Answer:**

*Specific:* "Technology teams" is not a specific audience (every technology team?). "AI effectively" is not a specific outcome (what does effective mean?). The statement could apply to consultants, professors, software vendors, and coaches simultaneously — it does not specify who it is for or what it uniquely produces.

*Archetype-consistent:* The statement has no archetype signal whatsoever. "Help teams use AI" is neutral — it could belong to a Caregiver, a Sage, a Hero, or a Ruler. The statement does not constrain what voice, approach, or values the positioning implies.

*Costly:* The statement is not costly to claim. Any AI consultant, vendor, or educator could put this exact statement on their website in an afternoon. There is no commitment, no specificity, and no proof required to make this claim.

*Testable:* There is no way to determine whether this positioning has been achieved. What would "help technology teams use AI effectively" look like when it succeeded? How would you know? The statement generates no measurable behavioral criteria.

**Revised statement (for a Sage archetype, ML engineer audience):** "I help machine learning engineers at early-stage AI companies diagnose why their production models degrade faster than expected — through structured post-deployment audits, not generic MLOps advice."

*Specific:* ML engineers, early-stage AI companies, production model degradation. *Archetype-consistent:* Sage — evidence-based, structured, diagnosis-first. *Costly:* "Post-deployment audits" implies a specific deliverable that requires deep ML expertise to produce. *Testable:* "Was there a structured audit? Did it produce a degradation diagnosis?" are answerable questions.

**What to notice:** Positioning is not about describing what you do — it is about specifying for whom, with what constraint, through what method, that makes your approach distinguishable from alternatives. The revised statement tells you who to call it for, what the problem is, and what is NOT offered (not generic MLOps advice).

### Contrastive Classification (Required)

**1.1** Below are two mission statements for technology brands. Classify each as either a *strong mission statement* or a *weak mission statement* relative to Chapter 6's criteria, and explain the classification in one sentence for each.

Statement A: "To organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful." (Google)

Statement B: "We exist to help people and companies work smarter."

`(Tests: mission statement — specificity and distinctiveness criteria)`

**Answer:** Statement A is a *strong* mission statement: it names a specific activity (organize information), a specific scope (the world's information), and a specific quality criteria (accessible and useful) — and it is differentiated enough that you would not confuse it with a mission statement from a bank, a hospital, or a consumer goods company. Statement B is a *weak* mission statement: "help people and companies work smarter" could belong to any productivity company, consulting firm, HR software vendor, or office supply brand — it is so generic that it does not constrain any brand decision or identify any specific contribution.

*Common error:* Students often rate Statement B positively because it is written in plain language and sounds aspirational. Plain language and aspiration are not the same as specificity and distinctiveness.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "Seven Components — Mission."

**1.2** Name the seven components of a brand strategy document as described in Chapter 6. For each component, write one sentence describing its role in the overall document and one sentence describing what typically goes wrong when that component is missing.

`(Tests: seven brand strategy components — identity and interdependence)`

**Answer:**

*Mission:* States why the brand exists and what it is trying to change in the world; without it, every other component lacks a north star and the document is a collection of preferences rather than a strategy.

*Vision:* Describes the future state the brand is working toward; without it, strategy is tactical and short-term rather than oriented toward a durable destination.

*Values:* Defines the principles the brand will not compromise under pressure; without it, the brand has no behavioral constraint and "values" become marketing copy that disappears when values are expensive to maintain.

*UVP (Unique Value Proposition):* Specifies what the brand offers that alternatives do not, for a specific audience in a specific context; without it, positioning is impossible to make precise and the brand competes on generic terms.

*Archetype:* Defines the brand's personality, motivation, and relationship to its audience; without it, voice, visual, and content decisions require deliberation rather than following from a forcing function.

*Voice:* Specifies how the brand speaks — vocabulary, register, what it refuses to say; without it, every piece of communication requires a new creative direction and consistency is impossible at scale.

*Positioning:* States specifically how the brand is placed in the audience's mind relative to alternatives; without it, the brand cannot accumulate a distinct recognition asset and competes in the market's default framing.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "Seven Components of Brand Strategy."

**1.3** The chapter identifies four criteria for a strong positioning statement (or UVP): specific, archetype-consistent, costly, and testable. Apply these four criteria to the following positioning statement and identify which criteria it meets and which it fails.

> "Riya is an AI engineer specializing in building customer-facing AI products that increase user retention for subscription software companies."

`(Tests: negative-space list — four criteria for UVP/positioning)`

**Answer:**

*Specific:* Passes. Audience (subscription software companies), use case (customer-facing AI products), and outcome (increase user retention) are all named. Not every AI engineer would claim this specialty.

*Archetype-consistent:* Needs evaluation. The statement is neutral — it could belong to several archetypes. A Hero would frame it in achievement terms; a Sage in evidence terms; a Creator in originality terms. The statement doesn't fail this criterion, but it also doesn't activate any archetype signal — it would need to be read alongside other brand signals to determine consistency.

*Costly:* Passes (partially). "Customer-facing AI products that increase user retention" implies a track record that would be verified through portfolio evidence — this is more costly to claim than a generic "AI engineer" label. However, the claim becomes fully costly only when backed by measurable retention data.

*Testable:* Passes (partially). "Increase user retention" is measurable, but the statement needs a metric and a comparison baseline to be fully testable. A testable version would specify: "quantifiable increase in 30-day user retention (target: >15 percentage points) in at least two prior deployments."

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "Negative-Space List — Four Criteria."

**1.4** The chapter uses the Stripe case as a worked example of a brand strategy document. Using the Stripe example, explain what the "coherence check on adjacent component pairs" means and give one example of a coherence failure that the check would surface.

`(Tests: coherence check — adjacent component pairs and the specification test)`

**Answer:** The coherence check examines whether consecutive components of the strategy document are mutually consistent — whether the mission logically connects to the vision, whether the values enable the UVP, whether the archetype is expressed in the voice, and so on. Adjacent components should reinforce rather than contradict each other; a gap between any pair indicates an under-specified or inconsistent strategy. In the Stripe example: the Sage archetype is coherent with a mission focused on increasing the GDP of the internet (a knowledge-expansion, infrastructure-building purpose), with values that include evidence and precision, and with a voice that is technical and direct — every adjacent pair pulls in the same direction. An example of a coherence failure the check would surface: if a company's stated archetype is Jester (playful, irreverent, humor-forward) but their values include "enterprise reliability" and "institutional trust" and their voice is formal and technical, the archetype-values pair would fail the coherence check — the personality and the operational commitments are in direct contradiction.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "Coherence Check," "Stripe Case."

**1.5** The chapter describes the "bar test," "lawyer test," and "domain test" for brand naming, plus the archetype alignment criterion. Apply all four to the following proposed brand name for an AI-powered legal research tool: *Veritas*.

`(Tests: naming criteria — four tests for brand name evaluation)`

**Answer:**

*Bar test:* "Veritas" does not immediately communicate what the product does — someone hearing the name in a noisy bar would need explanation. It is not memorable enough to be immediately identifiable, and it is ambiguous (it could be a consulting firm, a university publication, a food brand). Partial fail: the name works in a context where its meaning is explained, but fails at pure ambient communication.

*Lawyer test:* "Veritas" is a Latin word (meaning "truth") that is generic and widely used across industries — it would face significant challenges in trademark registration because it is descriptive/generic in multiple categories, particularly for legal or information services where "truth" is a common aspirational claim. Many existing companies and products use "Veritas" as a name. Significant risk of a lawyer test failure in the legal sector specifically.

*Domain test:* Veritas.com is almost certainly taken (it is — a major enterprise software company uses it). Any domain-available variation (veritas-legal.com, myveritas.com) reduces the brand signal from the name.

*Archetype alignment:* "Veritas" (truth) is strongly aligned with a Sage archetype for a legal research tool — evidence, accuracy, and truth-seeking are the Sage's core values, and the Latin register signals intellectual seriousness. This criterion passes clearly.

*Overall:* The name is archetype-coherent and evocative but faces practical obstacles on the lawyer test (trademark) and domain test that would require finding an available variation.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "Naming Criteria — Four Tests."

## Tier 2 — Apply and Analyze

### Error Analysis (Required)

**2.1** A student has submitted the following brand strategy document for review:

> **Mission:** To empower developers to build better software.
>
> **Vision:** A world where software development is accessible to everyone.
>
> **Values:** Innovation, Collaboration, Excellence, Customer Focus, Integrity.
>
> **UVP:** We provide powerful AI tools that make developers more productive.
>
> **Archetype:** Hero (we help developers overcome challenges).
>
> **Voice:** Professional, helpful, innovative, supportive.
>
> **Positioning:** The leading AI development tool for modern software teams.

Identify at least three specific problems with this document relative to Chapter 6's criteria. Then identify one adjacent component pair that fails the coherence check, explain why it fails, and propose a correction for that pair.

`(Tests: brand strategy document — quality criteria and coherence check)`

**Answer — Problems:**

Problem 1: *The mission, vision, and UVP are generic.* "Empower developers," "accessible to everyone," "powerful AI tools," and "more productive" could appear on the website of any developer tooling company in the world. None of these statements specifies who the specific developer is, what specific challenge is being addressed, or why this approach produces a result alternatives do not.

Problem 2: *The values are a list of generic virtues.* "Innovation, Collaboration, Excellence, Customer Focus, Integrity" are the same five values on half the values walls in North America. Values should specify what the brand will do when values are in conflict or when maintaining them is costly — these values produce no behavioral constraint.

Problem 3: *The archetype is derived from what users do, not from brand behavior.* "Hero (we help developers overcome challenges)" commits the same error as the digital health example — the archetype is assigned based on the user's journey, not the brand's expressed personality, motivation, and relationship to the audience.

Problem 4 (bonus): *The positioning uses the word "leading."* A positioning statement that claims to be "the leading" anything is untestable and makes no specific claim about differentiation.

**Coherence failure — UVP and Archetype:**
The UVP ("powerful AI tools that make developers more productive") is generic and feature-focused; it does not activate any archetype signal. The archetype is claimed as Hero. However, a Hero brand positions itself as the means by which the developer achieves mastery and overcomes obstacles — which implies a different UVP framing: not "we make you productive" but "we help you win the battles your competitors cannot." The current UVP is Everyman-voice (practical, useful, widely accessible) while the claimed archetype is Hero (competitive, achievement-focused, excellence-demanding). These are incoherent because a Hero brand would not describe its value as "more productive" — it would describe it as "the competitive edge your rivals don't have."

**Correction:** Either change the archetype to Everyman or Ruler (more coherent with a productivity/team-scale framing) or rewrite the UVP to match the Hero: "The only AI development tool designed for teams that refuse to ship second-best code — because 'good enough' isn't a competitive position."

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "Seven Components," "Coherence Check."

### Self-Explanation Exercise (Required)

**2.2** The chapter describes the one-page brand strategy document as a "specification test" — a constraint that determines whether the strategy is specific enough to generate decisions. Using only Chapter 6's concepts and vocabulary, explain what a specification test is in this context and why the one-page format specifically serves this function.

`(Tests: specification test and one-page constraint — principled reasoning about format as discipline)`

**Answer (model):** A specification test is a constraint that forces vagueness to become visible: a strategy that is too abstract to fit coherently on one page is a strategy that is too abstract to generate decisions. The one-page format serves this function in three ways. First, it requires compression: every component must be stated in a few sentences, which eliminates hedging, qualification, and the appearance of specificity without substance — a vague mission cannot hide behind three paragraphs of elaboration when it must occupy two sentences. Second, the single page allows adjacent component pairs to be read together quickly, making coherence failures immediately visible: if the archetype in line 4 contradicts the voice in line 6, the reader can see both in the same view without turning pages. Third, the page limit is a scope discipline that forces the brand team to decide what belongs in the strategy and what belongs in the execution plan — a one-page document cannot contain both, which forces the team to distinguish fundamental commitments (strategy) from implementation choices (execution). The format is therefore not a presentation convenience but an analytical tool: a document that cannot be produced on one page is evidence that the strategy has not yet been resolved to a specific-enough level.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "One-Page Document as Specification Test."

**2.3** Chapter 5 developed the five-input archetype identification procedure (tagline/visual/refusals/customer treatment/crisis behavior). Chapter 6 places the archetype as one of seven strategy components that must cohere with each other. Apply both chapters' frameworks to the following scenario:

A startup provides an AI-powered tool for academic researchers to find and synthesize relevant literature. Their stated archetype is Sage. Their brand strategy document includes the following:

- Mission: To accelerate scientific progress by making research accessible.
- Vision: Every researcher has access to the insights they need, regardless of institution or budget.
- Values: Openness, Speed, Scale, Disruption.
- Voice: Casual, friendly, accessible, encouraging.
- Positioning: The democratizing force in academic research.

Apply Chapter 5's coherence test between stated archetype and expressed behavior. Then apply Chapter 6's coherence check between adjacent component pairs.

*(References Chapter 5's three archetype validity tests; Chapter 6's coherence check and component definitions.)*

`(Tests: archetype coherence [Chapter 5] + strategy component coherence [Chapter 6] — integrated diagnosis)`

**Answer (model):** 

From Chapter 5: The Sage's core motivation is knowledge, evidence, and rigorous truth-seeking. Testing the stated components against the Sage's expressed behavior pattern:

- *Values — "Disruption"* conflicts with the Sage archetype. Disruption is the Rebel's value; the Sage is more likely to value accuracy, rigor, and comprehensiveness. "Openness" and "Accessibility" are consistent with the Sage but not "Disruption."
- *Voice — "Casual, friendly, accessible, encouraging"* is closer to the Caregiver or Everyman archetype than the Sage. The Sage's voice is precise, evidence-based, and intellectually demanding — not casual.
- *Positioning — "Democratizing force"* is again Rebel/Outlaw framing — it centers disruption of an existing power structure, not the Sage's characteristic focus on building better knowledge.

The expressed archetype (through values, voice, and positioning) is closer to a Rebel or Caregiver than to a Sage. The team has stated Sage but expressed something else.

From Chapter 6: Adjacent pair coherence failures:
- *Values → Voice:* "Disruption" (value) is in tension with "encouraging" and "accessible" (voice) — disruptive brands are challenging and provocative, not primarily encouraging.
- *Archetype (Sage) → Voice (casual/friendly):* A Sage archetype requires a voice that signals epistemic authority and rigor — casual and friendly signals are coherent with Caregiver, not Sage.
- *Mission ("accessible") → Values ("Disruption"):* "Making research accessible" is an inclusive mission; "disruption" is a combative value — the mission wants to welcome everyone while the value wants to challenge existing power structures.

Recommendation: Commit to either Sage (which requires rewriting values to include Rigor, Evidence, Precision, and rewriting voice to Technical and Direct) or Rebel (which requires rewriting mission and vision to focus on challenging the gatekeeping structures of academic publishing).

**2.5** The chapter argues that "negative space" in a brand strategy — what the brand explicitly will not do — is as important as what it will do. Using the concept of the negative-space list from Chapter 6, explain why a brand that cannot articulate what it refuses to do has a weaker strategic position than one that can.

`(Tests: negative-space list — rationale for explicit refusals in positioning)`

**Answer:** A brand that cannot articulate what it refuses to do has not actually made a positioning choice — it has described a set of aspirations while leaving the actual differentiation undefined. Positioning requires a specific claim, and a specific claim implies that its opposite is refused: "I am the precise, evidence-based tool" implies "I am not the fast, casual, exploratory tool." Without the explicit refusal, the brand is attempting to position to every potential audience simultaneously, which is the pooling equilibrium Spence describes — a claim so generic that it fails to separate the brand from alternatives. The negative-space list also functions as a budget constraint: if the brand commits to refusing social media monitoring (as in the Chapter 6 MVP boundary example), it commits those resources to doing the in-scope things better. A brand that refuses nothing has no resource constraint on its scope, which means it will build an incoherent product trying to serve everyone.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "Negative-Space List."

## Tier 3 — Analyze and Evaluate

**3.1** The chapter places the brand strategy document as the synthesis of all prior chapters — the point where archetype, audience, and positioning come together into a single specification. But critics of brand strategy argue: "A brand strategy document is a management performance artifact — it creates the *appearance* of strategic clarity without producing any constraint on actual decisions. Real brand clarity comes from leadership behavior, not from documents."

Evaluate this critique. Under what conditions is it correct? Under what conditions does the brand strategy document produce genuine constraint? Reference at least two components of the seven-component framework in your evaluation.

*(References Chapter 6's specification test and seven components; also draws on Chapter 5's archetype as forcing function.)*

`(Tests: brand strategy document — evaluating its function as a constraint vs. as a performance artifact)`

**Model answer:** The critique is correct under a specific condition: when the brand strategy document is written without the negative-space list, without the coherence check, and without the specification test — in other words, when it is written as an aspirational document rather than a decision-generating one. A values list of "Innovation, Collaboration, Excellence" passes no specification test: it cannot produce a specific decision when values are in conflict because the values are too generic to have an order of priority. When written this way, the document is indeed a management performance artifact.

The document produces genuine constraint when the components pass the specification test and when adjacent pairs are checked for coherence. Specifically: the archetype component (connecting to Chapter 5's forcing function) produces behavioral constraint — a Sage brand that has written "evidence-based, cites sources, never overstates" as its voice component has a document that creates a specific test for every piece of content produced. The positioning component — if it meets the four criteria (specific, archetype-consistent, costly, testable) — produces constraint by specifying what the brand will not compete on, which makes resource allocation decisions decidable. The critique is therefore an accurate description of how brand strategy documents typically fail, not of how they must fail.

*Common error:* Accepting the critique wholesale without identifying the condition under which documents produce constraint.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "Specification Test," "Seven Components," "Coherence Check"; Chapter 5, "Archetype as Forcing Function."

**3.2** The chapter presents the naming framework (bar/lawyer/domain/archetype tests) and argues that naming is a brand strategy decision, not a marketing execution decision. A startup founder argues: "Naming is the most overrated part of brand strategy. Products become what their behaviors make them — Amazon was a nonsense word until Amazon made it mean something. We should ship the product, build equity through behavior, and rename if needed." Evaluate this argument, using Chapter 6's naming framework and Chapter 2's brand equity concepts.

*(References Chapter 6's naming criteria; also draws on Chapter 2's brand equity accumulation.)*

`(Tests: naming criteria — evaluating the "behavior over name" argument against brand equity accumulation logic)`

**Model answer:** The founder's argument is partially correct about what ultimately builds brand equity — Chapter 2 is clear that equity accumulates through consistent experiences, not through name selection. Amazon is a genuine example: a geographically arbitrary name that became associated with its brand through behavior, not etymology.

However, the argument underestimates two naming costs. First, the lawyer test cost: a name that fails trademark clearance or infringes on an existing mark creates legal liability that grows with the brand's success. The cost of a name change after equity has accumulated (Chapter 2 addresses the Gap 2010 case) is far higher than the cost of selecting a defensible name upfront — not because the name was wrong but because the associations embedded in the old name are equity that cannot be simply transferred. Second, the domain and bar test costs are most expensive precisely when the founder's approach succeeds: a product that builds equity through behavior will eventually need a direct-access channel (a domain) and word-of-mouth transmission (the bar test). A name that fails the domain or bar test creates friction in the highest-leverage channels at the moment of success. The founder's approach and the chapter's framework are not incompatible — "ship fast and rename if needed" is viable for a product with minimal equity accumulated; it becomes expensive after equity accumulates.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 6, "Naming Criteria"; Chapter 2, "Brand Equity Accumulation."
