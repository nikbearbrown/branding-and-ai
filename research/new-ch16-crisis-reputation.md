# Research — Ch 16: Crisis & Reputation Management

**Slot:** Core, Part IV · **Status:** NEW (expands the reputation-monitor recipe) · **Madison:** `madison-brand-news-reputation-monitor`
**Running-project deliverable:** a reputation-monitoring setup + a crisis response playbook for the chosen brand.

## Why this chapter exists
The book had a reputation-*monitor* recipe but no theory of *responding* when the brand is under
attack — the highest-stakes brand moment, now compressed by social-media speed.

## Canon & sources
- **Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT):** W. Timothy Coombs (*Ongoing Crisis
  Communication*) — match the response strategy (deny / diminish / rebuild / bolster) to the crisis
  type and the organization's responsibility level.
- **Image Repair Theory:** Benoit — response options (denial, evasion of responsibility,
  corrective action, mortification).
- **The gold-standard case:** Johnson & Johnson **Tylenol (1982)** — fast recall, public
  transparency, customer-safety-first; contrast with slow/defensive failures.
- **Reputation management:** the speed asymmetry of social media (a crisis spreads in hours);
  pre-built playbooks, holding statements, escalation paths, monitoring → triage → response.
- Links back to Ch 13 (sentiment tracking) and Ch 15 (a values breach *is* the crisis).

## Core concepts to teach
Crisis types and matching response (SCCT); responsibility-level → strategy; speed and the holding
statement; corrective action vs. spin; the pre-written playbook (decide before the fire); when
silence vs. response is correct.

## Common misconceptions
- A crisis is a comms problem (it's an operations + values problem; comms is the visible part).
- Always respond fast (wrong-fast can worsen; the playbook sets thresholds).
- Monitoring = management (detecting ≠ responding well).
