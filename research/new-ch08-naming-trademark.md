## Why this chapter exists
The old book had a brief naming section (bar/lawyer/domain/archetype tests) and no IP coverage.
Naming is where branding meets law; getting it wrong is expensive and sometimes fatal.

## Canon & sources
- **Trademark strength spectrum (also a naming taxonomy):** generic → descriptive → suggestive →
  arbitrary → fanciful. Fanciful/arbitrary marks (Kodak, Apple-for-computers) are strongest and
  most protectable; descriptive marks are weak and need "secondary meaning." Source: US trademark
  law / *Abercrombie* spectrum.
- **Lanham Act** (US trademark statute); **likelihood of confusion** is the core infringement test.
- **USPTO / TESS** (Trademark Electronic Search System) for clearance; **Nice Classification**
  (international classes of goods/services) — software is typically Class 9/42. `[verify]` — USPTO
  replaced/updated TESS tooling; confirm the current search system name and process.
- **Trade dress** (look/feel protection) and **domains** (ICANN/UDRP for disputes); trademark ≠
  domain ≠ company registration — three separate things.
- **INTA** (International Trademark Association) for practitioner guidance.

## Core concepts to teach
The strength spectrum as both a creative and legal filter; clearance before commitment; the three
separate rights (mark, domain, entity); when to consult an attorney (the book gives literacy, not
legal advice — route real clearance to counsel).

## Common misconceptions
- "I bought the domain, so I own the name." (No — that's not trademark rights.)
- Descriptive names are safe because they're clear (they're the hardest to protect).
- A TESS/search "no result" = cleared (likelihood-of-confusion is broader than exact match).
