# Research — Ch 17: Global & Cross-Cultural Branding

**Slot:** Core, Part IV · **Status:** NEW · **Madison:** localization + `content-agent`
**Running-project deliverable:** a localization/adaptation plan for the chosen brand entering one new market.

## Why this chapter exists
The book was implicitly single-market/US-tech. Brands cross cultures, and the standardize-vs-adapt
choice is a core strategic decision with famous failure modes.

## Canon & sources
- **Standardization vs. adaptation (glocalization):** Theodore Levitt's "globalization of markets"
  (standardize) vs. the adaptation school; most brands land on **glocalization** (global strategy,
  local execution).
- **Cultural dimensions:** Geert Hofstede (power distance, individualism/collectivism, uncertainty
  avoidance, masculinity/femininity, long-term orientation, indulgence) — a lens for *why* a brand
  reads differently across cultures. `[verify]` current dimension set/data.
- **Cross-cultural advertising:** Marieke de Mooij, *Global Marketing and Advertising* —
  consumer behavior varies by culture; copy/imagery don't transfer unchanged.
- **Localization vs. translation vs. transcreation:** transcreation = re-create the intent, not the
  words; naming blunders across languages (the classic cautionary cases) — `[verify]` specific
  examples before citing (several popular ones are apocryphal).

## Core concepts to teach
The standardize↔adapt spectrum and its trade-offs (scale/consistency vs. local resonance); culture
as a real variable (Hofstede as a starting lens, not a stereotype generator); transcreation over
literal translation; what must stay global (the core) vs. what localizes (expression).

## Common misconceptions
- Localization = translation (it's cultural re-creation).
- A global brand should look identical everywhere (the core holds; the expression flexes).
- Cultural dimensions = stereotypes (they're population-level tendencies, not individual scripts).
