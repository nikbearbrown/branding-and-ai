## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* Pine and Gilmore's model progresses: Commodity (undifferentiated raw material, lowest value) → Good (manufactured, tangible product) → Service (intangible, customized to the buyer) → Experience (memorable, staged, personally meaningful — highest value). The chapter uses this model to explain why brands must invest in experience design as commoditization erodes margins at lower levels.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Service comes before Good in this ordering, which reverses the model.
- C) This is the correct order read in reverse.
*Chapter reference:* Pine/Gilmore Experience Economy

---

**Q2 — False**
*Correct because:* The ZMOT (Zero Moment of Truth), coined by Google, refers to the pre-purchase moment when a consumer researches a product online before deciding to buy — it occurs before the customer has used the product at all. The First Moment of Truth (FMOT) is the in-store purchase decision; the Second Moment of Truth (SMOT) is the actual use experience where the product proves (or fails to prove) its promise.
*Chapter reference:* ZMOT / FMOT / SMOT — moments of truth

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* Shostack's service blueprint uses a "line of visibility" to separate what the customer can see and experience (front-stage: interactions, visible processes, environment) from the operational infrastructure the customer never sees (back-stage: support systems, policies, staff training, IT infrastructure). The distinction matters because both sides affect the quality of the customer experience, but only front-stage elements are directly perceived.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The line does not rank quality; it separates visibility.
- C) The line is about operational visibility, not about alignment between claims and delivery.
*Chapter reference:* Shostack service blueprinting / line of visibility

---

**Q4 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines brand constants as the fixed elements that remain consistent regardless of context (the core archetype, foundational visual identity elements, voice principles) and brand variables as the elements that flex within the system to suit different contexts (tone warmth, visual weight, specific word choices). Both are part of the brand system; the difference is their degree of contextual adaptation.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) The chapter does not define variables as "optional" — they are a structured, intentional part of the brand system.
- C) The chapter does not define constants and variables by which team controls them.
*Chapter reference:* Brand constants vs. brand variables

---

**Q5 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter introduces the experience-strategy gap as the central diagnostic problem in brand experience design: the brand strategy specifies an intended experience, but the actual touchpoints often deliver something different. The gap is where brand equity erodes even when the strategy is well-designed.
*Chapter reference:* Experience-strategy gap

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* The SMOT is the moment of ongoing use — after the initial excitement of adoption, the moment when the product must prove it delivers on its promise consistently. A poor error experience during core workflow use is a SMOT failure: the product did not hold up under real conditions, breaking the path toward advocacy and loyalty. ZMOT occurs before purchase; FMOT is the initial adoption moment.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) ZMOT is pre-purchase research; the scenario describes a user already using the product.
- C) FMOT is the "store aisle" purchase decision moment — the initial commitment to try; the scenario is post-adoption.
*Chapter reference:* SMOT / moments of truth

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The four AI concierge failure states are: (1) Empty state — the user opens the tool and there is nothing there to guide them; (2) Error state — the system fails and the error message is confusing or unhelpful; (3) Frustrated-user state — the user has tried to use the tool correctly but keeps failing; (4) "Cannot answer" state. The "cannot answer" state is specifically damaging because the user came with a question the AI concierge is positioned to answer, and the tool explicitly refuses — creating the experience of being turned away by the entity that was supposed to help. Unlike an error (a technical failure) or frustration (a usability failure), "cannot answer" is a capability failure at the moment of highest intent, which directly contradicts the brand's core promise.
*Common error:* Listing only two or three failure states, or treating "cannot answer" as equivalent to an error state.
*Chapter reference:* Four AI concierge failure states

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter frames journey mapping as a diagnostic tool for finding the experience-strategy gap — where intended experience diverges most from delivered experience. The primary use from the brand's perspective is identifying which touchpoints need the most design investment to close the gap.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Traffic volume is not the chapter's primary criterion for touchpoint investment.
- C) Journey maps are not presented as investor presentation tools.
*Chapter reference:* Customer journey map / experience-strategy gap

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer)*
*Model answer:* Shostack's service blueprint reveals the back-stage infrastructure that enables (or fails to enable) the front-stage experience — the policies, training, systems, and support processes that customers never see but that determine whether the customer-facing experience is consistent. A customer journey map shows what the customer experiences at each touchpoint; a service blueprint shows what must be functioning invisibly for that experience to be possible. Blueprinting catches failure modes that journey mapping conceals: a touchpoint can look fine on a journey map while being unsustainably supported by back-stage processes that will break under load.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- What blueprinting adds (back-stage visibility)
- What journey mapping alone conceals (back-stage failure modes)
- Why the distinction matters for brand experience design
*Chapter reference:* Shostack service blueprinting

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The moments-of-truth framework suggests that FMOT (the first use experience) cannot be deferred because it is the moment when the user's expectations, set by ZMOT (pre-purchase research), are either confirmed or broken — and a broken FMOT creates churn before any advocacy is possible. For a SaaS product, FMOT includes onboarding, error handling, and core workflow success. These are the touchpoints that determine retention, not just acquisition. The PM's instinct to "polish later" misses that polish at the first use moment is structurally more valuable than polish on marketing surfaces — a bad FMOT creates negative equity that no amount of marketing can undo.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Identification of which touchpoints cannot be deferred (FMOT / SMOT)
- The moment-of-truth mechanism explaining why these are critical
- Why the PM's framing conflates marketing polish with experience integrity
*Chapter reference:* Moments of truth / experience-strategy gap

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* Most AI writing tools currently sit at the "service" level in Pine and Gilmore's model — they are customized to the user's input, but the output is relatively uniform and the interaction is transactional rather than memorable. To move toward "experience," an AI writing tool would need to create something that feels personally meaningful and staged — perhaps through a distinct creative interaction model (a collaborator that probes and challenges, rather than just completing prompts), a consistent voice that the user comes to recognize and value, or a ritual of use that becomes part of the writer's creative identity. The commodity pressure in AI writing tools is severe (many tools produce similar outputs), which is exactly the Pine/Gilmore situation that demands experience-level differentiation.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- A classification of where AI writing tools currently sit on the spectrum
- A concrete description of what "experience" would mean in this domain
- Connection to the commoditization pressure driving the progression
*Chapter reference:* Pine/Gilmore Experience Economy (transfer application)
