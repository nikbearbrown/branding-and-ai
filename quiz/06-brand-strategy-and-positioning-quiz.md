## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter's seven components include Mission, Vision, Values, UVP (Unique Value Proposition), Archetype, Voice, and Positioning. Option A correctly names four of the seven.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) Logo, color palette, and typography are visual identity elements (Chapter 9), not brand strategy components.
- C) Market size, revenue model, and competitive landscape are business strategy elements, not the chapter's brand strategy components.
*Chapter reference:* Seven components of brand strategy

---

**Q2 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly distinguishes brand strategy (what the brand is, what it stands for, and how it positions itself) from marketing strategy (how the brand acquires and retains customers through specific tactics, channels, and campaigns). Brand strategy is more durable and upstream; marketing strategy executes against it.
*Chapter reference:* Brand strategy vs. marketing strategy

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter uses "negative space" to mean the things a brand declines — the refusals that define its position. Just as white space in design gives shape to positive elements, a brand's refusals give shape to its commitments. The chapter is explicit that this is a strategic, not visual, concept.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) White space in visual design is a visual concept; the chapter's negative space is strategic.
- C) Untargeted future markets is not the chapter's definition; negative space is about active, declared refusals.
*Chapter reference:* Negative space in brand strategy

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter highlights Stripe's developer-first documentation, analytical voice, and treatment of customers as intelligent peers — all expressions of the Sage archetype's core motivation of providing authoritative, trustworthy knowledge to people who want to understand deeply.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Pricing strategy is not presented as Stripe's Sage archetype signal.
- C) International expansion and marketing volume are not Sage archetype markers.
*Chapter reference:* Stripe as Sage archetype model

---

**Q5 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter describes the bar test exactly this way: if you have to spell a name out in conversation for a stranger to understand it, the name fails the bar test. The test checks for phonetic clarity and ease of verbal transmission in noisy, informal settings.
*Chapter reference:* Bar test / naming tests

---

**Q6 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter's four criteria for valid negative space are: Specific (not vague — this is specific to enterprise mode), Archetype-consistent (refusing enterprise fits an archetype built on speed and opinionation), Costly (enterprise revenue is real money being turned down), and Testable (you can observe whether the brand has violated this commitment). All four criteria are met.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) The chapter does not argue that refusing enterprise revenue is never justifiable — it argues the refusal must meet four criteria, which this one does.
- C) The chapter explicitly argues that negative space is a brand strategy decision, not only a visual one.
*Chapter reference:* Four negative-space criteria (Specific / Archetype-consistent / Costly / Testable)

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The coherence check asks: "Would a reader who sees the archetype, the positioning, the voice, and the UVP feel they are all describing the same brand?" Coherence failure looks like a document where the archetype says Sage but the voice is playful and irreverent, or where the UVP promises one thing but the positioning statement claims a different audience or benefit. Specifically: inconsistencies between the seven components that reveal the document was assembled from parts rather than derived from a single coherent identity.
*Common error:* Describing the coherence check as a grammar or formatting review rather than as an internal consistency check across the seven components.
*Chapter reference:* Coherence check / one-page constraint

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The lawyer test specifically addresses trademark clearance — whether the name is already in use in a way that creates likelihood-of-confusion risk in the relevant category. The chapter presents it as one of four naming tests, alongside the bar test (phonetic clarity), the domain test (URL availability), and archetype alignment.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Legal team approval for company registration is the company registration process, not the trademark clearance process the lawyer test addresses.
- C) Misleading advertising language is governed by different legal standards, not the trademark clearance the lawyer test checks.
*Chapter reference:* Lawyer test / naming tests

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The one-page constraint forces the strategy to be genuinely decided rather than merely comprehensive. If a brand strategy requires five pages, it reveals that the core positioning has not been resolved — additional pages often paper over unresolved tensions between components (a mission that contradicts the archetype, a UVP that doesn't match the positioning). Forcing everything onto one page requires the hard choices: what to keep, what to cut, and what to resolve before publishing. A one-page strategy is also more likely to be read, remembered, and consistently applied.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The epistemic argument (one page forces resolution of tensions)
- The practical argument (one page is more likely to be used)
- What five pages reveals (unresolved tensions)
*Chapter reference:* One-page constraint

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The classmate's argument would be correct in a specific condition: when the brand has achieved its positioning goals and the new market is genuinely consistent with the archetype — expanding negative space rather than abandoning it. However, when expansion into adjacent markets requires contradicting the archetype (a Sage brand offering entertainment, a Hero brand offering passive consumption tools), relaxing the negative space erodes the coherence that made the brand valuable. The chapter's logic is that negative space is not arbitrary restriction — it is what gives the positive space its meaning. Relaxing the refusal without maintaining the underlying archetype commits the brand to serving everyone and resonating with no one.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Acknowledgment that the classmate has a valid point under specific conditions
- The archetype-consistency condition that determines when expansion is safe
- The coherence argument for why unconstrained expansion damages equity
*Chapter reference:* Negative space / coherence check

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* For a university computer science department, negative space might mean explicitly declining to pursue industry-funded applied research that restricts publication — a costly refusal that signals commitment to open science. This would strengthen the Sage-archetype brand position (authoritative, trustworthy, knowledge-for-its-own-sake) precisely because it is costly: departments that make this refusal are fewer, making their published work more credible. The refusal would meet the chapter's four criteria: it is specific, archetype-consistent, costly (turning down funding), and testable (public record of funding sources).
*What a strong answer includes:*
- A specific, named refusal (not a vague value statement)
- Why it strengthens positioning (the four-criteria logic)
- The archetype alignment argument
*Chapter reference:* Negative space criteria (transfer application)

- Cannot list four of the seven brand strategy components.
- Describes brand strategy and marketing strategy as the same thing.
