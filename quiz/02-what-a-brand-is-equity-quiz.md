## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines salience as the depth and breadth of brand awareness — specifically, whether customers think of the brand in the relevant purchase or use situation. It is the foundation layer because no other brand-building is possible without customers first knowing the brand exists and recalling it accurately.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Emotional connection is characteristic of the "feelings" layer and "resonance" (the top), not salience.
- C) Perceived quality is the "performance" layer — the second level — not salience.
*Chapter reference:* Keller CBBE pyramid / salience layer

---

**Q2 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines these in the opposite direction: brand identity is what the brand's creators intend to project (the signal sent), while brand image is what customers actually perceive (the signal received). The chapter explicitly marks this as a distinction students commonly confuse.
*Chapter reference:* Brand identity vs. brand image vs. brand equity

---

**Q3 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter names exactly these three behavioral indicators: willingness to pay a price premium above commodity alternatives, customer retention under competitive pressure or service disruption, and willingness to forgive a brand failure that would cause customers to defect if no equity had been built.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) Social media metrics and website traffic are awareness or engagement proxies, not direct behavioral tests of equity.
- C) NPS is an attitudinal measure, not a behavioral test; purchase intent is also attitudinal.
*Chapter reference:* Behavioral test for brand equity

---

**Q4 — C**
*Correct because:* The Aaker model's loyalty dimension specifically measures whether customers habitually return and purchase without requiring re-persuasion — it is the most directly financial of the four dimensions, since loyal customers reduce customer acquisition costs.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Perceived quality measures the customer's overall quality assessment relative to competitors, not repurchase behavior specifically.
- B) Brand associations measure the network of meanings attached to the brand, not behavioral loyalty.
*Chapter reference:* Aaker four dimensions / brand loyalty

---

**Q5 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter cites ISO 10668 as the standard governing monetary brand valuation, and specifies that it requires legal analysis (trademark protections), behavioral analysis (how customers respond), and financial analysis (converting those behaviors to cash flows).
*Chapter reference:* ISO 10668 / brand valuation

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* The CBBE pyramid's second block (performance and imagery) covers functional knowledge; the third block (judgments and feelings) covers evaluative responses. The scenario describes strong functional knowledge but neutral evaluative responses — the failure is at the transition between these two blocks, not at recall or loyalty.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Strong awareness is already established; the scenario says developers can recall and describe the brand accurately.
- C) The brand has not yet established judgments/feelings, so the resonance question is premature.
*Chapter reference:* Keller CBBE pyramid / performance → judgments transition

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Interbrand uses a royalty relief or earnings approach that combines brand strength scores with financial earnings. Kantar BrandZ uses consumer survey data on "meaningfully different" brand perceptions combined with financial measures to calculate brand contribution. Brand Finance uses a royalty relief method based on the estimated royalty a brand would command if licensed to a third party.
*Common error:* Naming only one or two methodologies, or confusing the methodologies' defining features (e.g., saying Kantar BrandZ is purely financial when it is heavily survey-based).
*Chapter reference:* Brand valuation methodologies / Interbrand / Kantar BrandZ / Brand Finance

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter's definition of brand image as audience-constructed perception, distinct from the brand team's intentions (identity), directly challenges the manager's claim. A well-executed launch can narrow the gap but cannot guarantee alignment because image is co-created by every customer experience.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Awareness is not the chapter's competing concept here; the identity/image distinction is.
- C) The chapter explicitly states that brand image and brand identity can and often do diverge.
*Chapter reference:* Brand identity vs. brand image

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Brand equity as an asset exists in customer cognition — it is the accumulated store of associations, loyalty, and trust that makes a brand more valuable than its physical assets. Brand equity as a measurement problem is the difficulty of converting those psychological assets into auditable numbers, since different methodologies (Interbrand, BrandZ, Brand Finance) produce different dollar figures for the same brand. The gap matters practically because financial decisions — acquisitions, licensing deals, portfolio pruning — require a number, yet the number is inherently methodologically contested.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Distinguishing the asset (what equity is) from the measurement challenge (how to quantify it)
- Noting that different methodologies produce different values for the same brand
- Identifying at least one practical context where the measurement matters
*Chapter reference:* Brand equity as asset / valuation problem

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* Behavioral evidence is stronger than attitudinal evidence because customers often say they prefer a brand in surveys while choosing differently when actual costs are involved — behavior has stakes that attitudes do not. The behavioral tests (premium payment, retention, forgiveness) are harder to fake than survey responses. However, the behavioral tests can give a misleading picture when equity is built on switching costs or lock-in rather than genuine preference — a customer who stays because leaving is expensive is not demonstrating equity-based loyalty.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The advantage of behavioral measures (stakes, observable, harder to fake)
- A specific condition under which the tests mislead (switching costs, monopoly, lock-in)
*Common error:* Saying behavioral tests are "better" without specifying the condition under which they overstate equity.
*Chapter reference:* Behavioral test for brand equity / equity audit

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* For a personal professional brand, salience means being recalled in the relevant hiring or collaboration context — engineers hear the problem described and think of your name. Performance means your published work, portfolio, and deployed tools give people accurate functional evidence of what you can do. Resonance would be active advocacy — colleagues recommending you unprompted, or organizations competing to work with you. The pyramid's structure is useful because it reveals that name recognition without demonstrated competence (no performance layer) cannot generate genuine resonance.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- A translation of at least two CBBE layers into personal brand terms
- A statement about whether the structure is still useful and why
*Chapter reference:* Keller CBBE pyramid (transfer application)

**Common errors to watch for in student work:**

- Inverting brand identity and brand image definitions (the most common error in this chapter).
- Treating the CBBE pyramid as a checklist to complete rather than a diagnostic framework.
- Conflating the Aaker model with the Keller model — they measure similar constructs but use different frameworks.

**Signs a student needs to return to the chapter before this quiz:**

- Cannot state the six layers of the CBBE pyramid in order.
- Uses "brand image" and "brand identity" interchangeably.

**Scaffolding adjustments:**

- *For students who score below 5:* Start with the behavioral test section — it is the most concrete and intuitive entry point into equity. Once the behavioral logic is clear, the pyramid and Aaker model follow more easily.
- *For students who score 11/11 on first attempt:* Ask them to apply the equity audit framework to a brand they use daily, identifying specific evidence for each CBBE layer and rating the brand's current position on the pyramid.
