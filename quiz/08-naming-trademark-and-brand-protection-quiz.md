## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter presents the Abercrombie spectrum in this exact order, from weakest to strongest protection: Generic (the name for the category itself — never protectable), Descriptive (describes a feature — protectable only with secondary meaning), Suggestive (suggests a quality without describing it directly), Arbitrary (real word in an unrelated context), Fanciful (invented word with no prior meaning — strongest protection).
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) This is the reverse order — from strongest to weakest.
- C) This scrambles the spectrum's order incorrectly.
*Chapter reference:* Abercrombie strength spectrum

---

**Q2 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter identifies three separate rights governed by three separate legal processes and agencies: trademark registration (via the USPTO or equivalent national body), domain name registration (via ICANN-accredited registrars), and company registration (via state/provincial governments). Each has different criteria, costs, and protections.
*Chapter reference:* Three separate rights (trademark / domain / company registration)

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines secondary meaning exactly this way: a descriptive term that has been used exclusively and consistently enough that consumers associate it with one source rather than its ordinary meaning. "American Airlines" is descriptive (describes geography and service type) but has acquired secondary meaning through decades of exclusive use. Secondary meaning is the path by which descriptive names become protectable.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Multiple dictionary definitions are not the chapter's definition of secondary meaning.
- C) A backup registration name is not what secondary meaning refers to.
*Chapter reference:* Secondary meaning / descriptive names

---

**Q4 — C**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly states that AI can generate names at volume but cannot perform clearance — the legal analysis of whether an existing registration creates likelihood of confusion in a specific category. Clearance requires accessing the live trademark database, applying legal judgment to the likelihood-of-confusion factors, and understanding the nuances of the specific category. These are legal analysis functions that AI cannot perform reliably.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Generating names in foreign languages is something AI can do; the chapter's limitation is about clearance.
- B) Memorability and phonetics are evaluation dimensions AI can assist with; the chapter's specific limitation is about legal clearance.
*Chapter reference:* AI limitations on clearance

---

**Q5 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter confirms that fanciful names (Xerox, Kodak, Häagen-Dazs) offer the strongest trademark protection because they are invented — there are no prior dictionary definitions, prior uses in commerce, or dilution from common usage. Registration is generally easier, and the scope of protection is broader.
*Chapter reference:* Fanciful names / Abercrombie spectrum

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* "SmartHire" combines "smart" (a quality descriptor) and "hire" (the category function) — it describes what the tool does. This places it in the descriptive category on the Abercrombie spectrum. Descriptive names are the most contested category for registration because the USPTO routinely refuses them without evidence of secondary meaning.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Fanciful requires an invented word with no prior meaning; "Smart" and "Hire" are both common words.
- C) Arbitrary names are real words used in completely unrelated contexts (Apple for computers) — SmartHire's words are directly related to its function, making it descriptive rather than arbitrary.
*Chapter reference:* Abercrombie spectrum / descriptive names

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Step 1 — USPTO/trademark database search: check whether the name is already registered in the relevant international class. This catches the most direct conflicts. Step 2 — Common law search: search for unregistered uses of the name in commerce (websites, social media, press) because unregistered marks can still have rights through prior use. Step 3 — Domain availability: check whether the exact domain (and close variants) is available. Step 4 — Archetype alignment check: confirm the name is consistent with the brand's archetype and positioning — a functionally clear name that contradicts the archetype creates a different kind of brand problem. The chapter treats all four as necessary before commitment.
*Common error:* Describing only one or two steps, or omitting the archetype alignment check as a naming-funnel step.
*Chapter reference:* Four-step clearance sequence

---

**Q8 — A**
*Correct because:* The likelihood-of-confusion test centers on: phonetic/visual similarity of the marks, similarity of the goods or services in which they are used, and the sophistication of the relevant consumer (sophisticated consumers are less likely to be confused). These are the core factors the chapter identifies; other factors (channels of trade, intent) are secondary.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) Name length, country of registration, and marketing budgets are not the core likelihood-of-confusion factors.
- C) Registration year, company size, and dictionary appearance are not the test's central factors.
*Chapter reference:* Likelihood-of-confusion test

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Pass 1 (Generate): Use AI and creative brainstorming to produce a large pool of candidate names — typically 50 or more — without filtering. The output is a raw list. Pass 2 (Filter): Apply the bar test, lawyer test, domain test, and archetype alignment test to eliminate names that fail any criterion. The output is a shorter list of names that pass basic viability checks. Pass 3 (Select): Apply deeper analysis to the surviving candidates — pronunciation, brand fit, international resonance — to select a final name or short list for legal clearance.
*Common error:* Describing the funnel as having only two stages, or conflating Pass 2 filtering with Pass 3 selection.
*Chapter reference:* Three-pass naming funnel

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The chapter's argument is that brand equity is stored in the name — customers' associations, memories, and trust accrue to the specific mark. Changing a name after equity has been built requires migrating all of that equity to a new mark, which is costly (marketing spend, confusion period) and risks losing equity that cannot be transferred. Additionally, if a name conflict is discovered after launch, the company may face an injunction requiring an immediate name change at the worst possible moment — when the brand has traction and a name change causes the most disruption. The classmate's position underestimates both the migration cost and the injunction risk.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The equity migration cost argument
- The legal risk of an injunction after launch
- Why timing matters (the cost of a name change compounds with brand traction)
*Chapter reference:* Naming and clearance / equity migration risk

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* Applying the spectrum: "Machine Learning" is the equivalent of a generic name — it names the entire field, making it impossible to attribute to any one source and preventing meaningful discoverability through title alone. "Attention Is All You Need" is more like an arbitrary or suggestive name — the words are real but their combination in this context is unexpected and memorable, making it highly distinctive and citable. "A Novel Framework for Efficient Processing of Large-Scale Natural Language Understanding Tasks" is fully descriptive — it describes what the paper does but offers no distinctive signal. In academic citation, "trademark strength" maps to citability and memorability: a distinctive title accumulates recognition more efficiently than a descriptive one.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Classification of each title using spectrum logic
- A translation of "trademark strength" into academic discoverability terms
- At least one explanation of why the distinctive title performs differently
*Chapter reference:* Abercrombie spectrum (transfer application)
