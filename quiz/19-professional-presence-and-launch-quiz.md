**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter names the three properties explicitly from the Airbnb deck analysis: every slide answered the same question (the deck made one argument across ten slides, each leaning on the previous one); specific numbers did load-bearing work ("87 bookings in the first six weeks" vs. "strong early user interest"); and the team slide carried weight (three founders named with relevant history).
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Strong visual design, founder narrative, and market size are elements of many decks but are not the three coherence properties the chapter names.
- C) Brevity, data visualization, and memorable metaphors are presentation virtues but are not the chapter's three coherence properties.
*Chapter reference:* The Coherence Principle / Airbnb deck analysis

**Q2 — False**

Both versions carry the same outcomes and accomplishments; the ATS version is format-optimized for software parsing, the designer version is visually optimized for the human reader who has already cleared the filter.
*Chapter reference:* The Resume — Two Versions, One Identity / format vs. content

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* "Opening in scene" means beginning the presentation not with a formal introduction or an abstract statement of the problem, but with a specific moment — one person, one situation, one pain — that makes the problem concrete before the audience is asked to believe it is worth solving. Scene version: "It is 9:15 on a Monday morning. A senior engineer at a mid-sized SaaS company opens her calendar and sees eleven meetings in the next five days. By Wednesday she knows she will have attended three that could have been emails. My tool reads her calendar, classifies each meeting's necessity, and sends her a fifteen-minute brief before Monday morning. Here is how." Generic version: "Knowledge workers spend too much time in meetings. This creates productivity loss across organizations. My tool addresses this problem." The scene version makes the problem specific, recognizable, and emotionally immediate; the generic version requires the audience to supply their own context for why it matters.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Definition of scene-opening as specific person, specific moment, specific pain
- A concrete example tied to the meeting-management context
- A contrast with the generic version that shows what is lost
*Chapter reference:* The Final Presentation / Opening and Closing / open in scene

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter states: "Write the post-course plan before the final presentation, not after." The specific reason is that the momentum is live during the course — the tool is recent, the network is activated, the work is fresh. A plan written at that moment is more likely to produce specific, actionable commitments than one written during the post-course period of declining energy. The chapter says to calendar three portfolio additions, three published pieces, and five relationship engagements as appointments.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The plan is not described as talking-point material for the presentation itself.
- C) The chapter presents the timing as a practical discipline, not an instructor requirement.
*Chapter reference:* Building for After / post-course plan

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* In Spence's signaling framework, a signal has value as a separator between candidates only if it is costly to produce — if it were cheap, every candidate would produce it regardless of quality, making it uninformative. The signals in this course — a deployed tool, a positioned brand, published writing, a coherent portfolio and presentation — are costly because they require genuine capability (building a working AI tool), extended sustained effort (developing a brand strategy and voice over multiple chapters), and accountability (public publication of writing that can be evaluated). The chapter notes that AI tooling did not make these signals cheap: generating a brand strategy with AI is easy, but building a positioned brand that is actually coherent, defensible under scrutiny, and expressed consistently across surfaces requires the judgment and execution that AI cannot supply. Cost produces value as a signal because it is the barrier that prevents imitation by candidates who do not have the underlying quality the signal is supposed to represent.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The Spence framework's logic (costly signals are informative because cheap ones are not)
- Specific identification of why the course's signals remain costly despite AI
- The connection to quality as the thing the signal represents
*Chapter reference:* The Assembled Creative Engineer / Spence signaling

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The coherence discipline — making all surfaces say the same thing over time — addresses the performative/present distinction indirectly but meaningfully. A speaker who has spent a course building one coherent brand across portfolio, writing, and tool, and who has practiced delivering it across multiple surfaces, has less to perform in the presentation moment because the brand is already internalized. The chapter's argument implies that presence is harder to perform convincingly when the coherence is constructed for the occasion, and harder to fake when it has been built over time. This does not fully solve the puzzle — a speaker could have fully internalized a persona and still read as performative — but the chapter's coherence discipline reduces the gap between internal experience and external expression, which is the most tractable part of the authenticity problem that practice and time can address.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The connection between sustained coherence practice and reduced performance gap
- Acknowledgment that this addresses but does not fully solve the puzzle
- The chapter's relevant mechanism (internalized brand vs. constructed-for-the-occasion brand)
*Chapter reference:* A Note on the Framework's Limits / Still Puzzling / coherence principle

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The coherence discipline would recommend that the nonprofit board presentation make one argument across all ten program areas rather than ten separate arguments — something like "here is the thesis of how our work changes one dimension of community life, and here is how each program advances that thesis." The sacrifice is that programs with distinct metrics, audiences, and theories of change would have to be reframed to fit a single overarching argument, which would require either genuine strategic coherence across the ten areas (the ideal) or a framing that oversimplifies some programs' real contribution to fit the unified story. Board members want to understand the organization's direction, not adjudicate ten independent program evaluations; coherence serves that audience. What the organization sacrifices is the granularity that program officers and funders who care about specific programs might want — suggesting that the board presentation should be one coherent argument, and the supporting program-by-program data should live in an appendix the board can access if needed.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Application of the "one argument across many slides" principle to the nonprofit context
- Identification of what is sacrificed (granularity per program)
- A practical resolution (coherent presentation plus appendix)
*Chapter reference:* The Coherence Principle / Airbnb deck / one argument across ten slides (transfer application)
