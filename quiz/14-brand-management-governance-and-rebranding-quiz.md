## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly names three layers: the DAM (Digital Asset Management) system as a single source of truth for assets — findable in under a minute; the design system of component libraries and templates making on-brand the easiest path; and the decision record of documented edge cases so governance conversations do not repeat.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Brand books, design tokens, and trademarks are related concepts but are not the chapter's three-layer framework.
- C) Color palette, logo rules, and voice guides are contents of guidelines, not the system layers the chapter describes.
*Chapter reference:* Guidelines That Live / three-layer living guidelines system

---

**Q2 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter states that distinguishing drift from intentional variation requires two things: knowing whether the departure was authorized (the decision), and recognizing whether a departure is a one-off or a repeated pattern across many assets (the signal). A single instance might be authorized; the same departure appearing across fifteen assets over three months signals something systemic.
*Chapter reference:* Drift vs. Intentional Variation

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly labels "the new executive who wants a mark of their own" as the illegitimate trigger that causes the most damage — it is driven by personal preference rather than genuine business need, and the cost is the equity abandoned in transition.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Merger-driven rebrands are named as legitimate triggers because they resolve two competing identities.
- C) Outgrowing the original identity is named as a real and legitimate trigger — a brand designed for fifteen people may not serve fifteen hundred.
*Chapter reference:* Refresh or Rebrand? / illegitimate trigger

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines refresh as evolution where the core identity holds (the previous brand remains legible in the new one) and rebrand as revolution where the previous brand is no longer legible — it is a deliberate attempt to leave the old brand behind. The key test is whether the old brand is recognizable in the new one.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The refresh/rebrand distinction is not about which elements change (visual vs. voice) but about whether the core identity is preserved.
- C) The distinction is substantive about brand identity, not procedural about who initiates the change.
*Chapter reference:* Refresh or Rebrand? / the spectrum

---

**Q5 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter is explicit that the AI audit produces the flags list, but the human steward routes every flag — deciding whether each departure is intentional variation or drift, and what response is appropriate. The AI+1 boundary in governance is precisely this: AI flags, human decides.
*Chapter reference:* Worked Example: Govern Your Brand / the governance workflow / AI+1 boundary

---

**Q6 — C**
*Correct because:* The chapter states that a pattern of the same departure appearing across many assets over time is "a signal that something has shifted" and that the response to a pattern "is not necessarily to enforce the original system — sometimes the drift reveals that the system needs to update." Treating a repeated independent choice as a system-update signal is the correct governance response.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Immediate correction of all fifteen assets assumes drift without the investigation the chapter requires — the pattern first needs to be assessed as drift or system signal.
- B) Closing as intentional variation without investigation ignores that none of the departures were documented or authorized.
*Chapter reference:* Drift vs. Intentional Variation / pattern as system signal

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Making the path of least resistance the on-system path means designing the system so that the easiest thing for someone to do is also the on-brand thing. In practice, this means assets are pre-built in brand-compliant templates, components follow documented patterns, and the right colors and typefaces are the first options available — so designers and marketers default to correct choices without having to consult the guidelines. The design system layer is the primary mechanism for achieving this, because it is component libraries and templates (not the DAM and not the decision record) that make on-system the lowest-friction choice.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The idea of designing for default correct behavior
- The design system as the responsible layer
- The contrast with a guidelines-only approach that requires interpretation at the moment of decision
*Chapter reference:* Guidelines That Live / design system

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter reads the three cases "through the lens of governance" and concludes that each is "a failure of the refresh/rebrand decision" — changes made without adequate accounting for the equity customers had built in the existing brand. Tropicana erased recognition cues without replacing them; Gap moved the brand on the archetype spectrum; New Coke rebranded the product driven by competitive anxiety.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Only New Coke is described as driven by competitive anxiety; Tropicana and Gap are described differently.
- C) The chapter does not attribute any of the three to new executive preference — that is named as a separate pattern.
*Chapter reference:* Refresh or Rebrand? / cautionary cases

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Equity stranding is what happens when a brand change makes customers' existing mental models — the recognition patterns they have built through repeated exposure — no longer match the new brand, causing them to stop finding the brand legible and to stop engaging with it. The chapter names two migration management practices that reduce stranding: (1) a transition period in which old and new marks appear together using the endorsed model ("formerly X" or "X, now Y"), giving customers time to update their mental models before the old mark disappears; and (2) deliberate preservation of the recognition cues that carry the most equity — often smaller and less obvious elements like a specific shade, copy rhythm, or way of addressing the customer.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The definition of stranding as a mismatch between customer mental models and the new brand
- The transition period / endorsed model practice
- The deliberate preservation of high-equity recognition cues
*Chapter reference:* The Migration Risk / migration management

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The chapter's core argument is that brand incoherence accumulates not through large deliberate decisions but through small uncorrected acts — a slightly-off color grabbed for a slide, an off-voice email from a new hire, a vendor using last year's logo. These acts happen because the brand assets are not findable, the templates are not accessible, and the edge cases are not documented. A five-person team communicating constantly is not immune to this pattern — communication is not a substitute for infrastructure, because the moment of decision is exactly when people do not have time to consult teammates. The governance infrastructure exists to eliminate the need for consultation by making the right choice the automatic choice.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The accumulation mechanism from the chapter's opening
- Why constant communication does not substitute for structural infrastructure
- The design system's role in making the right choice the default
*Chapter reference:* How a brand falls apart / Guidelines That Live

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* Applying the AI+1 boundary: the AI screening tool should do what AI does well at scale — pattern-matching for structural problems (missing controls, underpowered samples, methodology inconsistencies against the journal's documented standards) and producing a flags list with citations to the specific passage and the specific criterion. The human reviewer should do what AI cannot: decide whether each flagged item is a genuine problem requiring revision, an edge case the methodology accommodates, or a false positive based on the AI matching its training patterns rather than the actual journal criteria. The AI+1 line falls exactly where the chapter places it in brand governance — AI produces the flags, human makes every routing decision — because the judgment of whether a methodological departure is a flaw or an intentional design choice requires the kind of domain expertise and contextual reading that the model does not have.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- AI's role as pattern-matcher producing a flags list, not a verdict
- Human reviewer's role as the router of every flag
- The principle that the AI+1 line falls at the judgment call, not the detection task
*Chapter reference:* Governance workflow / AI+1 boundary (transfer application)

**Common errors to watch for in student work:**

- Conflating the DAM with the full living guidelines system — the DAM is one layer, not the system.
- Treating any departure from the documented system as drift, without distinguishing single instances from repeated patterns.
- Applying the new-executive-preference illegitimate trigger too broadly to all internally-motivated rebrands.

**Signs a student needs to return to the chapter before this quiz:**

- Cannot name all three layers of the living guidelines system.
- Does not understand why the AI+1 boundary places routing decisions with the human steward.

- *For students who score below 5:* Start with the three-layer living guidelines system as the concrete framework, then layer in the governance workflow (AI flags, human routes) as the operational discipline.
