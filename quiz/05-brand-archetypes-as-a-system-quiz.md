## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines the archetype shadow as the distorted or extreme version of the archetype's core motivation — what happens when the archetype's positive qualities become corrupted or taken too far. The Sage's shadow is dogmatism; the Creator's shadow is perfectionism; the Hero's shadow is recklessness. Knowing the shadow is essential for brand integrity because brands can drift into shadow territory without recognizing it.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) A competing brand occupying the same archetype is not the chapter's definition of shadow; it is a market positioning concern.
- C) The chapter does not define shadow as the scope of decisions outside the archetype.
*Chapter reference:* Archetype shadow

---

**Q2 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter attributes the Tropicana failure to destroying the brand's recognition assets — the orange-and-straw visual was the carrier of the brand's equity. The new packaging was designed well in isolation but erased the visual elements that triggered category recognition in the store. This is an archetype case because the recognition assets carry the archetype's energy.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter does not frame the failure as an agency problem; it frames it as a recognition-asset problem.
- C) Habit is a weak explanation — the chapter argues that the equity loss was real, not merely habitual preference.
*Chapter reference:* Tropicana 2009 / recognition assets / archetype drift

---

**Q3 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter traces the Mark and Pearson system to Jungian psychology, which holds that archetypes are universal patterns appearing across cultures, mythology, and literature. The chapter explicitly frames the twelve archetypes as grounded in this universal psychological claim rather than as invented marketing categories.
*Chapter reference:* Mark/Pearson twelve-archetype system / Jungian archetypes

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter's five-input procedure for reading a brand's archetype uses: origin story (how the brand came to exist), stated values (what the brand says it believes), visual and verbal cues (the aesthetic and language signals), audience expectation (what customers expect the brand to do), and competitive white space (what position is available in the market given competitor archetypes).
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Product features, market size, and marketing budget are not among the five inputs in the chapter's procedure.
- C) Revenue model, customer demographics, and employee culture are not the five inputs named.
*Chapter reference:* Five-input procedure for reading brand archetype

---

**Q5 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter presents the Gap case as an archetype drift example: the new logo abandoned the brand's established visual identity, generating immediate negative audience response and a six-day rollback. The chapter's point is that even if a new design is executed competently, changing the brand's identity signals disrupts the equity built in customer memory.
*Chapter reference:* Gap 2010 / archetype drift cases

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter's shadow concept identifies the Hero's shadow as recklessness and bullying — the courage that defines the Hero archetype distorts into aggression when taken to an extreme. The startup's social media behavior toward critics and mockery of regulatory compliance are textbook shadow expressions of a Hero-adjacent archetype, not authentic expressions of it.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter does not treat disruption as license for all confrontational behavior — it specifically names recklessness and bullying as the Hero shadow.
- C) The chapter's analysis here is not about archetype multiplicity but about shadow behavior within one archetype.
*Chapter reference:* Archetype shadow / Hero shadow

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* An archetype functions as a forcing function by eliminating decisions that would be inconsistent with the archetype before the decision is made — it creates a filter. For example, a Sage archetype brand asked whether to use humor in a campaign can quickly answer "only if the humor is intelligent and grounded in insight, not silly or irreverent" — because irreverent humor contradicts the Sage's core motivation of providing trustworthy knowledge. The archetype does not make the decision for you but eliminates a large class of wrong options.
*Common error:* Describing archetypes as guidelines rather than as decision filters — missing the forcing-function mechanism.
*Chapter reference:* Archetype as forcing function

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter uses New Coke to illustrate that brand equity is stored in cognitive associations — the emotional and experiential connections built over decades — not in the physical product formula. Taste tests failed to measure this equity, which is why research predicted acceptance but the actual launch produced rejection. The formula change disrupted the associations, which is where the value was.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter does not argue taste tests are unreliable in general — it argues they measured the wrong thing (taste preference) rather than the right thing (archetype-associated equity).
- C) The chapter explicitly rejects the "irrational consumer" framing — the consumer response was rational given what they were protecting (their equity).
*Chapter reference:* New Coke 1985 / recognition assets / brand equity in cognition

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The authenticity test asks: does this archetype reflect how the brand actually operates, not just how it wants to appear? A brand that claims the Sage archetype must actually demonstrate rigor, depth, and accuracy. The differentiation test asks: does this archetype give the brand a distinctive position relative to its competitors? If every competitor is a Hero brand, choosing Sage may create more space. The stress test asks: does the brand maintain this archetype when it is costly to do so — when following the archetype's logic requires passing up revenue or accepting criticism?
*Common error:* Describing the tests as sequential steps rather than as three independent criteria to apply simultaneously.
*Chapter reference:* Three tests for archetype selection (authenticity / differentiation / stress)

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The chapter argues against archetype mixing: a brand that borrows from multiple archetypes loses coherence — each archetype has a different core motivation, different voice, and different visual language, so mixing produces a brand that feels unfocused to audiences. The forcing-function logic cuts directly against mixing: the value of an archetype is precisely that it eliminates a large set of options, and mixing archetypes reintroduces all the options the forcing function was supposed to eliminate. The student's intuition about broader appeal mistakes reach for resonance — a coherent single archetype resonates deeply with a specific audience rather than superficially with many audiences.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The coherence argument against mixing
- The forcing-function logic and how mixing undermines it
- The reach-vs-resonance distinction
*Common error:* Agreeing with the student's framing that more archetypes means more appeal without engaging the coherence argument.
*Chapter reference:* Archetype as forcing function / single archetype coherence

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* A recognition asset for a trustworthy AI engineer might be a consistent visual format for technical writing (a specific header style, a characteristic diagram format) paired with a distinctive analytical voice — the combination that makes a reader instantly recognize their work before seeing the name. If that engineer suddenly adopted a casual, jargon-free style or a completely different visual presentation, audiences who had formed equity around the original signals would experience the same disruption Tropicana's customers did: the recognition cue that stored the trust is gone, and the trust has to be rebuilt from scratch. The disruption is not about the quality of the new style — it is about the erasure of the signal that carried the accumulated equity.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- A specific recognition asset defined for a personal professional brand
- Why changing it creates trust disruption (equity is stored in the signal, not the underlying quality)
- Connection to the Tropicana case's mechanism
*Chapter reference:* Recognition assets / Tropicana case (transfer application)
