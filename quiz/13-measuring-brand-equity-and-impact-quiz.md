## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines the patience gap as the mismatch between the time horizon of brand investment (equity builds over months and years) and the time horizon of organizational pressure (quarterly performance reviews, month-over-month dashboards). Organizations abandon brand investment before it produces measurable returns because the effect is slow and the organizational pressure is fast.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) The gap between stated positioning and customer experience is the experience-strategy gap (Chapter 11), not the patience gap.
- C) Campaign lag time is a related concept but not the chapter's definition of the patience gap.
*Chapter reference:* Patience gap / brand metrics vs. performance metrics

---

**Q2 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter cites academic research for each of the four NPS limits: single-item measurement (one question cannot capture multi-dimensional constructs reliably), cultural calibration (different cultures use rating scales differently, making cross-market comparisons unreliable), information compression (collapsing continuous loyalty into detractor/passive/promoter loses information), and correlation problems (NPS does not reliably predict financial outcomes better than other metrics). All four are documented limitations.
*Chapter reference:* NPS four documented limits

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* Unaided awareness is the "top-of-mind" measure — what brands does the customer spontaneously mention when asked about the category? Aided awareness is recognition — when shown the brand name, do they recognize it? Unaided is a more demanding test and more closely associated with purchase behavior.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) This is the reverse of the correct definitions.
- C) The chapter does not define the distinction by survey method vs. behavioral observation.
*Chapter reference:* Brand tracking study / aided vs. unaided awareness

---

**Q4 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter distinguishes MMM (uses aggregate historical data — total spend and total sales over time periods — to statistically estimate the contribution of each marketing activity) from MTA (tracks individual user touchpoints through the conversion journey to assign credit). Each has different data requirements and failure modes.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) The chapter does not restrict MMM to digital and MTA to offline; both can be applied to various channel mixes.
- C) MTA's individual-level data is not universally more accurate — it suffers from incomplete data (users who don't accept cookies, cross-device gaps) that MMM's aggregate approach avoids.
*Chapter reference:* MMM vs. multi-touch attribution

---

**Q5 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter presents the Farris test in exactly this form: a value metric changes when something meaningful changes (if the brand equity actually improves, the metric should move) and would not change if the company did something superficial or misleading (buying fake followers should not move a value metric). A metric that can be gamed without genuine change is a vanity metric.
*Chapter reference:* Farris test / vanity vs. value metrics

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* Consideration is a behavioral precursor to purchase — it measures whether the audience actively includes the brand in their evaluation set, which is a direct driver of sales. Impressions and followers measure exposure and accumulation, not active evaluation intent. Measuring consideration requires a survey of the target segment because it cannot be inferred from digital analytics.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Measurement difficulty does not correlate with metric importance — many important metrics are straightforward to measure.
- C) The chapter's argument for consideration over impressions is about what the metric measures (behavioral intent vs. passive exposure), not about survey vs. digital analytics reliability.
*Chapter reference:* Brand tracking study / consideration

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* A brand-lift study for a content marketing campaign: Control group — a matched sample of the target audience who did not receive the campaign content (exposed to no campaign, or to a control piece unrelated to the brand). Treatment group — a matched sample who did receive the campaign content. Measure before and after: aided brand awareness, brand consideration, and one key association strength score (e.g., "perceived as a trusted authority") measured by survey in both groups at the same two time points. The campaign's lift is the difference in metric change between treatment and control: if the treatment group's consideration increased 12 points and the control group's increased 3 points, the campaign's attributable lift is approximately 9 points. This isolates the campaign's contribution by controlling for background equity movement.
*Common error:* Designing a study without a control group, which makes it impossible to isolate the campaign's effect from general market trends.
*Chapter reference:* Brand-lift study design

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter's single-item limit and information-compression limit are most relevant: NPS compresses complex multi-dimensional brand equity and customer experience into one number, making it impossible to distinguish whether a change reflects genuine equity improvement or simply a recent positive service interaction or survey-timing effect. The cultural calibration limit also applies if the team spans multiple markets.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter does not restrict NPS validity to consumer vs. SaaS — its limits apply across contexts.
- C) The chapter does not specify a 10-point threshold for actionability — this is not a chapter concept.
*Chapter reference:* NPS four documented limits / single-item / information compression

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The attribution problem is the difficulty of establishing that a specific marketing activity caused a specific business outcome, because multiple factors influence a sale or a brand-equity change simultaneously. A customer who buys after seeing a paid ad may have also read three blog posts, seen a colleague's recommendation, and encountered a news article — which activity deserves credit? The attribution problem means that brand metrics should be interpreted as correlational evidence, not causal proof. The practical implication is that measurement should be designed to accumulate multiple signal types (tracking study changes, behavioral indicators, uplift studies) rather than relying on a single metric to prove causation.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The multi-factor attribution challenge
- The distinction between correlation and causation in brand metrics
- The practical implication for how metrics should be used
*Chapter reference:* Attribution problem / MMM / multi-touch attribution

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The colleague's argument fails the chapter's attribution and patience-gap logic. Without baseline measurements taken before the campaign, there is no way to know whether the metrics that change during or after the campaign reflect the campaign's effect or general market trends. The patience gap compounds this: brand effects manifest slowly, so the moment you want to attribute an improvement to a campaign (months later) is precisely the moment when baseline data collected in advance is essential. Designing a measurement plan with blank baselines is not overhead — it is the minimum infrastructure required to distinguish a meaningful change from noise. Relying only on what the analytics dashboard already shows means measuring what was easiest to instrument, not what matters most.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The baseline problem: no before means no comparison
- The patience gap connection: effects are slow, so pre-campaign baselines are essential
- Why dashboard-only metrics bias toward easy-to-measure rather than meaningful
*Chapter reference:* Measurement plan with blank baselines / attribution problem / patience gap

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* Applying the Farris test: (a) Number of papers published — vanity metric: a researcher can publish many low-impact papers without producing meaningful knowledge contribution; the number would rise even if the work were superficial. (b) Citation count — value metric: citations reflect whether others found the work meaningful enough to build on; the count changes only when the work has genuine influence. (c) h-index — somewhere between vanity and value: it rewards both quantity and citation impact, but can be gamed by self-citation and is difficult to compare across fields. (d) Number of collaborators — generally vanity: having many collaborators does not indicate productivity or impact; it could rise from networking events rather than intellectual contribution.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Classification of each metric using Farris test logic (does it change with meaningful change? Could it be gamed without genuine contribution?)
- At least two defended classifications
*Chapter reference:* Farris test / vanity vs. value metrics (transfer application)
