## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* SCCT's three clusters are victim (low public responsibility attribution → deny or bolster), accidental (moderate attribution → diminish), and preventable (high attribution → rebuild / mortification). Coombs developed these as empirically grounded clusters, not as intuitive categories.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) SCCT does not organize crises by internal/external/structural origin — it organizes by public responsibility attribution.
- C) Technical/reputational/ethical is not SCCT's framework; it is a common informal categorization that SCCT replaces.
*Chapter reference:* Matching Response to Crisis / SCCT crisis clusters

---

**Q2 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly classifies the Tylenol case as a victim crisis — "an organization is itself a victim of an external force." J&J did not cause the tampering; an unknown criminal tampered with the product after it left J&J's factories. The company's responsibility was nonexistent legally; its exceptional response was to act as though it bore responsibility anyway, because the Credo placed customer safety first.
*Chapter reference:* Matching Response to Crisis / victim crises / The Tylenol Standard

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines a holding statement as brief, pre-drafted, acknowledging an emerging situation without committing to a specific account, causal attribution, or resolution. Its purpose is to signal the organization is engaged while buying time for the real response. The example given: "We are aware of reports of [problem] and are investigating urgently. Customer safety is our priority. We will provide an update as soon as we have more information."
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) A holding statement is not a legal document denying liability — it is explicitly non-committal about responsibility while signaling engagement.
- C) A full statement prepared for each known scenario is described as part of the playbook, but a holding statement is shorter and non-specific by design.
*Chapter reference:* The Playbook / holding statements

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter analyzes United Airlines as an example of "wrong-fast" and SCCT mismatch: United deployed a low-responsibility strategy (defending its right to remove disruptive passengers) on a crisis in which public responsibility attribution was high and rising (a passenger was physically injured by United personnel). The chapter notes United's statement came after the narrative was established, and that the strategy made the crisis worse.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) United did post statements, though late — the error was the strategy, not only the speed.
- C) The chapter does not attribute United's error to the legal review issue; the error was strategy mismatch.
*Chapter reference:* Speed vs. Wrong-Speed / United Airlines April 2017

---

**Q5 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter presents the BP Deepwater Horizon case, including Hayward's "I'd like my life back" statement, as an example of applying the wrong strategy to a preventable crisis — diminishing and contextualizing a high-responsibility crisis instead of using rebuild/mortification. The error was strategy selection, not execution of the rebuild strategy.
*Chapter reference:* The Tylenol Standard / BP Deepwater Horizon

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* An AI-generated error in a catalog is an accidental crisis — the company did not intend the harm, the error was a technical failure in an otherwise good-faith process, and public responsibility attribution is moderate. The correct strategy is diminish: acknowledge the specific error, explain the context, correct it, and communicate preventive measures. Applying rebuild/mortification (full accountability language for unintended harm) overcorrects and may signal a larger institutional failure than actually occurred.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Using deny/bolster on an accidental crisis — claiming AI generated the content without human oversight — is an evasion strategy that audiences will find inadequate and that may backfire if the company's review processes are examined.
- C) Treating an unintended error as a preventable crisis requires evidence that the company knew about a risk and failed to act; a single AI-generated error does not meet that threshold.
*Chapter reference:* Matching Response to Crisis / SCCT / severity triage

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The four components of a crisis playbook are: (1) Monitoring and alert thresholds — defines what sources are watched and at what signal level monitoring escalates to response; its function is early detection before a situation spreads. (2) Severity triage — the SCCT classification step, asking "what kind of crisis is this?" to determine the response strategy before anything is written; its function is strategy selection at the moment of first awareness. (3) Holding statements — pre-drafted brief messages for each crisis category, ready to post quickly; their function is to signal engagement and buy time while the real response is developed. (4) Escalation path — names who decides, who speaks, and who must approve any public statement; its function is ensuring accountability and preventing unauthorized statements or decision voids when key personnel are unavailable.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- All four components named
- A distinct function for each, not just a description of contents
- The "slow decisions made in advance" logic connecting all four
*Chapter reference:* The Playbook / four components

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter states explicitly: "the facts will surface" in a preventable crisis. The organization knew or should have known about the risk; evidence will emerge in litigation, journalism, or regulatory investigation. When the denial cannot be sustained, it becomes a second crisis layered on the original — the organization has now both caused the original harm and lied about it.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter's argument is about communications failure and trust, not about legal liability — though legal consequences are real, that is not the mechanism the chapter identifies.
- C) The chapter's argument is that denial in a preventable crisis fails because it will be falsified, not because audiences are too hostile to accept it.
*Chapter reference:* Matching Response to Crisis / Image Repair Theory / preventable crises

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* "Wrong-fast" is when the instinct to respond quickly produces a statement before the organization understands the situation. A defensive statement issued before the facts are known will be shown to be false when the facts emerge; a minimizing statement issued before the scale of harm is known will look tone-deaf when the full scope becomes public; a denial issued before it can be sustained will become an additional crisis when it fails. The holding statement resolves the speed-accuracy tension because it can be posted within the first hour (fast enough to prevent narrative vacuum) while committing to nothing that cannot be sustained: "We are investigating" is true as long as the investigation is genuine; "Customer safety is our priority" holds as long as subsequent actions are consistent with it. The holding statement is both fast and accurate because it acknowledges without attributing — buying time for the full response without creating facts that might later need to be walked back.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Definition of wrong-fast and the specific failure mechanism
- How the holding statement achieves speed without committing to unsustainable claims
- The contrast between a wrong-fast statement and a holding statement
*Chapter reference:* Speed vs. Wrong-Speed / holding statements

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The consultant is partly right and partly wrong. The decision to pull the product was the most important single action, and a different company could theoretically have made that decision in the moment. But the chapter's analysis supports a deeper point: the Credo mattered not because it prescribed the decision but because it made the decision *credible* to audiences. J&J's response was trusted partly because the same values document predated the crisis by forty years — audiences could verify that the brand had publicly prioritized customers over shareholders before the crisis created any pressure to do so. The chapter's "authenticity problem" in the Still Puzzling section directly addresses this: performed empathy and performed values during a crisis tend to make things worse, and audiences are sophisticated readers of whether a response reflects real values or a strategic calculation. The Credo is evidence of the former — something the consultant's argument does not account for.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Acknowledgment of what the consultant gets right (the decision was primary)
- The credibility function the Credo served
- The chapter's authenticity problem and how the Credo addresses it
*Chapter reference:* The Tylenol Standard / J&J Credo / Still Puzzling

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The chapter's playbook logic reveals the answer: authenticity in the live moment is more likely if the values behind the response were already operationalized before the crisis arrived. The specific preparation would be making the slow decisions — what does this brand prioritize when forced to choose between customer safety and cost? between transparency and legal protection? — in advance, in calm, when no crisis pressure is present, and documenting those decisions so they can guide the live response rather than be improvised. This addresses the authenticity problem rather than only the speed problem because it converts the live response from a decision under pressure (where strategic calculation can contaminate genuineness) into an execution of a pre-existing commitment (where the authenticity of the original commitment can be legible). The J&J Credo is the worked example: it resolved the authentic-vs-performed question before 1982 by establishing the values in 1943.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The distinction between slow decisions made in advance and decisions improvised under pressure
- Why advance operationalization of values addresses authenticity, not just speed
- Connection to the J&J Credo as the model
*Chapter reference:* The Playbook / authenticity problem / Still Puzzling (transfer application)
