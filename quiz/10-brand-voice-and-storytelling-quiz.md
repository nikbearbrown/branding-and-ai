## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter presents Miller's StoryBrand as a "Customer-as-Hero" framework precisely because it positions the brand as the guide (the Yoda to the customer's Luke) rather than the hero. The customer has the problem, faces the challenge, and achieves the transformation — the brand equips them.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Making the brand the hero is the opposite of Miller's framework — it is one of the most common brand storytelling mistakes the chapter describes.
- C) Founder stories may use hero narratives, but Miller's framework is not founder-centric.
*Chapter reference:* Miller's Customer-as-Hero / StoryBrand framework

---

**Q2 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly argues that cadence (consistent, regular publishing) produces more compounding equity than volume bursts followed by silence. A body of work builds over time and becomes discoverable and citable; a one-time burst does not accumulate in the same way.
*Chapter reference:* Cadence beats volume principle

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter analyzes the Pepsi "Live for Now" failure as a three-layer mismatch: (1) message-moment mismatch — using a protest moment for product promotion; (2) archetype mismatch — Pepsi is an Everyman brand, and activist positioning is inconsistent with Everyman's core motivation; (3) protagonist mismatch — Kendall Jenner as the resolving figure was perceived as implausible and trivializing.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Execution quality is not the chapter's analysis; the chapter identifies structural mismatches, not craft failures.
- C) Budget, channel, and timing are not the chapter's three-layer analysis of this case.
*Chapter reference:* Pepsi "Live for Now" April 2017 / three-layer mismatch

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter discusses how the Hero's Journey arc — departure from ordinary world, trials, transformation — provides structural coherence for brand origin stories and case study narratives. The journey structure creates emotional engagement because it mirrors a pattern that audiences recognize and find meaningful.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Making the founder the central protagonist is a common choice for origin stories, but it is not the chapter's description of how the Hero's Journey framework is applied.
- C) The chapter treats the Hero's Journey as applicable to multiple content types, not only advertising scripts.
*Chapter reference:* Campbell's Hero's Journey / brand storytelling application

---

**Q5 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter analyzes the Jaguar "Copy Nothing" 2024 rebrand as a case study in mismatch, not as a success. The rebrand generated significant public controversy and appeared to contradict rather than execute the brand's archetype — the chapter uses it as an example of the risks of radical identity shifts, not as a model to emulate.
*Chapter reference:* Jaguar "Copy Nothing" 2024 / rebrand case

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter's protagonist mismatch failure pathway describes exactly this pattern: positioning the brand as "we who won" rather than the customer as the one who succeeded. Miller's Customer-as-Hero framework requires putting the customer in the protagonist role. An Explorer archetype compounds this — the Explorer archetype celebrates the customer's journey of discovery, not the brand's victory.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Success stories are not structurally incompatible with all archetypes — the issue is who is positioned as the protagonist in the story structure.
- C) Channel is not the chapter's mismatch failure pathway being triggered here.
*Chapter reference:* Protagonist mismatch / Miller's Customer-as-Hero

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Three of the five story types the chapter identifies: (1) Origin story — tells how the brand came to exist and why, building founder-credibility and archetype clarity; (2) Case study — shows a real customer outcome that validates the brand's promise with specific, verifiable evidence; (3) Thought-leadership piece — demonstrates the brand's intellectual depth and authority in its category, building Sage- or Sage-adjacent credibility. Additional types include: (4) Behind-the-scenes or process story — shows how the work is done, building transparency and authenticity; (5) Point-of-view piece — takes a position on a category debate, signaling the brand's values and differentiation.
*Common error:* Listing fewer than three types or describing them without explaining what each accomplishes for the brand.
*Chapter reference:* Five story types

---

**Q8 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter presents the Bud Light case as an Everyman archetype mismatch: Bud Light's core identity is built on inclusive, unpretentious belonging — "for everyone, but especially for us." When a segment of the brand's primary audience perceived the Dylan Mulvaney partnership as moving beyond their cultural comfort zone, the brand found itself unable to hold both audiences simultaneously, which is the structural tension the Everyman archetype creates in culturally polarized moments.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) Platform choice is not the chapter's analysis of this case.
- C) Production quality is not the chapter's diagnosis.
*Chapter reference:* Bud Light / Dylan Mulvaney 2023 / Everyman archetype mismatch

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Four of the six case study elements: (1) Problem statement — specific description of the user's pain and context; (2) Solution overview — what was built and how it works; (3) Architecture or technical approach — the key decisions and their reasoning; (4) Limits and what v2 would add. Admitting limits accomplishes two things: it builds credibility by demonstrating that the author knows what they do not know (the Feynman principle — claims without acknowledged limits are less trustworthy, not more); and it makes the case study more useful to readers who want to understand the real boundaries of the approach.
*Common error:* Treating the limits section as a weakness rather than as a credibility-building move.
*Chapter reference:* Six case study elements / Feynman principle in case studies

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* (1) Archetype-story mismatch check: "Does this story's emotional register and protagonist arc match what our archetype is designed to feel? If we are an Everyman brand, is this content accessible and inclusive rather than aspirational and exclusive?" (2) Protagonist check: "Who is the hero of this story — us or the customer? If the answer is us, can we reframe it so the customer's transformation is the central arc?" (3) Message-moment check: "Is this content inserting our brand into a cultural moment that is not ours to claim? Does the moment's meaning contradict our archetype's values?" These three questions map directly to the chapter's three mismatch failure pathways and can be asked before any campaign launches.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- One specific question per failure pathway
- Each question tied to a mechanism from the chapter
- Questions that are actionable (producible before launch, not post-mortem)
*Chapter reference:* Three mismatch failure pathways

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* Brand voice in technical documentation means that the tone, vocabulary choices, and structural approach to explaining complexity are consistent with the brand's archetype across all surfaces. A Sage-archetype AI brand with precise, authoritative marketing content that then publishes README files in casual, vague language creates a voice mismatch — users who encounter the documentation first may form different archetype expectations than users who encounter the marketing content first. The cost is trust fragmentation: the brand appears to be two different products, reducing the cumulative equity-building effect of consistent voice. For developer-facing brands especially, documentation voice is more credible than marketing copy because developers read the docs under high-stakes conditions.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- A definition of what brand voice means in technical documentation
- A specific cost of mismatch (trust fragmentation, coherence failure)
- Recognition that technical documentation carries high trust stakes for developer audiences
*Chapter reference:* Brand voice consistency / cadence and coherence (transfer application)
