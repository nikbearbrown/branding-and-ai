## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* STP stands for Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning, applied in that order: first divide the total market into meaningful segments, then select which segment(s) to serve, then define the brand's position within that targeted segment.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) "Strategy" is not one of the three STP stages; the framework's first stage is Segmentation.
- C) "Testing" and "Promotion" are not STP stages; the framework's second and third stages are Targeting and Positioning.
*Chapter reference:* STP framework

---

**Q2 — False**
*Correct because:* The milkshake study found that morning customers were hiring the milkshake to do the job of making a boring commute more interesting and to keep their hand and mouth occupied — not primarily for hunger satisfaction. This insight led to recommendations about texture and portability, not more flavors. The chapter cites this as evidence that JTBD reveals the real job behind a purchase.
*Chapter reference:* JTBD / Christensen milkshake study

---

**Q3 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter's three-tag system distinguishes: Confirmed (validated through direct research — interviews, observation, or primary data), Needs Evidence (plausible hypothesis that has not yet been tested), and Inferred (derived from secondary or behavioral patterns rather than direct primary research).
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) "Universal across all users" misreads Confirmed; a claim can be confirmed without being universal.
- C) The number of sources is not the chapter's criterion for any of the three tags — validity of the research method is.
*Chapter reference:* Persona status tags (Confirmed / Needs Evidence / Inferred)

---

**Q4 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter names geographic, demographic, psychographic, and behavioral as four of the five bases of segmentation. The fifth is firmographic (organization-level characteristics), relevant for B2B contexts.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) "Attitudinal," "temporal," and "financial" are not among the chapter's five named segmentation bases.
- C) "Economic" and "occupational" are not the chapter's named bases; demographic and psychographic are on this list but economic and occupational are not separate bases in the chapter's taxonomy.
*Chapter reference:* Five bases of segmentation

---

**Q5 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly argues the opposite: targeting a specific audience makes a brand's message more resonant for that audience, which drives stronger engagement, loyalty, and word-of-mouth — the paradox being that specificity increases effectiveness even while reducing raw reach. "Everyone" as a target produces generic messaging that resonates with no one strongly.
*Chapter reference:* Cost of "everyone" / STP framework

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter's argument about the cost of targeting "everyone" applies directly: a segment defined as "knowledge workers who use computers" is so broad it prevents specific, resonant messaging. No one in that segment feels the product was made for them, which weakens all marketing efforts even though the potential reach is large.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The problem is not the tagline but the segment definition itself.
- C) While the chapter does discuss psychographic segments, the core critique here is breadth, not which type of segmentation variable was chosen.
*Chapter reference:* Cost of "everyone" / segmentation

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Cultural tension in Holt's model is a genuine unresolved contradiction in the culture — a gap between what the culture says people should value and what they actually experience. A brand uses cultural tension by taking a visible, authentic stand on one side of the contradiction, giving the target audience a way to express their position. The chapter's Nike example: the cultural tension is between the idealization of athletic achievement and the reality that most people feel excluded from it; Nike's "Just Do It" gives ordinary people a way to claim athletic identity without being elite athletes.
*Common error:* Describing cultural tension as controversy or political debate rather than as an unresolved structural contradiction between cultural ideals and lived experience.
*Chapter reference:* Cultural tension / Holt / Nike or Dove

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* JTBD focuses on the situation and goal (the "job") rather than the product attribute or the purchase satisfaction. Option B asks about the context before the decision and the functional outcome — this targets the job being hired, not the product's quality or price.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) "How satisfied are you with quality" measures product performance, not the job being hired — this is a traditional satisfaction question, not a JTBD probe.
- C) "Would you pay more" is a price sensitivity question, not a job-to-be-done probe.
*Chapter reference:* JTBD / Christensen milkshake study

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* A research-grounded persona is built from primary or secondary data — interviews, behavioral observation, or validated secondary research — and every claim in the persona carries a status tag (Confirmed, Needs Evidence, or Inferred) indicating its evidentiary basis. A fictional persona is a convenient composite: it represents what the team imagines or wants its users to be, without data validation. The practical difference is accountability: a research-grounded persona can be updated when data contradicts a claim; a fictional persona cannot be falsified because it was never grounded in anything.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The distinction between data-grounded and assumption-based personas
- The role of the status tag system in maintaining evidentiary accountability
- The practical consequence of the distinction (what makes a persona useful vs. misleading)
*Chapter reference:* Persona as research artifact / status tags

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response)**
*Model answer:* The chapter's logic better supports the narrowing argument: specificity drives resonance, and resonance drives the behaviors that matter most (word-of-mouth, loyalty, price premium). "Leaving money on the table" assumes that reach equals revenue, but a brand that speaks generically to everyone captures little attention from anyone — so the apparently larger addressable market produces lower actual engagement. The classmate's argument would be correct in a market where all buyers are identical, but in any heterogeneous market, specific positioning outperforms generic positioning precisely because it creates the perception that the brand was made for a particular person.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Engagement with the classmate's "leaving money on the table" framing
- The chapter's resonance argument for why specificity beats reach
- Recognition of the conditions under which narrowing might genuinely cost revenue
*Common error:* Simply asserting the chapter is correct without engaging the classmate's revenue logic.
*Chapter reference:* Cost of "everyone" / STP

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* In the job search market, a genuine cultural tension exists between the ideal that credentials and effort should determine career outcomes and the lived experience that networks and relationships largely determine who gets hired. A brand could authentically use this tension by positioning for people who feel structurally excluded from the network — not by promising to replace networks but by helping people build authentic credibility that networks cannot ignore. This would require the brand to genuinely take the side of the credentialed-but-unconnected job seeker, which means design decisions that prioritize substance over social performance.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- A genuine contradiction in the chosen domain (not just a problem)
- How a brand could authentically take a position on that contradiction
- Recognition that authentic use requires real design or behavior choices, not just messaging
*Chapter reference:* Cultural tension / Holt (transfer application)
