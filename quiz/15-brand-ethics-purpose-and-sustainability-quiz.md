## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter states the test explicitly: "is it a commitment you maintain when maintaining it costs you something?" This is behavioral, not linguistic — the question is not what the brand says it believes but what decisions the belief requires, and whether those decisions are made when the cost is real.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Legal filing and CEO sign-off are not the chapter's test — purpose-washing brands frequently have signed legal commitments to their stated values.
- C) ESG frameworks measure and report on performance; they are not the chapter's behavioral test for whether a commitment is real.
*Chapter reference:* Purpose vs. Purpose-Washing / the behavioral test

---

**Q2 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter states both functions explicitly: the claim-proof map "forces the team to verify every public commitment before publication" (brand integrity function) and "regulators can ask to see the substantiation behind any environmental claim, and the map is the documentation that answers that request" (compliance function).
*Chapter reference:* The Claim-Proof Map / two functions

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter names both banned categories precisely: generic green claims without substantiation (listing "eco-friendly," "green," "sustainable," "environmentally responsible" as examples that are banned unless accompanied by recognized substantiation) and product-level "climate neutral" or "net zero" claims based primarily on carbon offsets rather than documented value-chain reductions.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The ECGT does not specifically target recyclable-materials claims or offset quantity claims in this framing; the two categories are generic unsubstantiated claims and offset-based neutrality claims.
- C) The ECGT targets the type of claim, not the channel (advertising vs. investor materials).
*Chapter reference:* Greenwashing Is Now Regulated / ECGT

---

**Q4 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter ranks these explicitly: CSR programs are voluntary and not third-party verified; ESG reporting is standardized and quantified but the frameworks are not harmonized and reporting is not the same as performing well; B Corp certification requires third-party verification by B Lab and periodic recertification.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) B Corp is described as the most rigorous of the three, not the least.
- C) ESG and CSR are both less rigorous than B Corp, but ESG is more standardized than CSR.
*Chapter reference:* CSR, ESG, and B Corp

---

**Q5 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter describes an asymmetry: "A brand that makes no values claims has not broken any promise. A brand that makes values claims and then fails to act on them has added hypocrisy to the original gap." The trust damage is greater for the values-claiming brand because the claim inflated expectations that the behavior then deflated by the full inflated amount.
*Chapter reference:* The Backlash Dynamics / trust asymmetry

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter specifies exactly what evidence is required for AI ethics claims: "explainable AI" requires documentation of what the system shows users and whether a non-technical user can act on it; a "bias-audited" claim requires a published audit specifying which protected characteristics were tested, what methodology was used, and who conducted it. "Responsible AI principles" is a generic claim with no specific methodology behind it. The discipline is to cut or revise any claim that cannot be substantiated.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) "Industry-standard terms" that are generic and unsubstantiated are exactly the claims the chapter says must be cut — ubiquity does not create evidence.
- C) The ECGT and the claim-proof discipline apply regardless of whether the copy appears in regulated investor materials.
*Chapter reference:* AI ethics claims / claim-proof map discipline

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The behavioral commitment test asks whether the brand maintains its commitment when maintaining it costs something. Patagonia's "Don't Buy This Jacket" campaign passes the test because Patagonia was actively discouraging its own revenue, and the brand has since made structural decisions that carry real costs — a repair program, a used-gear marketplace, and a legal restructuring that commits nearly all profits to environmental causes. Each of these decisions imposed a cost. The "Conscious Collection" fails the test because it represents 1% of the company's total volume, the company's core business model remains unchanged (overproduction and rapid disposal), and no core decision was changed by the "sustainability commitment." The claim signals concern without bearing its cost.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The specific costs Patagonia bore
- The specific absence of cost in the Conscious Collection case
- The distinction between linguistic commitment and behavioral commitment
*Chapter reference:* Purpose vs. Purpose-Washing / the behavioral test / Patagonia

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter describes the mechanism precisely: a values claim creates a behavioral expectation encoded in the audience's memory as a background standard. When the brand acts inconsistently with the claim, the violation is evaluated against that inflated standard — "the violation is not just the behavior; it is the hypocrisy of having claimed otherwise." The damage is greater than the behavior alone would produce.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Regulatory scrutiny is a real risk the chapter covers, but it is not the mechanism explaining why purpose-washing damages trust more than silence.
- C) Core-audience sensitivity is not the mechanism the chapter identifies; the mechanism is the expectation inflation that makes any subsequent inconsistency more damaging than it would be on its own.
*Chapter reference:* The Backlash Dynamics / backlash asymmetry mechanism

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* For a "bias-audited" claim, the chapter specifies that the required evidence is a published audit by a credible methodology — specifying which protected characteristics were tested, what test set was used, what the results were, and who conducted the audit. If this evidence does not exist, the chapter is explicit: the claim must be cut or revised to describe what the brand is working toward rather than what it has achieved. A defensible alternative might read: "We conduct quarterly bias audits on [specific protected characteristics] using [specific methodology] and publish results at [URL]" — but only if that statement is literally true and documented.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The specific evidence components (protected characteristics, test set, results, auditor)
- The instruction to cut or revise when evidence is absent
- The contrast between the unprovable generic claim and a defensible specific one
*Chapter reference:* AI ethics claims / claim-proof map for AI tool brands

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The uncertainty about causal direction matters for a startup because it means making purpose claims without behavioral commitment is a double liability: not only is the causal benefit uncertain, but the backlash asymmetry makes purpose-washing actively more damaging than silence if the claims are not sustained. For a startup without financial slack, the chapter's logic suggests a conservative path: claim only what can be demonstrated, make the claims specific and verifiable, and build the behavioral commitments before making the public claims rather than after. The selection-bias problem the chapter identifies — purpose-driven brands we study are the ones that survived — makes the data on purpose-performance correlation especially unreliable for undercapitalized startups, who cannot absorb the downside if the commitment collapses.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The backlash asymmetry and why it is especially risky for resource-constrained organizations
- The distinction between aspirational purpose language and behavioral commitment
- The chapter's selection-bias warning applied to the startup context
*Chapter reference:* Backlash Dynamics / Still Puzzling / causal direction uncertainty

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The claim-proof discipline recommends cutting or qualifying the generalizability claim if no specific evidence supports it — a sample study cannot claim broad applicability without data on whether the sample represents the broader population. Applied to scientific writing, this reveals that the discipline is structurally identical to the standard of evidence-based inference: a claim in a paper should be calibrated to what the data can actually support, and generalizability beyond the sample is a separate claim requiring its own evidence (measurement of sample representativeness, replication across contexts, or explicit limitation acknowledgment). The difference from brand ethics is that scientific journals have formal mechanisms — peer review, limitations sections — to enforce this discipline, while brand ethics has historically had no equivalent enforcement until regulatory bodies like the EU ECGT created one.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Application of HAVE/NEED/CANNOT SUBSTANTIATE logic to a scientific claim
- The structural parallel between brand ethics and scientific inference standards
- Acknowledgment of what is different in the transfer context (institutional enforcement)
*Chapter reference:* Claim-Proof Map / evidence standards (transfer application)
