## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* In Spence's model, a separating equilibrium occurs when costly signals sort candidates by quality — high-quality candidates find the signal worth the cost, low-quality candidates do not, so the signal is credible. A pooling equilibrium collapses this: all candidates look the same, and employers cannot distinguish them.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Separating equilibria do not benefit all candidates equally — they benefit high-quality candidates by making them distinguishable, while low-quality candidates lose the informational advantage of blending in.
- C) The relative cost relationship is reversed; pooling equilibria arise when signals are cheap or absent, not when they require more effort.
*Chapter reference:* Spence signaling theory / separating vs. pooling equilibrium

---

**Q2 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter uses the Peng et al. finding to argue that raising the floor for all developers compresses the visible spread — everyone can now produce more code, so code quantity becomes a weaker separating signal. Brand and Ship remain costly because AI cannot automatically produce a positioned professional identity.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) More output does not improve brand differentiation if the output is indistinguishable from AI-assisted output from other engineers.
- C) The chapter does not argue Copilot users are more hireable; it argues that the signal value of output quantity decreases when everyone's output increases.
*Chapter reference:* GitHub signal collapse / Peng et al. 2023

---

**Q3 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter presents all four verbs as necessary and integrated. "Brand" is the step that makes the built tool legible, positioned, and differentiated — without it, a technically excellent tool lacks identity and separating signal. Skipping Brand produces a pooled, undifferentiated product.
*Chapter reference:* Four-verb framework (Ideate / Build / Brand / Ship)

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly introduces archetypes as "forcing functions" — constraints that make decisions cohere and tell you which moves are on-brand. A forcing function is a decision-making tool, not merely a classification scheme or a visual system.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Visual identity is one output of archetype work, but it is not the primary function of the archetype itself.
- C) Classification for research is a secondary use; the chapter emphasizes the archetype's operational role in guiding decisions.
*Chapter reference:* Twelve Jungian archetypes / archetype as forcing function

---

**Q5 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter uses Anthropic and OpenAI to illustrate that technical peers can occupy distinct brand positions, but the archetypes assigned are Anthropic as Sage and OpenAI as Explorer — this matches the chapter. However, the statement as written is correct in its specifics, so this item is True. (Correction: Q5 is True — the chapter does contrast Anthropic as Sage and OpenAI as Explorer to demonstrate how technical peers occupy distinct brand positions.)
*Chapter reference:* Anthropic vs. OpenAI brand comparison / archetype differentiation

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* Spence's framework defines signal credibility by its cost to low-quality senders. Publishing and presenting requires effort that a low-quality engineer is less likely to sustain over time, so the signal credibly sorts by quality. The cost is what makes it a separating signal, not the content alone.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Communication skill is not the mechanism the chapter invokes; signal cost is the mechanism.
- C) Employer preferences are not the explanation; the cost structure of producing the signal is.
*Chapter reference:* Costly signaling / separating equilibrium applied to professional branding

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* "Ship" produces the deployed public URL of the tool (a live artifact someone outside the team can use) and the professional presence artifacts — portfolio, published writing, presentation — that make the work visible and attributable. "Build" produces the working code and pipeline; "Ship" produces the public-facing evidence that the tool exists and was created by this person.
*Common error:* Conflating "Ship" with "Build" by listing technical outputs (code, pipeline, architecture) rather than public-facing artifacts (URL, portfolio case study, launch post).
*Chapter reference:* Four-verb framework / Ship

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter's core argument is that AI raises the floor for technical outputs, making them weaker separating signals. The flaw in the developer's reasoning is that accumulating more cheap signals does not solve the differentiation problem — only costly signals do.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter does not argue that communication replaces technical skill; it argues that technical skill alone is insufficient for differentiation in an AI-augmented market.
- C) The chapter does not suggest branding is a senior-level concern; it argues it is necessary from the start because the market applies signal logic at every stage.
*Chapter reference:* Signal compression / costly vs. cheap signals

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* In a pooling equilibrium, all candidates appear identical to employers — high-quality and low-quality candidates are indistinguishable. For a high-quality candidate, this means they cannot command the premium their quality warrants; they are paid or treated as if they were average, because the employer has no credible signal to act on.
*What a strong answer includes:* Use of the term "pooling equilibrium," explanation that the employer cannot distinguish quality, and identification of the consequence for the high-quality candidate (loss of differentiation premium).
*Chapter reference:* Spence signaling theory / pooling equilibrium

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* A signal is "costly" in Spence's framework when it is expensive enough in time, effort, or resources that low-quality senders find it not worth faking. AI tooling made code quantity cheap because tools like Copilot dramatically reduce the effort required to produce code — this is why the Peng et al. finding matters. A positioned brand with a deployed tool, published case study, and coherent professional presence remains costly because AI cannot yet substitute for the judgment, consistency, and sustained effort required to produce and maintain it.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Definition of "costly" in terms of the self-selection logic (low-quality senders avoid it)
- A specific cheapened signal from the chapter (code quantity / GitHub commits)
- A specific still-costly signal (deployed branded tool, published writing, coherent presence)
*Common error:* Defining "costly" purely in financial terms rather than in terms of the signal's self-selection property.
*Chapter reference:* Costly signaling / signal compression / GitHub collapse

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* In academic research, the equivalent of a costly separating signal is a peer-reviewed publication in a high-scrutiny journal — it requires sustained effort, subject-matter expertise, and successfully passing expert review, which low-quality researchers are less likely to achieve. The pooling equivalent would be a market where only self-published work counts: all researchers look equivalent because anyone can publish, and employers (hiring committees) cannot distinguish quality from quantity. The deep structure matches the chapter's framework: cost that low-quality senders avoid is what makes the signal credible.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Identification of a domain-specific costly signal
- Explanation of why its cost makes it credible (self-selection logic)
- Description of what pooling looks like in that domain
*Chapter reference:* Spence signaling theory (transfer application)
