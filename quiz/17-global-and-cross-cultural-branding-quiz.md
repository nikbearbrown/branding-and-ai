## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines glocalization as "a global strategy with local execution" — the brand's core (purpose, archetype, fundamental promise) holds everywhere, while the expression (language, imagery, claims, channel mix, cultural references) adapts to each market. McDonald's is the chapter's primary example: identical Golden Arches globally, locally adapted menu items (McSpicy Paneer) and locally referenced marketing.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Identical execution in every market is pure standardization — the opposite pole from adaptation, which glocalization synthesizes.
- C) Acquiring regional brands is a portfolio strategy, not the chapter's definition of glocalization.
*Chapter reference:* Standardize or Adapt? / glocalization / McDonald's

---

**Q2 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter states explicitly: "Hofstede's dimensions are population-level tendencies, not scripts for individuals." Using the framework to predict individual behavior "produces exactly the kind of cultural error it is supposed to help avoid." The right use is to ask better questions about the market before doing empirical work, not to script individual responses.
*Chapter reference:* The Cultural Lens / Hofstede's dimensions / caveat

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* Translation converts words while preserving literal meaning. Localization adds context — formats, units, cultural references, date conventions — so content reads naturally in the target market. Transcreation works from the emotional intent and effect, accepting that the words may be entirely different, because brand language is not primarily about literal meaning but about emotional resonance and cultural connotation.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) A "cultural audit" and "deployment" are not the chapter's three levels; the chapter names translation, localization, and transcreation specifically.
- C) This reverses the order and the definitions — transcreation preserves emotional effect, not words; translation preserves words.
*Chapter reference:* Translation vs. Transcreation / three levels

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter names both failure modes explicitly. Too rigid means treating the expression as fixed along with the core — producing a brand that sounds like a foreigner making local references without knowing the culture. Too loose means flexing the core along with the expression — producing a brand that becomes a different thing in every country, unable to accumulate global equity.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Market entry and market quantity are not the failure modes the chapter names.
- C) AI-generated transcreation quality and translation investment are not the failure modes in the core-vs-expression framework.
*Chapter reference:* What Stays Global / too rigid, too loose failure modes

---

**Q5 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly cautions against the GM Nova story: "The GM Nova/no-va story... is contested — the car actually sold reasonably well in those markets, and 'nova' is not naturally parsed as 'no va' in Spanish." The chapter includes a verify warning: "many of the most-repeated cross-cultural blunder stories are apocryphal, exaggerated, or simply wrong." The Nova story is named as one of these.
*Chapter reference:* The Blunder File / verification warning

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter instructs that Hofstede's dimensions should be used "as a starting lens" to ask better questions about the market, not to make definitive substitution decisions without empirical work. The correct application is to identify individualism-vs-collectivism as a relevant dimension, generate questions about how the current messaging might land, and then verify with people actually embedded in the Japanese market.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter does not allow dismissing Hofstede's dimensions for tech audiences — it applies the framework across categories, with the caveat that it is a starting lens, not an exemption.
- C) Categorically replacing messaging without empirical verification is precisely the mechanistic application the chapter warns against when it says "do not use the framework to stereotype individuals."
*Chapter reference:* The Cultural Lens / applying Hofstede's dimensions correctly / Worked Example

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Humor is the most reliable victim of translation because a joke depends on a specific set of shared assumptions about what is surprising, transgressive, and true — assumptions that are cultural rather than linguistic. A joke that works in São Paulo operates on the audience's shared sense of what counts as subversive or absurd in that cultural context; those assumptions do not transfer across a border. Translating the words preserves the grammatical structure but not the shared assumptions that make the setup land and the punchline surprise. This reveals why transcreation must work from emotional intent: the question is not "what do these words mean in this language?" but "what would produce this feeling — surprise, recognition, warmth, irony — in this culture?" The answer may require entirely different premises, different targets, different cultural touchstones. The words are a delivery mechanism for the intent; if the cultural substrate for those specific words does not exist, different words must carry the same intent.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The cultural-assumption dependency of humor
- Why literal translation fails for jokes (preserves words, loses shared substrate)
- The transcreation principle: work from emotional intent, not words
*Chapter reference:* Translation vs. Transcreation / humor

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines core as purpose, archetype, and fundamental promise — why the brand exists, who it is, what it offers. These are not market-specific. Expression is everything through which the core is communicated: language, imagery, specific claims, tone calibration, channel, seasonal references, cultural examples.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) This reverses core and expression — specific taglines and campaign imagery are expression, not core.
- C) The visual identity and logo sit in the mostly-global middle band, but purpose and archetype are core, and the chapter does not categorize voice as more core than logo.
*Chapter reference:* What Stays Global / core vs. expression

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* In the three-stage market-entry plan, AI can generate and draft at volume across all three stages: producing the keep-global/localize split with rationale using Hofstede's lens, drafting transcreated taglines and messages working from the emotional intent, and flagging cultural risks for name, colors, imagery, and numerical associations. What AI cannot do is verify whether any of these outputs actually land with cultural authenticity in the target market — because that judgment requires the felt experience of being embedded in a culture, knowing what subtext a phrase carries, sensing whether an image feels familiar or foreign, and reading the resonance of a brand voice. The AI+1 line falls at the native speaker verification step in all three stages: AI generates drafts and flags, but a person embedded in the target market must verify each flag and each transcreation before it is used.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- AI's role in drafting, flagging, and applying the Hofstede lens at each stage
- The specific thing AI cannot do: verify cultural authenticity and felt resonance
- Where the AI+1 line falls (native verification step)
*Chapter reference:* Worked Example / AI+1 line / three-stage market-entry plan

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* Glocalization resolves the Levitt-vs-adaptation debate by accepting both insights: Levitt was right that some things travel globally (the brand's core can hold), and the adaptation critics were right that local execution matters (the expression must localize). But glocalization introduces its own tensions it does not fully resolve: the most important is determining exactly which elements belong to the core versus the expression, since the chapter's own examples show that this boundary is contested and requires judgment for each element. A second tension is that glocalization assumes the core is clearly enough defined to remain stable across markets — for brands whose core is itself ambiguous or still emerging, glocalization's prescription is technically correct but practically difficult to implement.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Acknowledgment of what glocalization resolves
- At least one genuine tension it does not eliminate (core-vs-expression boundary decisions, or implementation difficulty for ambiguous cores)
- Specific grounding in the chapter's framework
*Chapter reference:* Standardize or Adapt? / glocalization / What Stays Global

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* Applying Mead's insight: frequent self-assessment quizzes assume that regular self-evaluation is a natural and productive pedagogical mechanism, but in high-power-distance cultures (where India scores higher than the US on Hofstede's scale), assessment is often understood as a top-down authority function rather than a self-directed process — making student-initiated self-assessment feel culturally dissonant. Direct instructor challenge of student assumptions may carry respect implications in a high-power-distance context where public disagreement with a senior figure violates expected interaction norms. Verification for self-assessment would require talking to students embedded in the target Indian institutions and observing how they respond to self-assessment prompts under real course conditions. Verification for direct challenge would require similar fieldwork — asking instructors and students from that context whether the interaction feels like intellectual rigor or social violation, using Mead's fieldwork method rather than the platform team's assumptions about what is pedagogically universal.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- At least two specific platform elements identified as potentially culturally contingent
- Application of Hofstede's power-distance dimension as a starting lens (not a script)
- A specific verification method grounded in Mead's fieldwork approach
*Chapter reference:* The Cultural Lens / Hofstede power distance / Worked Example / AI Wayback Machine — Margaret Mead (transfer application)
