## Answer Key

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter names three channels explicitly: Direct hiring (a recruiter finds the portfolio and moves the candidate forward), Indirect reference (someone encounters the portfolio through a share or link, bookmarks it, and surfaces the name later when a role opens), and Template effects (the portfolio design or case study structure becomes a starting point for other developers, each derivative carrying the original creator's name in commit histories).
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) SEO, social reach, and recruiter databases are mechanisms within Channel 1, not the chapter's three-channel framework.
- C) Portfolio traffic, GitHub stars, and LinkedIn views are metrics within channels, not the channels themselves.
*Chapter reference:* Three compounding channels / direct hiring, indirect reference, template effects

---

**Q2 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly states the opposite: "Three to six projects is the right number. Three is better than six if the three are excellent and the six would include mediocre work." The argument is that ten projects reduces each to a thumbnail, making the portfolio read as "I have done things" rather than "I am this kind of engineer."
*Chapter reference:* Curation and negative space / three to six projects

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter names the four functions explicitly: frame the problem (specific user, specific pain, specific frequency), show the technical work with specific metrics (throughput, latency, accuracy, cost), connect to the brand (archetype, voice, visual choices, and alignment audit), and be honest about limits (what the tool does not do, MVP boundary, what Build-Measure-Learn has revealed).
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Listing tools, linking to GitHub, and including testimonials are not among the four named functions.
- C) Timeline, team roles, and cost estimates are not among the four named functions.
*Chapter reference:* The centerpiece case study / four functions

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter describes v0 as generating real React code (exportable, iterable, stronger technical signal to React-oriented teams) and Framer as design-to-site with visual-first workflow, less implementation flexibility, and Framer-hosted infrastructure. The deciding question is what the primary demonstration is: technical implementation capability (v0) vs. visual design execution (Framer).
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Cost comparison is not the chapter's primary distinction between the two tools.
- C) The chapter does not describe v0 as requiring no design knowledge or Framer as requiring advanced CSS; both are described as low-code paths.

*Chapter reference:* Deploy and align / coherence audit / convergence vs. uniformity

**Q6 — B**

A Sage AI engineer positioned around developer-first marketing intelligence tools should select projects that demonstrate intelligence-building. Projects 1 (competitor news sentiment), 3 (RAG document Q&A), and 5 (survey clustering) all demonstrate intelligence-building. The to-do list app and personal finance tracker are general-purpose development projects that do not reinforce the Sage-AI-intelligence positioning and would dilute the portfolio's point of view.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Including all five reduces each project and contradicts the three-to-six rule with its rationale for exclusion.
- C) One project is below the three minimum; the chapter supports three to six excellent projects as more powerful than one.
*Chapter reference:* Curation and negative space / negative-space rule

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The chapter applies the Feynman principle — that verifiable claims build trust in a way unverifiable claims cannot — to career development: a case study that says "processes articles quickly and accurately" is a marketing claim because no one can check it. A case study that says "processes 870 articles in under three minutes at a cost of approximately $0.80 per run, with 90% deduplication efficiency" is a case study because the numbers make the claim checkable. Trust is built through verifiability because the reader can ask: if these numbers are wrong, the candidate would know it and could be caught. A candidate willing to publish specific numbers is either honest or willing to be verified. That implied accountability is what makes the specific claim more credible than the generic one — not the quantity of information, but its falsifiability.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The distinction between verifiable (numbers) and unverifiable (marketing language) claims
- Why falsifiability produces trust (the Feynman principle)
- The contrast between "quickly and accurately" and specific metrics
*Chapter reference:* The centerpiece case study / metrics / Feynman principle

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter describes Chiang's portfolio as compounding through both Channel 2 (indirect reference, because it was distinctive and craftful enough to be worth sharing and referencing) and Channel 3 (template effects, because the code was open and the design was clean enough to clone and adapt). The chapter argues these two channels require compatible but not identical design properties — Channel 2 needs distinction, Channel 3 needs clarity and learn-ability.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) SEO ranking is not mentioned as the mechanism for Chiang's template effects.
- C) Chiang's professional network is acknowledged as one factor in her career, but the chapter attributes the template effects specifically to the portfolio's design and openness, not network distribution.
*Chapter reference:* Template effects / Brittany Chiang / three compounding channels

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The portfolio-as-product is distinguished from the other four by being the convergence point — the artifact where the same archetype, the same positioning sentence, the same selected projects, and the same voice are legible from all five surfaces simultaneously, so that a recruiter who encounters any two of the five surfaces encounters the same person from different angles. The four individual surfaces (résumé, website, GitHub, LinkedIn) exist independently; the portfolio-as-product is the intentional integration of all four into a coherent brand. The specific work required to achieve it is the coherence audit: verifying that the positioning sentence matches across surfaces, the same archetype is legible in all, the same three to five projects are featured everywhere, and the voice reads as the same person across the about section and LinkedIn about section.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Convergence as the defining property of portfolio-as-product
- The coherence audit as the mechanism for achieving it
- The distinction from the individual surfaces as standalone artifacts
*Chapter reference:* Five things "portfolio" means / portfolio-as-product / coherence audit

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The Channel 2 → Channel 3 ordering is defensible because the portfolio that no one finds worth referencing will not accumulate forks regardless of how clean the code is — Channel 3 requires Channel 2 as a prerequisite (someone has to encounter and value the design before cloning it). But the ordering creates a bias toward optimizing for human aesthetics and memorability at the expense of structural simplicity — Chiang's site worked partly because it was architecturally minimal, which made it both worth referencing and easy to adapt. A portfolio that is highly distinctive but architecturally complex may accrue Channel 2 returns without producing Channel 3 returns, which the chapter's heuristic does not directly address. A more principled rule might be: optimize for distinctiveness first (Channel 2), then impose structural simplicity as a second-pass discipline (enabling Channel 3 without sacrificing Channel 2).
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The causal dependency argument (Channel 2 as prerequisite for Channel 3)
- The systematic bias the ordering creates (toward memorability at cost of simplicity)
- A defensible alternative rule
*Chapter reference:* Still Puzzling / template effects / Brittany Chiang

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* A portfolio-as-product for a behavioral economist positioned around long-term financial decision-making under uncertainty would apply the coherence audit by ensuring the same positioning statement appears on the faculty page, Academia.edu bio, and ResearchGate profile; the same three to five papers (selected for archetype alignment — work demonstrating behavioral insight into long-term decision processes under uncertainty — not necessarily the most recent or most cited) are featured consistently; and the voice in the research statement reads as the same person as the commentary in published op-eds and conference presentations. The negative-space rule would recommend excluding working papers outside the positioning (a paper on short-term risk perception, for example, would dilute the positioning toward general behavioral economics rather than the more specific long-term/uncertainty focus). The analog to the coherence audit's "does this feel like the same person from three angles?" test would be: does a prospective collaborator encountering any two of the three profile surfaces understand, without ambiguity, that this scholar studies long-term financial decision-making under uncertainty specifically?
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Application of coherence audit logic to the scholarly context
- Application of negative-space rule to project/paper selection
- A specific positioning test analogous to the chapter's coherence audit question
*Chapter reference:* Coherence audit / negative-space rule / portfolio-as-product (transfer application)
