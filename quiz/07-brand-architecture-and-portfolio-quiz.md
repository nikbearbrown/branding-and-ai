## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* Apple is the chapter's example of a branded house: one master brand (Apple) appears on all products, and iPhone, Mac, and iPad are product descriptors rather than independent brand identities with separate equity. The master brand does the work of credentialing every product in the portfolio.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) House of brands means the parent company is invisible to consumers and each brand operates independently — P&G and Unilever fit this, not Apple.
- C) An endorsed brand structure has independent brand identities with the parent as endorser — "Courtyard by Marriott" fits this pattern, not Apple's iPhone.
*Chapter reference:* Branded house / Apple example

---

**Q2 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly presents the Brand Relationship Spectrum as a continuum with house of brands (maximum independence — the parent brand is not visible to consumers) at one pole and branded house (maximum unity — the master brand is the primary identity on everything) at the other.
*Chapter reference:* Aaker/Joachimsthaler Brand Relationship Spectrum

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* "Courtyard by Marriott" is the chapter's endorsed brand example: Courtyard operates with its own identity (name, physical design, service model) while Marriott appears as a visible endorser that signals quality and reliability. The structure gives Courtyard independence while allowing Marriott equity to credential it.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) A sub-brand in the chapter refers to Apple → iPhone, where the master brand leads and the product has a descriptor; "by Marriott" signals endorsement, not sub-brand subordination.
- C) In a branded house, the master brand is the primary identity — "Courtyard by Marriott" leads with the sub-brand name, making it an endorsed structure rather than a pure branded house.
*Chapter reference:* Endorsed brand / Courtyard by Marriott

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines dilution as the erosion of parent brand associations that results from extension into a category inconsistent with the parent brand's positioning. The damage is to the parent brand's equity in its core market, even if the extension itself generates sales.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Association with a new category alone does not define dilution — the chapter specifies the weakening of core category equity.
- C) The chapter does not define dilution as equity transfer to competitors; it defines it as weakening of the parent brand's own associations.
*Chapter reference:* Brand dilution / brand extension

---

**Q5 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter presents Intel Inside and Gore-Tex as canonical ingredient branding examples: the component brand is visible to and valued by end consumers, who use the ingredient brand's reputation to make purchasing decisions about finished products (computers, outdoor gear).
*Chapter reference:* Ingredient branding / Intel Inside / Gore-Tex

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter's dilution logic applies directly: extending a premium, clinical brand into a price-accessible drugstore segment introduces associations (affordable, mass-market) that contradict the core brand associations (premium, clinical, evidence-based) that support the price premium in the existing category. This is the dilution risk the architecture decision must address before manufacturing or marketing questions.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Manufacturing cost is an operational concern, not an architecture concern — the chapter focuses on equity before execution.
- C) Marketing budget is a marketing strategy question, not a brand architecture question.
*Chapter reference:* Brand dilution / brand extension / association dilution

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* A line extension adds a product within the existing category — for example, a soda brand launching a new flavor (Coca-Cola launching Coke Zero). A category extension moves the brand into an adjacent category — for example, a food brand launching a line of clothing. The chapter identifies category extension as carrying higher dilution risk because the new category may activate associations that contradict the parent brand's existing positioning in its core category.
*Common error:* Confusing line and category extension by treating any new product as a category extension.
*Chapter reference:* Line extension vs. category extension / dilution risk

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter frames the Unilever reduction as a concentration-of-investment decision: managing 1,600 brands diluted organizational attention and marketing investment across brands that were too small to build meaningful equity or that competed against each other within the same category. Reducing to 400 allowed Unilever to concentrate behind brands with the scale to dominate their categories.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Revenue decline is not the chapter's stated reason — the logic is about concentration, not failure.
- C) Regulatory pressure is not mentioned as the driver.
*Chapter reference:* Portfolio pruning / Unilever case

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Nike × Apple: Both brands benefit, but the primary equity flow is Apple gaining credibility in the fitness/athletic performance space by association with Nike's athletic authenticity. Nike gains by being associated with premium technology design. Supreme × Louis Vuitton: Louis Vuitton gains access to Supreme's credibility with younger, streetwear-adjacent consumers — the flow is primarily Louis Vuitton acquiring cultural currency it lacked in that demographic. Supreme gains luxury prestige credibility. The mechanism in both cases is category-transcending association: each brand accesses the other's strongest associations in a category where it was weaker.
*Common error:* Stating only one direction of equity flow when both are bidirectional.
*Chapter reference:* Co-branding / Nike × Apple / Supreme × Louis Vuitton

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* "Quill for Code" is a sub-brand extension that keeps the Quill master brand doing equity work — it can transfer Quill's writing-tool associations (precision, intelligent output) into a coding context. The risk is dilution if "Code" associations (speed, power, developer culture) contradict Quill's writing associations. "Meridian" as a separate brand requires building equity from zero but avoids dilution entirely — it allows the research tool to have its own positioning. For a startup, the better choice depends on whether the Quill equity is strong enough to credential the new product or whether the new product's target audience would be better reached with a clean brand.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Recognition that both paths have trade-offs
- The dilution risk of extending Quill
- The equity-building cost of Meridian
- A specific recommendation with reasoning
*Chapter reference:* Brand Relationship Spectrum / sub-brand / dilution risk

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* Maintaining three presence areas — AI tools, graphic design, and personal finance blogging — distributes attention and creates three different brand identities that may contradict each other. Portfolio pruning logic suggests concentrating on the area with the strongest equity and highest positioning clarity. If the professional's archetype and genuine expertise are in AI engineering, the graphic design and personal finance presences dilute the AI tool signal by suggesting a generalist rather than a specialist. The recommended prune would be to archive or minimize the less-aligned presences and consolidate investment in the AI engineering brand — the one closest to the professional's archetype and positioning.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Application of the concentration logic from the Unilever case
- Recognition that multiple presence areas create the equivalent of competing brands
- A specific recommendation grounded in the professional's archetype
*Chapter reference:* Portfolio pruning / Unilever case (transfer application)

**Common errors to watch for in student work:**

- Confusing endorsed brand with sub-brand — the "by [parent]" structure is the signal for endorsed brand, while sub-brand has the master brand leading.
- Treating dilution as merely commercial failure of an extension rather than as equity erosion in the parent brand.
- Missing the equity flow directionality in co-branding (both brands gain, but the flows are asymmetric).

- Uses "brand dilution" to mean "brand confusion" rather than "parent brand equity erosion."
