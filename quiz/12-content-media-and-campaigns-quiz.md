## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* PESO stands for Paid (advertising you pay for), Earned (coverage by others who choose to feature you), Shared (content spread through social sharing, not paid for), and Owned (content you control and publish on your own properties). These four categories form the chapter's media classification framework.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Performance, Engagement, Scale, and Optimization are analytics/growth marketing terms, not the PESO categories.
- C) Product, Experience, Strategy, and Output are not media types.
*Chapter reference:* PESO model

---

**Q2 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter attributes IMC to Don Schultz at Northwestern University and defines it as the discipline of coordinating all marketing communications channels to deliver a consistent, reinforcing message — so that a customer encounters the same essential message whether they see an ad, read a press piece, or engage with social content.
*Chapter reference:* IMC / Don Schultz / Northwestern

---

**Q3 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines the AI content flood paradox as the self-undermining dynamic created by AI content tools: by making it cheap to produce large volumes of content, AI simultaneously increases the supply of content and decreases the signal value of each individual piece (because audiences are flooded and filter more aggressively). More content from everyone means any given piece gets less attention.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter does not argue AI content is technically superior; the paradox is about signal value, not quality.
- C) Being both a production and distribution platform is not the chapter's definition of the paradox.
*Chapter reference:* AI content flood paradox

---

**Q4 — A**
*Correct because:* The chapter's six campaign brief sections are: Objective, Audience, Message, Channel mix, Budget, and Measurement. Option A correctly names four of the six.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) Legal review and distribution plan are not named as campaign brief sections in the chapter.
- C) Post-campaign analysis and media buy are not named campaign brief sections in the chapter's framework.
*Chapter reference:* Campaign brief six sections

---

**Q5 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter defines the trade-off in the opposite direction: reach means delivering the message to a larger (broader) audience, while frequency means delivering the message to the same audience more times. High reach and high frequency simultaneously requires a much larger budget; the trade-off is choosing which to prioritize on a fixed budget.
*Chapter reference:* Reach vs. frequency trade-off

---

**Q6 — A**
*Correct because:* PESO classification: (1) LinkedIn sponsored post = Paid (you pay for the placement); (2) CEO personal Twitter post = Shared (content distributed through a social network, even personally authored); (3) TechCrunch coverage = Earned (a third party chose to cover the story); (4) Company blog case study = Owned (company controls and publishes on its own property). All four are correctly classified.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- B) The blog is Owned, not Shared — Shared media is distributed through social networks by others, not published on your own property.
- C) A CEO's personal social post is Shared (distributed through a social network), not Earned; TechCrunch coverage is Earned, not Owned.
*Chapter reference:* PESO model / classification

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The Stripe Atlas launch used detailed technical documentation (Owned media) that provided genuine utility to its target audience — developers and founders — rather than marketing copy. This is IMC in practice: the educational content itself is the message, and the message is consistent whether encountered via the documentation, a press piece, or a developer forum. Second, Stripe seeded early discussions in developer communities, generating Earned and Shared coverage that carried the same core message (Atlas as infrastructure-grade financial infrastructure for global startups), reinforcing rather than contradicting the Owned content. IMC requires that each channel reinforce the others — the Stripe Atlas launch demonstrates this because every channel touched the same problem-solution frame.
*Common error:* Describing only one element or failing to connect it to the IMC consistency principle.
*Chapter reference:* Stripe Atlas launch / IMC case study

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter's AI content flood paradox explains precisely why maximum-volume AI content production is strategically counterproductive: when every competitor can produce high volumes, volume loses its signal function. The AI+1 discipline responds to this by adding irreplaceable human judgment — perspective, original insight, specific context — that differentiates content from the flood rather than contributing to it.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter does not claim AI content is universally lower quality; the problem is signal value, not technical quality.
- C) Single-channel focus is not the chapter's recommended response to the paradox.
*Chapter reference:* AI content flood paradox / AI+1 discipline

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Reach maximization delivers the message to the largest possible number of unique people — but each person may only encounter it once or twice, reducing the likelihood that the message registers and influences behavior. Frequency maximization delivers the message to a smaller group many more times, building recall and familiarity but limiting the total audience who can be influenced. The chapter suggests reach is more appropriate for awareness campaigns (building recognition in a new market), while frequency is more appropriate for persuasion campaigns (moving people who are already aware toward a decision). On a limited budget, the choice depends on the campaign objective: awareness favors reach; conversion favors frequency.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- What each approach gives up
- When each approach is appropriate
- The budget constraint as the determining context
*Chapter reference:* Reach vs. frequency trade-off

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* AI tools make IMC harder, not easier: they make it trivially easy to produce channel-specific creative variations, which introduces the risk that each variation optimizes for its channel's norms (casual on social, formal in press materials, technical in documentation) at the cost of message consistency across channels. The AI content flood paradox compounds this — more channels with more content creates more surface area for inconsistency. The AI+1 discipline contributes to resolving the tension by requiring human judgment at the synthesis layer: the human's job is to ensure that the AI-drafted content across channels tells the same story and maintains the same archetype, even when the tactical execution varies. IMC requires strategic consistency above tactical variation, and that is exactly the judgment AI cannot supply.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- The AI-makes-it-harder argument (more channels, more inconsistency risk)
- The AI+1 discipline's role (human judgment at strategic synthesis layer)
- Recognition that tactical variation is fine but strategic consistency requires human oversight
*Chapter reference:* IMC / AI content flood paradox / AI+1 discipline

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* For an academic researcher: Paid = sponsored conference presentations or promoted academic network posts; Earned = citations by other researchers, press coverage of the finding, invitations to speak; Shared = Twitter/X threads discussing the finding in researcher communities, LinkedIn posts by colleagues recommending the work; Owned = the lab's website, a Substack or blog explaining the research for non-specialists, preprint server posts. For a junior researcher launching a first major finding, the recommended mix would prioritize Owned (control the explanation and framing) and Shared (seed discussion in research communities) over Paid (low budget) and Earned (not yet established enough to attract organic press). Shared is especially valuable for junior researchers because it leverages the networks of more established colleagues without requiring their endorsement.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- A translation of each PESO category into academic context
- A recommended mix with reasoning
- Recognition of budget and credibility constraints for junior researchers
*Chapter reference:* PESO model (transfer application)
