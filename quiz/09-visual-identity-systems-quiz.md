## Answer Key

> Read this section only after completing all questions.

---

**Q1 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter specifies WCAG 2.2 AA standards exactly as 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text (18pt or 14pt bold and above). These are the minimum contrast ratios a design must meet to pass accessibility compliance.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) 3:1 is the standard for large text, not normal text; 2:1 is below both thresholds.
- C) 7:1 is the WCAG AAA (enhanced) standard, not the AA minimum that the chapter specifies.
*Chapter reference:* WCAG 2.2 AA standards

---

**Q2 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter uses the Pepsi 2008 case to illustrate "strategy-free rationale" — a lengthy, elaborate design justification document that did not derive from a real brand strategy. The process produced explanation without foundation: the visual changes were justified post-hoc rather than driven by a strategic position.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) The chapter does not attribute the failure to the design studio's brand knowledge.
- C) Logo frequency is not the chapter's diagnosis for the Pepsi case.
*Chapter reference:* Pepsi 2008 failure case / strategy-free rationale

---

**Q3 — True**
*Correct because:* The chapter's archetype-to-visual-language mapping explicitly assigns: Sage = restrained, cool, intellectually precise visual language; Hero = bold, high-saturation, action-signal visuals. These are directly stated in the chapter.
*Chapter reference:* Archetype-to-visual-language mapping / Sage / Hero

---

**Q4 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter lists motion and animation principles as one of the six visual identity components, alongside logo/wordmark, color palette, typography, imagery, and layout. Motion is increasingly important as brands operate in digital and video contexts.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Tone of voice and messaging are brand voice components (Chapter 10), not visual identity components.
- C) Product pricing and packaging materials are product/marketing elements, not visual identity system components.
*Chapter reference:* Six visual identity components

**Q5 — False**
*Correct because:* The chapter assigns different failure diagnoses to the three cases: Pepsi 2008 was strategy-free rationale; Yahoo 2013 was strategy-free process (no clear strategy guiding the redesign); Tropicana 2009 was the wrong archetypal foundation. Yahoo's failure was about process — a redesign executed without clear strategic direction — not about the right foundation executed poorly.
*Chapter reference:* Yahoo 2013 failure case / three failure cases

---

**Q6 — B**
*Correct because:* 4.2:1 is below the 4.5:1 minimum for normal text under WCAG 2.2 AA — the design fails the standard and must be revised. The brand implication is specifically pointed for a Caregiver archetype: a brand whose core motivation is caring for and including all users that ships an inaccessible interface has contradicted its own archetype's logic.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) 3:1 is the large-text threshold, and 4.2:1 exceeds it — but if the text is normal-sized, the 4.5:1 standard applies and 4.2:1 fails.
- C) 4.2:1 does not pass WCAG 2.2 AA for normal text.
*Chapter reference:* WCAG 2.2 AA / archetype-to-visual alignment

---

**Q7 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* The eight sections of the creative brief are: (1) Project overview, (2) Brand strategy summary (archetype, positioning, audience), (3) Design objectives, (4) Deliverables specification, (5) Visual direction and references, (6) Constraints (technical, budget, accessibility), (7) Approval process, (8) Timeline. The brand strategy summary section is critical because it connects the visual work to the brand's archetype and positioning — it is the section that prevents a design team from executing in a vacuum. Without it, the brief lacks the strategic anchor that the chapter's failure cases demonstrate is essential.
*Common error:* Listing fewer than five sections or omitting the brand strategy summary, which is the chapter's emphasized discipline.
*Chapter reference:* Creative brief eight sections

---

**Q8 — B**
*Correct because:* The chapter explicitly maps the Magician archetype to jewel tones and visually transformative imagery — visual cues that signal power, transformation, and hidden knowledge.
*Why the distractors are wrong:*
- A) Caregiver is mapped to warm, soft tones — not jewel tones.
- C) Explorer is mapped to earthy, natural palettes — not jewel tones.
*Chapter reference:* Archetype-to-visual-language mapping / Magician

---

**Q9 — Short-Answer**
*Model answer:* Tropicana replaced its iconic orange-with-straw packaging image with a glass of orange juice on a plain background. The orange-and-straw was a recognition asset — the visual trigger that let consumers identify Tropicana instantly in the store aisle. The new design was competently executed but stripped the category recognition cue, requiring consumers to read the label to identify the brand. Sales dropped 20% because the recognition asset was gone. The lesson for visual identity: recognition assets (the specific visual elements that trigger brand recall) are part of the brand's equity and must be preserved across redesigns unless the strategic intent is to deliberately break the association.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- What specifically changed (the orange-straw image)
- Why it failed (loss of recognition asset, not design quality)
- The general principle for visual identity management
*Chapter reference:* Tropicana 2009 / recognition assets / visual identity

---

**Q10 — Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* The creative brief would have prevented the Pepsi 2008 failure most directly: the brief's brand strategy summary section requires documenting what archetype and positioning the visual work serves. If Pepsi's design team had been required to articulate a coherent positioning before generating the elaborate rationale document, the absence of a clear strategy would have been visible before any design work began. The brief is not bureaucracy — it is the diagnostic tool that forces the strategic question before the execution question, which is exactly the order the three failure cases demonstrate matters most.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- Specific identification of which failure case the brief would have addressed
- Which section of the brief would have been the intervention point
- The mechanism (forcing strategy before rationale)
*Common error:* Arguing the brief would have prevented all three failures equally without identifying the specific mechanism.
*Chapter reference:* Creative brief / Pepsi 2008 failure case

---

**Q11 — Transfer Brief-Response**
*Model answer:* For a Sage-archetype AI engineer's portfolio: Color palette — cool, neutral colors (navy, off-white, slate) to signal precision and trustworthiness rather than entertainment or excitement. Typography — a serif or high-legibility sans-serif with strong hierarchical contrast between heading and body sizes, signaling intellectual rigor and information density. Imagery — technical diagrams, architecture visuals, and data visualizations rather than lifestyle photography, consistent with the Sage's evidence-based authority. Layout — dense information layout with clear grid, minimal decorative elements, rewarding close reading. Each decision eliminates the visual cues that would signal other archetypes (no playful colors of the Jester, no bold saturation of the Hero) and concentrates the Sage's intellectual-authority visual language.
*What a strong answer includes:*
- At least four components addressed
- A specific concrete decision for each
- The archetype reasoning connecting each decision to Sage identity
*Chapter reference:* Archetype-to-visual-language mapping / six components (transfer application)
