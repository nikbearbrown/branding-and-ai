## TL;DR

The final deliverable is not your portfolio. It is *you* — a coherent professional identity that integrates every artifact you've built and presents itself, in front of a room or on a screen, as a single recognizable thing. This chapter is about the last mile that closes the loop from build to brand to seen.

---

In early 2009, three founders walked into Y Combinator with a slide deck and a working prototype. Their company was called AirBed and Breakfast. Their idea was to let people rent air mattresses on the floors of strangers' apartments. Their previous fundraising had involved selling cereal at the Democratic and Republican conventions to keep the lights on while they figured out what they were doing. None of this was a strong opening.

Their deck was [ten slides](https://www.failory.com/pitch-deck/airbnb). The slides were not visually impressive — plain typography, simple bar charts, no design flourishes. But every slide carried specific work. Slide 1: the problem (price for hotels, no easy way for travelers to make local connections). Slide 2: the solution (a web platform to match hosts with travelers). Slide 3: market validation (specific traffic numbers from beta runs in San Francisco and Denver). Slide 4: a clear business model (10% commission). Slide 5 through 10: market size, adoption strategy, competition, team, financials, status.

Paul Graham listened. Y Combinator put in $20,000. Sequoia followed with the rest of the seed round. The deck raised $600,000 total. Today the company is called Airbnb, and that ten-slide deck is one of the most-studied artifacts in startup pitch literature.

The deck was not famous because of design. It became famous because *every slide reinforced the same story*. Problem implied solution implied market implied business model implied competition implied team implied opportunity. The slides were independent facts; the deck was a single argument. That coherence — not the slide design, not the cereal-selling story — closed the round.

This chapter is about producing that kind of coherence for *yourself* and *your work*. By the end of it you will have a final presentation you can deliver, a resume that survives the ATS filter while honoring your archetype, a social-media presence aligned with the rest of your brand, and a sense of how the work continues after the course is over.

The final presentation is the climactic moment of the book. It is also a starting point.

## Specifying "professional presence"

The phrase covers a system of artifacts that together signal who you are professionally:

1. **Resume** — text document, two versions: ATS-optimized for filter survival, designer-format for the human reader.
2. **Portfolio** — Chapter 11, deployed.
3. **LinkedIn presence** — your primary professional-network surface.
4. **Other social media** — Twitter/X, GitHub, sometimes blog or Substack. Optional in volume; mandatory in *coherence with everything else*.
5. **Pitch deck or presentation** — the artifact for moments when you present yourself or your work formally.
6. **Live presence** — how you actually appear in person or on video. Voice, posture, what you wear, what you sound like answering questions.
7. **Post-presentation followup** — emails, thank-you notes, sustained relationship-building.

The chapter focuses primarily on #1, #5, #6, and #7. Items #2 and #3 were Chapter 11; #4 is largely set by your archetype. The work here is integrating everything into a moment — the final presentation — and a system that survives past the course.

## Why the integrated pitch wins

The deep mechanism this chapter installs: a presentation is a unit of brand impression denser than any other surface. In ten minutes of pitching, your audience forms an impression of you that they would otherwise need months of LinkedIn scrolling to form. The density is the asset. The density is also the risk — the same ten minutes that can land you a job offer can land you a permanent "do not hire."

Three properties of integrated pitches that work:

**1. Every slide answers the same question.** Most graduate pitches make ten different arguments — here is my problem, here is my technical work, here is my brand, here is my career story, here is what I want from you. Each slide is a separate plea. The audience leaves remembering nothing because there was nothing to remember. Integrated pitches make *one* argument across ten slides. The Airbnb deck's argument: *the world wants this, here is the proof, we are the team to build it, here is what we need.* Every slide reinforces.

**2. Specific numbers do load-bearing work.** The Airbnb deck included real numbers — early traffic from San Francisco and Denver, specific hotel pricing comparisons, a 10% commission model with revenue projections. The numbers were not impressive in absolute terms. They were specific. Specificity is the signal that the founders had done the work; vagueness is the signal that they had not.

**3. The team slide carries weight.** Investors fund teams; recruiters hire people; the team slide is the heart of the pitch. For the AI tool you've built, *you are the team*. The team slide for your final presentation is your own brand — archetype, positioning, work shown — distilled to one slide. Done well, it does most of the persuasion work in the deck.

These properties extend beyond pitch decks. A LinkedIn profile that follows the same discipline — every section answering the same question, specific numbers where possible, the about section doing weight-bearing work — produces the same density of impression in a different format. The Airbnb pattern is a presentation pattern, not just a slide-deck pattern.

### A worked case: the Airbnb deck's coherence

Read the Airbnb deck slide by slide with the chapter's lens.

- **Slide 1 (Problem).** Specific framing: hotel prices, lack of local connection in unfamiliar cities. Already implies the solution.
- **Slide 2 (Solution).** A web platform matching hosts with travelers. Stated cleanly; no buzzwords.
- **Slide 3 (Market validation).** Specific beta data from San Francisco and Denver. Real numbers, even if small.
- **Slide 4 (Market size).** Top-down market sizing. Travelers, listings, possible commission.
- **Slide 5 (Product).** Screenshots of the working prototype. Not a vision; an artifact.
- **Slide 6 (Business model).** 10% commission per booking. Simple. Defensible. Aligned with how a marketplace creates value.
- **Slide 7 (Adoption).** How to grow. Specific channels, specific tactics.
- **Slide 8 (Competition).** Couchsurfing, hostels, Craigslist. Honest acknowledgement.
- **Slide 9 (Team).** Three founders, named, with relevant work history.
- **Slide 10 (Financials and status).** What's been spent, what's needed, what the runway buys.

Notice: every slide leans on specific evidence. Notice: every slide carries the same archetypal voice (Sage with Hero elements — methodical, specific, ambitious). Notice: every slide implies the next.

When you build your final presentation, follow the same discipline. Your problem slide should already imply your tool. Your tool slide should imply the audience your tool serves. Your team slide (which is mostly *you*) should imply the kind of work and partnership you offer. Coherence beats polish.

## The resume — two versions, one identity

The resume is the artifact that lives outside your presence. It travels independently — through ATS systems, through HR inboxes, through hiring-manager email forwards. It must survive the journey while still feeling like *you*.

Two versions are required, not optional:

**Version 1: ATS-optimized.** Plain text, single column, standard section headers (*Experience*, *Education*, *Skills*), keyword-rich based on the roles you target. Saved as PDF and Word. No fancy fonts, no graphics, no two-column layouts that confuse parsers. The ATS will read this version. If it fails the ATS read, the human reader never sees you. Tools like Jobscan let you check the parse rate before submission.

**Version 2: Designer format.** Visually distinctive, archetype-aligned, uses your brand's color palette and typography. Saved as PDF only. This is the version a human reader sees once your application has cleared the filter or once you've sent it directly. Designer resumes signal craft and intentionality; they also signal *which* candidate you are when several are being considered.

Same content, two formats. Both versions match your portfolio and LinkedIn — same project descriptions, same role narratives, same accomplishments. A reviewer reading all three in sequence should see one person, not three.

The single most important resume discipline: each role's bullet points should *show* outcomes, not list responsibilities. *"Built data pipeline ingesting 870 articles per day with 90% deduplication rate"* shows. *"Worked with team on data engineering tasks"* lists. The first is hireable; the second is not. Specificity again — the Airbnb deck pattern repeats.

## Social media as a brand-coherence asset

Most graduates either over-invest in social media (chasing follower counts) or under-invest (treating LinkedIn as a checkbox). Both are mistakes. The right framing: social media is a brand-coherence surface — its job is to confirm that you are the same person across artifacts, and to give your work somewhere to live and circulate.

**LinkedIn**: covered in Chapter 11. The featured section, the headline, the about, the experience descriptions. Cadence: post once a month minimum if you're publishing content from Chapter 10's calendar; weekly if you can sustain it. Engage with peers' work substantively; "great post!" comments are noise.

**Twitter/X**: optional, archetype-dependent. Sage and Hero archetypes can use Twitter productively for technical commentary and project announcements. Outlaw and Jester archetypes can leverage the platform's tendency to reward voice. Caregiver and Innocent archetypes often find Twitter culturally hostile and should avoid investing in it. Pick deliberately based on archetype, not on follower counts.

**GitHub**: keep your profile readme thoughtful. Pin your most relevant repositories. Star projects that align with your archetype (the starred list is a signal). The profile is a brand artifact, not just a code archive.

**Blog or Substack** (if your archetype calls for it): Sage archetypes especially benefit. Cadence: monthly, sustainable. Topic: what's actually interesting to you, archetype-aligned. Audience: people who would value your archetype, not the broadest possible audience.

The unifying rule: every social-media surface should reinforce the same brand. Cross-link them. Use the same headshot. Use the same bio language. The reader who finds you on LinkedIn, then your portfolio, then your GitHub, then your Twitter should encounter the same person, at increasing depth.

## The final presentation — your moment

The course's final presentation is the assembled artifact. Format: [Guy Kawasaki's 10/20/30 rule](https://guykawasaki.com/the_102030_rule/). Ten slides. Twenty minutes. Thirty-point font minimum.

The 30-point font rule is the most under-appreciated of the three. Kawasaki's argument: a presenter who reduces type below 30 points has too much content per slide and is reading from the slides instead of presenting. Forcing 30-point font forces editing, which is what good presentations need. If you cannot fit your content at 30-point font, the content is too dense for slides; move it to your speaking script.

Recommended slide structure for your AI-tool-and-personal-brand presentation, adapted from Kawasaki:

1. **Title and positioning sentence.** Your name, your tool's name, one sentence that positions both.
2. **Problem.** Who hurts, how often, how much.
3. **Solution (your tool).** What you built, in user-facing terms.
4. **How it works.** Architecture diagram. Madison-pattern reference noted.
5. **Demo or screenshots.** The deployed URL is real; show it.
6. **Brand position.** Archetype, audience, voice. Why the tool looks and reads the way it does.
7. **Validation.** Any users, any feedback, any usage metrics. Specific numbers.
8. **Roadmap.** What's next for the tool.
9. **You as the team.** Your archetype, your portfolio, your career direction.
10. **Ask.** What you want the audience to do — hire you, fund you, partner with you, follow your work.

Twenty minutes. Two minutes per slide on average; some slides longer (the demo, the team slide), some shorter. Practice the timing.

The thirty-point font keeps each slide focused. Your speaking content fills in the rest. Each slide should have one big idea, one big number, or one big visual.

The room you're presenting in is full of evaluators. They're forming opinions in the first sixty seconds. Open with a specific moment, not an introduction. *"Here is what a marketing manager's Monday morning looks like in 2026..."* lands harder than *"Hi, I'm [name] and I'm going to talk about my project."* Apply Chapter 10's storytelling: customer-as-hero. Open in scene.

## What happens after

The course ends. The work continues. Three habits to install for the year after:

**1. Refresh the portfolio quarterly.** Add one new case study per quarter. Update the bio. Replace the oldest project. The portfolio is a living asset; treat it that way.

**2. Publish on a sustainable cadence.** Whatever you committed to in Chapter 10's content calendar — keep it. The compound effect of one good piece a month over three years is enormous.

**3. Build relationships, not network.** A network is a list; relationships are people. Pick five to ten people whose work you respect and whose archetypes are compatible with yours. Engage substantively, infrequently, over years. The opportunities that matter most come from these relationships, not from cold-application channels.

The Creative Engineer version of you in 2027 or 2028 will be the cumulative product of a thousand small consistencies — every case study added, every published piece, every archetype-aligned decision. The course gives you the foundation. The compounding is your work.

## What you walk out with

A delivered presentation. A two-version resume. An archetype-aligned social-media presence. A roadmap for what you'll do in the months after the course. A sense that the work — the building, the branding, the launching — does not end with the course; it begins.

I should be honest about the limits of the framework. A great presentation does not guarantee a great career outcome. There are macroeconomic factors, hiring-pipeline luck, network access asymmetries, and biases in evaluation that the course cannot solve. The framework gives you the discipline to make your work *findable* by the people who would value it; it does not guarantee that the right people will be looking. The market still has to be willing to recognize the asset.

What I am confident about is the directional bet. Graduates who present coherently and continue to build coherently get more chances over time than graduates who do not. The compounding is real, and the discipline is teachable. The rest depends on how the world receives the work — which is something neither I nor any framework can promise.

## Embedded exercises (final integration)

1. **Build your two-version resume.** ATS-optimized (text-heavy, keyword-rich, PDF + Word) + designer format (visually distinctive, archetype-aligned, PDF). Test the ATS version with a parser tool. Review the designer version with one classmate.
2. **Draft your final presentation deck.** 10/20/30 format. Walk through it slide by slide. Time the talk-through. If you exceed 20 minutes, edit.
3. **Practice the delivery.** Record yourself once. Watch the recording. Note three things to fix. Practice again.
4. **Write your launch post.** LinkedIn or your blog. Tool URL, portfolio URL, archetype-aligned positioning sentence. Publish.
5. **Write your post-course plan.** One quarter, three to five concrete commitments. Calendar them.

The final presentation in class is the formal close of the course. The post-course plan is the actual close of the course's work in your career. Take both seriously.

**What would change my mind:** Strong evidence that integrated, archetype-coherent presentations do not produce better hiring or fundraising outcomes than incoherent presentations of equivalent technical merit. The case I am making is grounded in pattern observation across many cohorts and well-documented public cases (Airbnb, others); a controlled study would strengthen or weaken it.

**Still puzzling:** The relationship between presentation craft and authentic confidence. Some students give technically polished pitches that read as performative; others give visibly imperfect pitches that land powerfully because the speaker is unmistakably present. Craft matters, but it is not the whole story; what makes a pitch *land* beyond technical execution is something I cannot yet specify cleanly enough to teach. Worth more thought.

## Closing note for the book

This is the last chapter. By the time you reach this point, you have a deployed AI tool, a brand strategy, a visual identity, a portfolio, published writing, a presentation, a resume in two formats, and a plan for what's next.

You also have a frame. The four verbs from Chapter 1 — *ideate, build, brand, ship* — are the discipline you carry. The Madison reference architecture from Chapter 2, the archetype work from Chapter 3, the build sequence from Chapters 4–7, the brand work from Chapters 8–10, and the assembly from Chapters 11–12 — all of these compounded into the artifact you now own.

The Creative Engineer is not a credential. It is a practice. The market will reward it for as long as building stays cheap and positioning stays hard. The bet runs the length of your career.

Make work that is worth recognizing. Position it where people who would value it can find it. Refuse the work that does not fit. Trust the compounding.

Now go ship.

```
Build my complete launch package. This is the final integration. Every artifact must be consistent with the Personal Brand Strategy v1 (Ch 8), the Visual System (Ch 9), and the deployed portfolio (Ch 11).

DELIVERABLE 1 — RESUME, ATS-OPTIMIZED VERSION.
Single column. Standard section headers (Experience, Education, Skills, Projects). No graphics, no two-column layouts. Plain text saved as both .docx and .pdf. Keyword-rich for the kind of role described in my Career PRD (Ch 4).

Paste-ready text for: headline (under 220 characters), about section (under 2,000 characters), the "Featured" section's three pinned items (portfolio URL, AI-tool URL, published thought-leadership piece). DELIVERABLE 4 — 10/20/30 PITCH DECK OF MYSELF.
Ten slides. 20 minutes spoken. 30-point font minimum.

1. Title — name, role-claim, one-sentence positioning
2. Problem — what hiring teams in my target market struggle with
3. Solution — me as the answer, expressed in one specific value claim
4. How it works — my methodology (the Madison-pattern reference if relevant; the four-verb framework from Ch 1)
5. Demo / proof — my AI tool URL, screenshot, one-line outcome
6. Brand position — archetype, voice, what I refuse to do
7. Track record — work history compressed to its three most relevant accomplishments
8. Roadmap — what I want to be doing 12 months from now
9. Team / collaborators — one slide on who I work well with and why
10. Ask — what I want from the audience (interview, referral, advisor relationship, partnership)

For each slide, give me the headline (large, 30+ pt), the body content (3 bullets max OR a single image / chart description), and the speaker note (what I'd say while the slide is on screen — should fit in 1.5–2 minutes).

A single LinkedIn post (or Substack post if my archetype is Sage-publication-leaning) announcing the portfolio + AI tool + intent. Opens in a scene, not in a "I'm excited to announce." 200–400 words. Includes the portfolio URL and the AI tool URL. Ends with a specific ask.

```

**What this produces:** Five finished artifacts. Combined, they are the launch.

**How to adapt:** If you're applying to fewer than 10 roles, drop Deliverable 1 (ATS version) and lean on Deliverable 2 alone. ATS resume-tailoring matters at scale, not at single-digit application counts.
