A startup spent six months and a chunk of seed money building a brand around a name, only to receive a cease-and-desist from a company in the same software class with a prior registration. The name was memorable, the logo was done, the domain was bought — and none of it mattered, because they never ran a trademark search. The name had to go, and with it the six months.

That story is common enough to have a shape. The founders did real work. The creative decisions were sound. They just treated naming as a purely creative problem when it is also a legal one — and the legal half is the half that can unwind everything else. This chapter is about understanding both halves well enough to know which one you can handle yourself and which one you cannot.

## Three Things You Think Are One

Before anything else, a disambiguation that will save you a specific kind of grief.

Most founders treat "the name" as a single thing. It is not. There are three separate rights bundled under that phrase, and you can hold all three, one, or none of them independently.

A **trademark** is the legal right to use a name or mark for specific goods or services, enforceable against confusingly similar uses in the same category. It is the right that matters most and is the hardest to acquire — it requires either registration or demonstrable prior use in commerce.

A **domain** is a rented address. Buying `example.com` gives you the right to use that address on the web. It grants no trademark rights whatsoever, and a trademark registration grants no rights to a domain. These two systems are legally separate and frequently produce conflicts — a company can own the trademark and lose the domain arbitration, or own the domain and still receive a cease-and-desist.

A **company registration** is an entity name filed with a state. It means a state agency did not already have that name on file in that state. It is not a trademark. It does not protect the name federally or even against a similarly named company in the same state that operates in a different category.

"I bought the domain, so I own the name" is the most common and most dangerous version of this error. The three rights are separate. Clearance requires addressing all three. Only one of them — the trademark — requires a lawyer to clear properly.

## The Strength Spectrum

Here is the counterintuitive heart of trademark law. The same property that makes a name immediately understandable — the fact that it describes what the product does — is precisely what makes it hardest to protect. And the same property that makes a name feel opaque and arbitrary at first encounter is what makes it the most defensible legal asset.

US trademark law articulates this as the *Abercrombie* spectrum, named for a 1976 Second Circuit case that formalized it. From weakest to strongest:

**Generic.** The common name of the product or service itself. "Software" for a software company. "Bike" for a bicycle brand. Generic marks are never protectable, under any circumstances. The law will not let one company own the word that everyone needs to name the category.

**Descriptive.** A name that describes a feature, quality, or characteristic of the product. "Cold and Creamy" for an ice cream brand. "Vision Center" for an optometry practice. Descriptive marks are weak — they are not protectable when first filed, and they can only acquire protection after they build "secondary meaning," meaning the public has come to associate the phrase specifically with one source rather than the general description. That takes years and is expensive to prove.

**Suggestive.** A name that hints at a benefit or quality but requires some imaginative leap to connect the name to the product. "Netflix" suggests delivery of something, but you have to fill in the gaps. "Coppertone" suggests sun protection without stating it. Suggestive marks are protectable without secondary meaning.

**Arbitrary.** A real word in the language, applied to a product with no natural relationship to that word. "Apple" for computers. "Amazon" for retail. "Shell" for petroleum. The word has a dictionary definition, but the definition has nothing to do with the product. Arbitrary marks are strong — the company owns that word in that class, and no one else can use it there.

**Fanciful.** An invented word that did not exist before the brand created it. "Kodak." "Xerox." "Häagen-Dazs." Because the word has no prior meaning, the brand owns all of its associations from day one. Fanciful marks are the strongest category.

The lesson cuts against intuition in a specific way. When founders sit down to name something, they naturally gravitate toward names that communicate — names that tell customers what the product does. This is the descriptive end of the spectrum. Those names are the weakest from a legal standpoint and the most likely to be lost in a crowd of similar descriptors. The names that feel most original — arbitrary and fanciful — cost more to teach, because customers cannot infer meaning from them, but once taught they are defensible assets. "Apple" had to earn its meaning. Now no one else in the computer business can use it.

This is the design philosophy of the naming decision: you are trading off legibility against exclusivity. The more immediately legible the name, the less exclusively yours it is.

## Likelihood of Confusion

The test that determines whether a name infringes an existing mark is not a test of exact match. It is a test of **likelihood of confusion** — would a reasonable consumer be confused about the source of goods or services when encountering your mark alongside an existing one?

This standard is broader than it sounds. Several factors feed into it. The similarity of the marks (in appearance, sound, meaning). The relatedness of the goods or services. The channels through which they are sold. The sophistication of the buyers. The strength of the existing mark. Whether actual confusion has been reported.

Two marks do not have to look identical to trigger infringement. "Slickcraft" and "Speedcraft" for competing watercraft were found confusingly similar. "Polaroid" and "Polacolor" in related photographic classes were found confusingly similar. A mark that sounds like an existing mark, or that means the same thing in another language, or that differs by a single letter in the same product category — any of these can create a likelihood of confusion.

This is the specific reason a clean exact-match search is not the same as being cleared. Searching for your exact string and finding nothing tells you only that your exact string is not registered. It tells you nothing about phonetic variants, visual similarities, translation equivalents, or related marks in adjacent classes. A name that passes an exact-match search and fails a likelihood-of-confusion analysis will still generate a cease-and-desist.

## Clearance Before Commitment

The practical sequence for pre-screening a name before you invest in it has four steps, and they run in order of cost.

First, run the name and its phonetic variants through the relevant trademark register. In the United States, that is the USPTO database. Search your exact string, common misspellings, phonetic equivalents ("Brite" and "Bright"), and visual similarities. Search in your relevant Nice Classification — software products typically live in Class 9 (computer software) or Class 42 (software as a service and technology services), though your specific product may implicate other classes. `[verify: confirm current USPTO search tooling, as TESS has been replaced or updated — check the current search interface before advising students.]`

Second, check domain availability across the TLDs that matter for your product — at minimum `.com`, and increasingly `.io` and `.ai` for software. Note that an unavailable domain is not automatically a trademark conflict, and an available domain is not a trademark clearance.

Third, run a basic web search for the name in your category. You are looking for businesses using the name that have not registered a trademark — unregistered use in commerce can still create common-law trademark rights in certain jurisdictions, especially the United States.

Fourth, and this is the step that is not optional: consult a trademark attorney before committing to the name. This book gives you literacy — enough to understand what you are searching for and why, enough to have an intelligent conversation with counsel, enough to stop treating naming as a purely creative problem. It does not give you clearance. Clearance is a lawyer's job, and the cost of getting it wrong is measured in rebrands.

## What AI Can and Cannot Clear

There is a sharp line in this chapter, and it runs between two things that feel similar but are legally different.

AI is genuinely useful on the *generative* side of naming: producing large candidate sets quickly, classifying candidates on the strength spectrum, checking obvious domain availability, flagging names that mean something unintended in another language, identifying glaring phonetic conflicts with well-known marks. These are pattern-recognition and retrieval tasks. Models do them well.

AI is dangerous on the *clearance* side: it will confidently assert that a mark is "available" based on nothing. The model does not have access to current trademark registers. It does not run likelihood-of-confusion analysis against the live database. It cannot interpret the results of a search it did not run. When a language model tells you a name is available, it is telling you it has no memory of a conflict — which is meaningless, because its training data is not a trademark clearance search.

The failure mode is specific. A founder runs a name through an AI, gets back "no obvious trademark conflicts found," and treats that as a first pass clearance. It is not. Every AI-generated availability note in a naming exercise is `[verify with search + counsel]` — not because the model is wrong about everything, but because the one thing that matters here is the one thing the model cannot actually check.

The AI+1 line in this chapter is the sharpest in the book: AI owns the generation, humans own the decision, counsel owns the clearance. That division is not a formality. It is the difference between a name you can build on and a name that will cost you a rebrand.

## Worked Example: Name and Protect

For a running-project brand, the naming exercise has three passes.

The first pass is generative: produce a large set of candidates across the strength spectrum, label each on the *Abercrombie* scale, note memorability, flag obvious cross-language issues. The goal is options, not commitment. This is where AI does genuine work — generating forty candidates is tedious for a human and trivial for a model.

The second pass is screening: narrow the field by applying the brand's archetype (a Sage brand has different naming instincts than an Outlaw brand), checking obvious conflicts through a basic web search and a preliminary USPTO scan, eliminating any name that sits too far in the generic-to-descriptive range. After this pass you should have a shortlist of three to five names that survive initial scrutiny.

The third pass is commitment: pick one, run it through counsel, register the mark once cleared, secure the domain, file the entity. The commitment pass is not optional and it is not something to abbreviate. The cost of a lawyer's trademark clearance search is a rounding error compared to the cost of receiving a cease-and-desist after six months of brand-building.

The protection checklist is simple. Have you run the USPTO search and its phonetic variants? Have you secured the domain you will actually use? Have you filed the entity? Have you filed or begun the trademark application? Each of these is a separate checkbox, and none of them substitutes for the others.

## AI Wayback Machine

The ideas in this chapter didn't appear from nowhere. **Lawrence Lessig** is a legal scholar — at Stanford and later Harvard — who became one of the most influential thinkers on intellectual property in the internet age, co-founding Creative Commons in 2001 and arguing in books like *Code and Other Laws of Cyberspace* (1999) that "code is law": the technical architecture of a system regulates behavior as powerfully as any statute. His central concern was where the boundaries of ownership should sit — what a name, a work, or an idea can legitimately exclude others from using, and what it cannot. That is the exact terrain this chapter walks. A trademark is a state-granted right to exclude others from a name *in a specific class* — not a right to own a word outright — and the *Abercrombie* spectrum is, at bottom, an argument about how much exclusivity the law should grant: none for the generic word everyone needs, the most for the fanciful word no one needed before you invented it. Lessig's lifelong question — how far ownership should reach before it starts choking the commons it depends on — is the question underneath every clearance decision.

## LLM Exercises

### Exercise 1 — When to Use AI
*Run these tasks with an LLM and evaluate what it can and cannot do:*

Generate fifteen name candidates for a brand of your choosing, spanning the full trademark-strength spectrum. Ask the model to label each candidate by spectrum position and note any obvious cross-language issues or phonetic conflicts with well-known marks. Then evaluate: where did the model produce genuinely useful signal, and where did it produce confident-sounding assertions you cannot independently verify?

**The tell:** you can classify each candidate on the *Abercrombie* spectrum yourself. If you cannot, revisit the spectrum section before using the model's labels.

### Exercise 2 — When NOT to Use AI
*Identify the judgment the AI cannot make:*

Take one of the names from Exercise 1. Ask the model directly: "Is this name available as a trademark in Class 9 in the United States?" Evaluate the response. What is the model actually doing when it answers that question? What would a real clearance search require that the model cannot do?

**The tell:** any model answer to a trademark-availability question is `[verify with search + counsel]`. The failure mode is treating the model's confidence as a substitute for the search.

*Series connection:* Tier 4 (metacognitive) — knowing the AI cannot know this, and routing the question to counsel.
