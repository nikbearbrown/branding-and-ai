# Chapter 6 — Civil Liberties: When Rights Collide
*The price of constitutional government, paid one case at a time.*

---

January 2017. Border agents at El Paso are holding a three-year-old in one room and her mother in another. No court order. No finding of guilt. The government had a policy — separate families at the border, hold them in different facilities, reunite them later. The question that would travel through the courts for years was simple in principle and devastating in practice: did the government's power to enforce immigration law permit this? Or did the Fifth Amendment's guarantee that no person shall be deprived of "liberty" without due process protect the bond between a parent and a child?

Notice what that question actually is. It is not asking whether immigration enforcement is legitimate. It is not asking whether the mother broke the law. It is asking something more fundamental: *even when the government has a legitimate purpose, are there things it cannot do to individuals in pursuit of that purpose?*

That is the question civil liberties answer. Not whether government can act, but how.

---

## The Distinction That Matters

Civil liberties are not the same as civil rights, and the difference is worth being precise about.

Civil liberties are constraints on what government can do *to you*. The First Amendment does not give you the right to speak — you already have that. It forbids government from punishing you for speaking. The Fifth Amendment does not grant you the right to remain silent — it forbids government from compelling you to incriminate yourself. Civil liberties are negative. They are a list of things government cannot do.

Civil rights are different. They are guarantees that government will treat you *equally* — that it will not discriminate based on race, gender, religion, or other characteristics when it exercises its powers. Civil rights are positive. They are a list of things government must do.

You can have civil liberties without civil rights. For most of American history, that is exactly what existed: the government could not torture you or censor your newspaper, but it could segregate your children's school. The machinery that fixed the second problem is different from the machinery that fixed the first.

This chapter is about the first machinery. How civil liberties work. How they got applied to state governments — which was not automatic and took more than a century. And what they actually mean once applied, which turns out to be less clear than the original language suggests.

## The Incorporation Problem

Here is a fact that surprises most people when they first encounter it: the Bill of Rights was not originally binding on the states.

Read the First Amendment carefully. *Congress shall make no law* abridging freedom of speech. Congress. Not the states. Not city governments. Not state legislatures. The Bill of Rights was written in 1789 to constrain the new federal government, which the founders feared. The states were not the problem they were trying to solve.

So for more than a century, this was the constitutional landscape: the federal government could not censor your speech, but your state government could. The federal government could not conduct unreasonable searches, but your local police department might. A citizen in Alabama could be tried for murder without a lawyer, without an impartial jury, under conditions the federal government could never impose — and the Bill of Rights said nothing about it, because the Bill of Rights was not addressed to Alabama.

The mechanism that changed this came from an unexpected place: the Fourteenth Amendment, ratified in 1868 after the Civil War. It said that no state could deprive any person of "life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." That word — *liberty* — was undefined. The framers of the Fourteenth Amendment were trying to prevent the South from reimposing antebellum conditions. They did not leave a clean record of what "liberty" was supposed to mean.

For sixty years, the Supreme Court treated it as meaning almost nothing. Then, beginning in the 1920s, the Court started asking a different question: could the "liberty" protected against state interference by the Fourteenth Amendment include the specific liberties enumerated in the Bill of Rights?

The answer came case by case, freedom by freedom. The process is called the incorporation doctrine — the absorption of Bill of Rights protections into the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of liberty, making them binding on every state. It is still not finished. Most of the Bill of Rights has been incorporated. Some of it has not.

How does the Court decide? It asks two questions. First: is this freedom fundamental to ordered liberty — would the concept of liberty itself become hollow without it? Second: is it deeply rooted in the nation's history and tradition? If yes to both, the freedom is incorporated. States must respect it. Violations can be appealed to federal courts.

The right to counsel — the Sixth Amendment guarantee that you have a lawyer when you face criminal charges — was incorporated in 1963, in *Gideon v. Wainwright*. Clarence Gideon was arrested in Florida for breaking and entering. He could not afford a lawyer and asked the court for one. The judge refused. Gideon represented himself, lost, went to prison, and then — from his cell, in pencil, on prison stationery — wrote a petition to the Supreme Court asking whether Florida could force him to trial without a lawyer.

The Court said no. The right to counsel, the Court held, was so fundamental to a fair trial that it had to apply in every state. That ruling changed every criminal courtroom in America overnight.

The Second Amendment — the right to keep and bear arms — stayed un-incorporated until 2010, in *McDonald v. Chicago*. More than 140 years after the Fourteenth Amendment was ratified. The delay is not an anomaly. It reflects the case-by-case, contested, slow-moving nature of incorporation. The process is not a conveyor belt. It is a negotiation between the Court and the nation about what liberty means, conducted one case at a time.

Here is the trade-off inside incorporation that is worth naming explicitly. Incorporation *protects* individual freedoms by holding states to federal standards. But it also *removes* the ability of states to experiment — to provide different levels of protection, to move at different speeds, to respond differently to local conditions. Before the right to contraception was incorporated as a privacy right in 1965, some states protected it and some did not. After incorporation, all states had to permit it. The floor was set. Some people think that is exactly right. Others think it removed a legitimate democratic decision from state legislatures. Both views have logic. What incorporation does is settle the question by force of federal constitutional law.

## The First Amendment's Four Freedoms and the Tests That Govern Them

The First Amendment is forty-five words. It protects four things: religion (in two separate clauses), speech, press, and assembly. Courts have since collapsed speech and press into a single category called expression, because the distinction matters less than it did in 1791. A podcast is not quite speech and not quite press; treating them differently would be arbitrary.

The two religion clauses pull in opposite directions, and they collide constantly.

The Establishment Clause says government cannot establish religion — cannot make it official, sponsor it, require participation in it. The Free Exercise Clause says government cannot prohibit the free exercise of religion. Together they mean: government must stay neutral between religions and between religion and non-religion. That sounds simple. It is not.

In 2022, Maine had a program that reimbursed families for private school tuition. But only for secular private schools — not religious ones. A family sued. The Supreme Court held, in *Carson v. Makin*, that once the state opens a subsidy program, it cannot exclude religious schools just because they are religious. The Free Exercise Clause prevented that discrimination. Maine argued that funding religious schools would violate the Establishment Clause. The Court disagreed: subsidizing religious schools is not the same as establishing religion. The boundary between the two clauses moved.

It has moved before and will move again. That is the nature of the machinery.

The speech protections work through a different mechanism: categorical rules and tiered tests. Not all speech is treated the same.

Some speech gets no First Amendment protection at all. Incitement to imminent lawless action — a speaker urging a crowd at a door to rush a building *right now* — is unprotected. True threats — statements that a reasonable listener would understand as a credible threat of violence — are unprotected. Defamation — false statements made knowingly or recklessly — is unprotected. Obscenity — sexually explicit material that lacks serious social value — is unprotected, though defining "serious social value" has produced decades of litigation with no clean resolution.

Everything else is protected, but the level of protection depends on why the government wants to restrict it.

If a restriction is *content-neutral* — it applies to the method of delivery, not the message — the government only needs to show a substantial interest and a reasonable fit. A city can ban amplified music in public parks after 10 p.m. That applies to all amplified sound equally. The city is not targeting any message. It just wants people to sleep.

If a restriction is *content-based* — the government is targeting speech because of its message — the restriction must survive strict scrutiny. The government must show a compelling interest, narrow tailoring, and no less restrictive alternative. In practice, this standard is nearly impossible to meet. Content-based restrictions almost always fail.

The practical implication: the government can control where and when you speak, within limits. It cannot control what you say.

This produced one of the most counterintuitive results in American constitutional law: the United States protects hateful speech. There is no "hate speech" exception in American law. A person can say vile things about a racial or religious group, and as long as the speech does not meet the narrow definitions of incitement or true threats, the First Amendment protects it. This distinguishes American law from nearly every other democracy, most of which have hate speech laws. Whether the American approach is correct is a genuine debate. What it is not is an accident. It reflects a deliberate — and contested — constitutional choice that government cannot be trusted to define what speech is too harmful to permit.

The Pentagon Papers case shows the logic at its sharpest. In 1971, *The New York Times* received a classified Defense Department study on U.S. involvement in Vietnam and prepared to publish it. The government sued to stop publication, claiming national security. The Supreme Court sided with the newspaper — not because the government had no security interests, but because the government failed to prove that publishing *these specific documents* would cause imminent, grave harm. The burden was on the government. It was heavy. And the government could not meet it.

The principle: government may suppress speech in the name of security, but only if it can *prove* the necessity, not merely assert it.

## The Rights of the Accused

The Fifth, Sixth, and Eighth Amendments describe a different kind of civil liberty — not protection for what you say or believe, but protection for what happens to you once the criminal justice machinery has focused on you.

The Fifth Amendment says you cannot be compelled to be a witness against yourself. The Sixth says you have the right to counsel, to a speedy and public trial, to confront the witnesses against you. The Eighth says punishment cannot be cruel and unusual. Together they describe a process — a set of constraints on how the government may determine guilt.

Why do these protections exist? Because the government has enormous advantages over an individual defendant. It has investigators, prosecutors, laboratory resources, and time. The defendant may be poor, frightened, and ignorant of how the system works. Without procedural constraints, the system could convict people efficiently — but not accurately. The constraints slow it down in exchange for more reliable results. That is the trade-off.

The machinery of the Fifth Amendment's self-incrimination clause became concrete in 1966, in *Miranda v. Arizona*. The Court held that statements obtained during custodial interrogation could not be used as evidence unless police had first warned the suspect of four things: that he had the right to remain silent; that anything he said could be used against him in court; that he had the right to a lawyer; and that if he could not afford one, one would be provided.

These are the Miranda warnings. They are not in the Constitution. The Court invented them as a practical mechanism — because the pressure of custodial interrogation is inherent, even without physical force, and without warnings, any confession was of doubtful voluntariness.

Here is a common misconception worth clearing up directly: if police fail to read you your Miranda rights, you are not automatically released. The consequence is narrower. The statement you gave without warnings cannot be used as evidence. If there is other evidence, you can still be charged and convicted. Miranda is a rule of evidence, not a get-out-of-jail card.

The Sixth Amendment's right to counsel looks absolute on paper. In practice it is a resource question. After *Gideon*, every state had to provide lawyers for defendants who could not afford them. But what kind of lawyer? A public defender carrying five hundred cases simultaneously? A lawyer who spends eight hours on a case requiring forty? In 2011, in *Padilla v. Kentucky*, the Court held that a lawyer's failure to advise a client that a guilty plea would result in deportation constituted "ineffective assistance of counsel." The right to counsel was not just a warm body in the courtroom. It was competent representation.

But defining competence is hard. Proving that incompetent representation changed the outcome — the standard required to overturn a conviction — is harder still. The right exists on paper with more confidence than it is enforced in practice. That gap is not a constitutional flaw. It is a resource problem that constitutional law cannot solve by itself.

The Eighth Amendment's ban on cruel and unusual punishment raises a different problem: the language is old, and the world keeps producing new questions. In 1791, "cruel and unusual" meant torture. Thirty years ago courts began asking whether extended solitary confinement — years in a cell alone, with almost no human contact — constituted cruel punishment. In 2024, courts were asked whether nitrogen gas execution, a method never before tested on humans, violated the Eighth Amendment.

The text does not answer these questions. What the text does is give courts a standard — *cruel and unusual* — and require them to measure current practices against it. The Court has held that capital punishment itself is not inherently cruel. It has also held that the Eighth Amendment is not static — that what counts as cruel and unusual evolves with societal standards of decency. This means the amendment is a moving target, its meaning a conversation between courts and the society they govern.

## What Civil Liberties Actually Are

Here is the synthesis.

Civil liberties are constraints on government method. They do not guarantee any particular outcome. They constrain the *how* — how speech may be restricted, how religion may be treated, how a criminal defendant may be processed, how punishment may be administered. A person can have the full protection of the First Amendment and still lose their job for what they say. A defendant can have the full protection of the Sixth Amendment and still be convicted — because the Constitution guarantees a process, not a result.

This is not a bug. It is the design.

Every civil liberty protects the individual at some cost to the collective. Protecting speech means protecting vile speech. Protecting the accused means some guilty people go free. Protecting religious exercise means protecting religions the majority finds offensive. The constitutional choice — made repeatedly, across two centuries of cases — is that these costs are worth paying. That the alternative — a government with unconstrained power to suppress what it dislikes, to coerce confessions, to punish in any manner it chooses — is more dangerous than the costs of protecting individuals even when they are wrong, offensive, or guilty.

Whether that choice is correct is a genuine question. Courts debate it continuously. The incorporation doctrine is contested. The boundaries of the First Amendment shift. The meaning of cruel and unusual evolves. What does not shift is the basic structure: civil liberties exist to constrain the government, and they do so imperfectly, incompletely, and in a state of permanent negotiation between courts, legislatures, and the society they serve.

## LLM Exercises

**Exercise 1 — Incorporation mechanics**

Ask a language model: *"Explain the incorporation doctrine as if I have never heard of it. What problem does it solve? What mechanism does the Supreme Court use? Give me two examples of rights that were incorporated and one that has not been."* Then push back: ask the model to explain what happens if the Supreme Court gets the incorporation question wrong — what recourse exists?

**Exercise 2 — First Amendment categories**

Give the model this scenario: *"A city passes an ordinance banning any speech on public sidewalks that a reasonable person would find offensive. Is this constitutional under the First Amendment? What test would a court apply, and how would it likely come out?"* Use the model's answer to test whether you understand the difference between content-based and content-neutral restrictions.

**Exercise 3 — Miranda stress-test**

Ask the model: *"A suspect is arrested, not read Miranda warnings, and confesses. The police also find physical evidence at the scene. What happens at trial? Can the confession be used? Can the physical evidence be used? What if the police found the physical evidence because of what the suspect said in the unwarned confession?"* The last question is harder than it looks — the model should surface the "fruit of the poisonous tree" doctrine.

**Exercise 4 — Cruel and unusual**

Ask the model: *"The Eighth Amendment bans cruel and unusual punishment. The Supreme Court has said its meaning evolves with 'evolving standards of decency.' Who decides what those standards are? What evidence do courts use? Give me one example where the Court found a punishment unconstitutional under this standard and one where it did not."* Compare the model's examples to the ones in this chapter.

**Exercise 5 — The trade-off**

Ask the model: *"Every civil liberty protects the individual at some cost to the collective. Pick one civil liberty from the Bill of Rights and describe specifically what the individual gains and what the collective pays. Then argue the other side: why might a society be better off without that protection?"* The goal is to stress-test whether you can hold both sides of the trade-off at once.

## What Would Change My Mind

The argument in this chapter assumes that procedural protections for the accused produce more accurate verdicts over time — that the costs of protecting guilty people are outweighed by the benefit of not convicting innocent ones. If evidence showed that jurisdictions with fewer due process protections produced *lower* false conviction rates while maintaining public safety, I would reconsider whether some protections are calibrated incorrectly.

## Still Puzzling

The Eighth Amendment's meaning "evolves with societal standards of decency" — but courts decide what those standards are, not legislatures or voters. I do not have a clean account of why courts are the right institution to measure evolving standards, rather than the elected branches that are more directly accountable to the society whose standards are supposedly being measured. That tension runs through constitutional law generally, but it is sharpest in Eighth Amendment doctrine.
