## TL;DR

A startup brand strategy is a system of decisions about what your company does, what it declines, who its audience is, what archetype it commits to, and how it speaks across channels. The strategy's most important output is the list of things the company will *not* do — the negative space is what makes the brand legible and durable as the company grows.

---

In 2010, two brothers — Patrick and John Collison — launched a company called Stripe. There were already payment processors. PayPal had been around for a decade. Adyen was scaling rapidly. Authorize.net dominated the small-merchant market. The Collisons did not need to enter payments; they chose to. And when they did, [they made one specific commitment](https://medium.com/@yogevkimor/the-brain-behind-stripe-how-developer-first-marketing-created-a-95-billion-unicorn-bd988a0bacfa) that would shape every brand decision for the next fifteen years: they would obsess over developers.

Not "developers and merchants." Not "developers and finance teams." *Developers*. The audience they specified was a small fraction of the addressable market. The product they shipped was a payment integration that took *seven lines of code*. The marketing they did was documentation. The events they sponsored were technical conferences. The acquisitions they made — Indie Hackers chief among them — were communities, not companies.

By 2024, Stripe was processing payments for [a sizable portion of the world's online commerce](https://patrickcollison.com/about) and had been valued in private rounds at amounts in the tens of billions of dollars. They had not become bigger by pursuing more audiences. They had become bigger by serving the original audience excellently and letting it pull them upward. The Sage archetype — the company that knows the developer's experience better than the developer does — was the strategic commitment that scaled.

This chapter is about doing similar work for the AI tool you have built through Chapters 4–7. By the end of it you will have a one-page brand strategy document — mission, vision, values, UVP, archetype, voice, positioning — that you can defend in front of a faculty review committee and an early investor in equal measure.

## What "brand strategy" is — for a startup

The components are the same whether the brand is for a person or a company. For a startup, each component is a decision about *the company as an artifact in the market*:

- **Mission** — what the company exists to do. Stripe's stated mission: *increase the GDP of the internet*. Note the specificity — not "make payments easy," but a measurable, audacious aim that frames every product decision.
- **Vision** — the world if you succeed. Stripe's: *every business, anywhere in the world, can transact online with the same reliability as a Fortune 500 company*.
- **Values** — what the company doesn't compromise. *Documentation as product. Developer experience over enterprise sales process. Slow product expansion over breadth.*
- **UVP (Unique Value Proposition)** — what your product offers that competitors don't. Stripe's: *the cleanest API in payments, integrable in seven lines of code, with documentation that doesn't make developers want to throw their laptops*.
- **Archetype** — your strategic anchor. Stripe is a Sage; competitors who positioned as Heroes (PayPal in its heyday) or Rulers (enterprise payment processors) ceded the developer audience to Stripe by being the wrong archetype for that customer.
- **Voice** — how the company speaks. Patrick Collison's writing voice has set the corporate tone: precise, intellectually serious, slightly understated, pro-rigor.
- **Positioning** — where in the market the company sits. Stripe is positioned not against PayPal but against *the work of integrating any payment processor*. The competition is internal-build at the enterprise and abandonment at the small startup.

The components are not optional. A startup without an explicit mission optimizes for whatever closes the next sale. Without an explicit UVP, it competes on price. Without an archetype, every brand decision is made anew, by whichever marketer is loudest in the meeting.

## Why the negative space is the brand

The deep mechanism this chapter installs: a startup brand is more defined by what it does *not* build than by what it does. Most founders arrive thinking the opposite — that the brand is built by features shipped, audiences served, markets entered.

Look at Stripe by inversion. What did they decline?

- They declined enterprise sales processes for the first several years; they made the product self-serve and let developers bring it into companies.
- They declined the broad small-business market; their early documentation assumed technical fluency that locked out non-developers and locked in the audience they wanted.
- They declined to compete with PayPal on consumer trust signals; PayPal was Hero for consumers, Stripe was Sage for builders.
- They declined to launch products outside payments rapidly; Stripe Atlas, Stripe Issuing, Stripe Climate all came years apart, deliberately, after each predecessor was solid.
- They declined to write marketing-style content; the blog is technical, the documentation is the marketing, and the product is the brand.

Each "no" is consistent with the Sage archetype. Each "no" gave Stripe focus. Each "no" was a decision a competitor could have made differently — and several did, fragmenting their brands. The negative-space list IS the brand.

Your job, in this chapter, is to write your own negative-space list for your AI tool's startup. What customer will you not serve? What feature will you not build in v1? What channel will you not market in? What revenue model will you decline?

A useful test: when someone reading your brand strategy reaches the *Values* section, can they predict three specific things the company would say no to? If yes, the values are doing work. If no, the values are decoration.

### Stripe's archetype, applied

The Sage archetype's core motivation is *understanding through truth*. Its shadow is *dogmatism*. Its voice is *patient, evidence-based, willing to say "we don't know yet."*

Stripe's brand expression of Sage:
- **Mission decision:** *increase the GDP of the internet* — phrased as an empirical claim about a measurable thing, not a marketing slogan.
- **Voice decision:** documentation as the public face. Long-form, precise, technically honest. Patrick Collison's [public reading lists](https://patrickcollison.com/) and writing reinforce the same commitments.
- **Format decision:** The Stripe API and docs ARE the product. The marketing site is the docs. There is no separation.
- **Audience decision:** developers first, businesses through developers. The company has steadily declined to make the marketing site easier for non-developers — the Sage archetype rewards depth over reach.
- **Positioning decision:** complement to building a product, not competitor with other payment processors. Stripe's actual competitor is "rolling your own."

Each decision is internally consistent with the archetype. Stack them up across thousands of small choices over fifteen years and you get a brand that scales without losing coherence. Most startups lose coherence as they scale; Stripe held it because the archetype constrained the decisions.

Your archetype will yield a different stack. A Hero-archetype startup will pick competition and challenge as voice; an Outlaw will pick disruption; a Caregiver will pick service; a Magician will pick transformation. The archetype does not determine *what* you decide — it determines *that your decisions cohere*. Negative space is the brand because consistent absence produces legible identity.

## Naming, for a startup

A startup name is a load-bearing decision. It will appear on every business card, every URL, every legal document, every contract for the company's life. Three principles to follow.

**1. The name should be archetype-aligned.** A Sage company should not be named *Disrupt.io* — the Outlaw word "disrupt" violates the archetype. A Caregiver company should not be named *Conqueror* — the Hero word violates the archetype. The archetype filters name candidates aggressively; use it as a constraint, not a starting point.

**2. The name should pass three tests.** The bar test: can you say it once, in a noisy bar, and have someone remember it? The lawyer test: is it trademark-clearable in your category? The domain test: is the .com (or a credible alternative) available, or affordably acquirable? Stripe passed all three. Many candidates fail at the third. *Stripe* the word existed before Stripe the company; the founders bought the domain.

**3. The name should be sayable in the languages of your target markets.** Stripe is short, has clean phonetic spelling, and works in English, French, German, Japanese, Mandarin without modification. A name with awkward phonetics in a major target language is a real cost.

Trademark and domain search are not optional. Use [USPTO TESS](https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/search) for U.S. trademark search. Use a domain registrar's search for domain availability. Use a lawyer for the categorization conflict checks. The naming work happens before you put the name on a single asset.

For tool naming specifically (your AI tool may need a product name distinct from the company name): apply the same three tests, plus one — does the name signal what the tool does, even loosely, to a stranger? Stripe-the-product signals payments because the company has spent fifteen years training the association. *NotionAI* signals AI in Notion because the parent brand carries the second word. Your tool's name can either earn its meaning over time (Stripe approach) or borrow meaning from a parent (Notion approach). Both work; pick deliberately.

## What you build in this chapter

A one-page startup brand strategy document. Sections:

1. **Mission** (one sentence) — what the company exists to do. Specific and testable.
2. **Vision** (one or two sentences) — the world if you succeed.
3. **Values** (3–5 commitments) — what the company doesn't compromise. Each value should imply at least two specific decisions you would make differently from a competitor with different values.
4. **UVP** (one sentence) — what your product offers that competitors don't.
5. **Archetype** (named, with two sentences of expression) — your archetype from Chapter 1, now applied to the startup with strategic depth. Note your shadow as a known failure mode to watch.
6. **Voice** (notes) — sentence rhythm, vocabulary preferences, formats you favor and reject.
7. **Positioning** (one paragraph) — who you compete with, who you complement, the *actual* competitive set (which is often "internal-build" or "abandonment," not other named products).
8. **Negative space** (the no-list) — at least five specific things the company will not do that a competitor at your stage might.

Plus a name (chosen, justified, with trademark and domain status), and a tagline (one sentence).

The whole document fits on one page. If it is two pages, you have not yet specified enough; you are listing rather than deciding.

## A worked case: tracing Stripe's strategy from the public record

You can reverse-engineer a startup brand strategy from a company's public artifacts. Try it on Stripe.

- **Mission**: "Increase the GDP of the internet." Evidence: company tagline, founder talks, annual letter framing.
- **Vision**: "Online commerce as accessible to a one-person business as to a Fortune 500." Evidence: Atlas (incorporation tooling for global founders), focus on emerging markets.
- **Values**: documentation as product; developer experience over enterprise process; slow product expansion. Evidence: API design over fifteen years, sequence of product launches.
- **UVP**: cleanest payment API, seven-line integration, best-in-class docs. Evidence: every public Stripe-vs-competitor comparison.
- **Archetype**: Sage. Evidence: voice, format, content choices, competitor avoidance.
- **Voice**: precise, intellectually serious, slightly understated. Evidence: Collison brothers' writing, official blog posts, conference talks.
- **Positioning**: complement to building a product, not direct competitor with other processors. Evidence: marketing copy treats integration *with anything else* as the alternative, not "PayPal vs. Stripe."
- **Negative space**: no aggressive enterprise sales for years; no expansion to non-developer audiences in marketing; no rapid product proliferation; no competitor-bashing content; no celebrity-CEO theatrics.

The internal consistency of that list is the load-bearing structure. Stripe's brand is what it is because of what they declined.

Your strategy document should be similarly inferable. Someone reading it should be able to predict, with reasonable accuracy, what kinds of customer the company would pursue and what kinds it would decline. If they cannot, the document is not yet doing its job.

## What you walk out with

A one-page startup brand strategy document. A clear archetype-aligned set of commitments. A negative-space list that makes those commitments testable. A defensible name (or at least a clear path to one). A sense of why startup brand strategy is product-design work, not marketing fluff — every decision has a constraint, and the constraints compound into a legible identity that scales.

I should be honest about the limits of this framework. Stripe's success is over-determined; payments was a market structurally favorable to a developer-first entrant; the Collison brothers had access and skill that not every founder will have. Reading their brand strategy as a complete causal explanation of their outcome is wrong. What's defensible is the narrower claim: the brand strategy did not *hurt*; it almost certainly helped; the discipline produced compounding advantages over fifteen years that a less-disciplined competitor would not have accumulated.

The other limit: brand strategy at the startup stage is a hypothesis, not a fact. You will revise this document many times in the first year. The first version's job is to be specific enough to reason about, not to be the final answer. Versioning is fine and expected.

## Embedded exercises (Startup Brand Path)

1. **Reverse-engineer two startup brands.** Pick two AI-product startups whose positions you would like a version of. Read their docs, marketing site, founder writing, public talks. Write a one-page brand strategy document for each one, inferred from the public record. Notice what the negative space tells you.
2. **Draft your startup's one-page brand strategy.** Eight sections plus name and tagline. One page maximum.
3. **Run the three name tests.** Bar test, lawyer test, domain test. Document your name's status against each.
4. **Test the negative space.** Show your draft to a classmate. Ask them to predict three specific things the company would say no to. If they can't predict accurately, revise the values and negative-space sections.
5. **Stress-test the archetype fit.** Ask whether the archetype you committed to in Chapter 1 still fits the startup you've now described. Revising the archetype here is fine; the build sequence has shown you what kind of product you actually want to ship, and that data should inform the brand commitment.

The strategy document is the artifact you carry into Chapter 9 (visual identity) and Chapter 10 (storytelling). Both chapters depend on this document existing and being specific.

---

**What would change my mind:** A controlled study showing that, at the startup stage, brand strategy investment does not predict outcomes when holding product quality and team capability constant. The evidence I have is anecdotal and survivor-biased — the brand strategies of failed startups are rarely studied. The asymmetry of available data is a real limit on the case I am making.

**Still puzzling:** The relationship between archetype fit and founder personality. Stripe's Sage brand fit the Collison brothers; would the same brand strategy have worked with different founders? The brand and the founders co-evolved in a way the framework does not fully capture. Some founders cannot maintain certain archetypes — a naturally dramatic founder cannot lead a Sage company convincingly. The match between founder and archetype is a constraint I do not yet teach explicitly.
