## TL;DR

A brand without a story is a logo on a business card. A brand telling the *wrong* story — a story whose archetypal shape mismatches the brand — burns faster than no story at all. The frameworks the chapter teaches are not prescriptions; they are constraints that let you tell stories that match what your brand actually is.

---

On April 4, 2017, Pepsi released a two-and-a-half-minute commercial called [*Live for Now*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Live_for_Now). The ad starred model Kendall Jenner. In the spot, Jenner is at a high-fashion photo shoot when she notices a protest moving past on the street. She removes her wig, joins the protest, walks toward a line of police officers, and hands one of them a can of Pepsi. The officer takes a sip. The crowd erupts in celebration. The protest, the ad implies, has been resolved — by a young woman, by a pop song, by a soft drink.

By April 5, the ad had been pulled.

The backlash was immediate and unanimous across political lines. The ad was widely read as trivializing the [Black Lives Matter movement](https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2017/04/05/522750764/after-uproar-pepsi-halts-rollout-of-controversial-protest-themed-ad), which had spent the prior three years fighting against police violence — including imagery very close to a famous photograph of Iesha Evans being arrested in Baton Rouge. Pepsi issued a same-day apology: *"Pepsi was trying to project a global message of unity, peace and understanding. Clearly we missed the mark."*

The mark Pepsi missed was not a tone problem. It was a *narrative architecture* problem. The story Pepsi attempted to tell was a Hero's Journey — a young person hears a call, walks toward conflict, performs a heroic act with a magical object, and returns triumphant. Pepsi's actual archetype is Innocent with Jester elements — refreshment, joy, lightness. The Hero archetype the narrative required is not the archetype Pepsi inhabits. The story did not fit the storyteller, and the audience read the mismatch as cynicism.

This chapter is about why that happens, and how to avoid it. By the end of it, you will have a brand story for your tool — origin, customer, transformation — that fits your archetype, that you can tell across LinkedIn articles, blog posts, case studies, and pitch decks.

## Specifying "brand storytelling"

The phrase covers several different practices:

1. **Origin story.** How the brand came to be. The founder's narrative.
2. **Customer story.** The user's transformation, with the brand in a supporting role. (Most underused by technical founders.)
3. **Quest story.** The brand on a long-running journey toward a vision.
4. **Transformation story.** Before-and-after stories — how a customer or context changes through engagement with the brand.
5. **Methodology story.** How the work gets done. Most associated with Sage-archetype brands.

Pepsi's *Live for Now* attempted a customer story — Jenner-as-customer-as-hero — but cast the brand as the *resolver*, not the supporting cast. That structural choice was the failure mode. In well-told customer stories the brand is the guide (Yoda, Gandalf), and the customer is the hero. In Pepsi's ad, the soft drink itself was performing the heroic act. The grammar was wrong before the cultural specifics had a chance to amplify the failure.

The frameworks worth knowing are old and few:

**The Hero's Journey** — Joseph Campbell, [*The Hero with a Thousand Faces*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Hero_with_a_Thousand_Faces), 1949. A monomyth Campbell distilled from cross-cultural mythology. The simplified shape: a hero in an ordinary world receives a call, crosses a threshold into a special world, faces tests and an ordeal, returns home transformed and bringing back a boon for their community. Campbell's full framework has 17 stages; for brand-story purposes, the simplified Departure-Initiation-Return is usually enough.

**The Quest** — one of [Christopher Booker's seven basic plots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Seven_Basic_Plots) (2004). The hero pursues an important objective, encounters obstacles and helpers, achieves the goal (or fails meaningfully). The Quest is structurally close to the Hero's Journey but emphasizes the *objective* over the personal transformation.

**The Customer-as-Hero framing** — Donald Miller's *Building a StoryBrand* (2017) systematized this for brand work. The customer is the hero of every story; the brand is the guide. The brand has a plan; the customer takes action; the customer is transformed. Miller's framework is the most widely-applied storyteller framework in current brand strategy work, and it is downstream of Campbell.

These three are not competing frameworks; they are layered. Campbell describes what stories look like. Booker categorizes the plots. Miller adapts the structure for brand narrative specifically.

## Why archetype constrains the story

The deep mechanism this chapter installs: story shapes have archetypal commitments built into them. Choosing a story shape that does not match your brand's archetype produces a narrative the audience reads as *false*, even if every individual element is well-executed.

Trace the constraint through specific archetypes.

A **Hero archetype** can tell a Hero's Journey directly. The brand is the protagonist, sometimes; or the brand is the trainer of the protagonist. Nike's "Just Do It" is Hero archetypal storytelling executed natively — every Nike ad is a Hero's Journey at the customer level, with Nike (the brand) as the gear that supports the journey.

A **Sage archetype** tells a Quest story, or a Methodology story. Stripe does not tell Hero's Journey ads about merchants triumphing through Stripe; Stripe tells Methodology stories about how integration works, why their API is the way it is, what the deeper problem of online payments looks like. The Sage's audience is curious; the story shape that fits is one that *teaches*.

An **Outlaw archetype** tells a Rebellion story. Old Spice (in its 2010 reinvention), Patagonia (the activist brand), Vice Media at its peak — all tell stories about challenging the status quo, calling out norms, refusing the mainstream. An Outlaw telling a polite Customer-as-Hero story would feel wrong; the archetype demands tension.

A **Caregiver archetype** tells a Service or Transformation story. Johnson & Johnson, UNICEF, healthcare brands tell stories where the customer's struggle is met with attentive care and emerges into wellness. The shape is restorative, not adversarial.

A **Magician archetype** tells a Transformation story with a mystery in the middle. Disney, Tesla, Apple in its mature Magician period — all tell stories where something seemingly impossible becomes possible through the brand's particular vision.

Pepsi attempted a Hero story. Pepsi's archetype is Innocent/Jester. The story shape did not fit. The mismatch was perceptible even to viewers who couldn't articulate the mechanism — they just felt the ad was *off*, then read the cultural cues that confirmed the off-ness.

Your storytelling, in this chapter, has to fit the archetype you committed to in Chapter 8. A Sage tool telling Hero stories will read as performative. A Magician tool telling pure Methodology stories will read as small-stakes. The archetype filter saves you from these mismatches before you write a single piece of content.

### A worked case: three narrative-archetype mismatches

**Pepsi *Live for Now*, April 2017.** Already opened. Hero-archetype story attempted by Innocent/Jester brand using imagery adjacent to a serious social movement. Pulled in 24 hours. The mismatch was both archetypal (wrong story shape) and cultural (the imagery aligned with content the brand was not entitled to invoke).

**Bud Light's Dylan Mulvaney sponsored partnership, April 2023.** Bud Light's archetype is Everyman — beer for the ordinary American, the most-shared can at the cookout. The Mulvaney sponsorship attempted to layer a Magician archetype story (transformation, transcendence of categories) onto an Everyman foundation. The cultural backlash was severe, but the underlying mechanism was archetypal. Everyman brands signal *belonging to the mainstream*; Magician archetypes signal *transcending the mainstream*. The two are difficult to combine in a single brand without years of repositioning. Bud Light lost its position as the best-selling beer in the U.S. in the months following. The brand is still recalibrating.

**Jaguar's "Copy Nothing" campaign, November 2024.** Jaguar's archetype historically was Ruler (luxury, prestige, controlled performance). The 2024 rebrand attempted to pivot toward Magician/Outlaw — flamboyant typography, abstract imagery, no cars in the launch ad. The narrative shape attempted was transformation-via-disruption. The audience read the campaign as untethered from the brand's actual product reality, since Jaguar had not yet shipped the cars the new identity was supposed to introduce. The mismatch was between the *story* (Magician transformation) and the *substance* (the cars not yet existing). Coverage was widely negative; the parent company defended the rebrand and continued, but the launch is now a textbook example of narrative-substance mismatch in the design press [verify — I am summarizing my understanding from public coverage; primary sources for this case need a more careful pull].

Three archetype-narrative mismatches, three different industries, the same structural failure. Story shape mismatched the brand's actual archetype, audience perceived the dissonance, brand paid the cost.

## What a well-told brand story looks like

Three properties of well-told brand storytelling:

**1. The story shape matches the archetype.** Sage tells Methodology and Quest. Hero tells Hero's Journey at the customer level. Magician tells Transformation. Caregiver tells Service. Outlaw tells Rebellion. Innocent tells Restoration. Picking the right shape is the first move.

**2. The customer is the hero; the brand is the guide.** This is Donald Miller's contribution. In a customer story, the customer wakes up with a problem, the brand provides clarity and a plan, the customer takes action, the customer is transformed. The brand is Yoda, not Luke Skywalker. Most technical founders default to telling stories where the brand is the hero — the founders fought through obstacles, built the technology, won the prize. These can be effective for raising money but are weak for selling to customers, because the customer cannot identify with a story whose hero is someone else.

**3. The story has scale.** The transformation matters. The stakes are real. A story whose stakes are "the user's spreadsheet got a little easier" doesn't move people. A story whose stakes are "marketing teams got their three hours back per week and used it to do work they actually care about" begins to move. The scale doesn't have to be planet-saving; it has to be *meaningful* to the audience.

The Madison Intelligence Agent's [README](https://github.com/humanitariansai/madison) tells a Sage Methodology story, well-executed at small scale. The README opens with the throughput numbers (870 articles a day, 90% deduplication, sub-three-minute latency), explains the architecture, and walks through the technology stack. The customer (a marketing manager) is not framed as a hero but is the implicit beneficiary of the methodology. The story is told through the structure of the documentation itself. This is what Sage brand storytelling looks like in technical materials — the methodology *is* the story.

Your storytelling will fit your archetype. If you committed to Sage in Chapter 8, your case studies will be methodology-focused. If you committed to Hero, your case studies will showcase customer transformation through the tool. If you committed to Magician, your case studies will dramatize the impossible-becomes-possible arc. The framework's job is not to invent the story; it is to ensure the story matches the storyteller.

## Content strategy and channels

The story you choose has to live somewhere. Three primary channels for AI-tool brand storytelling at the graduate-and-early-startup stage:

**LinkedIn articles.** The native publishing surface for professional content. Long-form (500–2,500 words). Audience tilt: technical and managerial. Sage and Hero archetypes thrive here; Outlaw and Jester fit awkwardly. Article cadence at the start: monthly is sustainable; weekly is aspirational.

**Blog posts on your tool's site.** Hosted on your own domain, indexed by search engines, linked from your portfolio. Longer-form than LinkedIn (1,000–3,000 words) and more discoverable through SEO over time. The audience is the curious-prospect-becoming-customer.

**Case studies.** Published as part of your portfolio (Chapter 11). One per project. Shape: a customer-story arc — problem, approach, transformation. Length: 800–1,800 words plus screenshots and metrics where available.

The choice of channel is downstream of archetype. A Sage publishes long-form on LinkedIn and the blog. A Hero publishes short, punchy posts with strong visuals. A Magician publishes in formats that themselves feel transformational — interactive posts, video, ambitious layouts. The channel is part of the story.

Cadence beats volume. Two well-told case studies a year are worth more than ten mediocre LinkedIn posts a month. The graduate who consistently publishes one piece of work per month, archetypally aligned, ends the year with twelve portfolio assets. The graduate who tries to publish weekly and burns out at week six ends the year with six and a half embarrassed drafts.

## What you build in this chapter

A brand story system for your tool, plus your first piece of published work:

1. **Origin story** (300–500 words). How your tool came to be. Archetype-aligned. First person if appropriate to your archetype, third person if not.
2. **Customer story template** (a structure you can fill in for case studies). Hero is the customer, brand is guide, transformation is real.
3. **One published piece** — a LinkedIn article *or* a blog post on your tool's site. Topic: anything that fits your archetype. Length: 800–1,500 words. Published, not drafted.
4. **Content calendar** (one quarter, three to six pieces). Cadence sustainable for you. Each piece's archetype check noted.
5. **One case study** for the AI tool you built in Chapters 4–7. Hero is the customer (real or representative); brand-tool is guide; transformation includes specific outcomes where possible.

The work compounds with Chapter 11 (portfolio) and Chapter 12 (presentations) — the case studies live on the portfolio site, the stories appear in pitch decks, the published articles serve as proof-of-thought-leadership for hiring conversations. Treat the storytelling as foundational, not decorative.

## What you walk out with

A brand story system grounded in your archetype. A working customer-as-hero framing for case studies. The understanding that story shape is archetypal — picking the wrong shape is a brand failure regardless of how well the individual story is executed. One published piece of writing, real, on a public surface.

I should be honest about the limits. Storytelling is a craft that takes years to develop fully. A graduate's first published case study will not be Pulitzer-quality; that is fine. The goal at this stage is *archetypally aligned* and *honest*, not literary masterwork. As you publish more, the craft compounds. The key error to avoid is publishing *off-archetype* work to chase attention. Off-archetype attention is worse than no attention; it confuses your audience and erodes the brand you're building.

The other limit: storytelling frameworks are tools, not formulas. The Hero's Journey is a useful constraint when you are building a customer story; it is not a template that produces effective stories mechanically. The frameworks help you know when a story is missing structural elements; they do not generate the story for you. The story has to come from honest observation — what your customers actually struggle with, what your tool actually changes, what the transformation actually feels like.

The case for brand storytelling is largely qualitative; controlled empirical evidence is thinner than I'd like.

**Still puzzling:** When a brand can credibly evolve its archetype through storytelling versus when the attempt comes off as inauthentic. Apple shifted from Outlaw to Magician through carefully sequenced storytelling over years. Other brands have attempted similar shifts and been read as opportunistic. The mechanism that distinguishes credible evolution from opportunistic drift is real but not fully specified in the framework.

## 📌 LLM Exercise — Self-as-Project

**Project:** Self-as-Project
**What you're building this chapter:** Three stories — your **origin story**, one **case study** (in customer-as-hero format), and one **published thought-leadership piece**.
**Tool:** Claude Project for drafting; LinkedIn / Substack / your blog for publishing.

**The Prompt:**

```
Write three pieces of brand storytelling for me. All three must be archetype-aligned per my Personal Brand Strategy v1.

PIECE 1 — ORIGIN STORY (300–500 words).
How I became the engineer/designer/practitioner I am. Use the Hero's Journey simplified arc: ordinary world (where I started), call (the moment something pulled me toward this work), threshold crossing (the first real commitment), tests and allies (the work that shaped me), the boon I bring back (what I now offer).

The story must be true. No invented turning points. If I don't have a clear "call" moment, write the version that's closest to true and flag the synthetic compression.

The story must NOT be a resume in prose. A resume lists. A story shows. The reader should feel one specific scene clearly enough to picture it.

PIECE 2 — CASE STUDY (800–1,200 words).
Pick ONE project from my history (could be a class project, an internship project, my AI tool from this course, an open-source contribution, anything I've shipped). Write it as a customer-as-hero case study.

Format:
- Customer (the team / user / class / community I served — they are the protagonist)
- Problem they had (specific, recognizable, with stakes)
- What they tried before that didn't work
- Approach I brought (the work I did — but framed as serving the customer, not showcasing me)
- Transformation (what changed because of the work — specific outcomes, numbers if I have them)
- What I learned (the meta-lesson the project produced)

I am the GUIDE in this story, not the hero. Yoda, not Luke. Most engineers get this backwards. Hold the line.

PIECE 3 — THOUGHT-LEADERSHIP PIECE (800–1,500 words).
Write one substantive piece of public-facing writing. Topic must be archetype-aligned and connected to the kind of work I want to be hired for. Sage-archetype Substack post — explain a mechanism. Hero-archetype LinkedIn article — make a case for a position the field hasn't fully accepted. Magician-archetype blog post — show a transformation that surprised me.

The piece is not allowed to be:
- A "lessons learned from my AI tool project" post (lazy, every student writes this)
- A summary of a paper I read (no original work)
- An "I'm excited to announce" post (announcement, not thinking)

The piece should advance one specific idea I have about my field that not everyone holds. It should be defensible — a hostile reader could push back, but I have the evidence.

Output three Markdown documents:
1. "Origin Story — [my name]"
2. "Case Study — [project name] — [my name]"
3. "[Piece title] — [my name]"

For each, suggest the publication channel (LinkedIn article / Substack / personal blog / Medium) and the optimal day-of-week and time-of-day to publish it for my archetype's typical audience.
```

**What this produces:** Three pieces of finished writing. One published before Chapter 11. The other two slot directly into your portfolio's About and Case Studies sections.

**How to adapt:** Iterate on each piece separately. Run Piece 3 multiple times with different topics until one feels right. Pulls voice from Chapter 8 strategy.
