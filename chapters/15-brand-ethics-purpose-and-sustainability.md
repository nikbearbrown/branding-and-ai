It is also the chapter where the AI+1 discipline matters most, because AI is dangerously fluent at generating purpose language: noble, vague, and unfalsifiable. ## Purpose vs. Purpose-Washing

Simon Sinek's "Start With Why" made purpose fashionable; the fashion outran the substance. The test for a real purpose is the same values test from the strategy chapter: **is it a commitment you maintain when maintaining it costs you something?** Patagonia telling customers to buy less is purpose (it costs sales). A generic "we believe in a better world" that changes no decision is **purpose-washing** — and audiences, and increasingly regulators, can tell. Teach Sinek *and* its critique: purpose without proof is a liability.

## The Claim–Proof Map

The core deliverable: every ethics/sustainability claim paired with **verifiable evidence**. "Sustainably sourced" → which certification, which audit, what percentage, verifiable how? "Carbon neutral" → by what method, reductions or offsets, certified by whom? A claim without proof is not marketing; it is exposure. The claim–proof map is both a brand-integrity tool and, now, a compliance tool.

## Greenwashing Is Now Regulated

As of 2026, loose green claims carry legal risk, and the rules differ by region:

- **European Union.** The standalone **Green Claims Directive** proposal was **withdrawn in June 2025** and is not expected to enter force. The binding regime is the **Empowering Consumers for the Green Transition (ECGT) Directive** (adopted March 2024): member states must transpose it by **27 March 2026**, and the rules apply from **27 September 2026**. ECGT **bans generic green claims** ("eco-friendly," "green") without substantiation and **bans product-level "climate neutral" claims based on carbon offsets** rather than actual value-chain reductions.
- **United States.** The **FTC Green Guides** (last revised 2012) have been under review since 2022; as of early 2026 **no updated version has been released**, with revisions expected in 2026. Several US states are moving faster on recyclable/compostable labeling.

`[verify: ECGT transposition status by member state; FTC Green Guides release date; confirm before the chapter ships.]` The teachable point is durable regardless of the exact dates: unsubstantiated environmental claims are now a legal exposure, not just a reputational one.

## CSR, ESG, and B Corp

Brands signal purpose through **CSR** (corporate social responsibility programs), **ESG** (environmental/social/governance reporting), and **B Corp** certification (B Lab's third-party standard). These make purpose legible — but each can be gamed, and certification is a signal, not a guarantee. `[verify current B Corp standard — B Lab announced revised standards.]` Treat them as evidence to scrutinize, not proof to trust.

## The Backlash Dynamics

Values-signaling carries asymmetric risk. A brand that performs values without living them ("woke-washing," "purpose-washing") erodes trust *faster than saying nothing*, because it adds hypocrisy to the original gap. Douglas Holt's work on brand activism shows the upside of authentic cultural stances and the downside of inauthentic ones. The rule: only claim the values you can demonstrate; silence beats a hollow stance.

> AI drafts purpose language fluently and unprovably; *you* keep only what you can substantiate. Substantiation is the +1 — and now also the law.

### Exercise 1 — When to Use AI
- *Draft candidate purpose/values statements from your strategy.* **Why it works:** option generation.
- *Read the trust/anxiety signal in your category.* **Why it works:** retrieval (`consumer-trust-anxiety-index`).
- *List the evidence each claim would require.* **Why it works:** reformatting into a checklist.

**Tell:** you can produce proof for each claim you keep.

### Exercise 2 — When NOT to Use AI
- *Deciding which values the brand will actually live (and pay for).* **Why it fails:** a values judgment, not output.
- *Asserting a sustainability claim.* **Why it fails:** unprovable/illegal claims — AI invents substantiation that doesn't exist.
- *Judging whether a claim meets ECGT/FTC substantiation.* **Why it fails:** a legal/evidence judgment (route specifics to counsel).

**Tell:** you've crossed the line when AI's fluent purpose copy becomes the commitment, with no proof behind it.
**Series connection:** Tier 7 (wisdom) — values and accountability the machine cannot hold.

### Exercise 3 — Recipe Exercise
**Build:** a values statement + claims-proof map. **Run:** claims/proof + [`madison-consumer-trust-anxiety-index`](../madison/recipes/madison-consumer-trust-anxiety-index.md). **Tool:** Claude Project.

```
[HAVE / NEED / CANNOT SUBSTANTIATE]; (3) flag any claim that would fail EU ECGT

Strategy:
[PASTE]
```
**Adapt:** route any real regulatory question to counsel; the recipe flags risk, it doesn't clear it.

### Exercise 4 — CLI Exercise
**Build:** `your-brand/claims-proof-map.md`. **Tool:** [`wrap-your-tool`](../madison/wrap-your-tool/) or Claude Code.

```
Write your-brand/claims-proof-map.md: table claim | evidence | status [HAVE/NEED/
CANNOT SUBSTANTIATE] | greenwashing-risk [EU ECGT / FTC note]. Invent no

Stop after writing the file.
```
**Inspect:** every claim has an evidence status; no invented certifications. **If it goes wrong:** the model fabricates a certification or "neutral" basis — delete and mark CANNOT SUBSTANTIATE.

### Exercise 5 — AI Validation Exercise
**Validate:** the claims-proof map. Pass / Fail / Cannot-determine + evidence:
- **Correctness:** does each claim have real, nameable evidence (or an honest CANNOT)?
- **Completeness:** every ethics/sustainability claim mapped + risk-flagged?
- **Scope:** values + claims — no fabricated proof?
- **Brand-specific:** would any claim fail ECGT (generic/offset) or FTC substantiation?
- **Failure-mode:** any AI-invented certification, statistic, or "carbon neutral" basis? Hard fail.
**AI Use Disclosure:** two sentences — what the recipe produced; one thing it could not determine (whether a claim is true/legal) that needed your judgment and counsel.
