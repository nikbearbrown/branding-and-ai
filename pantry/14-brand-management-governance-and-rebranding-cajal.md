## FIGURE 1 · How a brand falls apart slowly · Supplementary · process flowchart
Nobody decides to destroy a brand; it erodes through small uncorrected acts. A sequence of innocuous events — a slightly-off blue grabbed for a slide, an uncorrected too-formal email from a new hire, a vendor using last year's logo — accumulating into incoherence. None are negligence; each is the natural result of treating maintenance as an afterthought.

## FIGURE 2 · The three-layer living guidelines system · Critical · hierarchy / systems diagram
Living guidelines are a system, not a document, in three layers. Layer 1 — DAM (digital asset management): single source of truth, assets findable in under a minute. Layer 2 — design system: components, templates, patterns that make staying on-brand the path of least resistance. Layer 3 — decision record: documented resolved edge cases so the same question isn't re-litigated. Each layer carries the failure that occurs when it's missing.
- Spatial: three stacked horizontal layer bands (DAM at base, design system middle, decision record top), each with its job and its "what fails without it" annotation.
- RED: none — fully greyscale. (De-redded under the ≤1/3 house cap: a three-layer hierarchy/systems diagram reads as a stack, not a single must-look mark; the "design system" layer now renders in ink #121212.)

## FIGURE 4 · Drift vs. intentional variation · Important · comparison panels
A healthy system flexes; drift breaks it. The outputs can look identical — an off-system color may be a deliberate seasonal choice or the first crack in a failing system; the meaning is entirely different. Left: intentional variation — departures authorized, within understood parameters, expressing the system. Right: drift — departures that break the system; especially the same departure across 15 assets over 3 months, which signals a shift. The distinguisher is the decision (who authorized) and the pattern (one vs. many).
- Spatial: two panels divided by a hairline; left = scattered authorized departures with a check; right = a repeated identical departure across many small asset marks, flagged.
- RED: none — fully greyscale. (De-redded under the ≤1/3 house cap: a two-panel comparison figure stays neutral; the repeated-pattern cluster and its caption now render in neutral gray #787878.)

## FIGURE 5 · Refresh vs. rebrand: the decision spectrum · Critical · timeline / spectrum
Change runs on a spectrum from refresh (evolution — update palette, modernize the mark, sharpen voice; core identity holds, old brand still legible) to rebrand (revolution — new name, new identity, new positioning; previous brand not legible, often by design). Triggers placed along it: refresh side — outgrown identity, contemporizing; rebrand side — merger/acquisition, genuine repositioning, damage to leave behind. The illegitimate trigger flagged: the new executive who wants a mark of their own.
- Spatial: a horizontal spectrum axis, "refresh / evolution" at left, "rebrand / revolution" at right; trigger labels positioned along it above; equity-risk rising left-to-right shown below.
- RED: the "new-executive preference" trigger marker — the illegitimate trigger the chapter says causes the most avoidable damage, the thing to look at first.
- EXCLUSIONS: no equity-migration spectrum (Fig 6), no Tropicana/Gap/New Coke detail rendering, no governance workflow.

## FIGURE 6 · Equity migration: stranding risk spectrum · Important · timeline / spectrum
The central risk is stranding equity — equity lives in customers' mental models, not in the brand itself. Spectrum: full rebrand (all recognition cues abandoned simultaneously — name, identity, voice, positioning — high stranding risk, nothing to anchor to) at one end; full refresh (core cues preserved — logo structure, typeface category, voice — low stranding risk, customers update without rebuilding) at the other; common hybrids in the middle, each annotated with what is preserved and what the customer must relearn.
- Spatial: horizontal spectrum, "full rebrand / high stranding" left, "full refresh / low stranding" right, hybrid midpoints; each point annotated with preserved vs. relearned.

## FIGURE 7 · The governance workflow: AI flags, human routes · Critical · process flowchart
The AI+1 boundary in governance. AI audit produces a flags list (off-system colors, drifting copy, deprecated logos) → steward reviews each flag → three branches: (1) intentional variation — document and close; (2) drift — correct against the system; (3) pattern of drift — update the system itself. AI produces the flags; the human routes every flag. The output is a flags list, not a decisions list.
- Spatial: left "AI audit → flags list" node → central "steward reviews each flag" node → three labeled outgoing branches to the three resolutions; a dashed boundary line marking where AI ends and human judgment begins.
- RED: the "steward reviews each flag" decision node — the non-delegable human routing step that the chapter insists is not a formality.
- EXCLUSIONS: no drift-vs-variation panels (Fig 4), no rebrand spectrum, no stewardship-scale table.
