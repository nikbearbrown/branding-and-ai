## FIGURE 1 · The patience gap: brand vs. performance over time · Critical · timeline / two-series time chart
Two metric families move on different clocks. A performance metric is fast and volatile; a brand metric is slow and smoothly rising. Plotted on a shared 0–24 month axis, the interval between when brand investment begins and when performance finally responds is the "patience gap" — the window in which a team watching only performance cancels a campaign that is actually working.
- Spatial: x-axis time 0–24 months, y-axis a normalized index; one jagged fast line (performance) and one smooth rising line (brand); a labeled bracket marks the patience gap between investment-start and performance-response.
- RED: none — two peer series being contrasted, not one focal element; greyscale (ink line + grey line) with a labeled gap.
- EXCLUSIONS: no NPS detail, no attribution touchpoints, no vanity/value table, no measurement-plan rows.

## FIGURE 3 · The NPS calculation and its four limits · Important · annotated diagram
NPS compresses a 0–10 recommend-likelihood scale into three zones — detractors (0–6), passives (7–8), promoters (9–10) — then subtracts: %promoters − %detractors, range −100 to +100. Around the scale, four labeled callouts flag the documented limits: the single-item problem (captures willingness, not why), cultural calibration (scores differ by country), information compression (a 6 equals a 0), and the correlation problem (lagging sentiment, not a growth forecast).
- Spatial: a horizontal 0–10 scale segmented into three zones with the subtraction formula below it; four callout boxes around the perimeter, each naming one limit.
- RED: none — the four limits are co-equal cautions; the figure is a balanced critique, no single mark dominates.
- EXCLUSIONS: no growth-prediction claim rendered as true, no tracking-metrics rows, no attribution content.

## FIGURE 4 · The attribution problem: observed vs. unobserved touchpoints · Critical · annotated timeline
When a metric moves, did the work cause it? A customer-journey timeline runs left to right toward a conversion. Observable touchpoints that attribution models can see (ad click, website visit, email open) sit on the line; unobservable touchpoints that they cannot see (a billboard, a friend's recommendation, a review read months earlier) sit below as ghosts. Multi-touch attribution assigns 100% of credit within the observed portion — and the unobserved portion is exactly where most brand-building lives.
- Spatial: a horizontal journey arrow ending in CONVERSION; observed touchpoints marked as solid nodes on the line, unobserved touchpoints as dashed/grey nodes below; a brace labels "invisible to multi-touch attribution."
- RED: the CONVERSION endpoint marker — the single event whose cause the reader is tempted to claim, and the one thing the whole figure warns against over-attributing. One red mark only.
- EXCLUSIONS: no MMM-vs-multitouch comparison table, no brand-lift design, no NPS content.

## FIGURE 5 · Vanity vs. value metrics (the Farris test) · Important · comparison panels / classification
Paul Farris's test: if this number changed tomorrow, would you do anything differently? No → vanity. A two-column sort places typical metrics: vanity (raw impressions, follower count, raw pageviews, raw video plays) on the left; value (qualified conversion, retention, price premium, referral rate) on the right. The dividing rule is the Farris question; the figure also notes that context can move a metric (impressions against a defined target can be value).
- Spatial: two stacked panels split by a vertical hairline; left "vanity — flatters, informs no decision," right "value — connects to a decision or a dollar," each listing example metrics; the Farris test stated across the top as the sorting rule.
- RED: none — a balanced two-sided classification of peers; neither column is the focal point.
- EXCLUSIONS: no NPS detail, no attribution timeline, no full measurement-plan table.
