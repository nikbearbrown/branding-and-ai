## FIGURE 2 · The Abercrombie strength spectrum · Critical · conceptual map (horizontal scale)
Horizontal scale, five stops left→right: Generic, Descriptive, Suggestive, Arbitrary, Fanciful. Plot one example mark at each (Software / Cold and Creamy / Netflix / Apple / Kodak). Two opposed annotation bands: protectability rising left→right; initial legibility (clarity) falling left→right. Mark the "never protectable" cutoff at Generic and the "protectable without secondary meaning" line beginning at Suggestive.
EXCLUSIONS: no red (balanced spectrum, no single focal stop); no axis numbers; exactly five stops; one example per stop.

## FIGURE 3 · The legibility–exclusivity trade-off · Important · bar/quant schematic (paired opposing bars)
For each of the five spectrum positions, two opposing schematic bars: legibility (high at generic end) and protectability (high at fanciful end), showing they cross. Direct-label each pair. Reinforces that the more legible the name, the less exclusively yours. Use ink + two neutral grays, no numbers (qualitative high/low).
EXCLUSIONS: no red; no precise values (schematic); zero baseline; do not duplicate the example marks from Fig 2 — use only position labels.

## FIGURE 4 · Likelihood-of-confusion factors · Important · systems diagram (hub-and-spoke)
Central node "Likelihood of confusion — would a reasonable consumer be confused about source?" with spokes to the factors: similarity of marks (appearance/sound/meaning), relatedness of goods/services, channels of trade, buyer sophistication, strength of existing mark, reported actual confusion. Annotate one spoke with the Slickcraft/Speedcraft and Polaroid/Polacolor point that exact match is not required.
EXCLUSIONS: no red; no quantitative weighting of factors; keep to six spokes max.

## FIGURE 5 · Clearance before commitment · Critical · process flowchart (cost-ordered)
Four left-to-right stages ordered by rising cost/stakes: (1) USPTO register + phonetic/visual variants, (2) domain availability across .com/.io/.ai, (3) web search for unregistered common-law use, (4) consult a trademark attorney. Each node notes what it catches and who runs it. Step 4 is the non-optional gate — the single element the reader must look at first — so it carries the one red accent (border/label).
EXCLUSIONS: only ONE red mark (step 4, the mandatory counsel gate); no rounded corners; no cost dollar figures (rough/qualitative only).

## FIGURE 6 · What AI can and cannot clear · Critical · comparison panels (two-column split, the AI+1 boundary)
Two columns divided by a heavy vertical rule that IS the AI+1 boundary. Left "AI can do this": generate candidates, classify on spectrum, flag obvious cross-language issues, check obvious domain availability. Right "AI cannot do this": run the live USPTO search, assess likelihood of confusion, clear a mark, give legal advice. Label the dividing rule "AI+1 boundary: generation vs. clearance."
EXCLUSIONS: no red (the dividing rule does the work; do not also redden it — keep boundary in ink); no per-item icons; do not imply AI is wrong about everything.

## FIGURE 7 · Three-pass naming funnel · Important · process flowchart (funnel)
Narrowing funnel, three passes left→right: Pass 1 Generative (~40 candidates; AI does genuine work) → Pass 2 Screening (3–5 shortlisted; AI + archetype filter, basic web + preliminary USPTO scan) → Pass 3 Commitment (1 name → counsel → register mark, secure domain, file entity). Mark the AI+1 boundary between Pass 2 and Pass 3 with a dashed reference line.
EXCLUSIONS: no red (the funnel + dashed boundary carry it; no single hottest element); show counts (40 / 3–5 / 1) as the only numerics; no logos.
