## FIGURE 1 · Purpose vs. purpose-washing: the behavioral test · Critical · comparison panels
The test that separates a real commitment from a performance: is it a commitment you maintain when maintaining it costs something? Two panels contrast Patagonia ("Don't Buy This Jacket," repair program, profit restructuring — costs are real, core decisions change) against the fast-fashion "Conscious Collection" (1% of volume, core model unchanged — concern signaled without cost). Rows compare: does it cost something, does it change core decisions, how it performs under scrutiny, regulatory risk level.
- Spatial: two side-by-side panels (real purpose / purpose-washing) split by a hairline, four comparison rows aligned across both; the behavioral test stated as the dividing question at top.
- RED: none — a balanced two-case comparison; the contrast is the lesson, neither side is a single focal mark.
- EXCLUSIONS: no claim-proof map rows, no regulatory dates, no trust-asymmetry curves, no CSR/ESG/B-Corp content.

## FIGURE 2 · The claim–proof map · Critical · reference table / process
Every ethics or sustainability claim is paired with its evidence. Columns: the claim · evidence required · evidence we actually have · status (HAVE / NEED / CANNOT SUBSTANTIATE). The status column is the honest reckoning: "cannot substantiate" is a failure of the claim, not the brand — the fix is to remove the claim, not manufacture evidence. Worked rows include "eco-friendly" (generic, no cert → CANNOT), a bias-audit claim with published methodology (HAVE), and a supply-chain figure awaiting an audit (NEED).
- Spatial: a four-column table with a few worked rows; the three status values rendered as distinct typographic labels (HAVE / NEED / CANNOT SUBSTANTIATE) in the status column.
- RED: the single CANNOT SUBSTANTIATE status label — the chapter's sharpest instruction (cut the claim, don't fake the proof); the one cell the reader must look at first. One red mark only.
- EXCLUSIONS: no evidence-standards-by-category breakdown (Fig 3), no regulatory comparison, no trust-asymmetry diagram.

## FIGURE 5 · CSR, ESG, B Corp: signals that invite scrutiny · Supplementary · comparison / classification
Three instruments make purpose legible, none is proof. CSR — voluntary programs, not third-party verified, may be disconnected from the core business. ESG reporting — standardized disclosure metrics, frameworks not globally harmonized, reporting ≠ performing well. B Corp — third-party verification by B Lab, harder to obtain, but a floor not a ceiling. Each shown with what it verifies and its limit, ranked roughly by verification rigor.
- Spatial: three stacked bands (CSR, ESG, B Corp) ordered by increasing third-party rigor; each band states what it signals and its named limit.
- RED: none — three peer signals on a rigor gradient; a comparison, not a verdict.

## FIGURE 6 · The cost of hypocrisy: trust asymmetry · Critical · annotated line chart / before-after
Values claims without behavior erode trust faster than silence. Two trajectories on shared axes (time horizontal, audience trust vertical) drop at the same scandal event: (1) brand that made no values claim falls to a recoverable level and climbs back; (2) brand that claimed to lead on the very thing it then violated falls further and recovers slower. The vertical gap between the two ending levels is "the cost of hypocrisy" — the claim inflated the expectation, the violation deflates it by the full inflated amount.
- Spatial: two lines from a shared starting trust level, both stepping down at a marked "scandal" event; the no-claim line recovers higher/faster, the claimed-and-violated line drops lower; a labeled bracket marks the gap between final levels.
- RED: none — two peer trajectories being compared; the gap is annotated in ink, the lesson is the contrast not a single focal mark. (Greyscale: ink line + grey line.)
