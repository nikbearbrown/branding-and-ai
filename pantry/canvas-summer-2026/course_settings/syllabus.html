Course Description

This course prepares technically skilled graduate students to compete in a market that demands more than credentials and resumes. Through hands-on work at the intersection of branding and artificial intelligence, students develop the creative, strategic, and technical skills to position themselves as Creative Engineers — professionals who can ideate, build, brand, and ship.

During the first half of the course, students study the Madison framework — an agent-based AI marketing intelligence platform — and use its architecture as a foundation to design and build their own AI-powered tool from scratch. Students choose their own project direction, with examples ranging from social media analytics tools to brand assessment platforms to archetype-driven content generators. Each student works independently as a solo founder, scoping, building, and deploying a working MVP.

The second half of the course shifts focus to branding. Students choose one of two paths: building a personal professional brand and portfolio, or developing a startup brand concept around the tool they created. Using AI-powered design and content tools, they craft brand strategy, visual identity, narrative, portfolio, and social media presence.

By course completion, students own a working AI-powered tool, a polished brand and portfolio, and the creative confidence to present both to employers or investors. Students retain full ownership of the tools they build and are encouraged to continue developing them beyond the course.

Course Learning Outcomes

Upon completing this course, students will be able to:

  1. Analyze the Madison framework's agent-based architecture and apply its design patterns to an original AI-powered tool
  2. Scope, build, and deploy a functional AI-powered tool as an independent project, from product requirements through working MVP
  3. Identify a Jungian brand archetype and apply it consistently across messaging, visual identity, and content strategy
  4. Develop a comprehensive brand strategy — including mission, vision, values, and unique value proposition — for either a personal professional brand or a startup concept
  5. Design a cohesive visual identity system (logo, color palette, typography, imagery) aligned with brand strategy and archetype
  6. Create a professional portfolio using AI-powered design tools that showcases both technical and creative capabilities
  7. Write compelling brand narratives using established storytelling frameworks (Hero's Journey, The Quest) through an archetype-informed lens
  8. Produce and publish professional content — including LinkedIn articles, case studies, and social media assets — that demonstrates thought leadership
  9. Build two versions of a professional resume (ATS-optimized and designer format) tailored to creative technology roles
  10. Present a complete brand portfolio and AI tool demonstration in a professional, investor-ready format using the Guy Kawasaki 10/20/30 framework

How to Succeed in This Course

This course moves fast and asks you to work across disciplines — building technical tools, developing brand strategy, creating visual assets, and presenting your work professionally. That range is intentional. Employers and clients are looking for people who can work across these areas, and this course is designed to build that capability.

Here is what successful students in this course typically do:

  • Start early and iterate. Assignments build on each other. The tool you begin in Week 1 becomes the case study in your portfolio. Waiting until the deadline limits your ability to get feedback and improve.
  • Ship, then polish. This course follows an 80/20 philosophy — a working version submitted on time is always better than a perfect version submitted late. Get it working first, then refine.
  • Ask for help. The instructors and TA hold office hours by appointment via Zoom. If you are stuck on a technical problem, a design decision, or a branding question, reach out. There is no penalty for asking questions — it is expected.
  • Commit to a lane in the second half. You will choose between a personal brand or a startup brand in Week 6. Make a decision and build depth rather than switching between the two.
  • Treat your portfolio as a professional product. Everything you create in this course — your tool, your brand assets, your LinkedIn presence — is designed to be used after graduation. Build accordingly.

Use of AI Tools

This course teaches the strategic and practical use of AI tools for branding, design, and development. Students are expected — and encouraged — to use AI tools (including but not limited to ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney, DALL·E, Adobe Firefly, and GitHub Copilot) throughout their coursework.

The expectation is that students use AI as a professional tool, not as a substitute for their own thinking. In all assignments, students must be able to explain their creative and strategic decisions, demonstrate understanding of the tools they used, and articulate how AI contributed to their process.

Any assignment that is generated entirely by AI without evidence of student judgment, iteration, or strategic intent will be evaluated accordingly under the 80/20 grading model. The goal is not to avoid AI — it is to use it with intention and skill.

Grading Philosophy: The 80/20 Rule

This course uses an 80/20 grading model that reflects how creative professional work is evaluated in industry:

  • 80% of each assignment grade is based on completion and requirements. Did you ship it? Does it meet the stated deliverables? Is it submitted on time? Students who consistently complete assignments to specification will earn a solid grade.
  • 20% of each assignment grade is based on creative quality. Does the work show strategic thinking? Is the brand archetype applied thoughtfully? Is there evidence of craft, polish, and creative ambition beyond the minimum requirements?

This model rewards reliability and follow-through while recognizing that creative excellence is built through practice. Students are encouraged to take creative risks — the grading structure is designed to make that safe.

Academic Integrity

A commitment to the principles of academic integrity is essential to the mission of Northeastern University. The promotion of independent and original scholarship ensures that students derive the most from their educational experience and their pursuit of knowledge. Academic dishonesty violates the most fundamental values of an intellectual community and undermines the achievements of the entire University.

As members of the academic community, students must become familiar with their rights and responsibilities. In each course, they are responsible for knowing the requirements and restrictions regarding research and writing, examinations of whatever kind, collaborative work, the use of study aids, the appropriateness of assistance, and other issues. Students are responsible for learning the conventions of documentation and acknowledgment of sources in their fields. Northeastern University expects students to complete all examinations, tests, papers, creative projects, and assignments of any kind according to the highest ethical standards, as set forth either explicitly or implicitly in this Code or by the direction of instructors.

The following is a broad overview, but not an all-encompassing definition, of what constitutes a violation of academic integrity:

Cheating: The University defines cheating as using or attempting to use unauthorized materials, information, or study aids in any academic exercise. When completing any academic assignment, a student shall rely on their own mastery of the subject.

Fabrication: The University defines fabrication as falsification, misrepresentation, or invention of any information, data, or citation in an academic exercise.

Plagiarism: The University defines plagiarism as using as one's own the words, ideas, data, code, or other original academic material of another without providing proper citation or attribution. Plagiarism can apply to any assignment, either final or drafted copies, and it can occur either accidentally or deliberately. Claiming that one has "forgotten" to document ideas or material taken from another source does not exempt one from plagiarizing.

Unauthorized Collaboration: The University defines unauthorized collaboration as instances when students submit individual academic works that are substantially similar to one another. While several students may have the same source material, any analysis, interpretation, or reporting of data required by an assignment must be each individual's independent work unless the instructor has explicitly granted permission for group work.

Participation in Academically Dishonest Activities: The University defines participation in academically dishonest activities as any action taken by a student with the intention of gaining an unfair advantage over other students.

Facilitating Academic Dishonesty: The University defines facilitating academic dishonesty as intentionally or knowingly helping or contributing to the violation of any provision of this policy.

Please visit https://osccr.sites.northeastern.edu/academic-integrity-policy/ to access the full academic integrity policy.
