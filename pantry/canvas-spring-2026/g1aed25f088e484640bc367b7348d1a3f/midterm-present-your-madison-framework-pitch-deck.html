The Madison Pitch

Midterm Assignment: Present Your Madison Framework Vision to Investors

Format: Live Investor Pitch Presentation Total Points: 100 (80 Core Completion + 20 Relative Quality) Presentation Date: February 28, 2026 PDF Submission Deadline: February 27, 2026 (24 hours before presentation) Required: PDF export of your presentation deck

Core Philosophy: The 80/20 Rule + Guy Kawasaki's 10/20/30

80 points = Successfully delivering all pitch components (ship the pitch!) 20 points = Relative excellence compared to peers 10/20/30 Rule = Maximum 10 slides, 8-minute presentation, 30-point minimum font

Remember: A compelling vision beats incomplete code. Done is better than perfect.

Assignment Overview

Present your Madison Framework project idea or prototype as an investor pitch to a non-technical audience. Whether you have working code, mockups, or just a compelling concept, your job is to sell the vision.

Your Audience: Smart people with money who know nothing about n8n, APIs, or Python. They care about problems, solutions, and profits.

What You're Pitching:

  • If you have a working prototype: Show it!
  • If you have mockups/designs: Present them!
  • If you have a concept: Sell the vision!

All stages are valid — investors fund ideas, not just finished products.

Presentation Logistics

Time per student: 8 minutes pitch + 3 minutes Q&A = 11 minutes total

Session Format:

  • Opening (3 minutes) — Session introduction
  • Presentations (all students x 11 minutes each)
  • Buffer/Wrap-up — Final thoughts and announcements

Attendance: Expected for the full session to support peers and learn from each pitch.

Guy Kawasaki Pitch Structure

Suggested Timing for 8-Minute Pitch

Slide 1: Title Slide (15 seconds) Project name, your name, one-line description. Make it memorable.

Slide 2: The Problem/Opportunity (75 seconds) What sucks in the world that you're fixing? Why should anyone care? Quantify the pain in dollars, time, or frustration.

Slide 3: Your Solution (75 seconds) How your Madison Framework tool fixes the problem. The "aha!" moment explained simply. No technical jargon — explain it to a non-technical investor.

Slide 4: The Magic (Simplified) (45 seconds) Your secret sauce in human terms. "We use AI to..." (one sentence). Why this wasn't possible before. Think "Google's PageRank" level of simplicity.

Slide 5: Business Model (50 seconds) How you'll make money. Pricing strategy (subscription, one-time, freemium). Revenue projections, even rough estimates. Having a basic model is better than none.

Slide 6: Go-to-Market Strategy (50 seconds) How you'll get your first 10 customers. Distribution channels. Marketing approach. Use Claude to help brainstorm GTM strategies for your specific tool.

Slide 7: Competition (45 seconds) Who else solves this problem? Why you'll win. Your unfair advantage.

Slide 8: Development Plan (40 seconds) Current status (concept/mockup/prototype/MVP). What you still need to build. Timeline to launch and key milestones.

Slide 9: Proof of Concept (75 seconds)

  • Working code: Show it running
  • Mockups: Walk through the user journey
  • Concept: Show detailed wireframes or workflow diagrams

All stages are valid — just be clear about where you are.

Slide 10: The Ask (30 seconds) What you need (funding, resources, technical help). Timeline and next steps. Clear call to action.

Total: 8 minutes or less.

Core Requirements (80 Points)

1. Complete Pitch Delivery (30 points)

All pitch components covered, within the 8-minute limit, in clear non-technical language with professional presence.

  • 30 pts: Full pitch delivered professionally
  • 22 pts: Most components covered well
  • 15 pts: Basic pitch completed
  • 8 pts: Incomplete or significantly over time

2. Problem/Solution Clarity (15 points)

Crystal clear problem definition, solution explained without jargon, clear value proposition, obvious connection between problem and solution.

  • 15 pts: Compelling problem/solution fit
  • 11 pts: Good explanation
  • 8 pts: Adequate clarity
  • 5 pts: Confusing or overly technical

3. Business Viability (15 points)

Realistic business model, clear monetization strategy, identified target market, go-to-market plan. Use Claude or ChatGPT to help develop these business aspects.

  • 15 pts: Strong business case
  • 11 pts: Good commercial thinking
  • 8 pts: Basic business model
  • 5 pts: No clear business angle

4. Proof of Concept (10 points)

Show what you have at your current stage: working prototype, mockups, or detailed wireframes and architecture diagrams. Investors appreciate transparency about where you are.

  • 10 pts: Compelling visualization of concept (any stage)
  • 7 pts: Good representation of idea
  • 5 pts: Basic concept shown
  • 3 pts: Vague or unclear concept

5. Presentation Quality (10 points)

Professional slide design, 30-point minimum font, effective visuals over text, smooth delivery.

  • 10 pts: Polished, professional deck
  • 7 pts: Good presentation quality
  • 5 pts: Adequate slides
  • 3 pts: Poor design or tiny fonts

Quality Score (20 Points) — Relative Performance

Top 25% (20 points) Venture-ready pitch quality. Compelling narrative arc. A genuine "shut up and take my money" moment. Clear competitive advantage. Memorable and quotable. Would actually get funded regardless of development stage.

51–75th Percentile (15 points) Strong business case. Professional delivery. Clear value proposition. Good understanding of market. Solid concept visualization.

26–50th Percentile (10 points) Competent pitch. All elements present. Basic business thinking. Adequate presentation.

Bottom 25% (5 points) Minimum requirements met. Technical focus over business. Unclear value proposition. Basic presentation.

Peer Learning Component: Take notes on other pitches. Identify one strength from each presenter. Ask yourself: "Would I invest? Why or why not?" Best practices emerge from watching others.

Success Strategies

For the 80% (Core Completion):

  • Practice your 8-minute timing until it's automatic
  • Use the 10-slide structure as your guide
  • Explain everything in plain English
  • Show something visual — a mockup, diagram, or demo
  • Have a clear ask at the end
  • Use Claude for help with business concepts, GTM strategies, or competitive analysis
  • Be honest about your development stage — concept phase is completely acceptable

For the 20% (Excellence):

  • Tell a story, not a spec sheet
  • Open with a hook that grabs attention in the first 15 seconds
  • Use analogies everyone understands
  • Show personality and genuine passion for the problem
  • Make complex ideas feel obvious
  • Leave them wanting more

Showing Your Concept (Based on Your Stage)

If you have working code: Live demo (always have a backup video), show actual outputs, walk through the user experience.

If you have mockups: Figma/Canva designs, user journey flows, interface screenshots showing how it would work.

If you have a concept: Detailed wireframes, architecture diagrams, process flow charts, use cases and scenarios. This is completely fine — many successful pitches are concept-only.

Getting Help with Business Concepts

Use AI assistants (Claude, ChatGPT) to help with developing go-to-market strategies, creating realistic pricing models, identifying target customer segments, competitive analysis, revenue projections, and marketing channel recommendations.

Example prompts:

  • "Help me create a go-to-market strategy for my brand archetype detection tool"
  • "What pricing models work for B2B SaaS marketing tools?"
  • "Who would be the ideal first 10 customers for my Madison Framework tool?"

Technical-to-Investor Translation Guide

Instead of saying... Say this...
"n8n workflow automation" "Automated system"
"API integration" "Connects different services"
"Machine learning model" "Smart AI that learns patterns"
"Jungian archetype detection" "Understands brand personality"
"Multi-agent orchestration" "Multiple AI assistants working together"
"LLM prompt engineering" "Teaching AI to understand brands"
"Vector embeddings" "Smart matching technology"

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Getting too technical — they don't care about your n8n nodes
  • Reading from slides — slides support you, not replace you
  • Tiny fonts — if they're squinting, you're failing
  • Apologizing for not having finished code — sell what you have
  • Weak problem definition — if the problem isn't clear, nothing else matters
  • Going over time — practice, practice, practice
  • No clear ask — always end with what you need

What Investors Care About (Your Grading Mindset)

  • Size of problem — Is this a vitamin or a painkiller?
  • Clarity of solution — Can I explain it to someone else?
  • Market opportunity — How many people need this?
  • Business model — How does this make money?
  • Competitive advantage — Why you, why now?
  • Vision and potential — Could this be big?
  • Team capability — Can you actually execute?
  • The ask — What do you need to get there?
