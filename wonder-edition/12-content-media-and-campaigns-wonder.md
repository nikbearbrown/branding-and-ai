## The Strange Question

In 2023, AI writing tools generated more content in one quarter than human writers produced across all of recorded history combined — approximately 15 exabytes of text.

Advertising click-through rates during the same period fell to historic lows. The average display ad received a click from approximately 0.1% of people who saw it.

More content, fewer responses. Why?

## First Intuition

The natural explanation: audiences have become more sophisticated and more skeptical. They have learned to ignore advertising because advertising has trained them to expect irrelevance. Ad-blindness is a psychological adaptation to an oversaturated environment.

This feels right because it matches a visible trend: people skip ads, install blockers, scroll past sponsored content. The audience has learned to filter.

## The Surprise

But the chapter's editorial filter argument reveals a more precise mechanism. The content flood is not damaging attention because audiences have become sophisticated. It is damaging attention because the signal-to-noise ratio has collapsed. When everyone produces content, the infrastructure that identified high-quality content — editorial gatekeeping, curation, reputation systems — is overwhelmed. There is too much content for the curators to process.

The result is paradoxical: more total content in the channel, but less content finding the audience that would value it. The 0.1% click-through rate is not evidence that audiences do not want content — it is evidence that the content reaching them is increasingly not the content they need. The distribution infrastructure is failing, not the audience.

And the implication for a Creative Engineer is precise: in a world where content production is nearly free, the editorial judgment — "this is the right message, for this specific person, in this specific moment" — is the scarce input. AI can produce content. It cannot exercise the editorial judgment about which content to produce for whom.

## The Hidden Structure

Therefore, the PESO model (Paid, Earned, Shared, Owned) is not a taxonomy of channel types — it is a resource allocation framework for a campaign. Each channel type has a different cost structure, a different trust level, and a different reach mechanism. Paid media reaches audiences by purchase (display ads, sponsored content, paid search). Earned media reaches audiences through editorial endorsement (press coverage, reviews, third-party mentions). Shared media reaches audiences through peer distribution (social shares, referrals, user-generated content). Owned media reaches audiences through direct access (email lists, websites, podcast audiences, the brand's own platforms).

The IMC (Integrated Marketing Communications) principle: these channels should not be planned independently. A campaign that runs a paid media burst without the earned and owned infrastructure to capture the attention it generates will lose most of its investment. The campaign brief's six questions — objective, audience, message, channel mix, budget, measurement — force these decisions to be made explicitly before spending begins.

The reach vs. frequency tradeoff: reaching 1,000 people once each and reaching 100 people ten times each may have the same total impressions but very different brand-building outcomes. Research on advertising response consistently shows that multiple exposures in a short period produce stronger recall than single high-reach exposures — with diminishing returns after approximately six to eight exposures. Campaign planning requires an explicit choice.

> "It is tempting to think that more content in more channels is always better — that distributing widely maximizes exposure. But the editorial filter argument shows that distribution without curation reaches the wrong people with the wrong message. The correct model holds that campaign effectiveness depends on the quality of the match between message, audience, and moment — and AI can improve production speed but cannot substitute for the judgment about which match to make. The key distinction is between content production (what AI can do at volume) and editorial selection (what the human must still determine)."

**The campaign brief — six questions:**

| Question | What it forces | Common failure when skipped |
|---|---|---|
| Objective | What specific behavior change are we driving? | Campaign evaluated on vanity metrics |
| Audience | Which specific person in which specific moment? | Message written for nobody |
| Message | One thing: what do we want them to know, feel, or do? | Campaign with three equally important messages |
| Channel mix | PESO allocation: why this mix for this audience? | Channel chosen by habit, not strategy |
| Budget | How much, and how split across channels? | Over-investment in paid, under-investment in earned |
| Measurement | What will change in the data if this worked? | No baseline, no measurement plan |

## Try Looking At It This Way

**Target:** The PESO media model — a framework for allocating campaign resources across Paid, Earned, Shared, and Owned channels to reach a specific audience with a specific message at the right cost structure.

**Base:** A river irrigation system.

**Features:**
- The water source (budget and content) in the base corresponds to the campaign's resources in the target
- The primary irrigation channel (owned media — the brand's direct access to its audience) corresponds to the main river channel — the most reliable and controllable path
- The secondary channels (paid media) correspond to the pumped irrigation that fills areas the main river cannot reach naturally — more expensive per unit delivered, but extensible
- Earned media corresponds to rainfall — reaches additional area at no direct cost, but cannot be reliably scheduled or controlled
- Shared media corresponds to natural runoff from neighboring fields — reaches areas the brand did not specifically target, with amplification that depends on the terrain (the network structure)

**Commonalities:** In both systems, the most reliable channel (owned/main river) requires investment to maintain its capacity but delivers at the lowest variable cost per unit. The least reliable channel (earned/rainfall) is the most efficient when it works but cannot be depended on for a campaign with a specific timeline. Sustainable irrigation does not depend on rainfall alone; sustainable brand reach does not depend on earned media alone. Both require deliberate infrastructure investment in the controllable channels.

**Boundaries:** Water in an irrigation system is fungible — water that reaches one field is equivalent to water that reaches another. Content is not fungible — a message delivered through owned media (email to a subscriber who opted in) is received very differently than the same message delivered through paid media (display ad to a cold audience). The irrigation analogy implies that reaching one person through paid channels and reaching the same person through owned channels is equivalent; the trust levels and reception contexts are entirely different.

**Conclusions:** Invest in owned media infrastructure first. It is the only channel where the brand has reliable access and where the trust relationship is strongest. Paid, earned, and shared amplify from that foundation.

## Where The Analogy Breaks

Unlike irrigation water, the content that flows through brand media channels is not consumed and gone — it accumulates and compounds. An article published on owned media three years ago still generates search traffic. A press mention from a credible source still appears in Google results. Email open rates from a well-maintained list are not diminished by the age of the list. The irrigation analogy implies that the value of the content resource depletes as it is distributed; owned media content can appreciate over time as it accumulates search authority and back-links. This is the compounding property of owned media that makes early investment in it disproportionately valuable.

## Small Discovery

**Raw data:**

A community garden distributed surplus vegetables to neighborhood members in two ways. In Year 1, they posted a generic notice on the community board: "Vegetables available Saturday, take what you need." In Year 2, they sent personalized notes to 40 specific families based on observed preferences: "We have extra cherry tomatoes — your family used six pounds last season. Come Saturday."

| Condition | Total vegetables distributed | % of available vegetables taken | Families who returned the following week |
|---|---|---|---|
| Year 1: Generic notice | 84 lbs | 41% | 28% |
| Year 2: Personalized notes | 127 lbs | 92% | 67% |

**Pattern search:** The personalized approach distributed 51% more vegetables and engaged 67% more families for sustained participation. What specifically about the personalization produced these outcomes — was it the accuracy of the preference data, the effort signal, or something else?

**Prediction:** If in Year 3 the garden sent personalized notes to the same 40 families but with incorrect preference data (wrong vegetables), what would happen to distribution volume and return rates? Write your prediction before continuing.

---

**Revelation:** Incorrect personalization typically performs worse than generic notices — families who received inaccurate notes about their preferences feel the mismatch more sharply than families who received no personalization at all. The correct preference data was necessary, but the performance gain came from combining accurate data with the effort signal (someone tracked our preferences and wrote to us specifically). Inaccurate personalization signals both bad data and lack of care — a worse outcome than honest generic communication. This is the PESO model's editorial judgment requirement applied to personalization: AI can produce personalized content at volume, but the accuracy of the underlying data and the quality of the match is the editorial judgment that determines whether personalization helps or harms.
