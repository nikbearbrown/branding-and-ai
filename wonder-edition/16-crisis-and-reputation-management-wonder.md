## The Strange Question

On October 1, 1982, someone poisoned Johnson & Johnson's Tylenol and killed seven people. J&J did not cause the poisoning. The criminal was never identified. J&J's legal liability was, at minimum, ambiguous.

J&J then spent $100 million pulling 31 million bottles from shelves nationwide — not just the affected lot numbers, not just Chicago stores, but the entire national inventory. They spent this money protecting customers from a threat they did not create.

Tylenol's market share collapsed from 35% to 8%.

By 1984, it had exceeded its pre-crisis level.

Now consider United Airlines in April 2017. A passenger was physically removed from a flight by United personnel and injured in the process. United's CEO posted a statement the following morning that defended the airline's right to remove "disruptive" passengers. The statement was legally defensible and strategically catastrophic. United's share price dropped, competitor bookings surged, and the video had already been seen by millions before any official response appeared.

J&J was not responsible for its crisis and behaved as if it were. United bore real responsibility for its crisis and behaved as if it did not.

J&J's brand recovered to above pre-crisis levels. United's recovery took years.

Why did accepting responsibility for a crisis you did not cause work better than deflecting responsibility for one you did?

## First Intuition

The intuitive model: crisis response is primarily a legal and communications task. Minimize disclosed liability. Avoid statements that can be used against you. Get the message out before the narrative sets. The goal is to limit damage by controlling what the organization says.

This model feels right because organizations do face legal exposure in crises, and admissions of liability are genuinely costly. Legal counsel's advice to minimize statements is structurally sound. The instinct to protect the organization from its most exposed flank — legal liability — produces a predictable communication posture: deflect, contextualize, limit admissions.

## The Surprise

But legal liability minimization is the wrong optimization target. The Situational Crisis Communication Theory shows that audiences are reading organizational behavior under pressure — and what they read is whether the response strategy matches the type of responsibility the crisis actually involves.

J&J's crisis was a victim crisis: an external criminal attacked the product. The legally appropriate response was to clarify that J&J did not cause the harm. J&J did the opposite — they behaved as if they bore full responsibility. That mismatch between responsibility (low) and response (fully accountable) produced the signal audiences were reading: these people actually care about customers. The recovery followed from that signal.

United's crisis was a preventable crisis: a passenger was physically harmed by United personnel during a process United controlled. The appropriate response was full acknowledgment of responsibility and corrective action. United deployed a diminish strategy — contextualizing the incident, defending policy. Audiences read the mismatch: this organization is protecting itself, not its customers. The compounding damage followed from that signal.

The matched response is not what intuition predicts. Intuition says: minimize exposure, limit admissions, respond fast with whatever protects you. The evidence says: audiences read the strategy match, not the legal exposure.

## The Hidden Structure

Therefore, the discipline of crisis response is not primarily communications or legal management — it is crisis type identification, followed by the strategy that matches the identified type.

Coombs's Situational Crisis Communication Theory maps three crisis clusters. Victim crises (low public responsibility attribution) call for denial or bolstering. Accidental crises (moderate attribution) call for diminish strategies — acknowledge, contextualize, explain corrective action. Preventable crises (high attribution, known or knowable risk that was not acted on) require rebuild: full acknowledgment, sincere apology, genuine corrective action, visible evidence the correction is real.

The strategic error that produces most corporate crisis failures is applying the wrong strategy. The instinct to use diminish or deny strategies on a preventable crisis is understandable — preventable crises carry the highest legal exposure, the most pressure from counsel, the greatest organizational threat. But audiences are sophisticated readers of organizational behavior under pressure. A low-responsibility strategy on a high-responsibility crisis signals self-protection. That signal is often more damaging than the original crisis.

> "It is tempting to think crisis response is primarily about managing legal liability and controlling the public narrative — that organizations should minimize admissions and protect their most exposed flank. But J&J's Tylenol recovery and United's compounding damage show that audiences read whether the response strategy matches the responsibility type, and a mismatched strategy signals self-protection rather than stakeholder concern. The correct model holds that the first decision in any crisis is identifying the responsibility attribution — victim, accidental, or preventable — and deploying the matching strategy regardless of legal instinct. The key distinction is between a crisis response optimized for the organization's exposure and one optimized for the stakeholder experience of the organization's character under pressure."

**SCCT decision sequence:**

| Crisis type | Attribution | Strategy | Fatal mismatch |
|---|---|---|---|
| Victim (external attack, natural disaster) | Low | Deny / bolster | Using rebuild — implies guilt where none exists |
| Accidental (unintended harm, good faith error) | Moderate | Diminish | Using deny — sounds evasive when harm is visible |
| Preventable (known risk, willful negligence) | High | Rebuild / mortification | Using diminish — confirms audience suspicion of self-protection |

## Try Looking At It This Way

**Target:** SCCT — the principle that crisis response strategy must match the responsibility type attributed by the public, and that strategy mismatches compound the original damage.

**Base:** A triage system in an emergency room.

**Features:**
- The crisis type (victim/accidental/preventable) corresponds to the triage category (minor/moderate/critical)
- The SCCT-matched response strategy corresponds to the treatment protocol assigned to each triage level
- The organization's instinct to minimize exposure corresponds to a patient's family insisting the condition is less serious than assessed
- The SCCT mismatch (diminish on a preventable crisis) corresponds to applying a minor-injury protocol to a critical patient
- The audience reading the response strategy corresponds to clinical staff observing whether the triage protocol is being followed

**Commonalities:** In both systems, the correct response is determined by the severity of the situation as assessed by the evaluating party — not by what the responding party would prefer the severity to be. A family that insists a critical patient is fine does not change the clinical triage category; it creates a conflict between the assessed severity and the applied protocol, which makes the situation worse. Similarly, an organization that applies a low-responsibility response to a high-responsibility crisis does not change the audience's attribution of responsibility; it creates a visible gap between what the audience believes is warranted and what the organization is doing, which deepens the damage. Both systems work only when the response matches the assessed severity, not the preferred severity.

**Boundaries:** Emergency triage is performed by trained professionals with clinical expertise, using established assessment criteria, in a controlled environment. Crisis triage is performed under time pressure, by people with personal and organizational stakes in the outcome, against a public whose responsibility attribution may shift as new information emerges. A triage nurse does not have to worry that the patient's friends are simultaneously forming independent diagnoses and broadcasting them to millions of people. An organization in a crisis does. The triage analogy implies more control over the assessment process than organizations actually have — audiences perform their own triage, in real time, and the organization cannot correct a mistaken attribution by asserting one.

**Conclusions:** Identify the crisis type first. Match the response strategy to the identified type. The instinct to manage exposure is real and should inform legal review, not communication strategy.

## Where The Analogy Breaks

Unlike a triage assessment, which is performed once and then updates only when clinical condition changes, a crisis's public responsibility attribution can shift rapidly as new information emerges. An organization that correctly identifies its crisis as accidental and deploys a diminish strategy can find, mid-response, that investigative reporting reveals the crisis was actually preventable — that someone inside the organization knew about the risk. The triage analogy implies the category is stable once assessed; crisis reality is that the category can shift, and the strategy must shift with it. An organization whose diminish strategy was correct on day one can be using the wrong strategy by day five if the emerging facts recharacterize the crisis from accidental to preventable. Monitoring the crisis type in real time, not just at the triage stage, is a discipline the triage analogy does not suggest.

## Small Discovery

**Raw data:**

A regional restaurant chain experienced three distinct incidents over a five-year period. Here are the incidents and the chain's responses.

**Incident 1:** A customer reported finding a metal fragment in a dish. Investigation traced it to a defective batch of equipment from a supplier. The chain issued a statement noting the supplier defect, offered the customer a refund, and changed suppliers. No recall, no apology beyond the individual incident.

**Incident 2:** A kitchen fire caused by a grease accumulation that staff training records showed had been flagged in two prior safety audits. One customer was treated for smoke inhalation. The chain's statement said it was "investigating the cause" and offered affected customers gift cards.

**Incident 3:** A regional flood caused significant damage to three locations and disrupted service for two weeks. The chain communicated directly with customers about the closure timeline, partnered with a local food bank, and reopened with an event donating 20% of sales to flood recovery efforts.

Customer satisfaction scores one year after each incident:
- Incident 1: +4 points above baseline
- Incident 2: −18 points below baseline
- Incident 3: +11 points above baseline

**Pattern search:** Incident 1 (supplier defect) produced a minor positive. Incident 3 (external disaster, no fault) produced the largest positive. Incident 2 (known safety risk not addressed) produced the largest negative. What does the response to Incident 2 specifically get wrong, and what would SCCT predict as the correct response?

**Prediction:** If the chain had, on the day of Incident 2, published a statement saying "our safety records show this risk was identified and not acted upon — we take full responsibility, we have suspended service at all locations for inspection, and we are committed to a complete account of what happened within 48 hours" — would the one-year satisfaction score have been better or worse than −18? Write your prediction before continuing.

---

**Revelation:** The evidence from SCCT research and case analysis consistently shows that proactive acknowledgment of responsibility in a preventable crisis — full mortification before the audience discovers the concealment — produces better long-term trust outcomes than discovered responsibility concealment, even when the underlying failure is identical. The chain's "investigating the cause" response on Incident 2 was a diminish strategy applied to a preventable crisis. The audience knew (or eventually learned) that the grease hazard had been flagged twice. The diminish response did not prevent that discovery — it added concealment to the original negligence. A rebuild response on day one would not have prevented satisfaction damage; preventable crises always produce trust drops. But it would have given the organization a path back. A concealment followed by discovery forecloses that path. The −18 score reflected both the original failure and the response failure compounded together.

## What This Changes

1. **What question can the reader now answer?** Why J&J's Tylenol recovery was not accidental — it was the predictable outcome of matching a victim crisis with a low-responsibility response that showed, through expensive action, that the brand's values were real.

2. **What looks different in a specific design decision?** The decision about what to say in the first two hours of a crisis. Before this chapter, the question is "what is safest to say?" After this chapter, the question is "what crisis type is this, and what response does that type require?" The first question produces legally cautious, strategically wrong statements. The second produces matched responses.

3. **Practice Bridge:** The student can now build a crisis playbook for their personal brand that includes three scenario-typed holding statement templates — one for victim crisis, one for accidental, one for preventable — with the SCCT response strategy pre-decided for each. The preventable holding statement is the hardest to write because it requires accepting responsibility before the full facts are confirmed. Writing it in advance, when there is no live pressure, is the preparation the chapter's "still puzzling" section identifies as the unsolved problem.

4. **What question does this leave open?** SCCT tells the organization which strategy to deploy. It does not tell the organization how to ensure the strategy is authentic rather than performed. J&J's Credo predated the Tylenol crisis by forty years; the response was evidence of real values. The chapter's "still puzzling" section identifies the unresolved question: how does an organization distinguish, in the live moment, between expressing authentic values and performing authenticity? Module 15 (brand ethics and purpose) provides part of the answer — real purpose is maintained when maintaining it costs something, and the pre-crisis commitment to the behavioral test is what makes the crisis response legible as authentic.

## Wonder Questions

1. The chapter identifies the Tylenol response as a victim crisis handled with a rebuild-level response — far more accountability than the situation required. J&J's CEO led with customer safety rather than the fact that J&J was itself a victim. The response worked. Would the same over-response work for every victim crisis, or are there victim crises where mortification-level response would be misread as an admission of fault and actually make recovery harder?

2. The United Airlines April 2017 statement was legally defensible — United did have the right to remove passengers — and strategically catastrophic. A communications director who disagreed with that statement in the drafting room faced an organization, a legal team, and a CEO who believed the statement was correct. What would be required of that director to change the outcome, and is that a communications skill or something else entirely?

3. A personal brand — a solo consultant or educator — has a preventable crisis: advice they gave produced bad outcomes for a client who followed it. The consultant knew the advice was based on incomplete evidence. They issued a "learning and improving" statement. SCCT says this is wrong — they need mortification. What exactly does mortification look like for a one-person brand, and what is the recovery timeline when there is no organization to implement systemic corrective action?

4. The chapter distinguishes drift (slow erosion of brand associations) from crisis (acute trust events). The backlash asymmetry from Module 15 describes how purpose-washing erodes trust gradually. A brand could be experiencing slow trust erosion without meeting the threshold for a "crisis." At what point does drift become a crisis that requires an active response rather than a governance update?

5. The holding statement buys time for the real response. But audiences now know what a holding statement is. "We are aware of reports and investigating urgently" is legible as a holding statement — a delay tactic. When the holding statement itself signals delay, has the tool lost its function? Or is it still the correct move even when everyone knows what it is?

---

> **What the concept is:** Situational Crisis Communication Theory — the framework that matches crisis response strategy (deny/bolster, diminish, rebuild/mortification) to the public's attribution of organizational responsibility (low, moderate, high), with the central finding that mismatched strategies compound the original crisis damage.
>
> **What it explains:** Why J&J's Tylenol over-response (rebuild-level behavior on a victim crisis) produced faster, more complete recovery than a legally calibrated response would have; why United's diminish response on a preventable crisis produced compounding damage; why the holding statement resolves the speed-versus-accuracy tension by committing to nothing that cannot be sustained.
>
> **What it does NOT mean:** That organizations should accept responsibility for every crisis regardless of actual responsibility. The SCCT framework is a strategy-matching tool, not a blame-acceptance mandate. Victim crises — where the organization was genuinely harmed by an external actor — call for clarifying that fact, not accepting responsibility for it. The discipline is accurate triage, not universal mortification.
>
> **What comes next:** The crisis playbook from this chapter assumes the organization knows what its values are before the crisis arrives. Module 15 (brand ethics and purpose) is the upstream discipline — the behavioral commitment test and claim-proof map that determine whether, when a crisis arrives, the response can be authentic rather than performed.
