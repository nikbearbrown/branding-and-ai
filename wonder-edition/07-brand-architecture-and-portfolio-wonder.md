## The Strange Question

Procter & Gamble sells Tide, Ariel, Bold, Gain, Era, and Cheer — six laundry detergent brands in the same market. These brands compete with each other on the same shelf. P&G spends separate advertising budgets on each. They maintain separate identities, separate packaging, separate brand teams.

Why would a company pay to compete with itself?

## First Intuition

The natural assumption: this is waste — duplicated costs, fragmented market share, internal competition that benefits nobody. A rational company would consolidate to one brand, invest all resources in it, and dominate the category.

This feels right because efficiency is a governing principle in most organizational decisions. Redundancy costs money. A focused portfolio should outperform a scattered one.

Acquisition legacy? Marketing experimentation? Predict: what is P&G's actual strategic rationale for maintaining six laundry brands simultaneously? Write it down before continuing.

## The Surprise

But P&G's house-of-brands architecture is not a mistake or an inefficiency. It is a deliberate strategy for capturing different segments of the same market with brands that occupy different perceptual positions — positions that a single brand cannot occupy simultaneously.

Tide is "top-performing, professional, trusted by experts." Gain is "bright, fun, fresh-smelling, upbeat." Era is "value-oriented, no-nonsense." A single brand cannot coherently be all three — "top-performing and fun and value-oriented" is not a position, it is a description of a brand trying to be everything. Each brand occupies a specific position, attracts a specific customer persona, and can be optimized for that persona without compromising the others.

The Tide brand manager's job is not to maximize P&G's laundry revenue — it is to maximize Tide's equity with Tide's target customer. The architecture makes each brand's success measurable and its positioning defensible.

## The Hidden Structure

Therefore, brand architecture is the structural decision about how a company organizes its brands, products, and sub-brands in relation to each other — and specifically, how much equity each entity borrows from, contributes to, or risks transferring to the others. The Aaker-Joachimsthaler spectrum runs from branded house (one master brand, all products live under it — like FedEx and its sub-brands FedEx Express, FedEx Ground) to house of brands (each product brand is independent — like P&G). Between these poles are endorsed brands (sub-brands with parent visible but subordinate) and sub-brands (parent brand visible, sub-brand adds specific meaning).

The architecture decision is not primarily a marketing decision. It is a risk-allocation decision. A branded house concentrates all equity in one name — one crisis can damage all products. A house of brands distributes equity across separate brands — one brand's crisis is contained. The chapter's dilution risk is the specific failure mode of over-extension: when a brand extends into a category that contradicts its core associations, the extension does not just fail — it damages the parent brand's credibility in its original category.

> "It is tempting to think brand extension is a low-cost way to grow — you already have the brand equity, why not apply it to a new product? But brand equity is category-specific. The credibility Tide has in laundry care does not automatically transfer to Tide kitchen soap, because the associations that make Tide credible (heavy-duty cleaning, whitening, expert performance) may conflict with what kitchen soap buyers want (gentle, food-safe, pleasant scent). The correct model holds that brand equity transfers only when the new category shares the core associations of the parent brand. The key distinction is between a brand's equity (earned in a specific category) and a brand's name (portable to any category, but potentially damaging in the wrong one)."

**The architecture spectrum:**

| Architecture type | Brand visibility | Equity transfer | Risk profile | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Branded house | Master brand dominant | High — all products share equity | Concentrated — one crisis hits all | FedEx, Virgin |
| Sub-brands | Master brand + sub-brand visible | Moderate | Partial — sub-brand can absorb some damage | Apple iPhone, iPad |
| Endorsed brands | Sub-brand primary, parent endorses | Low | Distributed — parent visible but not primary | Courtyard by Marriott |
| House of brands | Each brand independent | Minimal | Dispersed — each brand stands alone | P&G, Unilever |

## Try Looking At It This Way

**Target:** Brand architecture — the structural relationship between a company's brands that determines how equity flows and risk concentrates.

**Base:** A financial investment portfolio.

**Features:**
- The company in the target corresponds to the investor in the base
- Each brand in the company's portfolio corresponds to each asset in the investment portfolio
- The branded house corresponds to putting all assets in a single instrument — maximum concentration, maximum efficiency if it succeeds, maximum damage if it fails
- The house of brands corresponds to broad diversification — each asset managed independently, correlated losses minimized
- Brand equity transfer corresponds to correlation between assets — when brands share equity, they share both gains and losses, like correlated assets

**Commonalities:** In both systems, the key architectural decision is how much to concentrate versus diversify. Concentration buys efficiency (less overhead, clearer identity, stronger individual position) at the cost of correlated risk. Diversification buys risk containment at the cost of efficiency. Neither architecture is universally superior — the right structure depends on the company's risk tolerance, the category's competitive dynamics, and the specific assets being managed.

**Boundaries:** A financial portfolio can be rebalanced at relatively low cost — selling one asset and buying another takes minutes. Restructuring a brand architecture takes years, is visible to customers and competitors, and can damage the equity of brands whose relationship to the parent changes. A Marriott brand that moves from endorsed to independent cannot simply revert if the move fails — customer associations have already shifted. The portfolio analogy implies more reversibility than brand architecture actually has.

**Conclusions:** The architecture decision should be made before the portfolio grows complex enough that restructuring is painful. Like a financial portfolio, a brand portfolio left unmanaged will drift toward a structure nobody chose.

## Where The Analogy Breaks

Unlike a financial investment portfolio, brand equity cannot be liquidated on demand. A stock can be sold at market price; a brand can only be sold to an acquirer who believes the equity will transfer into their portfolio — and transfer is imperfect and often destroys significant value. When Kraft acquired Cadbury in 2010, brand analysts estimated that a significant portion of Cadbury's equity (specifically, the associations tied to British ownership, craft heritage, and community connection) did not survive the acquisition. The portfolio analogy suggests that brand equity, like financial assets, can be bought and sold at fair value. Brand reality is that equity is often damaged by the transaction itself.

## Small Discovery

**Raw data:**

A small software company offers three products: a project management tool (ProTask), a time-tracking add-on (ProTrack), and a client invoicing tool (ProBill). All three carry the "Pro" prefix and are sold as a suite. A competitor launches a free version of a similar project management tool that captures significant market share.

The company considers two responses:
- **Option A:** Rebrand the entire suite as "ProSuite," unify the visual identity across all three products, and emphasize the integrated suite over individual products.
- **Option B:** Spin off ProBill as a standalone brand targeting freelancers with a separate identity, while keeping ProTask and ProTrack as the core suite.

Customer research shows: ProTask users are primarily small agencies (5-25 people). ProBill users are primarily independent freelancers. There is almost no overlap between the user bases.

**Pattern search:** What does the customer research reveal about the architecture the company should adopt, and what does it reveal about Option A's likely failure?

**Prediction:** If the company chooses Option A (unified suite brand), what would happen to ProBill's freelancer users? Write your prediction before continuing.

---

**Revelation:** Option A would likely accelerate freelancer churn from ProBill. Freelancers who use ProBill do so precisely because it is a standalone invoicing tool, not because it is part of a project management suite they do not need. Unifying the identity under "ProSuite" signals that the product is for teams — which actively alienates the freelancer segment. The architecture decision is not primarily about visual identity; it is about which audiences the brand is trying to serve simultaneously. Two audiences with different jobs, different self-images, and different competitive contexts are a signal that the house-of-brands approach may serve the company better than a branded suite. The architecture decision follows from the audience research, not from aesthetic preference.

Before this chapter, this is primarily a recognition question: will customers find the product more easily if it carries a familiar name? ---

> **What the concept is:** Brand architecture — the structural decision about how a company's brands, sub-brands, and product lines relate to each other, governing how equity flows between entities and how risk concentrates or disperses.
>
> **What it explains:** Why P&G maintains six competing detergent brands; why brand extensions fail when they conflict with the parent brand's core associations; why the architecture decision is a risk-allocation decision as much as a marketing one.
>
> **What it does NOT mean:** That more brands are always better (house of brands) or that a single unified brand is always better (branded house). The right architecture depends on the audience diversity the portfolio must serve and the risk profile the organization can sustain.
