## The Strange Question

A coffee shop sells a $6 latte. The beans cost $0.30. The milk costs $0.40. The cup, lid, and sleeve cost $0.15. The labor and overhead to make it costs about $1.50. The total cost of goods is roughly $2.35.

The customer pays $6 and returns tomorrow.

What did the customer buy for the remaining $3.65?

## First Intuition

The natural answer: location, convenience, brand recognition. Starbucks can charge more because it is Starbucks — people trust the quality and know what they are getting. The premium is a reliability tax.

This feels right because it matches a familiar economic intuition: branded goods command a premium for reducing uncertainty. You pay more for the known quantity.

## The Surprise

But Pine and Gilmore's experience economy research reveals something the reliability premium explanation misses. In 1999, they tracked coffee prices across delivery contexts: commodity beans ($0.02/cup), packaged retail coffee ($0.26/cup), prepared coffee at a diner ($1.10/cup), coffee at a premium café ($4-6/cup). Same beans. Same caffeine. Different price at each stage. The difference was not the bean or its reliability. It was the experience delivered alongside the bean.

And the research reveals a second finding: the experience the customer remembers is not the average quality of all the moments they experienced. It is the peak moment and the final moment — the emotional high point and how it ended. This is Kahneman's peak-end rule. A coffee experience that had one exceptional moment (a barista who remembered the customer's name and order from three weeks ago) and ended pleasantly produces higher return rates than an experience that was consistently good but had no memorable peak.

The customer is not paying $3.65 for reliability. They are paying for the specific emotional peak and ending that the café reliably produces.

## The Hidden Structure

Therefore, brand experience is the sum of all interactions a customer has with a brand — but not the sum weighted equally. The customer's memory of the experience is disproportionately shaped by peaks and endings, which means experience design must identify and engineer specific peak moments rather than trying to uniformly elevate all moments.

The chapter's journey map framework divides touchpoints into front-stage (visible to the customer: the interface, the product, the customer service interaction) and back-stage (invisible: the logistics, the data processing, the support ticket routing). Back-stage failures become front-stage failures when they break the customer's experience — a delayed notification, a missing order, a password reset that does not work. The brand constants are the touchpoints that must remain consistent regardless of context; the brand variables are the touchpoints that can be customized, localized, or personalized.

Moments of Truth (Carlzon): the ZMOT (Zero Moment of Truth) is the research phase before any purchase — the Google search, the review scan, the social proof evaluation. The FMOT (First Moment of Truth) is the first encounter with the product on the shelf or at the interface. The SMOT (Second Moment of Truth) is the actual use experience. Getting ZMOT and FMOT right but failing at SMOT produces a customer who was well-acquired and poorly retained.

> "It is tempting to think that a great brand experience requires great service at every touchpoint — consistent excellence across all interactions. But the peak-end rule shows that memory is not a mean. The correct model holds that one exceptional moment and a satisfying ending produce better experience memory than consistently good moments with a flat ending. The key distinction is between experience delivery (the operational quality of every moment) and experience memory (what the customer retains and acts on)."

**Moments of Truth as a decision framework:**

| Moment | What the customer is doing | What brand failure looks like |
|---|---|---|
| ZMOT | Researching before committing | No reviews, inconsistent information, competitor dominates search |
| FMOT | First encounter with the product/interface | Confusing interface, misaligned with the promise, no on-boarding |
| SMOT | Using the product for real | Delivers less than promised, crashes, fails on edge cases |
| TMOT (Third) | Reviewing and recommending | No prompt to share, no way to escalate quality feedback |

## Try Looking At It This Way

**Target:** Brand experience design — engineering specific touchpoints to produce memorable peaks and positive endings that drive return behavior, rather than uniformly elevating all moments.

**Base:** Theatrical lighting design.

**Features:**
- The journey map in the target corresponds to the scene breakdown in the base — every moment mapped and its emotional function identified
- Brand constants (logo, voice, core experience) correspond to the set design that remains consistent across all scenes
- Brand variables (channel-specific, context-specific adaptations) correspond to the lighting that changes to reflect the scene's emotional moment
- The engineered peak moment corresponds to the key lighting moment — the spotlight at the climax, the dramatic shift that the audience remembers
- The ending corresponds to the final tableau — the state the audience is in when they leave

**Commonalities:** In both systems, resource allocation is not uniform. A theatre production does not invest equally in every moment — the lighting designer chooses which moments to elevate, knowing that the audience will remember the peaks and the ending more than the ordinary scenes between them. Brand experience design operates the same way: not every touchpoint can be exceptional, so the designer must choose which moments to elevate and engineer specifically for peak and ending.

**Boundaries:** A theatre production is a fixed sequence — the audience experiences Act 1 before Act 2, and the playwright controls the order. A customer journey is non-linear — different customers arrive at different touchpoints first, stay for different durations, and have different personal histories that color every interaction. The theatrical analogy implies more control over sequence and context than a brand's experience designers actually have.

**Conclusions:** Design for peak and ending explicitly, not just for average quality. The customer remembers those two moments disproportionately.

## Where The Analogy Breaks

Unlike a theatre production, brand experience is experienced repeatedly — the same customer visits again and again. A lighting moment that was peak in the first visit becomes expected in the fifth; what was exceptional becomes ordinary. This means experience designers cannot simply engineer one peak and rest — they must understand how quickly customers habituate to specific moments and refresh the peak accordingly. The theatrical analogy implies that the same performance will produce the same emotional response for every audience. Brand experience design must account for the diminishing emotional return of a peak moment repeated identically across many customer visits.

## Small Discovery

**Raw data:**

An airport lounge tracked customer satisfaction scores across arrival, lounge access, in-lounge experience, and departure. They also tracked Net Promoter Score (likelihood to recommend) and compared it to the peak and final moments of the visit.

| Customer group | Avg in-lounge rating | Peak moment rating | Final moment rating | NPS |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Group A | 7.2/10 | 9.1/10 (staff called by name) | 8.0/10 (warm departure farewell) | 68 |
| Group B | 8.4/10 | 7.8/10 (no distinctive peak) | 6.2/10 (long security line visible at exit) | 41 |

**Pattern search:** Group B had a higher average in-lounge rating but a significantly lower NPS. What specifically explains the gap?

**Prediction:** If the lounge improved Group B's peak moment to 9.0/10 (adding a personalized experience element) but did not change the final moment (the visible security line at exit), would NPS reach Group A's level? Write your prediction before continuing.

---

**Revelation:** Research consistently shows that improving the peak without improving the ending produces partial improvement — NPS would likely rise to 50-55, not to Group A's 68. The ending has roughly equal weight to the peak in determining recommendation behavior. This is the peak-end rule applied: both dimensions must be engineered. The lounge's best move is to do both — introduce a personalized peak element (remembering preferences, calling by name) and address the ending (a dedicated, visually separated departure path that avoids the main terminal security line). The practical implication for brand experience design: identify your two most important interventions per journey, one at peak and one at the final touchpoint.

## What This Changes

1. **What question can the reader now answer?** Why a coffee shop charges $6 for $2.35 worth of inputs — and specifically, what the customer is buying in the gap between cost and price.

2. **What looks different in a specific design decision?** The choice of which features to build into an AI tool's interface. Before this chapter, features are prioritized by utility or user request frequency. After this chapter, features are prioritized by their position in the journey — peak-moment features (the wow that creates a memorable positive experience) and ending features (the satisfying close that drives return) receive disproportionate investment relative to middle-journey features.

3. **Practice Bridge:** The student can now design the peak moment for their AI tool. This is the specific interaction — the output that appears after the first successful run, the notification that the sheet has been updated, the dashboard view that reveals the intelligence — that is engineered to produce the positive emotional peak. Identifying this moment and designing it explicitly is different from building a tool that works well at every step.

4. **What question does this leave open?** An AI concierge or agent that interacts with customers on behalf of a brand creates its own set of touchpoints. When the AI fails — gives wrong information, loops, cannot understand the request — how does the brand maintain experience standards? Module 16 (crisis and reputation) and Module 22 (AI intelligence) both address failure-state management.

---

> **What the concept is:** Brand experience design — engineering specific moments in the customer journey, particularly peak moments and final touchpoints, to produce experience memories that drive return behavior, drawing on the insight that memory is not an average of all moments but is disproportionately shaped by peaks and endings.
>
> **What it explains:** Why the $6 latte returns customers who could get caffeine for a fraction of the cost; why Group A's lower average but higher peak and ending produced better NPS than Group B; why back-stage pipeline failures produce front-stage brand damage.
>
> **What it does NOT mean:** That average experience quality does not matter. The peak-end rule explains memory formation, not threshold effects — a sufficiently poor average experience will prevent customers from reaching the peak, regardless of how well-engineered the peak is.
>
> **What comes next:** Module 12 (content, media, and campaigns) extends experience design into the channels through which customers are reached before they arrive at the product — the ZMOT and FMOT touchpoints that determine who gets to experience the peak at all.
