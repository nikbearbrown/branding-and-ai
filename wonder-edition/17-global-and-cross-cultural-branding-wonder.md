## The Strange Question

McDonald's serves a McSpicy Paneer in India, a McAloo Tikki in Pakistan, a Samurai Pork Burger in Thailand, and a Tsukimi Burger in Japan. The menu items share almost nothing with each other or with the American menu. The formulations, the proteins, the spices, the cultural references are all different.

The Golden Arches logo is the same in every market.

The company name is the same. The red and yellow color scheme is the same. The service model is the same. The brand promise — convenient, affordable, consistent fast food — is the same.

McDonald's is simultaneously one brand and dozens of different products. It maintains a coherent global identity while offering radically different things to radically different people.

Now consider a brand that does the opposite: enters a new market with its exact home-market product, name, slogan, imagery, and pricing. The brand team argues that consistency is the strength — why dilute the identity that made the brand successful at home?

Which approach produces better outcomes in new markets, and why does that not depend on whether "consistency" or "local relevance" is the more important value?

## First Intuition

The intuitive model: brand strength comes from consistency. A brand that means the same thing everywhere, expressed the same way everywhere, accumulates global recognition. Changing the expression for each market dilutes the brand and increases costs. The lesson from successful global brands is to be clear about what you are and say it the same way everywhere.

This feels right because consistency is how individual brand experiences compound. Every time someone encounters the same brand signal, the association is reinforced. Vary the signal and the reinforcement is diluted. The stronger the consistency, the stronger the accumulated equity.

## The Surprise

But the same message, translated literally, can mean the opposite across a border — and this is not primarily a language problem.

The chapter draws the distinction between translation (words), localization (words plus context), and transcreation (re-creating the intent and emotional effect). A brand tagline built on American individualism — personal achievement, self-expression, individual distinction — can be grammatically correct in Japanese and culturally inert in a collectivist market where belonging and community do the emotional work that self-expression does in the US.

The surprise is not that words translate badly. It is that the brand's emotional architecture — the associations it relies on to produce resonance — can be opposite from what produces resonance in the target market. The same feeling requires a completely different expression. Hofstede's research shows that the individualism-collectivism dimension alone produces systematically different advertising effectiveness across national markets. The brand that says the same thing everywhere is not being consistent. It is being relevant in one cultural context and irrelevant in every other.

## The Hidden Structure

Therefore, the standardize-versus-adapt question has a more precise answer: the core holds globally, the expression localizes. These are not the same thing, and conflating them is the mistake that produces both over-rigid brands (same expression everywhere, wrong for most markets) and incoherent brands (different core in every market, no global equity).

The core is the brand's purpose, archetype, and fundamental promise. These are not market-specific. A brand that is a Sage in the United States and a Jester in Japan does not have a global brand — it has two brands with the same name. The core is what makes the brand recognizable as itself regardless of where it operates.

The expression is the everything through which the core is communicated: language, imagery, specific claims, tone calibration, cultural references, channel mix, seasonal references. These adapt. They have to. The same archetype expressed through culturally specific imagery that only resonates in the home market is not a consistent brand; it is a brand that looks consistent to one audience and invisible to every other.

> "It is tempting to think that brand consistency means expressing the brand the same way in every market. But the standardization that produces consistency at home produces irrelevance abroad — a tagline built on cultural assumptions that do not transfer lands as neutral noise in markets organized around different values. The correct model holds that the brand's core (purpose, archetype, fundamental promise) should be identical everywhere, while the expression (language, imagery, cultural references, tone) adapts to produce the same feeling in each market that the home-market expression produces at home. The key distinction is between the brand's identity (fixed) and the brand's voice in any given cultural context (adaptive)."

**Glocalization decision matrix — which elements typically hold global and which localize:**

| Brand element | Typically holds global | Typically localizes |
|---|---|---|
| Brand name | Usually | Sometimes (phonetic adaptation) |
| Visual identity / logo | Yes | Rarely |
| Core archetype | Yes | No |
| Tagline | Core feeling, not words | Words, register, references |
| Imagery | Structural composition | People, settings, cultural touchstones |
| Humor | Almost never | Always |
| Pricing | No | Yes |
| Product formulation | Sometimes | Often (taste, dietary requirements) |

## Try Looking At It This Way

**Target:** Transcreation — the discipline of re-creating a brand message's intent and emotional effect in a new cultural context, accepting that the words may be entirely different from the original.

**Base:** A musical arrangement for a different instrument family.

**Features:**
- The original brand message corresponds to a melody written for a string quartet
- The emotional intent of the message corresponds to the feeling the melody produces in its original instrumentation
- Transcreation corresponds to arranging the same melody for a brass ensemble or a jazz trio
- A word-for-word translation corresponds to notating the exact same notes for instruments whose register makes them unplayable or tonally wrong
- A native cultural consultant who evaluates whether the arrangement "sounds right" corresponds to the embedded native speaker who verifies whether the transcreation lands

**Commonalities:** In both systems, the goal is to produce a specific emotional effect in the listener, not to preserve the surface form of the original. A string quartet melody transposed directly to a tuba section may be technically faithful — every note correct — and completely wrong in effect, because the instrument's register, timbre, and cultural associations produce a different emotional experience than strings. Similarly, a brand tagline transcribed word-for-word into a new language may be grammatically accurate and emotionally inert — because the cultural associations of those words, the register they carry, the feelings they evoke in native speakers may be entirely different from what the original words evoke in the source culture. The faithful transcription preserves form at the cost of feeling. The successful arrangement preserves feeling at the cost of form.

**Boundaries:** A musical arrangement can be evaluated immediately by anyone with ears — the effect is perceptible in real time and requires no specialized knowledge of the target culture. Transcreation evaluation requires insider cultural knowledge that cannot be replicated from outside. A composer who has never lived in a culture cannot reliably predict whether an arrangement will "sound right" to its audiences, even if they understand the culture intellectually. This is the chapter's AI+1 line: the model can produce a transcreation that is grammatically fluent and culturally plausible. Whether it actually lands with the resonance intended requires a native who can read the subtext. The musical analogy does not capture how unavoidable that native judgment is — it implies that a skilled arranger can predict the effect without field testing.

**Conclusions:** Transcreation asks not "what do these words mean in this language?" but "what would produce this feeling in this culture?" The answer may share no words with the original. The standard is the feeling produced, not the text preserved.

## Where The Analogy Breaks

Unlike a musical arrangement, which is typically evaluated by a single audience at one time, a transcreated brand message must hold across all the members of a market who will encounter it — including audiences for whom the transcreation may land differently depending on age, region, subculture, and media context. A jazz arrangement that works for one listener probably works for most listeners who enjoy jazz. A transcreated tagline that works for one native speaker may not work for native speakers from a different region, class, or generation. The arrangement analogy implies a single right answer for a specific audience; transcreation operates in a market that is internally diverse, and what resonates with one segment may actively not resonate with another. The test is not "does this land with one native speaker?" but "does this land across the breadth of the target market we are trying to reach?" That requires multiple native informants from different segments, not a single check.

## Small Discovery

**Raw data:**

A European consumer electronics brand launched its noise-canceling headphones in South Korea. The campaign used the original German tagline (translated literally into Korean), which emphasized individual focus and personal performance: "Your world. Your sound. Your peak."

The headphones were technically well-reviewed. The price was competitive. Distribution was broad.

Twelve-month sell-through: 23% of target.

The brand then partnered with a local transcreation team. The new campaign used a different concept entirely: the headphones were positioned as creating a moment of rest and restoration in a demanding, collective environment — a gift you give yourself before giving more to others. The visual campaign showed people in quiet moments between group activities.

Twelve-month sell-through after the new campaign: 67% of target.

**Pattern search:** The product did not change. The price did not change. The distribution did not change. The only change was the cultural framing and the imagery. What specific Hofstede dimension explains the gap between the original campaign's underperformance and the transcreated campaign's success?

**Prediction:** If the brand had run the original "Your world. Your sound. Your peak." campaign in the United States instead of South Korea, would sell-through have been above or below 23% of target? Write your prediction before continuing.

---

**Revelation:** South Korea scores high on collectivism in Hofstede's framework — social belonging, group identity, and harmony are primary values, while individual achievement and self-distinction are less culturally salient than in high-individualism markets like the United States or Germany. The original tagline's emphasis on individual peak performance ("Your world. Your sound. Your peak.") activated high-individualism assumptions that do not resonate in a high-collectivism context. The transcreated campaign repositioned the same product as a collective-harmony benefit — restoration that enables better participation in shared life — which aligned with the target market's value structure. The same emotional need (relief from noise and stimulation) was re-framed in terms of values that the market's cultural context makes salient. In the United States, the original campaign would likely perform substantially better — high-individualism markets respond to personal-achievement and self-distinction framing in ways that high-collectivism markets do not.

## What This Changes

1. **What question can the reader now answer?** Why McDonald's McSpicy Paneer and McAloo Tikki are expressions of the same brand, not evidence that McDonald's has lost coherence — because the core (convenient, affordable, consistent) is identical while the expression (product formulation, local references, advertising imagery) adapts.

2. **What looks different in a specific design decision?** The decision about which brand elements to standardize and which to adapt when entering a new market. Before this chapter, the question is "should we localize?" After this chapter, the question is applied element by element: "Does this element carry the core identity (hold global) or carry cultural expression (localize)?" The tagline's words localize; the tagline's feeling holds.

3. **Practice Bridge:** The student can now build a market-entry adaptation plan for their personal brand entering a new professional cultural context — a different national market, a different industry context, a different audience demographic. The plan has three stages: (1) identify which brand elements hold (archetype, fundamental positioning) and which adapt (voice register, cultural references, imagery), (2) transcreate the positioning sentence for the new context — not translate it, re-create the intent — and (3) flag every cultural assumption in the adapted materials for verification with someone embedded in the target context.

4. **What question does this leave open?** The chapter applies to the expression layer of branding. The product layer — what the brand actually offers — may also require adaptation in ways that go beyond what brand strategy alone can resolve. Module 20 (product requirements and scope) addresses when adaptation of the product itself (not just the message) is warranted, and what discipline governs those decisions.

> **What the concept is:** Glocalization — the discipline of holding a brand's core (purpose, archetype, fundamental promise) globally constant while adapting the expression (language, imagery, cultural references, tone) to produce the same emotional effect in each market that the home-market expression produces at home.
>
> **What it explains:** Why McDonald's can be one global brand with dozens of local menus and campaigns; why a grammatically correct translation of a brand tagline can be culturally inert in the target market; why transcreation asks "what would produce this feeling in this culture?" rather than "what do these words mean in this language?"
>
> **What it does NOT mean:** That every brand element must be adapted for every market. The discipline requires explicit, element-by-element decisions about what holds and what flexes — not wholesale localization and not reflexive standardization. Elements that carry the core identity hold; elements that carry cultural expression adapt. The decision requires a rationale for each element, not a blanket policy.
