## The Strange Question

McDonald's hired researchers to find out why people bought milkshakes. The researchers found that almost half of all milkshakes were purchased before 9 a.m. by lone adults who drove away without ordering anything else.

McDonald's had assumed milkshakes compete with Dairy Queen milkshakes. But the morning buyers were not choosing between milkshakes. They were choosing between a milkshake and a banana, a bagel, a granola bar.

What job were they hiring a milkshake to do — and what does that tell you about who the audience actually is?

## First Intuition

The natural assumption about audience identification: find the people who already buy your product, describe their demographics, and aim your marketing at people who match those demographics. The audience for a milkshake is people who like milkshakes — sweet-toothed consumers, families, teenagers, dessert lovers.

This feels right because demographic targeting is visible, measurable, and widely practiced. Age, income, location, gender, education — these categories are real and traceable. Marketing textbooks teach segmentation this way. Ad platforms sell this way.

> **► Planning prompt:** Before reading further, write down how you would identify the target audience for a new product — say, a $15 reusable coffee cup. List the specific characteristics you would look for and explain why. Predict: will the customers who actually buy the product most often match the demographic profile you described? Write it down before continuing.

## The Surprise

But the JTBD (Jobs To Be Done) research produced a finding that demolished the demographic assumption. The lone adults buying milkshakes before 9 a.m. were not milkshake enthusiasts. They had a long, boring commute. They needed something to do with their hands. They needed something that would hold them until lunch without making a mess in the car. They needed something that took several minutes to consume — slowly, through a thick straw — so the drive felt shorter.

They were hiring the milkshake to do the job of "make a dull commute more bearable." A banana could technically do the same job — except it finishes too fast, makes your hands sticky, and cannot be consumed through a straw while steering. A granola bar crumbles. The milkshake won because it was better at the job, not because it tasted better or was marketed to commuters.

Demographic targeting would have found "adults who buy milkshakes." JTBD found "people with boring commutes who need something to occupy them." These are different audiences, and only one of them tells you how to improve the product.

> **► Monitoring prompt:** In your own words, describe what your initial audience-identification approach assumed about the relationship between who buys a product and why they buy it. What specific part of the JTBD finding contradicts that assumption? What does your current model still fail to explain about the 9 a.m. milkshake buyer?

## The Hidden Structure

Therefore, STP (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning) and JTBD are not competing frameworks — they operate at different levels. STP answers "who is in this market?" JTBD answers "what specific outcome is this customer trying to achieve, and what is making it hard?"

The chapter adds a third lens: cultural tension (Douglas Holt). Some audiences cohere not around demographics or jobs but around a felt contradiction in their lives — a tension between who they are and who they are expected to be, between a value they hold and a norm that contradicts it. Nike's original audience was not "athletes" or "people who want better athletic performance." It was people who felt the cultural tension between their desire for individual excellence and a world that rewarded conformity. "Just Do It" resolved that tension symbolically. Patagonia's audience is people who feel the tension between loving outdoor activities and knowing that consumption destroys the outdoors.

> "It is tempting to think that 'everyone' is a valid target audience — that a product with broad appeal should claim broad reach. But without a specific audience, the brand cannot make specific promises, cannot build specific associations, cannot test whether it is actually delivering value. The correct model holds that a specific audience is a precondition for brand coherence, not a constraint that limits growth. The key distinction is between demographic breadth (many people could use this) and audience specificity (these specific people have this specific unmet need)."

**Persona anatomy from the chapter — the right elements:**

| Element | What it captures | Status tag |
|---|---|---|
| Role and context | The situation where the need arises | Required |
| Primary job (JTBD) | What outcome they are trying to achieve | Required |
| Current workaround | What they do now (reveals the gap) | Required |
| Frustration with workaround | What makes the current solution fail | Required |
| Cultural tension (if applicable) | What value conflict makes the need sharper | Optional |
| Status: [research-based] or [hypothetical] | Source of the data | Required |

The status tag matters. A persona labeled [research-based] was derived from interviews, usage data, or ethnographic observation. A persona labeled [hypothetical] was derived from reasonable inference. Treating a hypothetical persona as verified audience data is the most common research failure in brand strategy.

## Try Looking At It This Way

**Target:** The JTBD framework — understanding audiences by the outcomes they are trying to achieve and the obstacles preventing those outcomes, rather than by demographic characteristics.

**Base:** A doctor diagnosing a patient's symptom.

**Features:**
- The patient's stated complaint ("I have a headache") in the base corresponds to the stated product preference ("I want a milkshake") in the target
- The underlying cause the doctor investigates (dehydration? stress? vision problem?) corresponds to the underlying job (make the commute bearable? reward myself after a difficult meeting?)
- The diagnostic question "what changed right before the symptom appeared?" corresponds to JTBD's "what was the customer doing right before they hired this product?"
- The treatment choice (which depends on the cause, not the symptom) corresponds to the product improvement choice (which depends on the job, not the stated preference)

**Commonalities:** In both cases, treating the surface manifestation (symptom / stated preference) as the actual problem produces interventions that may look right but do not address the real cause. A doctor who treats a headache with painkillers without investigating the cause may temporarily relieve the symptom while the underlying condition worsens. A product team that improves milkshake flavor based on customer taste preferences may improve the product without addressing the commute-companion job at all.

**Boundaries:** A doctor operates within an established diagnostic taxonomy — there are a finite number of known conditions that cause headaches, and diagnostic protocols exist. JTBD has no equivalent taxonomy of human jobs. The framework prompts the right questions but does not provide a catalog of the jobs that might emerge from the answers. The doctor analogy suggests more structure in the investigation than JTBD actually provides.

**Conclusions:** Ask "what outcome are they trying to achieve and what is preventing it?" before asking "who are they?" The outcome question generates product insight; the who question generates targeting insight. Both are necessary; the order matters.

## Where The Analogy Breaks

Unlike a medical diagnosis, JTBD findings do not travel well across contexts. A headache that responds to ibuprofen in one patient responds to ibuprofen in a comparable patient — the physiology is shared. But a job that one customer hires a product to do is not necessarily the job another customer with similar demographics hires it for. The morning milkshake buyer and the afternoon milkshake buyer may have entirely different jobs, and designing for one may actively harm the other. The medical analogy implies that correct diagnosis leads to universal treatment; JTBD implies that correct job identification leads to a product decision that serves one segment of customers while potentially serving another segment less well.

## Small Discovery

**Raw data:**

A public library tracked why patrons visited in person rather than accessing its digital catalog from home. The library had assumed in-person visits were driven by the need to borrow physical books — which the digital catalog could not provide. A research team observed and interviewed visitors for three weeks.

| Stated reason for in-person visit | % of visitors |
|---|---|
| Needed a physical book unavailable digitally | 31% |
| Needed to use the printer or scanner | 28% |
| Came to study in a quiet space | 22% |
| Attended a program or event | 11% |
| Other / unclear | 8% |

**Pattern search:** The most common reason for visiting the library in person was not the stated assumption (physical books). What specific job does this data reveal that the digital catalog cannot perform?

**Prediction:** If the library invested its improvement budget in expanding its digital catalog — adding 5,000 more ebooks — what would happen to in-person visitor counts? Write your prediction before continuing.

---

**Revelation:** Expanding the digital catalog would likely have minimal effect on in-person visits, because the majority of visits are driven by jobs the digital catalog cannot do: printing, quiet study space, community programming. The library's decision about where to invest depends entirely on which job it is prioritizing — and the jobs are being performed by people who look demographically identical (all are library patrons). This is the JTBD finding in a public institution: the users of the digital catalog and the users of the quiet study space may be the same people at different moments, hiring different products for different jobs. The "audience" for the library's physical space is not "library users" — it is "people who need a quiet place to focus," which is a job-based definition, not a demographic one.

## What This Changes

1. **What question can the reader now answer?** Why "everyone is our audience" is not a strategy but a signal that the brand has not done the work of identifying what specific job it is solving for a specific group of people.

2. **What looks different in a specific design decision?** The choice of which persona to build a product around. Before this chapter, persona selection is an exercise in demographic plausibility. After this chapter, persona selection requires identifying a specific job, a specific current workaround, and a specific frustration with that workaround — and tagging the result with a status indicating how it was derived.

3. **Practice Bridge:** The student can now write a JTBD statement for their AI tool's primary user: "When [situation], this person wants to [desired outcome] but cannot because [obstacle]. They currently [workaround], which fails because [frustration]." This statement, complete and specific, is the foundation for the PRD's problem section in Module 20.

4. **What question does this leave open?** Once the job is identified, how does the brand position itself as the best candidate for that job? Module 6 (brand strategy and positioning) addresses the positioning question that JTBD research makes possible.

---

> **What the concept is:** Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) — a framework that identifies audiences by the outcomes they are trying to achieve in specific situations, rather than by demographic characteristics or stated product preferences.
>
> **What it explains:** Why the milkshake's real competition was a banana, not a Dairy Queen milkshake; why demographic targeting misses the morning buyer entirely; why "everyone" as an audience produces a brand with no specific promise.
>
> **What it does NOT mean:** That demographics are irrelevant. Demographics determine who is in the market; JTBD determines why they are in it and what they will pay a premium for. Both are necessary for complete audience understanding.
>
> **What comes next:** Module 6 (brand strategy) uses the audience insight from JTBD to drive positioning — how the brand presents itself as the best candidate for the identified job.
