## The Strange Question

In 2017, Pepsi released a commercial featuring Kendall Jenner. In it, Jenner leaves a photo shoot, joins a street protest, and hands a can of Pepsi to a police officer. The crowd cheers. The protest resolves.

Pepsi pulled the ad within 24 hours. The backlash was immediate and severe. Critics accused the ad of trivializing the Black Lives Matter movement and real protest violence.

But here is what is strange: Pepsi had used protest imagery, social causes, and generational solidarity in advertising for decades. The 1960s "Pepsi Generation" campaign had been celebrated for exactly this kind of youth-culture identification. Why did the same narrative frame that worked in 1964 fail catastrophically in 2017?

## First Intuition

The natural assumption: the 2017 ad was offensive because the political climate had changed. In 2017, protest was a charged topic. What was acceptable imagery in 1964 is unacceptable in a more politically aware moment.

This feels right because it explains the failure in terms of context: the same creative elements produce different responses in different cultural moments. The problem was timing, not concept.

## The Surprise

But the chapter's three mismatch pathways reveal something the timing explanation misses. The Pepsi Kendall Jenner ad did not fail because protest is politically charged in 2017. Nike's "Believe in Something" campaign featuring Colin Kaepernick launched in 2018 — a more charged moment — and succeeded commercially. The NFL player who took a knee before every game; the brand positioned around human achievement, courage, and sacrifice.

The failure was archetype mismatch. Nike's Hero archetype can authentically claim the courage narrative — the brand's entire history is built on athletes who sacrifice, push through pain, and risk failure. Pepsi's Everyman archetype cannot claim the same narrative. Pepsi's story is shared pleasure, everyday moments, the refreshment that brings people together. When Pepsi appropriated the sacrifice-and-courage narrative, customers felt the mismatch — not because protest was too hot to touch, but because Pepsi was not the brand to touch it.

The story was not Pepsi's story to tell. And customers could sense that, even if they could not articulate it.

## The Hidden Structure

Therefore, brand voice and storytelling are not separate activities from brand strategy — they are the implementation of the archetype in language and narrative form. The chapter's three frameworks (Campbell's Hero's Journey, Booker's seven basic plots, Miller's StoryBrand) each describe the structural patterns that stories follow. The archetype determines which patterns are available. A Rebel archetype can authentically tell Booker's "Rags to Riches" story, the "Overcoming the Monster" story, and the "Voyage and Return" — stories of disruption, challenge, and transformation. A Caregiver archetype can authentically tell "Rebirth" and "Quest" stories organized around helping others achieve their goals. The archetype commits to story shapes before the story is written.

The three mismatch pathways explain how brand storytelling fails specifically:
1. **Borrowed values** — claiming a value the brand's behavior does not demonstrate (Pepsi claiming sacrifice-and-courage)
2. **Borrowed aesthetics** — using the visual or tonal grammar of a different archetype without the underlying values (a Sage brand using explosive, high-contrast creative to feel "energetic")
3. **Borrowed occasion** — inserting the brand into a cultural moment it has no authentic relationship with (any brand using a crisis as a marketing opportunity without genuine organizational connection to the issue)

> "It is tempting to think that any brand can tell any story if it is executed well enough. But the archetype constrains which narratives are available, because narrative authenticity requires a matching history. The correct model holds that a brand can only tell stories that its actual behavior and values can credibly underwrite. The key distinction is between a story that is true about the brand (credibly told, drawing on actual behavior) and a story that is aspirationally true (claiming values the brand wants to have but has not demonstrated)."

**Archetype-narrative alignment table from the chapter:**

| Archetype | Campbell fit | Booker fit | Voice register |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hero | Hero's journey itself | Overcoming the monster, Rags to riches | Active, direct, present-tense |
| Sage | Call to adventure via knowledge | Quest (knowledge-seeking), Comedy of understanding | Authoritative, measured, precise |
| Explorer | Road of trials, Return with elixir | Voyage and return | Expansive, curious, first-person discovery |
| Creator | Apotheosis, Meeting with the goddess | Rebirth (creative transformation) | Expressive, process-visible, detail-rich |
| Caregiver | Rescue of the other, Helper figure | Rebirth (through care), Quest for others | Warm, second-person focus, patient |

## Try Looking At It This Way

**Target:** Brand voice — the consistent verbal register and narrative pattern that expresses the archetype authentically across all brand communications.

**Base:** A professional musician's live performance style.

**Features:**
- The archetype in the target corresponds to the musician's genre commitment in the base — the musical framework that defines which notes, rhythms, and performance conventions are available
- The brand's specific voice characteristics (tone words: "direct," "curious," "warm") correspond to the musician's specific performance style within the genre (staccato vs. legato, quiet vs. powerful, improvised vs. composed)
- Archetype mismatch in the target corresponds to genre mismatch in the base — a jazz musician performing heavy metal riffs in a jazz set
- The audience's sense that something is "off" when a brand tells the wrong story corresponds to the audience's sense that a musician is "playing wrong notes" for the genre

**Commonalities:** In both systems, there is a space of possible expressions within the genre/archetype, and specific choices within that space define the individual style. A jazz musician can be free-flowing or structured, quiet or explosive — but these choices remain within jazz. Similarly, a Sage brand can be warm or cold, detailed or high-level — but these choices remain within Sage's authoritative-trustworthy register. In both systems, the mismatch is recognizable even by audiences who cannot name the genre or archetype — they feel that something is wrong before they can explain why.

**Boundaries:** A musician can choose to play multiple genres in different sets — the same performer might release a country album and a hip-hop album under different names. A brand generally cannot. A brand that tells a Sage story in one campaign and a Rebel story in the next does not benefit from genre versatility — it loses coherence. The musician analogy implies more flexibility across genre contexts than brand voice management typically allows.

**Conclusions:** Voice consistency matters more than voice brilliance in any single instance. A distinctive, consistent voice builds recognition over time; a brilliant but inconsistent voice is forgettable.

## Where The Analogy Breaks

Unlike a musician's performance, a brand voice must scale across dozens of creators writing simultaneously — marketing team, social media manager, customer support, legal boilerplate, error messages. A jazz musician can maintain genre coherence because one person is performing. Brand voice is maintained by a style guide applied by many people with varying levels of care. The musician analogy implies a level of authorial control over every expression that brand voice management cannot achieve. The result is that brand voice consistency depends on systems (documented tone guidelines, review processes, training) rather than on individual authenticity — which is the opposite of how musicians typically maintain their voice.

## Small Discovery

**Raw data:**

A software company's customer support team responded to the same complaint ("Your app crashed and I lost an hour of work") in three different styles. Each response was tested with 100 customers who had experienced a similar issue.

| Response style | Resolution offered | Customer satisfaction (1-10) | Likelihood to renew subscription |
|---|---|---|---|
| A: Formal apology, technical explanation, ticket number | Full refund for the month | 6.4 | 61% |
| B: Conversational apology, acknowledgment of frustration, personal name used | Full refund for the month | 7.9 | 74% |
| C: Conversational apology, acknowledgment of frustration, specific commitment about the fix | No refund | 7.1 | 71% |

**Pattern search:** Response C offered no refund but nearly matched Response B's renewal rate. Response A offered the same refund as B but had significantly lower satisfaction. What specifically is driving the gap between A and B, and between B and C?

**Prediction:** If Response A were rewritten with a conversational tone but kept the technical explanation and ticket number (no name, no acknowledgment of frustration), where would its satisfaction score fall — closer to A or closer to B? Write your prediction before continuing.

---

**Revelation:** Tone and acknowledgment of feeling drive the satisfaction gap between A and B more than the refund. Response A fails because it is in a register (formal, technical, process-focused) that does not match what the customer actually needs at the moment of frustration — to feel heard. Response C's relatively high renewal rate shows that the substantive commitment (we will fix this) is worth nearly as much as the financial restitution, when delivered in the right register. A rewrite of Response A with conversational tone but kept technical content typically scores in the 7.0-7.4 range — tone accounts for most of the gap, but the failure to acknowledge frustration specifically is also contributing. This is the brand voice principle in customer experience: the same resolution produces different outcomes depending on the voice register of the delivery.

## What This Changes

1. **What question can the reader now answer?** Why Pepsi's Kendall Jenner ad failed even with professional production and significant budget — and why Nike's equally charged campaign succeeded. The answer is archetype-narrative alignment, not political timing.

2. **What looks different in a specific design decision?** The process of writing a case study or portfolio project description. Before this chapter, the case study is technical documentation. After this chapter, it is brand storytelling: the six elements (protagonist, obstacle, mentor, guide, transformation, evidence) must be present, and the story shape must be consistent with the archetype.

3. **Practice Bridge:** The student can now write an origin story using their chosen archetype and one of the chapter's three frameworks. The origin story — the narrative of how the student came to build what they build and why — is the first piece of brand storytelling that appears in the portfolio, the LinkedIn about section, and the final presentation. It must be archetype-consistent, factually true, and structured using one of the story frameworks.

4. **What question does this leave open?** A brand voice must be expressed across multiple platforms with different format constraints — LinkedIn posts, case studies, presentation scripts, tool documentation. Module 19 (professional presence) addresses how to maintain voice coherence across these formats.
