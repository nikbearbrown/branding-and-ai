## Worked Example

*Study this example before attempting Tier 1. After reading it, close it and try to recall the key steps from memory before moving on.*

**Problem:** Tomás is building a personal brand as a data scientist with a Sage archetype. He has designed his portfolio site using bright yellow as the primary color, a rounded bubbly sans-serif typeface, and large playful icons throughout. A classmate says it "looks great." Tomás wonders if the visual identity is working. How should he evaluate it?

**Approach:**
- Step 1: Identify the archetype (Sage) and what visual logic follows from it — what does the chapter specify about Sage color, typography, and layout direction?
- Step 2: Audit each visual component against the archetype's requirements — not against general aesthetic taste.
- Step 3: Identify the mismatch: which components contradict the archetype, and what failure mode does this represent?
- Step 4: Name the Tropicana parallel if relevant — is this "wrong archetypal foundation"?

**Answer:** The Sage archetype's visual logic, as the chapter specifies, is restrained, high-contrast, often serif-anchored, with generous whitespace and an approach that uses color to organize information rather than to decorate. The visual language signals careful thinking and depth. Bright yellow is a high-energy, high-saturation choice — closer to Jester or Innocent archetype territory. A rounded bubbly sans-serif signals approachability and warmth — closer to Caregiver territory. Large playful icons signal accessibility and friendliness, again closer to Caregiver or Jester.

Tomás's visual identity does not express his archetype. It expresses a different archetype — friendly, approachable, warm — while his brand strategy commits to insight, depth, and evidence-based trustworthiness. This is the Tropicana failure mode: technically competent design expressing the wrong archetypal foundation.

The fix is not to make the design "better" by general aesthetic standards — the design may be perfectly good for a Caregiver or Jester brand. The fix is to rebuild the visual system from the archetype's requirements: restrained palette (near-neutral primary, sparse accent), serif or humanist sans-serif typography at high contrast, and layout that leads with text and evidence rather than icons and decoration.

**What to notice:** The classmate's aesthetic approval is irrelevant. The evaluation criterion is archetype alignment, not taste. A visual identity is evaluated by asking: does this express the committed archetype, or does it express a different one?

**Exercise 1**

Name the six components of a visual identity system as introduced in this chapter. For each, write one sentence describing what it contains and one sentence explaining how it expresses the brand's archetype.

*(Tests: six visual identity system components — description and archetype expression)*

**Answer:**
1. **Logo/wordmark:** The single most-replicated mark — wordmark (typographically styled name), symbol (standalone graphic), or lockup (both combined). Expresses archetype through form complexity, letterform character, and visual weight.
2. **Color palette:** Four to six committed hex values (primary, secondary, neutral). Expresses archetype through hue temperature, saturation level, and how color is used — for decoration or for information organization.
3. **Typography:** Display face for headlines, body face for running text, with full size and weight specifications. Expresses archetype through whether type signals patience and depth (serif) or speed and efficiency (compressed geometric sans-serif).
4. **Imagery direction:** What kinds of photographs, illustrations, and icons fit the brand — a style commitment, not a specific asset list. Expresses archetype through the emotional register of the visual choices.
5. **Layout system:** Grid, spacing, and hierarchy rules governing how all components arrange. Expresses archetype through density, whitespace, and section rhythm even when no logo is visible.
6. **Motion** (secondary, for digital products): How elements appear, transition, and behave. Expresses archetype through pacing — deliberate and unhurried (Sage) versus dynamic and directional (Hero).

**Common error:** Students often list "logo" and "colors" and stop, missing layout system and motion. The chapter is explicit that all six are components of the system, and that layout system is often what makes a brand recognizable when no logo is visible.

*Chapter reference:* "What 'Visual Identity' Actually Means"

**Exercise 2**

True / False: The Pepsi 2008 redesign failed primarily because the new logo was aesthetically inferior to the original.

*Explain your reasoning in one sentence:*

*(Tests: Pepsi 2008 failure mode — strategy-free rationale)*

**Answer:** False. The Pepsi 2008 failure was not aesthetic — the visual change itself was modest. It failed because Pepsi's brand had not committed to anything specific enough for the visual to express, so when the designers had to justify their choices, they reached for borrowed authority (the Mona Lisa, the Golden Ratio) rather than a strategic commitment. **Common error:** Students often assume visual identity failures are caused by bad design. The chapter's three cases all involve design that was technically competent — the failures were strategic, not aesthetic.

**Exercise 3**

The chapter identifies three **tone-word anti-words** that do not constrain design decisions: "innovative," "modern," and "clean." In your own words, explain why these words fail as creative brief constraints. Then provide a replacement for each that would work for a **Hero archetype** brand, and justify each replacement in one sentence.

*(Tests: tone word discipline — anti-words vs. constraining description)*

**Answer:** Anti-words fail because they could apply to any brand in any category — every brand aspires to seem innovative, modern, and clean. A constraint is only useful if it makes some design decisions obviously right and others obviously wrong. "Innovative" eliminates nothing; "decisive" eliminates soft, hedging, or cautious visual choices.

Hero archetype replacements:
- "Innovative" → "Kinetic": signals motion-implied, directional energy that makes static, stable layouts obviously wrong for a Hero brand.
- "Modern" → "Saturated": points toward bold, high-contrast color application and eliminates the muted or restrained palette direction appropriate to a Sage but wrong for a Hero.
- "Clean" → "Decisive": eliminates equivocating layouts, vague hierarchies, and multi-accent palettes; forces clear primary visual action in every composition.

**Common error:** Students replace anti-words with slightly more specific anti-words (e.g., replacing "modern" with "contemporary"). A replacement must be specific enough to exclude a design direction, not just synonym for the original.

*Chapter reference:* "The Creative Brief — Translating Strategy into Design"

**Exercise 4** *(Contrastive Classification)*

For each of the following visual identity descriptions, classify the implied archetype and name the one component that most strongly signals it. Then identify any component that contradicts the apparent archetype.

- (a) A brand using a compact geometric sans-serif at heavy weight, a saturated electric blue primary color, diagonal layout elements, and full-bleed photography of people mid-action.
- (b) A brand using a classical serif display face, a near-black/near-white color palette with a single deep teal accent used sparingly, generous whitespace, and dense text-forward sections.
- (c) A brand using rounded sans-serif typeface, warm terracotta and sage green palette, soft-edged illustrations of diverse people, and wide margins.
- (d) A brand using a jewel-tone purple and emerald palette, dramatic typographic scale contrasts, and layout sections that intentionally break the grid.

*(Tests: archetype-visual logic alignment — contrastive classification)*

**Answer:**
- (a) Hero archetype. Strongest signal: saturated electric blue primary + diagonal layout elements implying momentum. No obvious contradiction if the photography genuinely shows effort and achievement rather than stock-photo perfection.
- (b) Sage archetype. Strongest signal: classical serif display face combined with near-neutral high-contrast palette. The description is internally consistent — no contradiction.
- (c) Caregiver archetype. Strongest signal: warm earth-tone palette (terracotta/sage green) and soft-edged diverse people illustrations. Wide margins reinforce the unhurried, approachable register. Consistent.
- (d) Magician archetype. Strongest signal: jewel tones (purple/emerald) plus layout that breaks the grid intentionally — the chapter's visual logic for Magician includes "unexpected combinations that imply transformation." Consistent.

**Common error:** Students often classify (b) as "minimalist" or "professional" without naming the archetype. The contrastive classification requires archetype identification, not aesthetic genre labeling.

**Exercise 5**

Describe the WCAG 2.2 AA contrast requirements for normal text and large text. Then explain: why is the accessibility audit described as non-optional, and what is the consequence of a failing combination that is not addressed before launch?

*(Tests: WCAG 2.2 AA accessibility standards — requirements and non-optional status)*

**Answer:** WCAG 2.2 AA requires a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text (below 18pt) on any background it appears on, and at least 3:1 for large text (18pt and above) and graphical objects. These are specific, measurable standards that can be tested with a contrast-checking tool (such as WebAIM Contrast Checker) using two hex values.

The accessibility audit is non-optional because WCAG 2.2 AA is a legal requirement in many jurisdictions for public-facing digital products — not a best-practice recommendation. A failing combination that is not addressed before launch is a compliance failure with legal exposure, not just an aesthetic compromise. The fix is typically straightforward — darkening the text color or lightening the background by small hex adjustments brings most failures into compliance without changing the palette's visual character.

**Common error:** Students treat accessibility as a design preference ("users with normal vision won't notice"). The chapter is explicit that this is a legal obligation in many jurisdictions, not a recommendation.

*Chapter reference:* "Color Palette — Construction and Accessibility"

**Exercise 6**

Explain the difference between a **wireframe** and a finished visual design, and explain why making layout decisions at the wireframe stage is preferable to making them in code. Your answer should use the term **archetype** correctly.

*(Tests: wireframe — purpose and structural encoding of archetype)*

**Answer:** A wireframe is a low-fidelity structural plan showing page hierarchy, section order, layout grid, and component reuse — using boxes and labels rather than colors, images, or finished typography. A finished visual design applies the full visual system to that structure. The wireframe stage is the right moment for layout decisions because changes at this stage cost nothing — moving a section is moving a box on a page. Changes made in code are expensive to reverse.

The wireframe also encodes the archetype in structural rhythm before any visual rendering happens. A Sage wireframe leads with the positioning sentence, uses text-forward sections, and limits full-bleed images — these structural choices are archetype expressions visible even at low fidelity. A Hero wireframe leads with a large project image and a metrics strip. Making these archetypal structural commitments at the wireframe stage prevents the finished design from inadvertently expressing a different archetype through layout, regardless of how strong the color and typography choices are.

**Common error:** Students treat the wireframe as a rough sketch that can be ignored once design begins. The chapter presents the wireframe as the load-bearing structural document — the place where the archetype gets encoded in layout before expensive visual decisions lock it in.

*Chapter reference:* "Wireframes — Encoding the Archetype Structurally"

**Exercise 7** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

Mei is building an AI-powered productivity tool with a Hero archetype. She has written the following tone-word section for her creative brief: "The design should feel innovative, efficient, and modern."

Diagnose the problem with this tone-word selection, then rewrite the tone-word section for Mei's Hero-archetype brand. Your rewrite must include three to five words that each specifically constrain a design decision — identify what each word rules out.

*(Tests: creative brief — tone word discipline applied to Hero archetype)*

**Answer:** Problem: "Innovative," "efficient," and "modern" are the chapter's anti-words — they describe nothing, constrain nothing, and could apply to any brand in any product category. A design team receiving this brief cannot use these words to make a single design decision, because no design direction is ruled out by any of them.

Rewrite for Hero archetype:
- **Decisive**: rules out soft gradients, hedging multi-accent palettes, and layout hierarchies without a clear primary action.
- **Saturated**: rules out the restrained, near-neutral palette appropriate to Sage; the Hero palette lives in the high-saturation zone.
- **Kinetic**: rules out static, text-only layouts with no implied motion or momentum; the Hero visual language implies action and direction.
- **High-contrast**: rules out low-contrast type combinations and mid-tone palettes that read as ambiguous rather than bold.
- **Impact-forward**: rules out layouts that bury the tool's results or outputs — the Hero leads with evidence of what has been achieved.

**Common error:** Students replace anti-words with synonymous anti-words ("fresh" for "modern," "cutting-edge" for "innovative"). The test is whether each word could exclude a design direction. Words that cannot exclude anything are anti-words regardless of how they sound.

*Chapter reference:* "The Creative Brief — Translating Strategy into Design"

**Exercise 8** *(Error Analysis)*

Soren is writing a creative brief for his AI research assistant tool with a Sage archetype. His "References" section reads:
> "I like the way Stripe looks. I like how Apple keeps things simple. I like how Google is clean and functional."

2. Explain the misconception — what did the student misunderstand about what a creative brief reference requires?

*(Tests: creative brief — references vs. preference statements)*

**Answer:**
1. The error is in every reference entry: Soren has written preference statements, not references.
2. The chapter specifies that a reference must name a specific visual work and state what specifically about it works for this brief. **Exercise 10** *(Self-Explanation)*

The chapter states that typography "does two jobs simultaneously" — a legibility job and an archetype job. Using this chapter's vocabulary, explain why a brand might choose a humanist sans-serif for body text even when its archetype is Sage (which leans toward serifs for display). Your explanation must use the terms **display face** and **body face** correctly.

*(Tests: typography — display face vs. body face dual function — self-explanation)*

**Answer:** The display face (used for headlines and large-format headings) carries more archetypal weight because it is seen at sizes where readers notice its personality — a classical serif display face signals historical authority and depth, which are core Sage associations. The body face (used for running text and UI elements) carries more legibility weight because it is read at small sizes over long passages, where readability in low-attention contexts matters more than expressive character. A humanist sans-serif for body text — like Inter or IBM Plex Sans — reads cleanly in UI contexts and at body sizes without the serifs becoming visually noisy at 16px. The Sage brand gets its archetypal signal from the display face; the body face prioritizes the reader's experience. The two faces work as a system because together they signal "we have thought carefully and written clearly" — the display face does the archetype work, the body face does the legibility work.

**Common error:** Students often think a Sage brand should use serifs everywhere, including body text. The chapter specifies that the body face prioritizes legibility in UI contexts — the archetypal signal is primarily delivered by the display face.

*Chapter reference:* "Typography — Pair Logic and Specification"

**Exercise 11** *(Cumulative — prior chapter concept)*

In Chapter 3 (Brand Archetypes as a System), you learned that an archetype functions as a consistency-enforcement device — a constraint that makes downstream decisions obviously right or obviously wrong. In this chapter, you are building a visual identity system anchored to that archetype.

Aroha has a Caregiver archetype for her mental health support platform. She is choosing between two color palettes:

- **Palette A:** Warm terracotta primary (#C1674D), muted sage green accent (#7A9E7E), warm off-white background (#FAF7F2)
- **Palette B:** Electric cobalt primary (#1E5AFF), bright lime accent (#A3F000), cool white background (#F5F5FF)

Using the archetype-as-constraint logic from Chapter 3 AND the palette construction guidance from Chapter 9, evaluate which palette is appropriate for Aroha's brand and explain the specific mechanism by which the archetype makes the decision visible.

*(Tests: color palette construction from archetype + archetype as consistency-enforcement device — interleaved)*

**Answer:** Palette A is appropriate for the Caregiver archetype. The chapter specifies that Caregiver palettes are warm, approachable, and soft — earth tones, muted greens, warm grays — because nothing reads as aggressive, cold, or clinical. Terracotta, muted sage green, and warm off-white are all in the Caregiver's documented palette territory. They signal approachability, warmth, and natural groundedness — exactly what a mental health support platform's Caregiver archetype should communicate.

Palette B is inappropriate: electric cobalt and bright lime are high-saturation, high-contrast choices that the chapter associates with Hero archetype energy — bold, kinetic, confident. These colors read as driven and high-performance, not warm and receptive. For a mental health context, where users may be in vulnerable states, Hero-archetype visual signals could communicate the wrong emotional register and undermine the Caregiver's core promise of attentive receptivity.

The mechanism from Chapter 3: the archetype functions as a consistency-enforcement device by making some decisions obviously right (Palette A's warm terracotta) and others obviously wrong (Palette B's electric cobalt) — not because of taste, but because each choice expresses a specific archetypal emotional logic that is or is not consistent with the brand's committed identity. Without the archetype, both palettes are "good" colors; with the archetype, one follows from the commitment and the other contradicts it.

**Common error:** Students often evaluate palettes on general aesthetic grounds ("Palette B looks more professional") without applying the archetype as the decision criterion. **Exercise 12** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

Run a partial accessibility audit on the following palette combinations for a Sage-archetype academic research tool. For each combination, calculate whether it meets WCAG 2.2 AA requirements for normal text (4.5:1), and if it fails, propose a specific hex adjustment.

Use the chapter's guidance: the WebAIM Contrast Checker is the appropriate tool. Assume you are running this audit with a contrast-checking tool.

- (a) Body text #2C3E50 on background #FFFFFF (contrast ratio: approximately 10.7:1)
- (b) Secondary text #8E9BA8 on background #FFFFFF (contrast ratio: approximately 3.1:1)
- (c) Accent text #0A7EA4 on background #FFFFFF (contrast ratio: approximately 5.2:1)
- (d) Caption text #B0BEC5 on background #F9FAFB (contrast ratio: approximately 2.2:1)

For each failing combination, propose a hex adjustment that would bring it into compliance for normal text, and explain why the adjustment preserves the palette's character.

*(Tests: WCAG 2.2 AA accessibility audit — pass/fail evaluation and fix logic)*

**Answer:**
- (a) #2C3E50 on #FFFFFF at 10.7:1 — **PASS** for both normal and large text (4.5:1 threshold exceeded substantially).
- (b) #8E9BA8 on #FFFFFF at 3.1:1 — **FAIL** for normal text (3.1:1 < 4.5:1). The fix: darken the text color to approximately #5E6E7F — this will push the ratio above 4.5:1 while remaining a cool-neutral mid-gray appropriate for secondary text on a Sage brand. The palette character is preserved because the adjusted color stays in the same gray-blue family, just with higher contrast.
- (c) #0A7EA4 on #FFFFFF at 5.2:1 — **PASS** for normal text (5.2:1 > 4.5:1).
- (d) #B0BEC5 on #F9FAFB at 2.2:1 — **FAIL** for both normal and large text (2.2:1 < 3:1). Caption text failures are the most common. Fix: darken to approximately #6B7280 — at approximately 4.6:1 this passes normal text requirements while remaining appropriate for caption-weight use. The Sage palette's character (cool, organized, restrained) is preserved — the adjustment is a value shift, not a hue change.

**Common error:** Students often propose an entirely different color rather than a targeted value adjustment. The chapter's guidance is that small shifts in value can bring a failing combination into compliance without changing the palette's visual character — a hue change is unnecessary and potentially damaging to system coherence.

Describe the **Tropicana 2009** redesign failure as introduced in this chapter. Identify: (1) what changed visually; (2) what archetypal foundation the original packaging expressed; (3) what archetypal foundation the new packaging expressed; (4) the audience response; and (5) which of the chapter's three failure modes this case represents.

**Exercise 15** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

As introduced in Chapter 6 (Brand Strategy and Positioning), a brand's positioning commits to a specific audience, a specific tension the audience faces, and a specific proof that the brand resolves that tension. In this chapter, you learned that the creative brief translates strategy into design constraints — and that every section of the brief is a constraint, not a description.

A technical writing platform has the following positioning: "For software documentation teams who need to publish complex API references quickly without sacrificing clarity." The brand's archetype is Sage.

Write the **tone words** and **references** sections of the creative brief for this brand. Your tone words section must include three to five words that each constrain a design decision (no anti-words). Your references section must include three entries, each with a specific note on what exactly about that reference works for this brief.

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Tone words eliminate specific design directions rather than describing desired qualities in general terms
- References name specific visual elements (not just brands), tied to the specific brief's audience and archetype
- The brief sections are internally consistent — the tone words and references point toward the same design direction

*(Tests: creative brief construction — tone words and references + brand positioning — strategy-to-design translation — Ch. 9 + Ch. 6)*

**Answer:**

Tone Words (Sage archetype, software documentation audience):

**Exercise 16** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

As introduced in Chapter 8 (Naming, Trademark, and Brand Protection), the Abercrombie spectrum shows that fanciful and arbitrary names are the strongest legal assets because they have no prior meaning — the brand teaches all of it. In this chapter, you learned that a wordmark is the most frequently replicated mark and that for a new brand, a simple wordmark or wordmark-with-symbol is the right starting point because "complex symbols require brand recognition that takes years to build."

Connect these insights: in what sense do the naming and visual-identity frameworks share the same underlying logic about the costs and benefits of starting with no prior meaning? And what does this shared logic suggest about the ideal visual identity system for a brand launching with a fanciful name like "Quilo" (from Chapter 8's scenario)?

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Names the shared mechanism explicitly: both frameworks favor starting without borrowed meaning and earning it through coherent, compounding investment
- Applies the shared logic to specify what "Quilo's" visual identity should prioritize (wordmark simplicity, system coherence) and what it should avoid (complex symbols, borrowed visual authority)
- Shows how the visual identity and the name together constitute a coordinated investment in a single equity pool

*(Tests: wordmark simplicity — visual identity for new brands + Abercrombie spectrum — fanciful name strategy — Ch. 9 + Ch. 8)*

**Answer:** The shared logic in both frameworks is that starting without prior meaning is more expensive in the short term and more valuable in the long term. A fanciful name has no prior associations — the brand must teach customers what it means, investing in recognition that eventually becomes exclusive and defensible. A wordmark for a new brand with no recognition starts with no visual authority — but once built, a simple wordmark carries all of the brand's equity in concentrated form and becomes immediately recognizable at any scale.

Complex visual symbols (abstract marks, logos with many components) have the same problem as descriptive names: they borrow from existing visual vocabularies (the swoosh implies speed, the shield implies security), and that borrowed meaning dilutes the brand's ability to build its own. Just as a fanciful name eventually owns all of its associations, a simple wordmark built through consistent, coherent use eventually owns its visual territory completely.

For "Quilo" specifically: the visual identity should start with a wordmark — the brand name, typographically styled to express the archetype — without a complex symbol. The wordmark is the only thing that appears in every context (favicon to billboard), and its simplicity is what allows it to accumulate equity efficiently. As "Quilo" teaches consumers its meaning through consistent use, the wordmark's recognition builds. A complex logo added at launch would divide attention between the visual symbol and the name — both of which the brand is trying to teach from scratch. The coordinated investment in name recognition and wordmark recognition, both starting simple and building through compounding use, is the right strategy for a fanciful-name brand.

*Common error:* A surface answer notes that both frameworks involve "building equity from scratch" without naming the specific mechanism — that starting without borrowed meaning is a coordinated investment strategy, not just a constraint.
