## Worked Example

*Study this example before attempting Tier 1. After reading it, close it and try to recall the key steps from memory before moving on.*

**Problem:** Kenji manages marketing for a B2B software company that sells AI-powered invoice processing to accounting teams. He runs a brand-building campaign — a long-form white paper and three webinars — in Q1. In Q2, customer acquisition cost drops 12%. His CEO says: "The campaign worked. Our CAC dropped." Kenji is uncertain whether this is a valid attribution. Walk through the attribution problem for this case.

**Approach:**
- Step 1: Identify what the CEO is claiming. The claim is that the Q1 brand campaign caused the Q2 CAC drop. This is a causal attribution claim.
- Step 2: Name the alternative explanations that would also produce a Q2 CAC drop without the campaign being the cause: (a) competitor raised prices or exited a market segment; (b) the sales team changed its outreach methodology; (c) the company ran paid search in Q2 at higher budget; (d) seasonal buying patterns in accounting software favor Q2; (e) the company's product improved in Q1, making conversion easier.
- Step 3: Assess the evidence available. The CAC drop is real but is a performance metric. The campaign was aimed at brand building — brand metrics (awareness, consideration, association strength) would need to move to support the causal claim. Were brand metrics tracked before and after? Without baseline brand metric data from before the campaign and a comparison condition (an audience exposed to the campaign vs. one that was not), the causal inference is not supportable.
- Step 4: Identify the measurement design that would have given the cleanest estimate. A brand-lift study — exposing one matched audience segment to the campaign and withholding it from another, then comparing both brand metrics and downstream conversion — would have isolated campaign effect from competing explanations.

**Answer:** The CEO's attribution is plausible but not supportable with the available evidence. A Q2 CAC drop is consistent with the campaign working, but it is equally consistent with at least five other explanations. Without pre-campaign brand metric baselines, a comparison condition, or a brand-lift study design, Kenji cannot accept or reject the CEO's attribution. He should note the CAC drop as an encouraging signal, identify the alternative explanations, and recommend a brand-lift study design for the next campaign.

**What to notice:** "A number moved" is evidence that something changed — not evidence of what caused the change. The causal inference requires a plausible mechanism, absence of competing explanations, and ideally a comparison condition. The attribution problem is structural, not a measurement failure.

**Exercise 4** *(Contrastive Classification)*

Below are six metrics. Classify each as a *vanity metric* or a *value metric* by applying the Farris test: "If this number rose 20% next quarter, would you do anything differently?" For each vanity classification, explain why the metric fails the test. For each value classification, state the specific action a 20% rise would prompt.

- (a) Total LinkedIn followers
- (b) Unaided brand awareness among target segment
- (c) Raw video views on product demo clips
- (d) Newsletter-to-course conversion rate (for subscribers who have been on the list 90+ days)
- (e) Raw impressions on a paid campaign
- (f) Referral rate (fraction of new customers who report hearing about the brand from another person)

*(Tests: vanity vs. value metric distinction — Farris test applied)*

**Answer:**
- (a) Vanity: total followers can be increased through tactics (follow-for-follow, giveaways, broad hashtag targeting) that have no connection to reaching the audience that matters. A 20% rise would not prompt a specific decision because the composition of those followers is unknown.
- (b) Value: a 20% rise in unaided awareness among the specific target segment would prompt a decision to hold current channel strategy, since it is working, and potentially increase investment in the channels producing that awareness lift.
- (c) Vanity: raw view counts include accidental plays, autoplay impressions, and completions by irrelevant audiences. A 20% rise would not prompt a specific product or distribution decision.
- (d) Value: a 20% rise in long-subscriber conversion would prompt an increase in content investment producing similar content and a re-examination of the conversion pathway that seems to be working.
- (e) Context-dependent: raw impressions against an undefined audience are vanity. Impressions against a defined target audience segment can be value — if the Farris test can be completed (a 20% rise against the target segment would prompt a budget reallocation decision), the metric qualifies.
- (f) Value: a 20% rise in referral rate would prompt investment in the practices that produce referrals — community building, customer success, post-purchase experience — and would confirm that the brand's organic growth mechanism is working.

**Common error:** Classifying all impressions as vanity (the chapter notes context determines whether a metric is vanity or value) or classifying all numbers as value because they sound measurable.

**Exercise 5**

Name all four documented limits of NPS covered in this chapter. For each, write one sentence stating what the limit means in practice.

*(Tests: NPS — four documented limits)*

**Answer:** (1) Single-item problem: NPS captures willingness to recommend but not why, so two customers with identical scores can have completely different underlying attitudes that a single question cannot distinguish. (2) Cultural calibration problem: scoring norms vary significantly by country (Japanese consumers tend to score lower, American consumers higher), so cross-geography NPS comparisons measure different things using the same scale. (3) Information compression problem: the conversion of a 0–10 scale to three categories (promoter, passive, detractor) discards information — a score of 6 is treated identically to a score of 0, and the category boundary at 8/9 is mathematically arbitrary. (4) Correlation problem: a rising NPS does not tell you whether recommendations are actually happening, whether they are generating new customers, or whether the rise is connected to anything the brand did — it is a lagging measure of sentiment, not a leading measure of growth.

**Common error:** Students name only the simplest limits (single-item, compression) and miss the correlation problem, which is the most consequential for how NPS is misused — as a growth forecast rather than a sentiment signal.

**Exercise 8** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

A brand tracking study for a B2B software company shows the following trend over three quarters:

| Metric | Q1 | Q2 | Q3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unaided awareness (target segment) | 11% | 13% | 15% |
| Consideration | 34% | 34% | 33% |
| Preference | 21% | 18% | 16% |
| Association: "trustworthy" | 67% | 65% | 61% |

The marketing team declares Q3 a success because awareness is rising. The head of sales is concerned. Apply the brand tracking framework to explain who is more justified in their reading of the data, and identify the most important signal in this pattern.

*(Tests: brand tracking — aided/unaided awareness, consideration, preference, and association strength interpretation)*

**Answer:** The head of sales is more justified. Rising unaided awareness is a positive signal — more of the target segment knows the company exists — but the consideration-to-preference funnel tells a more troubling story. Consideration has held flat at roughly 33%, meaning openness to trying the product has not grown despite growing awareness. Preference has declined three consecutive quarters (21% → 16%), which means the company is becoming more known but less chosen when the audience evaluates alternatives. This is the pattern the chapter describes for a brand with a conversion problem, not an awareness problem. The "trustworthy" association decline compounds this: if the association most relevant to a B2B purchase decision is eroding, the preference decline is likely to continue. Declaring success because awareness rose misreads the data by treating one positive metric in isolation.

**Common error:** Students focus on the awareness rise as definitively positive without engaging the divergence between awareness growth and preference decline. The chapter's framework specifically describes this as a pattern that reveals something in the evaluation process is failing.

**Exercise 9** *(Self-Explanation)*

In this chapter, brand-lift study design is presented as producing "the most defensible causal claim available" for measuring campaign effect. Explain in 2–3 sentences why a brand-lift study is more defensible than multi-touch attribution for measuring a brand campaign's effect. Your explanation must use the term "attribution problem" correctly and must reason from the structural differences between the two approaches, not just list their features.

*(Tests: attribution problem — brand-lift study vs. multi-touch attribution — self-explanation)*

**Answer:** A strong answer captures: multi-touch attribution tracks only the observable portion of the customer journey (clicks, visits, email opens) and attributes 100% of the credit within that observable portion — brand campaign touchpoints (a billboard seen, a long-form article read without clicking through) are invisible to it, so the attribution model systematically underweights or completely omits brand campaign effects. The attribution problem is structural: the model gives a clean-looking answer that is incomplete by design. A brand-lift study avoids this by working with a comparison condition (exposed vs. control group) — the difference in brand metrics between the two groups is the best available estimate of campaign effect, and it does not depend on tracking every individual touchpoint. The causal claim is more defensible because the study design isolates the campaign variable rather than attributing credit within a tracked subset.

**Common error:** Students describe multi-touch attribution as "less accurate" and brand-lift study as "more accurate" without explaining the structural reason — the attribution problem is not about accuracy, it is about what the model can see. A surface answer describes features rather than the structural mechanism.

**Exercise 12** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

A founder runs an AI writing tool for healthcare documentation teams. She asks an AI assistant to help her build a measurement plan. The AI produces this:

> "A good measurement plan for your brand should track: total signups, monthly active users, churn rate, NPS, social media follower growth, and brand awareness. Apply the chapter's guidance on AI-assisted measurement planning to evaluate this output. Identify specifically what the AI got right, what it got wrong, and what the founder must not accept from this response.

*(Tests: vanity vs. value metric distinction and fabricated baselines — AI+1 discipline in measurement planning)*

**Answer:**
- What the AI got right: the structural categories (signups, active users, churn rate) are reasonable starting points and not intrinsically vanity. NPS has uses as one lagging sentiment signal.

**Exercise 13** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

In Chapter 12: Content, Media, and Campaigns, the campaign brief requires a measurement section with one primary KPI per objective, set before the campaign launches. In this chapter, the attribution problem warns that a metric moving does not prove the work caused it.

A campaign manager has just launched a campaign with a single KPI: demo request conversion rate. Two months later, the conversion rate is up 18%. She says: "The KPI rose. The campaign worked. The brief's measurement plan did its job."

Apply the attribution problem framework to evaluate this conclusion. Does having a pre-specified KPI solve the attribution problem? What would need to be true for the 18% rise in conversion rate to be defensible evidence that the campaign caused it?

*This question connects the attribution problem (Ch. 13) to campaign brief measurement discipline (Ch. 12).*

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Surface: "correlation is not causation"
- Strong: explains the specific mechanism — a pre-specified KPI reduces the risk of post-hoc metric selection but does not eliminate alternative explanations
- Strong: names specific alternative explanations for the conversion rate rise and specifies what measurement design would have produced a defensible causal claim

*(Tests: attribution problem + campaign brief measurement discipline — Ch. 13 + Ch. 12)*

**Answer:** Having a pre-specified KPI is a necessary but insufficient condition for a defensible attribution. Pre-specification eliminates one threat to validity — post-hoc metric selection to find the number that shows a positive result — but it does not eliminate the structural attribution problem. Alternative explanations for the 18% conversion rate rise that the KPI pre-specification does not rule out: (1) a competitor exited the market or had a product failure; (2) the company's sales process improved independently; (3) seasonal demand patterns favor this period; (4) a pricing change made the offer easier to accept; (5) organic search ranking improved from content produced before the campaign. For the rise to be defensible evidence the campaign caused it, the manager would need either: (a) a brand-lift study with an exposed group and a matched control group showing the exposed group converted at a higher rate; or (b) a marketing-mix model that isolates campaign contribution from seasonal, competitive, and other inputs. The brief's measurement section sets up the right question — it does not answer it.

**Common error:** A surface answer says "correlation is not causation" without specifying the mechanism or the alternative explanations. A strong answer shows that pre-specified KPIs solve one problem (post-hoc selection bias) but not the structural attribution problem the chapter describes.

**Exercise 14** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

In Chapter 1: The Creative Engineer, Spence signaling theory established that the value of a signal depends on whether it is observable and verifiable by the receiver. In this chapter, brand tracking measures association strength — the degree to which target audiences connect specific attributes to a brand.

A personal brand practitioner argues: "Association tracking is unnecessary for a personal brand. If I am consistently publishing verifiable work — shipped products, cited articles, public talks — the market will track associations for me. My signals speak for themselves."

Evaluate this argument using both the Spence signaling framework and the brand tracking framework from this chapter. What does the practitioner get right? Where does the argument break down?

*This question connects association strength tracking (Ch. 13) to Spence signaling — observable, verifiable costly signals (Ch. 1).*

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Surface: "both frameworks agree that evidence matters"
- Strong: explains what brand tracking adds that Spence signaling does not — the receiver's perception, which may not match the signal sent
- Strong: identifies the specific failure mode: a practitioner can produce costly, verifiable signals that are associated with the wrong attribute in the audience's mind, and without tracking, the gap is invisible

*(Tests: association strength tracking + Spence signaling — Ch. 13 + Ch. 1)*

**Answer:** The practitioner gets Spence signaling right: consistently shipped, verifiable, costly work does create separating signals that distinguish the practitioner from low-quality competitors. The argument breaks down at the receiver's perception. Spence's framework describes the signal's properties — cost and verifiability — but does not guarantee what the receiver associates with the signal. A practitioner can produce exemplary technical work and be associated with "fast executor" rather than "deep thinker," or with "helpful to junior engineers" rather than "strategic advisor" — associations that may be inconsistent with the intended positioning. Brand tracking's association strength measurement is the feedback loop that shows whether the associations building in the audience's mind match the intended positioning. Without that tracking, the practitioner knows the signals are being sent but cannot know what they are accumulating. The Spence framework and brand tracking are complementary: Spence explains how to produce signals that work; brand tracking shows what they are communicating to the receiver.

**Common error:** A surface answer agrees with the practitioner, noting that "good work speaks for itself." A strong answer identifies the gap between signal production and receiver perception — the practitioner can be producing the right signals while accumulating the wrong associations without knowing it.
