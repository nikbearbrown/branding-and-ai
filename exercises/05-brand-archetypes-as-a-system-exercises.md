## Worked Example

**Problem:** A career development platform for software engineers is reviewing its brand. The founding team believes the brand is a Creator archetype—they emphasize building original learning paths, customized courses, and user-generated content. However, a brand audit reveals the following: their marketing copy heavily features "the fastest path to your next promotion"; their visual identity uses racing metaphors and red/orange urgency palette; their homepage CTA is "Beat the clock to your dream role." A competitor uses the Sage archetype and owns "evidence-based career intelligence." 

Apply the five-input archetype identification procedure and the three archetype tests from Chapter 5 to determine whether this brand is functioning as a Creator, and if not, what archetype it is actually expressing.

**Approach:** Apply the five inputs (tagline/visual/refusals/customer treatment/crisis behavior) to determine the expressed archetype. Then apply the three tests (authenticity/differentiation/stress).

**Answer:** The five-input procedure reveals a significant gap between the claimed archetype (Creator) and the expressed archetype.

(1) *Tagline and copy:* "Fastest path to your next promotion," "Beat the clock" — these are urgency and competition frames, not creation frames. The brand is emphasizing winning a race, not building something original.

(2) *Visual identity:* Racing metaphors and urgency palette (red/orange) signal the Hero archetype's competitive, goal-achievement orientation, not the Creator's originality and expressive commitment.

(3) *Refusals:* The brand does not appear to refuse shortcuts or "fastest path" claims — refusals would be characteristic of a Creator brand that insists on authentic development over speed.

(4) *Customer treatment:* The implicit message to users is "you are a competitor in a race" — a Hero positioning — not "you are a creator of something original."

(5) *Crisis behavior:* Not available for analysis.

Synthesis: The brand is expressing a *Hero* archetype (with competitive, achievement-focused motivation) while claiming to be a Creator. The shadow of the Hero — recklessness, the need for an enemy to beat — is latent in the "beat the clock" framing.

Three tests: Authenticity test — does the brand behave consistently with the Hero under stress? Likely yes, given the pervasive competition framing. Differentiation test — the Hero archetype in career development is crowded (many career platforms compete on "fastest path"); a Creator brand would differentiate meaningfully. Stress test — the Hero framing would hold consistently in a product delay or market downturn scenario ("we're still ahead of the competition"), while the Creator framing would create incoherence (a Creator doesn't race).

**What to notice:** The failure is not that the founding team chose the wrong archetype—it is that they claimed one archetype and expressed another. The visual identity, copy, and CTA all pulled toward Hero while the team believed they were building Creator. The five-input procedure surfaces this gap because it looks at behavior rather than intention.

## Tier 1 — Remember and Understand

### Contrastive Classification (Required)

**1.1** Two brand descriptions are provided below. For each, identify which of the three failure cases from Chapter 5 (Tropicana 2009, Gap 2010, or New Coke 1985) it most closely resembles, and explain the failure type in one sentence.

Brand A: A beloved open-source developer tool with a strong Rebel archetype—known for its irreverent documentation and anti-corporate voice—is acquired by a large enterprise software company. The new owners redesign the interface to match the parent company's clean, neutral, corporate aesthetic and remove the irreverent copy. Users immediately report that "it doesn't feel like the same tool anymore."

Brand B: A productivity app with a minimalist Sage aesthetic decides to add bold, playful illustrations and a friendly, conversational chatbot because a UX consultant recommends it will "feel more approachable." The existing user base stops recommending it; the new users gained from the redesign are more price-sensitive and churn at higher rates.

`(Tests: three archetype failure cases — category-error drift vs. ontological violation)`

**Answer:**

Brand A most closely resembles *New Coke 1985* — an ontological violation. The Rebel tool's identity was inseparable from its irreverence and anti-corporate stance; removing those elements did not update the brand, it destroyed the brand's identity. The chapter's New Coke lesson is that some brand equities are ontological — the brand *is* the thing users love, and changing it constitutes breaking the contract that the equity was built on. The parent company can own the code; it cannot easily transfer what made the code meaningful.

Brand B most closely resembles *Tropicana 2009* — category-error drift. The Sage aesthetic was part of what distinguished the app's value proposition (trust, precision, intellectual seriousness). Adding playful illustrations and a conversational chatbot shifted the brand into a different category (approachable/friendly) that attracted a different customer type with different expectations, while signaling to the existing Sage-seeking users that the brand had drifted. The new users churn more because they were attracted by the wrong signal; the original users left because the signal changed.

*Common error:* Classifying Brand A as Tropicana rather than New Coke. The key distinction is scope: Tropicana changed an expression of the brand (packaging, not formulation); New Coke changed the fundamental product that the brand was. Brand A changed the fundamental identity of the tool (the voice and stance that constituted its Rebel personality), not just a surface expression.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Three Failure Cases."

**1.2** The chapter identifies four meanings of the term "archetype" used in different contexts. A colleague uses the word "archetype" in each of the following four sentences. Identify which of the four meanings is being used in each sentence.

(a) "Every thriller novel follows the archetype of the detective who breaks the rules to find the truth."
(b) "Jung argued that the hero archetype appears spontaneously across cultures because it is a universal unconscious structure."
(c) "The Caregiver archetype is overrepresented in nursing career branding."
(d) "Our archetype is Sage — that shapes everything from our content strategy to our error messages."

`(Tests: four meanings of "archetype" — contextual identification)`

**Answer:**
(a) *Genre archetype:* The sentence describes a recurring narrative pattern in a specific genre of fiction — a structural template for stories, not a personality or brand identity.

(b) *Jungian archetype:* The sentence references Jung's original psychological concept — innate, cross-cultural patterns in the collective unconscious that shape human behavior and story.

(c) *Personality archetype:* The sentence uses archetype to describe a recurring psychological type or orientation (Caregiver) applied to people choosing a profession — a personality pattern, not a brand strategy.

(d) *Brand archetype:* The sentence uses archetype to describe a brand's strategic identity — the personality, motivation, and relationship it expresses to its audience — as a forcing function for brand decisions.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Four Meanings of Archetype."

**1.3** The chapter argues that the shadow of an archetype is "structurally related to its strength." Using the Sage archetype as an example, explain what this means and why it matters for brand risk management.

`(Tests: archetype shadow — structural relationship to strength)`

**Answer:** The Sage archetype's strength is its commitment to knowledge, evidence, and reliable truth-telling — these properties build trust and attract audiences who value intellectual rigor. The shadow of the Sage is dogmatism: the Sage's strength (unwillingness to be wrong) inverts into an inability to admit uncertainty or update in the face of new evidence. The shadow is not a separate failure mode — it is the Sage's own strength applied beyond its range of validity. For brand risk management, this means: a Sage brand that overreaches its evidence — making confident claims in domains where the evidence is thin, presenting contested findings as settled, or refusing to update after being shown to be wrong — is activating its own shadow. The brand's greatest vulnerability is internal to its strength, not external to it. Risk management for a Sage brand therefore focuses on maintaining epistemic humility: citing sources, distinguishing certainty levels, and updating publicly when new evidence emerges.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Archetype Shadows," "Sage."

**1.4** The five-input archetype identification procedure from Chapter 5 uses behavioral evidence rather than stated claims. Explain what each of the five inputs reveals about a brand's archetype that the brand's self-description cannot.

`(Tests: five-input archetype identification procedure — rationale for each input)`

**Answer:**

*Tagline and copy:* Reveals the brand's actual vocabulary and emotional frame — the words chosen under public scrutiny reflect the brand's instinctive mode of relating to its audience, which may differ from internal self-conception.

*Visual identity:* Reveals the brand's emotional register and cultural references — visual systems encode values and personality cues that audiences read intuitively, often before reading copy.

*Refusals:* Reveals what the brand will not do, even under competitive or financial pressure — refusals are the most behavioral signal of archetype because they show what the brand values when there is a cost to maintaining it.

*Customer treatment:* Reveals the relationship model the brand enacts — how the brand actually speaks to, about, and for its customers in product copy, support interactions, and community engagement, independent of how the brand describes that relationship in marketing materials.

*Crisis behavior:* Reveals the archetype under stress — the most reliable evidence of actual archetype because in crisis, the brand cannot maintain a performance and must act from its actual values. What a brand does when it is costly to maintain the archetype is more revealing than what it does when the archetype is easy to express.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Five-Input Archetype Identification Procedure."

**1.5** Apply the three archetype tests (authenticity, differentiation, stress) to a brand of your choice in the technology or career development space. For each test, identify what evidence you would look for and what a passing or failing result looks like.

`(Tests: three archetype validity tests — authenticity/differentiation/stress)`

**Answer (model — any defensible technology brand is acceptable):** Using Stripe as the case (Sage archetype, as the chapter notes in Chapter 6):

*Authenticity test:* Ask whether the brand's expressed archetype is consistent with its actual operational history. For Stripe, evidence: Stripe Press publishes serious non-fiction books on technology and economics — this is not a marketing exercise but a genuine investment in intellectual publishing. Their developer documentation reads as a technical reference, not as a marketing pitch. Authenticity passes: the Sage identity is expressed in high-cost, non-marketing channels.

*Differentiation test:* Ask whether the Sage archetype differentiates Stripe from its primary competitors in the payments market. Competing payment providers (PayPal, Square) do not consistently express a Sage identity — they emphasize accessibility, simplicity, and small-business empowerment. Differentiation passes: the Sage archetype is uncommon in the payments category and creates a distinct position for developers and technical teams.

*Stress test:* Ask whether the Sage archetype would hold consistently through a product failure, a pricing controversy, or a competitive attack. Historically, Stripe's communications during service incidents have been detailed, technically honest, and post-incident reports have included specific root causes and preventative measures — consistent with the Sage's commitment to evidence and transparency. Stress test passes: the archetype appears to hold under adversity.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Three Archetype Tests."

## Tier 2 — Apply and Analyze

### Error Analysis (Required)

**2.1** A student has written the following archetype identification for a digital health platform:

> "MindWell is a Hero archetype because it helps users overcome mental health challenges and achieve wellness goals. The hero's journey is about facing difficulties and emerging stronger—which is exactly what therapy and wellness apps help users do. The Hero archetype is appropriate because mental health is a significant challenge and overcoming it is heroic."

Identify the methodological error in this analysis. What has the student confused? Then apply the five-input procedure to produce a more defensible archetype identification. You may invent plausible observable evidence for a digital health platform.

`(Tests: archetype identification procedure — confusing what users do with what the brand is)`

**Answer — Error:** The student has repeated the same error as the Notion analysis in Chapter 1 exercises: they have identified what *users experience* when using the product (overcoming challenges, achieving wellness goals) rather than what the *brand itself* expresses as its personality and motivation. The Hero archetype describes the brand's identity — its orientation to the world, its characteristic mode of engaging with customers — not the nature of the transformation users undergo. By the student's logic, virtually every health product would be a Hero because health challenges are universally difficult and overcoming them is universally heroic.

**Corrected five-input analysis for a digital health platform (plausible evidence):**

(1) *Tagline and copy:* "We're here when you need us. No judgment, no pressure, just support." — Warmth, presence, and unconditional support framing.

(2) *Visual identity:* Soft typography, muted earth tones, imagery of people in quiet moments rather than achievement poses.

(3) *Refusals:* Does not use gamification, competition, or leaderboards — declines to add achievement dynamics that would be consistent with the Hero archetype but would undermine the safety of the space.

(4) *Customer treatment:* Matches users with therapists based on specific needs; sends check-in messages after difficult sessions; offers pause options rather than cancellation with penalties.

(5) *Crisis behavior:* (hypothetical) When a user-safety incident was reported, the platform communicated with affected users directly, acknowledged the failure, and added a safety review layer — prioritizing user safety over reputation management.

Synthesis: The expressed archetype is *Caregiver* (core motivation: protecting and nurturing others) — the platform provides safe, supportive, non-judgmental care. A Hero brand would position users as warriors; a Caregiver brand positions users as people deserving of support without conditions.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Five-Input Procedure," "Archetype System."

### Self-Explanation Exercise (Required)

**2.2** The chapter argues that an archetype functions as a "forcing function" for brand decisions because it creates "a stable set of constraints that make some decisions obvious and others unavailable." Using only the concepts from Chapter 5, explain why a well-chosen archetype produces *more* creative freedom in brand execution, not less.

`(Tests: archetype as forcing function — principled reasoning about constraint and creative latitude)`

**Answer (model):** Paradoxically, a forcing function increases effective creative freedom by eliminating decisions that would otherwise require deliberation. Without an archetype, every brand decision—the tone of an error message, the style of a partnership announcement, the visual language for a new feature launch—requires a full creative process starting from no constraint. Most of that creative process is spent on decisions that are arbitrary in the absence of a consistent identity: should this error message be formal or casual? Should this partnership announcement be serious or playful? The archetype answers all of these in advance by providing the identity from which every expression follows. A Sage brand does not deliberate about whether its error message should be playful—the archetype makes that unavailable. A Jester brand does not deliberate about whether its partnership announcement should be formal—the archetype makes formality unavailable. This elimination of arbitrary decisions is what makes creative execution faster and more internally coherent: the creative team is working within a defined personality, and within that personality they have genuine latitude for originality in expression. The archetype constrains the *category* of expression while maximizing latitude within it.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Archetype as Forcing Function."

**2.5** The chapter describes the Gap 2010 rebrand as a failure distinct from New Coke and Tropicana: Gap's failure was speed and reversibility (six days, then rolled back), but the failure also exposed that Gap did not have a clear archetype to defend. Explain in two to three sentences what it means for a brand to "not have a clear archetype to defend" and why that condition is itself a brand problem independent of the specific redesign.

`(Tests: archetype as brand asset — clarity and its absence)`

**Answer:** A brand with a clear archetype has a stable identity that generates a recognizable recognition asset—audiences know what the brand stands for and can detect when it deviates, which is what produced the backlash in New Coke and the petition in Snapchat's case. When a brand lacks a clear archetype, it has no coherent identity to defend—customers may not notice or care about the redesign change because they had no strong expectation of what the brand was in the first place. This condition is itself a brand problem because it means the brand cannot accumulate the recognition asset that Chapter 5 identifies as compounding over time: without archetype clarity, each brand expression starts from zero rather than building on established associations.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Three Failure Cases," "Gap 2010."

## Tier 3 — Analyze and Evaluate

**3.1** The chapter presents two categories of archetype failure: failures of drift (Tropicana, Gap) and failures of ontological violation (New Coke). A brand strategist argues: "The New Coke failure was just a worse version of the Tropicana failure—both were cases of changing something customers valued. The distinction between 'drift' and 'ontological violation' is academic; the practical lesson is the same: don't change things customers love." Evaluate this argument. Identify where it is correct and where the distinction matters for brand management practice.

*(References Chapter 5's three failure cases; also draws on Chapter 2's brand equity concepts.)*

`(Tests: three failure cases — evaluating the drift vs. ontological violation distinction)`

**Model answer:** The strategist is partially correct: both failure types involve changing something customers valued, and the practical warning "don't change things customers love" is derivable from both. However, the distinction matters for brand management because the *recoverable* failures and the *unrecoverable* ones require different responses. Tropicana and Gap's drift failures were recoverable: the original expression could be restored (Tropicana rolled back the packaging; Gap retreated within six days). The brand equity, while bruised, was not destroyed because the underlying identity remained intact. New Coke's ontological violation was partially unrecoverable in a different way: Coca-Cola Classic was reintroduced, but the episode permanently altered the brand's relationship with its own identity — the company now knew the brand was owned by its customers in a way it had not fully understood before. From Chapter 2's equity perspective, drift failures damage the *associations* layer of equity (what the brand is known for); ontological violations damage the *loyalty* layer (the relationship between brand and customer). These require different interventions: associations can be refreshed; loyalty once betrayed requires genuine behavioral reconstruction to rebuild. The distinction is not academic for a manager deciding how to respond to a backlash.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Three Failure Cases"; Chapter 2, "Brand Equity Layers."

**3.2** The chapter argues that an archetype's shadow is the most serious internal brand risk—more serious than external competitive threats. Using the Rebel archetype, construct the argument for why this claim is true. Then identify a real or plausible technology brand with a Rebel identity and describe a specific scenario in which the brand's shadow would constitute a more serious threat than any competitive action from a rival brand.

*(References Chapter 5's shadow discussion; also draws on Chapter 4's cultural tension lens.)*

`(Tests: archetype shadow as brand risk — argument construction with real brand application)`

**Model answer:** The shadow is the most serious internal risk because it is structurally connected to the brand's strength. The Rebel archetype's strength is its willingness to challenge, disrupt, and refuse convention—properties that attract audiences who are alienated from mainstream alternatives and generate strong loyalty from users who feel the brand speaks for their frustration with the status quo. The shadow is nihilism: disruption without a constructive alternative, rejection without a vision of what should replace what is rejected. The shadow activates precisely when the Rebel's core strength is most fully expressed—when the brand is most committed to its anti-establishment identity, it is at greatest risk of tipping into aimless provocation that destroys the thing it disrupted without building anything in its place.

Real brand application: Rust, the programming language community, has significant Rebel characteristics—it explicitly disrupts the memory-safety failures of C and C++, challenges established systems programming conventions, and has a culture of productive provocation. A competitive threat from any rival language (Go, C++23, Zig) is less dangerous than the shadow: if the Rust community begins to express its Rebel identity as unconstructive gatekeeping—rejecting legitimate contributions because they don't match an arbitrary style ideal, or fracturing the community over ideological purity rather than technical improvement—the shadow would damage the brand more than any competitor. The competitive threat is external and navigable; the shadow threat is internal and self-inflicted.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 5, "Archetype Shadows," "Rebel"; Chapter 4, "Cultural Tension Lens."

## Tier 4 — Evaluate and Create

The system has critics who argue it is too rigid (not all brands fit cleanly into 12 categories), too culturally specific (the archetypes reflect Western psychological and narrative traditions), and empirically unsupported (there is no peer-reviewed evidence that archetype-based brand strategies produce better business outcomes than non-archetype-based strategies).

Evaluate these three criticisms. For each, determine whether it is (a) a fundamental challenge that undermines the system's usefulness, (b) a legitimate limitation that should be disclosed when using the system, or (c) a misunderstanding of how the system is meant to be used.

Your response should be 500–700 words. Evaluate based on:
- Does the response engage each criticism on its strongest terms rather than dismissing it?
- Does the response distinguish between criticisms of the system's theoretical validity and criticisms of its practical utility?
- Does the response acknowledge what the system cannot do?
- Does the response make a defensible conclusion about how the system should be used given these limitations?
