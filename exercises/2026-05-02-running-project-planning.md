## Step 2 — Five Running Project Candidates

### Project Option 1: Brand Reverse-Engineering Dossier

**What it is:** Pick one existing real-world brand or AI product the learner finds interesting. Apply every chapter's lens to it across the semester. Build a 25-40 page dossier that reads like a strategic intelligence brief on the target.

**Final deliverable:** A complete brand dossier on the chosen target, covering archetype identification, build-architecture inference, brand strategy reconstruction, visual identity audit, narrative analysis, and a recommended evolution path. Publishable as a Substack post, a LinkedIn long-form, or a portfolio piece.

**Why it fits this book:** Every chapter teaches a lens. The dossier exercises the lens on a real artifact. By Chapter 12, the learner has applied all twelve lenses to one target and can defend a comprehensive reading of its strategy.

**Adaptability:**
- *Finance student:* Stripe, Robinhood, Mercury, Plaid, Brex, Wise. Apply lenses to a fintech product whose technical and brand work are both public.
- *Branding student:* Glossier, Liquid Death, Notion, Linear, Patagonia. Pick a brand-forward company.
- *Healthcare AI:* Hippocratic AI, Abridge, K Health.
- *Climate Tech:* Watershed, Tesla, Helion.

**Tool path:** Claude chat (most chapters) + Claude Project for persistent context across the semester (recommended) + Cowork for any chapters that involve reading the target's documentation/files locally.

### Project Option 2: Synthetic Startup Pitch Book

**What it is:** Design a fictional-but-realistic startup from scratch as a writing exercise. Each chapter contributes one section to a complete startup pitch book — the kind of artifact a founder would prepare before fundraising. The startup never gets built; the *thinking* gets built.

**Final deliverable:** A 40-60 page pitch book containing PRD, architecture diagrams, brand strategy, visual identity guidelines, narrative arc, portfolio site mockups, and a final 10-slide investor deck. Adaptable for portfolio inclusion ("see how I think about strategy") or for actual fundraising if the learner wants to pursue the idea.

**Why it fits this book:** Mirrors the book's structure exactly — every chapter's deliverable lands in a specific section of the pitch book. The learner *exercises* the book's frameworks on a self-chosen domain rather than just learning about them.

**Adaptability:**
- *Finance student:* an AI-powered M&A advisory tool, a personal finance assistant for first-generation investors, a fraud-detection tool for small businesses.
- *Branding student:* a consumer-AI brand-coaching tool, a portfolio-builder for designers, a brand-archetype-detection service.
- *Healthcare AI:* a triage tool for rural clinics, a medical-record summarizer for caregivers.
- *Climate Tech:* a residential energy optimizer, a carbon-footprint reporting tool for SMBs.

**Tool path:** Claude Project (strongly recommended — persistent context lets the startup concept evolve coherently across chapters) with Cowork for any file-based deliverables.

### Project Option 3: Three-Brand Comparative Audit

**What it is:** Pick three direct competitors in one industry segment. Apply each chapter's lens comparatively across all three. Build a competitive intelligence report that reveals strategic patterns no individual brand analysis would surface.

**Final deliverable:** A 30-50 page comparative audit, presented as a strategic briefing. Includes archetype matrix, architecture comparison, brand-strategy diff, visual-identity comparison, narrative analysis, and a strategic recommendation for a hypothetical fourth entrant who would enter this market.

**Why it fits this book:** Comparison exposes mechanism. A Stripe brand audit teaches you Stripe; a Stripe-vs-Plaid-vs-Adyen comparison teaches you the structural choices that produced different brands. Every chapter generates comparative data.

**Adaptability:**
- *Finance student:* Stripe vs. Adyen vs. Plaid; Robinhood vs. Public vs. Webull; Mercury vs. Brex vs. Ramp.
- *Branding student:* Glossier vs. Rare Beauty vs. Fenty; Liquid Death vs. Olipop vs. Poppi; Linear vs. Notion vs. Asana.
- *Healthcare AI:* Hippocratic AI vs. Abridge vs. Ambience.
- *Climate Tech:* Watershed vs. Persefoni vs. Sweep.

**Tool path:** Claude chat for individual analyses + Claude Project to maintain comparison frameworks across the semester + Cowork for spreadsheet-style comparison matrices and final report generation.

### Project Option 4: Self-as-Project (The Meta Track)

**What it is:** The learner *is* the running project. Every chapter produces one artifact about the learner's own emerging professional brand. By the end, the learner has not built a product or audited a target — they have built themselves.

**Final deliverable:** A complete personal-brand "system" — archetype analysis, career-positioning strategy, visual identity for the learner's name, portfolio strategy, narrative arc (origin story + thought-leadership content), and a final personal-brand pitch deck. Functions as a parallel personal track to the book's primary AI-tool track.

**Why it fits this book:** Chapter 1 already asks the learner to identify their archetype. Chapter 11 already asks them to build a portfolio. This project formalizes the personal track as a deliberate, archetype-coherent build — instead of treating personal brand as a side effect of the tool work, it becomes its own finished artifact. Strong for graduating students who need an immediate career-positioning tool.

**Adaptability:**
- *Finance student:* personal brand for an early-career analyst aiming at quantitative trading, M&A, or fintech founder paths.
- *Branding student:* personal brand for a creative technologist, design strategist, or independent consultant.
- The project automatically adapts to whoever the learner *is* — that's the point.

**Tool path:** Claude chat for reflection-heavy chapters + Claude Project (highly recommended — the persistent context becomes the learner's own evolving "self-knowledge document") + Cowork for the final assembled artifact + Claude Code for the deployed personal site (Chapter 11).

### Project Option 5: Thought-Leadership Publication Arc

**What it is:** The learner builds a published body of work — a Substack, a personal blog, a LinkedIn series — that produces one or two pieces of public-facing content per chapter. By the end of the semester, the learner has 12-14 published artifacts forming a coherent thought-leadership position aligned with their archetype and intended career direction.

**Final deliverable:** A live publication (Substack newsletter, blog, or LinkedIn collection) with 12+ posts, an "About" page, a clear archetype-aligned voice, and demonstrable growth (subscribers, followers, engagement). Each chapter's artifact is a finished post, not a draft.

**Why it fits this book:** Brand strategy without published work is theoretical. This project forces the learner to *practice* the chapters in public, which compounds the personal-brand arc the book is already teaching. Especially strong for Sage, Hero, and Magician archetypes whose careers benefit most from public output.

**Adaptability:**
- *Finance student:* Substack on AI in financial decision-making, with each chapter contributing one analysis or framework post.
- *Branding student:* a public design-and-strategy newsletter; chapters contribute case studies, framework explainers, and opinion pieces.
- *Healthcare AI:* a publication on AI's role in patient experience or clinical workflow.
- *Climate Tech:* analysis of how climate-tech brands communicate (or fail to) with their stakeholders.

**Tool path:** Claude chat for drafting + Claude Project for ongoing voice consistency + Cowork for managing publishing workflow + Claude Code optional for the publication's tech stack if self-hosted.
