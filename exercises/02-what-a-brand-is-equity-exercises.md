## Worked Example

**Problem:** A technology education startup, TechPath, offers online courses on machine learning. TechPath has 90% unaided brand awareness among ML engineers in North America and overwhelmingly positive reviews. However, when TechPath raises its annual subscription price by 30%, renewal rates drop by 25%. A competitor, DeepLearn, has 45% unaided awareness but only 8% subscription cancellations when it raises prices by the same 30%. Using Aaker's four dimensions of brand equity, explain which company has stronger brand equity and why the awareness gap does not resolve the question.

**Approach:** Apply Aaker's four dimensions—awareness, perceived quality, associations, and loyalty—to both companies. Identify which dimension is driving the behavioral difference (the renewal-rate gap), and explain why high scores on awareness alone are insufficient evidence of strong brand equity.

**Answer:** Aaker's four dimensions together constitute brand equity; no single dimension is sufficient. TechPath scores high on awareness (90% unaided) and likely on perceived quality (positive reviews), but its loyalty score—measured by willingness to pay through a price increase—is weak: a 25% cancellation rate at +30% pricing indicates customers have not deeply committed to the brand. DeepLearn's equity is stronger on the dimension that matters most for business value: loyalty. Its 8% cancellation rate at the same price increase indicates that subscribers stay because they value the brand relationship, not merely because they are aware of it or have had positive experiences. Awareness and quality reviews are necessary conditions for equity building but are not themselves equity. The equity gap shows up in the moment of friction—the price increase—when customers decide whether the brand is worth the additional cost. DeepLearn's loyal base demonstrates that its associations and customer relationship are stronger than TechPath's despite lower awareness.

**What to notice:** Brand equity is revealed under stress (price increase, competitive alternative, public criticism), not under favorable conditions. A company can have high awareness and positive reviews—what Keller would call salience and judgments—but weak equity if those do not translate into the financial behaviors (premium pricing, retention, forgiveness of errors) that constitute equity as an asset.

## Tier 1 — Remember and Understand

### Contrastive Classification (Required)

**1.1** Two statements about brand equity appear below. One describes *brand image* (how external audiences currently perceive the brand). The other describes *brand identity* (how the brand intends to be perceived, as designed by the organization). Classify each statement and explain the classification in one sentence.

Statement A: "Our brand promises to be the most rigorous and evidence-based voice in the AI policy space. We speak precisely, cite sources, and never overstate what the research shows."

Statement B: "When we survey ML researchers, they consistently describe our brand as 'cautious,' 'trustworthy,' and 'dry'—but rarely as 'innovative' or 'cutting-edge.'"

`(Tests: brand identity vs. brand image distinction)`

**Answer:** Statement A is *brand identity*: it describes how the brand *intends* to be perceived—a set of designed commitments about voice, evidence standards, and communication style. Statement B is *brand image*: it describes how external audiences *actually* perceive the brand based on their experience, using the specific words ("cautious," "trustworthy," "dry") that researchers apply to it unsolicited. The gap between A and B is the *brand equity gap*: the brand intends innovation alongside rigor, but its audience perceives rigor without innovation.

*Common error:* Students often classify A as image because it describes the brand in positive terms, and image is perceived as the "public" dimension. The distinction is who is speaking: identity is designed by the organization; image is perceived by the audience.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "Brand Identity vs. Brand Image vs. Brand Equity."

**1.2** Place the following brand behaviors at the correct layer of Keller's CBBE pyramid: (a) a customer who has heard of a software brand but cannot name a single product feature; (b) a customer who has used the product and considers it excellent at its core task; (c) a customer who actively advocates for the brand in online communities and considers it part of their professional identity.

`(Tests: Keller CBBE pyramid — layer identification)`

**Answer:** (a) Salience — the customer has basic brand awareness (they have heard of it) but nothing deeper has been established. (b) Judgments (and possibly Performance) — the customer has used the product and formed a positive quality evaluation based on direct experience. (c) Resonance — the customer has integrated the brand into their identity and advocates actively; resonance is the apex of the pyramid, representing the deepest and most commercially valuable relationship.

*Common error:* Students often classify (b) at "resonance" because "excellent at its core task" sounds like strong sentiment. Resonance requires identity-integration and advocacy, not just a positive quality judgment.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "Keller CBBE Pyramid."

**1.3** Name the three types of equity outcomes the chapter identifies—the three ways brand equity manifests as a measurable business asset—and give one specific example of each that does not appear in the chapter.

`(Tests: brand equity as asset — three manifestations)`

**Answer:** The three outcomes are: (1) *Price premium*—customers pay more for the branded version than for an equivalent unbranded or lower-equity alternative. Example: a developer pays $20/month more for a coding assistant from a brand they trust deeply, even when a comparable-feature competitor offers the same capability at lower cost. (2) *Retention*—customers continue purchasing from the brand at higher rates, reducing customer acquisition costs. Example: a cloud infrastructure provider retains customers during a service outage because accumulated trust overrides the rational case for switching. (3) *Forgiveness*—customers extend goodwill during failures or controversies, moderating reputational damage. Example: a popular developer tool that introduces a controversial pricing change loses fewer subscribers than an equivalent tool with lower equity.

*Common error:* Listing awareness, quality, and loyalty from Aaker's dimensions instead of the three manifestations of equity as a business asset. These are different frameworks answering different questions.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "Brand Equity as an Asset."

**1.4** Interbrand, Kantar BrandZ, and Brand Finance use different methodologies to value brand equity. Identify one dimension on which the three methods differ and explain the practical consequence of that difference for a company trying to use brand valuation in a contract or transaction.

`(Tests: brand equity valuation methods — methodological differences and ISO 10668)`

**Answer:** The methods differ most consequentially on what constitutes the *brand's contribution* to revenue: Interbrand separates brand contribution from non-brand drivers using a brand strength score; Kantar BrandZ measures consumer "voltage" (the premium attributable to the brand in customer choice); Brand Finance uses a royalty relief method that calculates what the brand would cost to license. This disagreement means the same brand can produce substantially different valuations across the three methods—a gap that matters significantly in M&A transactions, licensing negotiations, or legal disputes where the brand is being transferred or valued as collateral. ISO 10668 (the international standard for monetary brand valuation) exists precisely to provide a common methodology framework when brand values must be auditable and consistent, rather than proprietary.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "Brand Valuation Methods."

**1.5** The chapter introduces the equity audit as a pattern for diagnosing the gap between brand identity and brand image. Name the four elements of an equity audit as the chapter describes them.

`(Tests: equity audit pattern — four elements)`

**Answer:** The four elements are: (1) brand identity as designed (the intended positioning, promise, and associations); (2) brand image as perceived (how the audience actually describes the brand, measured through surveys, interviews, or reviews); (3) the gap between identity and image (where they diverge, in what direction, and by how much); (4) the equity lever (what specific brand activity could close the gap—what needs to be changed, reinforced, or dropped to bring image into alignment with identity).

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "The Equity Audit."

## Tier 2 — Apply and Analyze

### Error Analysis (Required)

**2.1** A brand manager at a fintech company presents the following equity analysis to her leadership team:

> "Our brand equity is strong. We have 78% aided awareness among our target segment and our NPS is +42, which is excellent for our category. Our equity is therefore high on awareness, judgments, and feelings in the Keller pyramid. The brand needs no significant investment this quarter."

Identify the specific methodological errors in this analysis (there are at least two). Then produce a corrected analysis using the same data points appropriately.

`(Tests: CBBE pyramid — misapplication; brand equity as asset — confusion with sentiment metrics)`

**Answer — Errors:**

Error 1: *Aided awareness is not the same as the CBBE pyramid's "salience" layer.* Aided awareness (the percentage who recognize the brand when prompted) is a much weaker signal than unaided awareness or top-of-mind awareness. The manager has used a softer metric to claim the hardest-to-build layer without noting the distinction.

Error 2: *NPS is a single behavioral intention metric, not a measure of equity.* The chapter notes that NPS compression (all brands scoring within a narrow range) and cultural calibration issues mean NPS scores cannot be directly compared across categories or reliably mapped to equity levels. An NPS of +42 may be excellent for this category but it does not tell us whether customers are exhibiting the three equity outcomes—premium willingness, retention, or forgiveness—that constitute equity as a measurable asset.

Error 3 (implicit): *The analysis claims equity at the "feelings" layer on the basis of NPS.* NPS measures likelihood to recommend, which is a behavior at the resonance level—but the measurement is hypothetical, not observed. Resonance requires demonstrated behavior (actual advocacy, identity integration), not a stated likelihood.

**Corrected analysis:** "Our 78% aided awareness indicates our brand is recognized when prompted, though we do not yet have unaided awareness data to assess true salience. Our NPS of +42 is encouraging and suggests positive judgments, but we should not treat this as a measure of brand equity. To assess our equity as a business asset, we need to measure: (1) whether customers pay a price premium relative to competitors; (2) what our renewal/retention rate looks like compared to category average; (3) how our customer response compares to our competitors' after our last service incident. These are the equity outcomes the investment decision should be based on."

*Common error:* Students accept the aided awareness number at face value and only critique the NPS application, missing the awareness-quality issue.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "Keller CBBE Pyramid," "Brand Equity as Asset."

### Self-Explanation Exercise (Required)

**2.2** The chapter argues that brand equity is "revealed under adversity, not under favorable conditions." Using only the concepts and vocabulary from Chapter 2, explain *why* this must be true from the structural logic of equity as an asset. Your explanation should reason from the definition of equity and its three manifestations—do not simply assert that the chapter says this.

`(Tests: brand equity as asset — principled reasoning about when equity is measurable)`

**Answer (model):** Brand equity is defined as the incremental value that attaches to a product or service *because of the brand*—not because of the product's features or the prevailing market conditions. Under favorable conditions (no competing alternatives, no price increases, no product failures), customers continue purchasing for reasons that may have nothing to do with brand equity: convenience, inertia, lack of alternatives. The three manifestations of equity—premium willingness, retention, and forgiveness—are only observable as *distinguishable from baseline* in conditions of friction. A customer's willingness to pay a premium becomes visible when an unbranded alternative is offered at lower cost. Retention reveals itself when a competitor emerges or a subscription renewal decision arises. Forgiveness appears when the brand fails and the customer must decide whether to stay or leave. Under adversity, everything other than brand equity is controlled away, leaving the equity itself as the variable that explains behavioral differences. Under favorable conditions, no such isolation is possible.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "Brand Equity as Asset," "Three Manifestations."

### Cumulative Exercise (Required — integrates Chapter 1 concepts)

**2.3** In Chapter 1, Spence's signaling theory explains why costly, verifiable signals separate high-quality candidates from low-quality ones in a market. Apply this framework to brand equity: argue that strong brand equity functions as a separating signal in a product market, and identify which of Aaker's four dimensions is most analogous to the "costly signal" in the Spence model. Reference both Chapter 1's signaling framework and Chapter 2's brand equity framework explicitly.

`(Tests: brand equity as market signal — integration of Spence signaling theory [Chapter 1] with Aaker's four dimensions [Chapter 2])`

**Answer (model):** Brand equity functions as a separating signal in the Spence sense because it is costly to accumulate and verifiable by buyers through their direct experience. Of Aaker's four dimensions, *perceived quality* is most analogous to the costly signal: unlike awareness (which can be purchased through advertising) or associations (which can be manufactured through PR), perceived quality is built through consistent product performance over time—a cost that low-quality competitors would not rationally pay because their product would not sustain the quality reputation even if they tried. A customer who has experienced a high-quality product repeatedly has verified the signal through direct observation; they cannot be fooled by a competitor's awareness spend. The Cadbury case (Chapter 2) illustrates the point: Kraft acquired Cadbury partly to access the brand equity signal, which they could not replicate from scratch because the signal was built through over a century of product consistency. Awareness can be manufactured; loyalty based on experienced quality cannot.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "Aaker's Four Dimensions"; Chapter 1, "Spence Signaling Mechanism."

**2.5** The Harley-Davidson HOG (Harley Owners Group) is cited in the chapter as an example of the resonance layer of the CBBE pyramid. Explain in two sentences what distinguishes resonance from loyalty as Keller defines the two concepts, using Harley-Davidson as the illustration.

`(Tests: CBBE pyramid — resonance vs. loyalty distinction)`

**Answer:** Loyalty in Keller's framework is repeated purchase behavior driven by preference and habit, which is present at the lower levels of the pyramid; resonance is the apex state where the brand becomes part of the customer's identity and generates active community engagement and advocacy beyond purchase. Harley-Davidson's HOG community illustrates resonance because members do not merely buy motorcycles repeatedly (loyalty); they organize their social lives, travel, and self-concept around the brand identity—the brand has been incorporated into who they are, which is what Keller means by resonance.

## Tier 3 — Analyze and Evaluate

**3.1** The chapter describes the Cadbury/Kraft acquisition as a case study in brand equity as an asset. Analyze the acquisition from two angles: (a) what the acquisition reveals about how brand equity is valued in M&A transactions; (b) what risks the acquiring company takes on when the acquired brand equity was built in a specific cultural context. Use the chapter's valuation framework and at least one concept from Aaker's four dimensions.

*(References Chapter 2's Cadbury case, valuation methods, and Aaker's framework.)*

`(Tests: brand equity valuation, Aaker's dimensions, cross-cultural brand equity — multi-angle analysis)`

**Model answer:** (a) The Cadbury acquisition reveals that brand equity commands a substantial premium over book value in M&A: Kraft paid approximately £11.5 billion, far above Cadbury's tangible asset value, explicitly because of the brand equity—the loyalty, perceived quality, and associations built over more than a century. This is the royalty-relief logic of brand finance: the acquirer is paying for the right to use a name that will generate incremental revenue beyond what an unbranded chocolate product would earn. Valuation methods (Interbrand, Brand Finance, Kantar) would give different numbers, which is why ISO 10668 exists—to provide auditable standards when equity must be formally valued. (b) Cadbury's equity was built in a specific cultural context (British confectionery, associated with quality manufacturing and charitable heritage under the Quaker Cadbury family). The associations dimension of Aaker's framework means the brand's meaning is anchored to cultural and historical content that Kraft, as an American conglomerate, did not share and could not natively maintain. The risk: post-acquisition decisions that conflict with those associations (moving production, reformulating recipes, reducing quality for margin) erode the equity that was the acquisition's primary justification.

*Common error:* Describing the Cadbury case as simply "Kraft bought Cadbury" without engaging the equity premium logic or the associations risk.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "Cadbury/Kraft Case," valuation methods, Aaker's dimensions.

**3.2** A student argues: "The three manifestations of brand equity—price premium, retention, and forgiveness—are really the same thing measured at different moments. They all just reflect how much a customer trusts the brand." Evaluate this argument: is it essentially correct, partially correct, or wrong? Provide at least two pieces of evidence from the chapter's framework to support your evaluation.

*(References Chapter 2, brand equity manifestations and CBBE pyramid.)*

`(Tests: brand equity manifestations — distinguishing the three outcomes and their drivers)`

**Model answer:** The argument is partially correct but collapses important distinctions. It is correct that trust underlies all three outcomes—customers who pay a premium, stay through adversity, and forgive failures do so because their relationship with the brand includes a trust element. However, the three manifestations are not identical: they are driven by different layers of the CBBE pyramid and are detectable at different points of friction. Price premium is driven primarily by the quality/imagery/associations layers (the customer believes the branded version is worth more); retention is driven primarily by loyalty (habit and relationship); forgiveness is driven primarily by resonance (identity integration and emotional connection). A brand can have strong price-premium dynamics (high perceived quality) but weak forgiveness (no resonance layer)—if a customer values the product but has not integrated the brand into their identity, they will pay a premium but leave after a significant failure. The Harley-Davidson case illustrates the reverse: strong forgiveness and retention (resonance-layer equity) without necessarily the highest price premium in the motorcycle market.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 2, "Brand Equity Manifestations," "Keller CBBE Pyramid."
