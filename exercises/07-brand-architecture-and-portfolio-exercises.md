## Learning Objectives

- Identify and distinguish the five structural positions on the Aaker-Joachimsthaler Brand Relationship Spectrum — from branded house to house of brands — and explain the equity and risk trade-offs of each
- Explain how sub-brands and endorsed brands differ in their relationship to the master brand, including how equity flows and dependency is structured
- Analyze the conditions under which brand extension (line or category) strengthens versus dilutes the parent brand
- Evaluate co-branding and ingredient branding partnerships for symmetric risk and mutual equity benefit
- Apply portfolio pruning discipline: identify redundant or dilutive offerings and justify their removal

*Every exercise below maps to at least one of these objectives. The `(Tests: ...)` tag on each exercise identifies which one(s) by full concept name.*

## Worked Example

*Study this example before attempting Tier 1. After reading it, close it and try to recall the key steps from memory before moving on.*

**Problem:** Amara runs a freelance brand with three offerings: a weekly newsletter on AI for non-technical marketing professionals, a paid Notion template kit for marketing workflows, and a consulting service helping enterprise teams implement AI tools. She is considering launching a fourth offering: a six-week cohort course on AI marketing strategy at $1,500 per seat. How should she map this portfolio on the Brand Relationship Spectrum, and what architecture should she use for the new course?

**Approach:**
- Step 1: Identify the master brand (Amara's professional identity) and list every offering's audience, associations, and failure modes.
- Step 2: Check audience correlation — do the newsletter readers, template buyers, consulting clients, and potential course students substantially overlap, or are they different people at different stages?
- Step 3: Check risk correlation — if the consulting practice were publicly associated with a client failure, would that damage the newsletter and template sales?
- Step 4: Apply the spectrum logic: if audiences overlap and risks are correlated, concentrate equity under the branded house. If audiences diverge significantly, consider sub-brand or endorsed-brand separation.
- Step 5: Name the architecture decision and state the rationale explicitly.

**Answer:** The newsletter, templates, and consulting practice all serve marketing professionals at different engagement depths — the newsletter is awareness, the templates are DIY application, and consulting is high-touch implementation. The audiences are substantially the same people at different stages. This suggests a branded house model: all offerings under Amara's master brand, with the offerings named as sub-brands (Amara's AI Newsletter, Amara's Workflow Templates, Amara's Consulting). The new cohort course fits the same audience profile and should launch as a sub-brand (Amara's AI Strategy Cohort). The risk correlation is a consideration: a consulting failure could touch the course's reputation since both involve Amara's expertise directly. But the equity benefit of the master brand's accumulated trust outweighs this risk as long as Amara maintains quality standards across all four offerings.

**What to notice:** The architecture decision is driven first by audience correlation, then by risk correlation — not by what "sounds good." The branded house is not chosen because it is simpler; it is chosen because the audiences are substantively the same people at different points in their journey with Amara's brand.

**Exercise 1**

In your own words, explain the difference between a **branded house** and a **house of brands** as introduced in this chapter. Your explanation must name one trade-off each structure is making — not just what each structure looks like.

*(Tests: Brand Relationship Spectrum — branded house vs. house of brands trade-offs)*

**Answer:** A branded house uses one master brand name across all offerings, concentrating equity so each new product inherits the parent's trust — but coupling all risk, so a failure in one product damages the whole portfolio. A house of brands uses independent brand names under a largely invisible parent, isolating risk so a crisis in one brand does not propagate — but requiring each brand to be built from scratch at full investment cost.

**Common error:** Students often describe the structural difference (one name vs. many names) without naming the underlying trade-off (shared equity vs. risk containment). A strong answer explains why each structure is chosen, not just what it looks like.

*Chapter reference:* "The Relationship Spectrum"

**Exercise 2**

True / False: When Samsung's Galaxy Note 7 batteries caught fire in 2016, only the Note product line suffered brand damage — the Samsung master brand was largely unaffected because the Note is a distinct product.

*Explain your reasoning in one sentence:*

*(Tests: branded house — shared equity as shared risk)*

**Answer:** False. Because Samsung operates a branded house — every product carries the Samsung name — the Galaxy Note 7 crisis propagated across the entire portfolio. Every Samsung product was temporarily associated with the failure, demonstrating the structural vulnerability of the branded house: shared equity is shared risk.

**Common error:** Students who focus on the Note 7 as a "separate product line" miss the structural point. The Samsung name on every product is the mechanism by which risk spreads. Brand architecture, not product distinction, determines how damage travels.

*Chapter reference:* "The Relationship Spectrum"

**Exercise 3**

In your own words, explain what a **sub-brand** is and how it differs from an **endorsed brand**. Use one example of each that is not from the chapter.

*(Tests: sub-brand vs. endorsed brand — structure and equity flow)*

**Answer:** A sub-brand attaches a qualifier to a master brand name — the master brand leads and the sub-brand borrows almost all of its equity from the parent (e.g., Google Maps — Maps qualifies Google, which leads). An endorsed brand has the child brand leading with the parent appearing as a trust signal, typically in a "by" or "from" construction (e.g., PlayStation by Sony — PlayStation leads, Sony endorses). In a sub-brand, the child brand has little independent identity; in an endorsed brand, the child carries its own identity and targets its own audience.

**Common error:** Students frequently reverse the relationship — saying the child leads in a sub-brand or that the parent leads in an endorsed brand. The key distinguishing test is orientation: which brand name appears first and largest?

*Chapter reference:* "Sub-brands and Endorsements"

**Exercise 4** *(Contrastive Classification)*

Classify each of the following brand relationships as either a **sub-brand**, an **endorsed brand**, or a **house-of-brands** structure, and explain the distinguishing feature in one sentence for each.

- (a) **Courtyard by Marriott** — the Courtyard name is prominent; "by Marriott" appears as a smaller credential below.
- (b) **Amazon Echo** — "Echo" is the product name; Amazon appears with equal prominence and the product is clearly presented as an Amazon product.
- (c) **Dove** (Unilever) — the Dove name is fully prominent on packaging; Unilever appears in fine print only, if at all.
- (d) **FedEx Ground** — Ground describes a service; FedEx is the name that does all the heavy lifting.

*(Tests: sub-brand vs. endorsed brand vs. house of brands — contrastive classification)*

**Answer:**
- (a) Endorsed brand: Courtyard leads; Marriott provides the trust credential in a subordinate "by" construction.
- (b) Sub-brand: Amazon leads; Echo is the qualifier that names a specific product in the Amazon ecosystem.
- (c) House of brands: Dove is fully independent in consumer perception; Unilever is invisible.
- (d) Sub-brand: FedEx leads; Ground qualifies a service variant within the FedEx branded house.

**Common error:** Students often classify Amazon Echo as an endorsed brand because "Echo" appears as a distinct name. The test is not whether the child has its own name — it is which brand leads in consumer perception. Echo is firmly within the Amazon ecosystem; Amazon is not a subordinate trust signal.

*Chapter reference:* "Sub-brands and Endorsements"

**Exercise 5**

Explain the difference between a **line extension** and a **category extension**, using one example of each from outside the chapter. Then name one risk that applies more acutely to category extensions than to line extensions.

*(Tests: brand extension — line extension vs. category extension and associated risk)*

**Answer:** A line extension adds a new variant within the same category — for example, Oreo Double Stuf (adding a filling variant to the existing cookie line). A category extension crosses into a new category entirely — for example, Virgin expanding from music retail into airlines and financial services. Category extensions carry higher dilution risk: each new category adds something to what the brand name means and potentially subtracts something specific, diffusing the core associations that made the brand legible. Line extensions stay within the category where associations are already established, so the dilution risk is lower.

**Common error:** Students often conflate extension risk with newness — assuming any new product is risky. The risk mechanism is association dilution, which is much higher when the extension crosses into a category with different skill requirements, audiences, and quality signals.

*Chapter reference:* "Extension and Its Risk"

**Exercise 6**

The chapter argues that **portfolio pruning** — removing brands — usually increases total portfolio value. This runs against the intuition that more brands means more coverage. In two to three sentences, explain the mechanism by which pruning increases value rather than destroying it.

*(Tests: portfolio pruning — concentration mechanism)*

**Answer:** When a company has more brands than it can actively invest in, the under-invested brands free-ride on equity that is slowly depreciating without maintenance — diluting the value of the whole portfolio without contributing growth. Pruning redirects marketing budget, organizational attention, and executive decision-making capacity toward fewer brands, allowing equity to compound in those survivors instead of spreading thin across redundant offerings. Unilever's reduction from approximately 1,600 to 400 brands demonstrated this: revenue per brand increased because the survivors received concentrated investment.

**Common error:** Students often confuse "walking away from equity" (the counterargument) with the chapter's actual mechanism. The pruning argument is not that the pruned brands had no equity — it is that the equity was depreciating from neglect and competing with surviving brands for the same audiences.

*Chapter reference:* "Pruning Is Strategy"

**Exercise 7** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

Kenji is a software engineer who has built: a personal blog on system architecture, an open-source CLI tool that has accumulated 2,000 GitHub stars from practicing engineers, a freelance consulting practice serving small startups, and a paid course on backend architecture patterns targeted at mid-career engineers.

Map Kenji's portfolio on the Aaker-Joachimsthaler Brand Relationship Spectrum. For each offering, state: (1) its relationship to Kenji's master brand (branded house, sub-brand, endorsed, or independent); (2) what equity it borrows from the master brand; and (3) the most significant overlap risk it creates with any other offering. Then name one offering that is a candidate for pruning or repositioning, and explain the rationale.

*(Tests: Brand Relationship Spectrum — portfolio mapping and architecture decision)*

**Answer:** The blog, consulting practice, and course all serve overlapping audiences (practicing engineers interested in system architecture) at different engagement depths, and all depend on the same equity asset: Kenji's demonstrated expertise in backend architecture. These should run as sub-brands under Kenji's master brand.

The CLI tool is a different case: its 2,000-star audience is practicing engineers who may never encounter the blog or course, and the tool's associations (code quality, API design, technical depth) are different from the consulting and course positioning (teaching, transformation). This is a strong candidate for endorsed-brand or independent treatment — "ArchCLI, by Kenji" — so it can build its own reputation among contributors and users who care about the tool's specific utility without necessarily following Kenji as a thought leader.

Pruning candidate: if the blog and course are targeting the same audience with similar content at similar depths, the blog may be redundant with the course's marketing content. The blog earns its place only if it builds distinct associations (thought leadership, depth of analysis) the course cannot replicate.

**Common error:** Students often give every offering the same architecture without considering audience and risk correlation. The key questions from the chapter — how correlated are the audiences? how correlated is the risk? — must drive the decision.

*Chapter reference:* "Worked Example: Map Your Portfolio"

**Exercise 8** *(Error Analysis)*

> The following brand architecture argument was submitted by a student. It contains a conceptual error.

A classmate has written:
> "Marriott uses a house-of-brands strategy because they have so many different hotel brands — Ritz-Carlton, Courtyard, Fairfield, Aloft, W Hotels. Each brand has its own identity and targets a different customer segment. This proves Marriott is a house of brands just like P&G."

1. Identify exactly where the error occurs.
2. Explain the misconception — what did the student misunderstand about the house-of-brands structure?
3. Write a corrected version of the argument.

*(Tests: house of brands vs. endorsed brand — architecture classification misconception)*

**Answer:**
1. The error occurs in the conclusion: "This proves Marriott is a house of brands just like P&G."
2. The misconception is equating multiple distinct sub-brands with a house-of-brands structure. The house-of-brands model requires that the parent company be largely invisible to consumers — P&G's name does not appear on Tide or Pampers packaging in any prominent way. Marriott's brands, by contrast, frequently use endorsed-brand constructions ("Courtyard by Marriott," "Fairfield by Marriott") where the Marriott name appears explicitly as a credential. This is not a house of brands; it is an endorsed-brand portfolio where the parent is present and deliberate. Even the Ritz-Carlton, which does not carry "by Marriott" in its name, is publicly known to be a Marriott property. The distinguishing feature of a house of brands is parent invisibility, not mere brand plurality.
3. Corrected: Marriott manages a complex portfolio that spans from endorsed-brand structures (Courtyard by Marriott) to nearly-independent luxury brands (Ritz-Carlton), but it is not a house of brands in the P&G sense. The Marriott name appears as an active endorser across much of the portfolio, providing a trust signal that helps customers calibrate quality expectations. P&G, by contrast, keeps the parent invisible so each brand can target its audience without the parent's associations contaminating or restricting the brand's positioning.

**Common error:** Treating any portfolio with multiple distinct brands as a "house of brands." The operative definition requires parent invisibility, not just brand plurality.

*Chapter reference:* "Sub-brands and Endorsements"

**Exercise 10** *(Self-Explanation)*

In this chapter, the sub-brand structure is distinguished from the endorsed-brand structure partly by which direction equity flows. Explain in 2–3 sentences why an endorsed brand is described as the *reverse orientation* of a sub-brand. Your explanation must use the term **equity** correctly and must explain what "reverse" specifically means in this context.

*(Tests: sub-brand vs. endorsed brand — equity flow direction — self-explanation)*

**Answer:** In a sub-brand, equity flows almost entirely from the master brand to the child: the master brand leads, and the sub-brand borrows its credibility, trust, and associations from the parent. In an endorsed brand, the orientation is reversed: the child brand leads with its own identity and its own associations, and the parent appears in a secondary position to provide a trust credential — passing a limited form of credibility upward into the child's consumer relationship rather than defining it. The "reverse" refers to which brand does the identity-building (the child, in an endorsed structure) versus which brand is relied upon for borrowed equity (the parent, in a sub-brand structure).

**Common error:** Students often say both structures "share equity" without explaining that the directionality differs. A strong answer names which brand leads in each structure and why that orientation determines which direction equity flows.

*Chapter reference:* "Sub-brands and Endorsements"

**Exercise 11** *(Cumulative — prior chapter concept)*

In Chapter 6 (Brand Strategy and Positioning), you learned that a brand's positioning must answer to a specific audience and a specific competitive context. In this chapter, you are working with the extension risk decision — whether a brand extension will strengthen or dilute the parent brand.

Priya manages a project management software brand whose positioning is: "For distributed engineering teams who need to coordinate without adding process overhead." The brand is considering two extensions: (a) a time-tracking feature add-on for the same engineering teams; (b) a project management tool aimed at creative agency teams.

Using the chapter's two extension-risk questions (relevance and reinforcement) AND the positioning logic from Chapter 6, analyze which extension is the stronger move. How does the audience specificity in the positioning affect the extension risk analysis?

*(Tests: brand extension — relevance and reinforcement criteria + brand positioning — audience specificity — interleaved)*

**Answer:** The time-tracking add-on (a) is the stronger extension. Relevance: it serves the same audience (distributed engineering teams) with a capability that directly addresses their coordination challenge, so the extension borrows equity credibly — the brand's proven understanding of engineering team workflow is directly applicable. Reinforcement: a time-tracking tool for engineering teams deepens the brand's association with engineering-team productivity rather than diffusing it. The positioning from Chapter 6 supports this: the audience specificity ("distributed engineering teams") is the very quality that makes the extension coherent, because the add-on serves the same specific audience in the same context.

The creative agency tool (b) is the riskier extension. Relevance: creative agencies have different workflow structures, different process expectations, and different vocabulary than engineering teams. The brand's associations with engineering-team coordination do not transfer credibly to an entirely different professional context. Reinforcement: launching a creative-agency tool would make the brand more diffuse — it would no longer clearly mean "for engineering teams" because it now also means "for creative teams." The positioning specificity from Chapter 6 is an asset for coherent extensions and a constraint on divergent ones.

**Common error:** Students often evaluate the extensions based on feature overlap alone ("both are project management tools") without applying the audience specificity from the positioning. The chapter's extension test requires relevance to the parent brand's associations, and those associations are built around a specific audience.

**Exercise 12** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

Supreme and Louis Vuitton co-branded a limited-edition collection in 2017. BMW and Louis Vuitton co-designed a luggage set sized for the i8. 

For each partnership:
1. Identify what each brand gains from the co-branding arrangement.
2. Identify what risk each party takes on.
3. Evaluate whether the co-branding "works" — whether the two brands reinforce each other's positioning or create a tension.

*(Tests: co-branding — mutual equity benefit and symmetric risk analysis)*

**Answer:**
Supreme × Louis Vuitton: Supreme gains access to LV's luxury positioning and global distribution awareness — the collaboration elevates Supreme from streetwear to luxury-adjacent. LV gains cultural credibility with a younger, streetwear-aligned audience and a hype-driven scarcity mechanism that LV alone cannot generate. Both risk: if Supreme's street-culture associations read as inconsistent with LV's craftsmanship-and-heritage positioning, the luxury associations could be diluted (risk to LV) and Supreme's counter-cultural edge could be softened by luxury association (risk to Supreme). The partnership works because both brands reinforce a shared positioning around exclusivity and cultural authority — just from opposite directions.

BMW × Louis Vuitton: BMW gains LV's craftsmanship and refined-taste associations, which complement BMW's precision-engineering positioning. LV gains access to BMW's engineering-precision associations, which complement LV's craftsmanship narrative. Both risk their partner's future crises — a BMW safety recall or an LV counterfeit scandal touches the other party. The partnership works because precision engineering and refined craftsmanship are adjacent positioning territories that amplify each other.

**Common error:** Students analyze only one party's perspective. The chapter is explicit that co-branding is symmetric: both parties gain and both parties take on risk. A one-sided analysis misses the structural point.

*Chapter reference:* "Co-branding and Ingredients"

**Exercise 13** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

Intel's "Intel Inside" campaign made a component-manufacturer brand visible to consumers who had no natural touchpoint with Intel itself. Gore-Tex tags on outerwear signal a specific performance property the garment brand may not be able to claim independently.

Describe **ingredient branding** as introduced in this chapter and explain the two conditions under which it works. Then identify a technology product you use that could plausibly be strengthened by ingredient branding — name the component brand that would be featured and explain why the conditions would be met.

*(Tests: ingredient branding — conditions for effectiveness)*

**Answer:** Ingredient branding makes a component brand visible inside a finished product — the component has consumer-facing associations that the product manufacturer benefits from displaying. The two conditions: (1) the component must have associations that the finished product cannot independently claim (Gore-Tex signals waterproofing and breathability that a generic "waterproof jacket" label does not); (2) consumers must be sophisticated enough to know what the component does — the signal only works if the audience recognizes the component brand's meaning.

Student responses will vary by technology product. A strong answer names a specific component (e.g., a display manufactured by Samsung in a Dell laptop; ARM architecture in a smartphone), explains the specific associations the component carries (performance, energy efficiency, quality), and evaluates whether the target consumer audience is sophisticated enough to recognize those associations. A weak answer names a component without addressing whether consumers would understand its significance.

**Common error:** Students often propose ingredient branding for purely aesthetic or engineering components that have no consumer-facing associations. The condition is not just that the component is good — it is that consumers know and trust the component brand specifically.

**Exercise 15** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

As introduced in Chapter 6: Brand Strategy and Positioning, a brand's positioning must commit to a specific audience, a specific tension the audience faces, and a specific proof that the brand resolves that tension. In this chapter, you learned that extension decisions require two tests: relevance (does the extension fit the parent's core associations?) and reinforcement (does the extension make the parent brand more coherent or more diffuse?).

Alphabet/Google has extended from search (Google Search) to smartphones (Pixel), wearables (Fitbit, acquired), autonomous vehicles (Waymo), and cloud infrastructure (Google Cloud). Using the extension-risk framework from this chapter AND the positioning logic from Chapter 6, evaluate whether Google's extension into autonomous vehicles (Waymo) was positioned as a strategically coherent extension or a potentially dilutive one.

*This question connects brand extension — relevance and reinforcement criteria to brand positioning — audience specificity and proof structure.*

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Names the specific associations that constitute Google's core brand equity (information access, search, AI-powered organization)
- Applies both extension tests (relevance and reinforcement) explicitly, not just one
- Uses the positioning logic to evaluate whether Google's stated audience and proof are continuous with Waymo's positioning or require a fundamentally different brand logic

*(Tests: brand extension — relevance and reinforcement + brand positioning — audience and proof structure — Ch. 7 + Ch. 6)*

**Answer:** Google's core associations are organized around information access, search, and AI-powered intelligence at scale — the brand's proof has historically been that Google makes the world's information findable and useful. Waymo extends these associations into transportation intelligence, which has relevance: autonomous vehicles are AI-powered systems that process complex environmental data to make navigation decisions, and Google's deep AI capability is a credible foundation. The relevance test is passed.

The reinforcement test is less clear. Search and Gmail are services that make Google more specific as an information and AI company. Waymo makes Google broader — it is now also a transportation company. The Waymo brand is held at arm's length from the Google name (Waymo rarely leads with Google in consumer communication), which suggests Google itself has intuited the dilution risk and made an endorsed-brand choice. This is the chapter's insight: the architectural separation is the risk-management strategy. By letting Waymo develop a partially independent identity, Google limits the equity contamination in both directions — a Waymo accident does not immediately become a Google crisis, and Waymo's specialized transportation associations do not diffuse Google's core search/AI positioning.

From a positioning standpoint (Chapter 6), Waymo's target audience (commercial fleets, ride-hailing operators, eventually consumers) is different from Google's core audience (anyone seeking information), and the proof required is different (physical safety and reliability vs. information accuracy and accessibility). This suggests the extension requires a separate positioning logic, which the architectural separation partly accommodates.

*Common error:* A surface answer notes that Waymo is an AI company, like Google, and concludes the extension is coherent. A strong answer uses both extension tests and the positioning framework to identify where the extension is genuinely continuous (AI capability) and where it requires separate positioning logic (audience, safety-proof vs. information-proof).

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 7, "Extension and Its Risk"; Chapter 6, Brand Strategy and Positioning

**Exercise 16** *(Cross-Chapter Synthesis)*

As introduced in Chapter 3: Brand Archetypes as a System, an archetype functions as a consistency-enforcement device — a constraint that makes downstream brand decisions decidable. In this chapter, you learned that portfolio pruning requires identifying which offerings are redundant (targeting the same audience, building the same associations) and which carry distinct value.

A technical educator's brand has the Sage archetype and a portfolio of: a long-form newsletter on machine learning fundamentals, an online course on ML for product managers, a YouTube channel with five-minute ML explainers, and a B2B consulting practice helping companies implement ML systems.

The Sage archetype's core commitment is to insight, depth, and trustworthy knowledge. Apply the archetype-as-constraint logic from Chapter 3 to the pruning decision from Chapter 7: which offerings in this portfolio are most coherent with the Sage archetype, which might create archetype drift, and how does the archetype help resolve the pruning question?

*What distinguishes a surface answer from a strong one:*
- Uses the archetype not just as a style label but as a decision-forcing constraint that makes pruning candidates visible
- Names specific aspects of each offering that fit or conflict with the Sage's core commitments to depth and trustworthy knowledge
- Shows how the archetype-as-constraint logic from Chapter 3 does actual pruning work in Chapter 7's framework

*(Tests: portfolio pruning — redundancy and dilution criteria + archetype as consistency-enforcement device — Ch. 7 + Ch. 3)*

**Answer:** The Sage archetype's core commitments are depth, insight, and trustworthy knowledge — the Sage demonstrates understanding rather than performs energy or entertainment. Applying this as a constraint:

The long-form newsletter and the ML course are strongly Sage-aligned: both require sustained engagement from an audience willing to invest time in depth. They serve different audience depths (newsletter for awareness-stage; course for structured learning) but reinforce the same associations. Not candidates for pruning.

The consulting practice is Sage-aligned: it positions the educator as the trustworthy expert guide, which is the Sage's native relationship to clients. High coherence with the archetype.

The YouTube five-minute explainer channel is the pruning candidate. Five-minute explainers require simplification that may conflict with the Sage's depth commitment — the format rewards entertainment and accessibility over the rigor that makes a Sage's knowledge trustworthy. If the explainer channel's associations trend toward "accessible ML," it begins to dilute the "deep ML expertise" associations that support consulting rates and course enrollments. The archetype-as-constraint logic makes this decision visible: the five-minute format is not a Sage format. A Sage can use YouTube, but a five-minute explainer series is closer to Jester or Everyman territory. Either the channel changes its format (longer, more analytical) or it becomes a pruning candidate.

*Common error:* A surface answer lists which offerings are good or bad without using the archetype as the decision mechanism. A strong answer shows how the Sage's specific commitments (depth, rigor, trustworthy knowledge) make the YouTube format visibly inconsistent and therefore a pruning candidate — not because it is bad content, but because it builds different associations than the archetype requires.

*Chapter reference:* Chapter 7, "Pruning Is Strategy"; Chapter 3, Archetype as Consistency-Enforcement Device

**Exercise 17** *(Transfer Challenge)*

The chapter teaches the brand architecture decision as a strategic choice made at a point in time. But real brand portfolios rarely start as clean decisions — they accumulate through acquisitions, opportunistic launches, and historical accident, and then get rationalized post-hoc.

Alphabet (Google's parent) has accumulated a portfolio that includes Search, Gmail, YouTube, Maps, Android, Google Cloud, Waymo, DeepMind, Google Health (via Fitbit), Google Workspace, and several others. Some carry the Google name prominently; others are partially independent; some (DeepMind, Waymo) rarely foreground the Google/Alphabet connection in consumer-facing communication.

Design a portfolio audit process that Alphabet's brand leadership could use to make principled architecture decisions across this portfolio. Your process should:
- Identify the specific information needed to classify each offering on the spectrum
- Specify the criteria for deciding which offerings should be branded, sub-branded, endorsed, or independent
- Address the specific complication that Alphabet/Google is both a consumer brand and an enterprise/B2B brand simultaneously
- Produce a recommendation for at least three specific offerings, with explicit rationale

*A strong response will address:*
- The two diagnostic questions from the chapter (audience correlation and risk correlation) applied systematically, not just case-by-case
- The tension between concentrating equity (branded house efficiency) and risk containment (house-of-brands protection) at Alphabet's scale
- The judgment that this portfolio cannot be resolved by the spectrum alone — the chapter's framework reveals the decisions, but does not make them

*(Tests: Brand Relationship Spectrum — full spectrum application + portfolio pruning — systematic decision process applied to novel corporate context)*
