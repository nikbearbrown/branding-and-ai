## Worked Example

*Study this example before attempting Tier 1. After reading it, close it and try to recall the key steps from memory before moving on.*

**Problem:** Yara is the founder of an AI-powered legal document review tool used by small law firms. On a Thursday afternoon, she receives three direct messages in quick succession from different clients saying that her tool produced an incorrect legal citation that was included in a brief filed with a court. The citation was fabricated — a real-sounding case that does not exist. She doesn't yet know whether this is an isolated incident or a systematic problem. She has not yet confirmed the full scope. Her first instinct is to investigate before saying anything publicly.

Apply the SCCT framework and the holding statement discipline to advise Yara on what to do in the first two hours.

**Approach:**
- Step 1: Classify the crisis type using SCCT. This is most likely an accidental crisis — Yara did not intend to produce incorrect citations, and if the tool has a systematic hallucination issue, it was presumably not known and deliberately ignored. If investigation later reveals that the problem was known and not disclosed, it escalates to preventable.
- Step 2: Identify the appropriate response strategy for an accidental crisis: diminish — acknowledge the harm, explain context, communicate what is being done to address it. Do not use denial ("this did not happen") which is unsupportable, and do not use a rebuild strategy prematurely, which would imply greater responsibility than current information supports.
- Step 3: Identify the speed-vs-accuracy tension. Yara wants to investigate before speaking. But without a public response, the affected clients may share their experience more broadly, framing the narrative before she has a chance to speak. The holding statement resolves this.
- Step 4: Draft a holding statement for an accidental crisis. The statement should: acknowledge that she is aware of the reports, avoid committing to a causal account, express that she is investigating urgently, and specify the next step.

**Answer:**

Yara should post a holding statement to the platforms where she is publicly accessible (social, email list, community channels) within the first two hours. The statement should not wait for the investigation to complete. Example:

> "I've received reports from several clients that our document review tool generated an incorrect legal citation that was included in a court brief. I take this seriously and am investigating urgently. If you have encountered this issue, please contact me directly at [contact]. I will share a full account of what happened and what I am doing about it within 24 hours."

This statement is honest (she has received reports), non-committal about cause (she does not yet know whether this is isolated or systematic), and demonstrates engagement rather than silence. It buys 24 hours for the investigation without ceding the narrative.

**What to notice:** The holding statement commits to nothing that cannot be sustained. "I am investigating" is true as long as she is actually investigating. The 24-hour promise is a commitment she can meet. If investigation reveals the problem was known and unreported, she must escalate to a rebuild/mortification response — the holding statement did not prevent that; it only bought time without creating a false account that would need to be retracted.

**Exercise 1**

Name the three crisis clusters from Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT) and the response strategy appropriate for each. For each, write one sentence describing what kind of harm it involves and who bears public responsibility attribution.

*(Tests: SCCT — three crisis types and matched response strategies)*

**Answer:** (1) Victim crisis: the organization is itself a victim of an external force (natural disaster, product tampering by an outside party). Public responsibility attribution is low. Appropriate strategy: deny or bolster — clarify the organization was not responsible, reinforce positive prior associations. (2) Accidental crisis: unintended harm from technical failure, unexpected side effect, or good-faith error. Public responsibility attribution is moderate. Appropriate strategy: diminish — acknowledge harm, explain context, communicate corrective action. (3) Preventable crisis: the organization knew or should have known about the risk and did not act. Misconduct, willful negligence, deferred safety decisions. Public responsibility attribution is high. Appropriate strategy: rebuild — full acknowledgment, sincere apology, genuine corrective action, evidence the correction is real.

**Common error:** Students reverse the diminish and rebuild strategies, assigning rebuild to accidental crises. The chapter is precise: rebuild is required for preventable crises because high responsibility attribution means any lesser strategy reads as evasion. Diminish is appropriate for moderate attribution; rebuild for high attribution.

**Exercise 2**

True / False: In a preventable crisis, explaining the business context that led to the organization's decision is the most effective opening strategy, because it helps the audience understand the pressures the organization was under.

*Explain your reasoning in one sentence.*

*(Tests: SCCT — mismatch between diminish strategy and preventable crisis type)*

**Answer:** False.

**Correct because:** Explaining context in a preventable crisis deploys a diminish strategy on a high-responsibility situation — the chapter identifies this as exactly the strategic error that compounds crisis damage, because the audience reads "here is the context" as organizational self-protection rather than stakeholder-concern. The appropriate opening for a preventable crisis is full acknowledgment of responsibility, not contextualization.

**Common error:** Students accept the explanation-of-context approach as reasonable because "context is important." The SCCT framework is about matched strategy, not about what feels fair. For preventable crises, any opening that contextualizes before acknowledging full responsibility signals evasion.

**Exercise 3**

In your own words, explain the purpose of a holding statement in crisis response, as introduced in this chapter. Your explanation should include: what specific problem it solves, what it commits to and what it does not commit to, and why it is effective at its specific function.

*(Tests: holding statement — purpose and design discipline)*

**Answer:** A strong answer captures: (1) The holding statement solves the speed-vs-accuracy tension — organizations need to respond quickly to avoid ceding the narrative, but responding before the facts are established produces statements that are falsified when those facts emerge. (2) The holding statement is fast — it can be posted within the first hour — and commits to nothing that cannot be sustained: "we are investigating" is true as long as investigation is ongoing; "we will provide an update" is a commitment the organization can meet. (3) It is effective because it demonstrates engagement (the organization is aware and taking the situation seriously) without creating a false account that would become a second crisis. It buys time for the real response to be developed without the cost of silence.

**What a strong answer includes:**
- The specific tension it resolves (speed vs. accuracy, not "speed vs. quality")
- What it does not commit to (causal account, responsibility assignment)
- Why non-commitment is a feature, not a weakness

**Exercise 4** *(Contrastive Classification)*

Below are four real or hypothetical brand responses to an emerging crisis situation. Classify each as either *wrong-fast*, *appropriate holding statement*, *silence* (ceding the narrative), or *full response* (appropriate if facts are established). Explain your classification in one sentence for each.

- (a) A software company's server goes down and they tweet within 20 minutes: "We're aware of the outage and our team is working on a fix. We'll update every 30 minutes until service is restored."
- (b) A fitness app receives reports that its AI coach gave unsafe exercise advice to users with a specific medical condition. The company's communications team issues a statement the next morning defending the AI's general safety record without having investigated the specific reports.
- (c) An e-commerce platform receives dozens of customer complaints about a payment processing error on a Friday afternoon. No one responds publicly through the weekend.
- (d) A consulting firm whose founder is accused of plagiarism in a viral LinkedIn post issues a statement: "We take concerns about intellectual integrity seriously and are investigating this matter. We will respond fully within 48 hours."

*(Tests: holding statement, wrong-fast, and silence — distinguishing response postures)*

**Answer:**
- (a) Appropriate holding statement: acknowledges the situation, commits to nothing about cause or resolution timeline, demonstrates engagement, and provides a concrete next update cadence. Technically this could also be classified as a full response if the facts are established (the outage is real and confirmed) — the classification is appropriate holding statement because it acknowledges without over-committing on resolution.
- (b) Wrong-fast: the company responded quickly but deployed the wrong strategy (defending general safety record before investigating the specific complaint). If the specific reports are accurate, this statement will need to be retracted — creating an additional crisis layer on top of the original. Speed without accuracy is wrong-fast.
- (c) Silence: multiple complaints without any public response cedes the narrative to the complainants and observers. By Monday, the framing of the situation has been set by the complaints, not by the company.
- (d) Appropriate holding statement: acknowledges a serious concern, commits to investigation, does not commit to a causal account or responsibility assignment, and specifies a concrete timeline (48 hours). Does not defend or deny before investigating.

**Common error:** Students classify (a) as "full response" because the company has enough information to acknowledge the outage. The distinction between holding statement and full response is about completeness of the account — a holding statement is explicitly not a full account. (a) is a well-executed holding statement regardless of whether the facts are established.

**Exercise 5**

What distinguishes the Tylenol 1982 case (Johnson & Johnson) from the BP Deepwater Horizon 2010 case in terms of crisis type and response strategy match? Name the SCCT crisis type for each and the strategy each organization deployed, and explain why one worked and the other did not.

*(Tests: SCCT — Tylenol and BP as matched and mismatched response cases)*

**Answer:** Tylenol 1982: the crisis was a victim crisis — J&J's product was tampered with by an unknown criminal after leaving the factory. Public responsibility attribution was low. J&J deployed a response that exceeded what the victim crisis required: rather than simply clarifying they were not responsible (the appropriate deny/bolster strategy), they pulled 31 million bottles nationwide at $100 million cost and invented tamper-evident packaging. This went beyond strategy — it demonstrated that the stated commitment to consumer safety in the company's 1943 Credo was real and not decorative. The brand recovered to above its pre-crisis market share by 1984. BP Deepwater Horizon 2010: the crisis was a preventable crisis — internal documents revealed safety concerns had been raised and overridden for cost and schedule reasons. Public responsibility attribution was high and rising. BP deployed a diminish strategy: CEO Tony Hayward minimized ("the Gulf is a very big ocean"), centered personal experience over victims ("I'd like my life back"), and attempted to contextualize rather than rebuild. A diminish strategy applied to a high-responsibility crisis reads as evasion. The strategy mismatch compounded the damage. Hayward resigned in July 2010.

**Common error:** Students describe Tylenol as "they did the right thing" and BP as "they said the wrong things" without naming the SCCT mechanism — the strategy mismatch is the analytical point, not just the outcome difference.

**Exercise 6**

True / False: A crisis playbook is most valuable at the moment a crisis occurs, because that is when it gets used.

*Explain your reasoning in one sentence.*

*(Tests: crisis playbook — pre-crisis construction as the discipline)*

**Answer:** False — with a precise qualification.

**Correct because:** The playbook is most valuable before a crisis occurs. It is used during the crisis, but its value comes from having made the slow decisions in advance so that the fast moment is execution rather than judgment. A playbook written during a crisis cannot perform its primary function — eliminating the need for improvised judgment under pressure.

**Common error:** Students read "most valuable when used" as equivalent to "most valuable at the moment of use." The chapter is explicit: the playbook is written before the crisis because the live moment requires speed, and speed is only possible when the decisions have already been made.

**Exercise 7** *(Error Analysis)*

A student has submitted the following holding statement draft for the following scenario: a food tech startup receives three social media posts claiming that their AI meal planning tool recommended a meal that triggered an allergic reaction in a user.

> "We at NourishAI are deeply committed to the safety of our users and deeply regret that this incident occurred. We want to assure all of our customers that our AI system has been rigorously tested for safety and that what happened in this case was an extremely unusual event that does not reflect the quality of our service. We will be reaching out to the affected user directly to make this right."

1. Identify the most significant error in this response.
2. Explain the misconception that produced it — what does the student misunderstand about holding statement design?
3. Write a corrected holding statement for this scenario.

*(Tests: holding statement discipline — commitments that cannot be sustained)*

**Answer:**
1. The most significant error is that the response commits to a causal account before the facts are established: "an extremely unusual event that does not reflect the quality of our service" asserts both cause and scope without investigation. If the investigation reveals this is not unusual but systematic, this statement becomes an additional crisis — the company falsely minimized the severity before knowing the facts.
2. The misconception: the student confused a holding statement with a full response and added defensive framing that requires factual support the company does not yet have. A holding statement should not assert anything about cause, typicality, or the quality of the service — those claims require investigation to be sustain.
3. Corrected holding statement: "We are aware of reports that NourishAI recommended a meal that may have triggered an allergic reaction for one of our users. We are investigating this urgently. If you have had a similar experience, please contact us immediately at [contact]. We will share a complete account of what happened and what we are doing about it within 24 hours. User safety is our priority."

**Common error:** Students identify the tone as the problem ("too corporate") rather than the factual overcommitment. The corrected statement is not improved by a warmer tone — it is improved by removing the unsupported assertions about cause and scope.

**Exercise 8** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

Priya is the founder of a personal brand focused on technical writing for engineering teams. She has 8,000 newsletter subscribers and an active LinkedIn following. On a Tuesday morning, she wakes up to 40+ replies to a newsletter she sent the previous evening. Multiple readers are pointing out that a template she included contained a critical accessibility error — her recommended HTML structure would be invisible to screen readers, and she explicitly labeled it "best practice."

Apply SCCT to classify this crisis and determine the appropriate response strategy. Then write the actual response Priya should send — both a holding statement (if one is appropriate) and the full response once she has investigated.

*(Tests: SCCT crisis classification and full response construction for an accidental-to-preventable spectrum case)*

**Answer:** SCCT classification: this is most likely an accidental crisis — Priya did not intend to publish incorrect guidance, and if she was unaware of the accessibility error, the harm was unintentional. However, if investigation reveals she had encountered this criticism before and did not update the template, it would move toward preventable. For an accidental crisis, the appropriate strategy is diminish: acknowledge the harm specifically, explain what she is doing to correct it, and communicate what changes she is making to prevent recurrence.

Holding statement (appropriate as the immediate response because she needs to investigate the full scope):
> "I've received a significant number of replies pointing out that the HTML template in last night's newsletter contains an accessibility error that would make the content invisible to screen readers. I labeled it 'best practice' — that was wrong. I'm reviewing the template now and will send a correction with an accurate replacement within the next few hours. Thank you to everyone who caught this. I take accuracy seriously and this matters."

Note: this holding statement is already moving toward a full response because the error is simple and immediately confirmable. The full response (once she has verified the correct HTML structure and reviewed whether other templates have similar issues):
> "I sent incorrect guidance in last night's newsletter. The HTML template I labeled 'best practice' will render content invisible to screen readers — the opposite of accessible. Here is the corrected template: [template]. I've also reviewed the three other templates I've published in the past year; two have similar issues that I am correcting now and will publish in next week's newsletter. I'm adding an accessibility review step to my template testing process going forward. I'm sorry for the error."

**Common error:** Students classify this as a victim crisis because "she didn't mean to" and recommend a bolster strategy. Lack of intent determines accidental, not victim — victim crises require an external actor causing the harm. The missing intent affects the severity of the diminish strategy but does not change the crisis type.

**Exercise 9** *(Self-Explanation)*

The chapter distinguishes "speed" from "wrong-fast" in crisis communication. Explain in 2–3 sentences why the holding statement resolves this tension rather than just choosing one over the other. Your explanation must use the term "holding statement" correctly and must reason from the principle, not just describe what the holding statement does.

*(Tests: speed vs. wrong-fast — holding statement as the resolution mechanism)*

**Answer:** A strong answer captures: the speed-vs.-accuracy tension is not a choice between two options — either respond quickly (risking a statement that cannot be sustained) or wait for facts (ceding the narrative). The holding statement resolves the tension by separating the obligations: the obligation to be present and engaged in the first hour can be met by a statement that commits only to what is already known (there are reports, investigation is underway) without committing to what is not yet known (cause, responsibility, scope). The accuracy obligation is preserved because the holding statement does not assert anything that needs to be retracted when facts emerge — it makes only claims that are true for the duration of the investigation itself. The resolution is structural, not a compromise: both speed and accuracy are achieved by scoping the first statement to only what can be sustained.

**Common error:** Students describe the holding statement as a "compromise" between speed and accuracy. A strong answer shows it is a resolution: both requirements are met fully by separating what can be stated immediately (acknowledgment of reports, investigation underway) from what requires investigation (cause, responsibility).

**Exercise 11** *(Cumulative — Chapter 15)*

In Chapter 15: Brand Ethics, Purpose, and Sustainability, the backlash asymmetry shows that values claims without behavioral follow-through erode trust faster than silence — the claim inflates the expectation, and a violation deflates trust by the full inflated amount. In this chapter, J&J's Tylenol response succeeded partly because the company's Credo — predating the crisis by forty years — meant the response was evidence of real values, not a performance of them.

A personal brand practitioner argues: "The Tylenol lesson is simple: always do the right thing when a crisis hits. Values don't need to be pre-established — you can demonstrate them through your crisis response behavior."

*(Tests: SCCT — Tylenol values-as-evidence + backlash asymmetry from Chapter 15)*

**Answer:** The argument misreads the Tylenol lesson in a specific way. **Exercise 12** *(Scenario-Based Application)*

A brand monitoring system has just flagged a significant spike in negative mentions of a mobile banking app. The mentions fall into three categories: (a) 200 posts complaining that the app was slow during peak hours yesterday — a normal complaint pattern that appears periodically; (b) 15 posts from fintech journalists noting that the app's new fee structure is "predatory" — this is new, following a fee change announced yesterday; (c) 1 detailed thread from a security researcher claiming the app has a vulnerability that exposes transaction data, with technical detail that appears credible.

Apply the chapter's severity triage framework to each signal. For each, identify: the crisis type it represents (if any), the appropriate first response, and the escalation priority order.

*(Tests: crisis playbook — severity triage applied to a multi-signal monitoring scenario)*

**Answer:**
- (a) Slow app performance complaints: not a crisis. This is a background reputation management issue — periodic complaints about performance are within the normal variation the chapter calls "background noise." First response: route to customer support team, monitor for whether it continues or escalates. No holding statement warranted. Lowest escalation priority.
- (b) Fee structure criticism from fintech journalists: potential accidental or preventable crisis, depending on whether the fee change was adequately reviewed before launch. The "predatory" framing from journalists with reach is a signal worth engaging but not a crisis yet — it is a reputation management situation that may require a policy communication response (explaining the rationale for the fee change clearly) rather than a crisis holding statement. First response: brief the communications and product teams, determine whether the fee rationale needs public communication. Medium escalation priority; if multiple journalists amplify, escalate to holding statement territory.
- (c) Security vulnerability claim from a credible researcher: this is a potential preventable crisis if the vulnerability is real and was not identified in the company's own security review. First response: immediately route to the security team for technical assessment; simultaneously escalate to the CEO and legal counsel. Do not respond publicly until the technical claim is assessed — posting a "we are investigating" holding statement before confirming whether the vulnerability is real could be worse than silence if the assessment takes less than an hour. If the vulnerability is confirmed, the escalation path goes immediately to the CEO, legal counsel, and crisis communications lead. Highest escalation priority by a significant margin.

**Common error:** Students treat all three signals as equivalent in severity and route all three to the same holding statement response. The triage function is precisely to distinguish background noise (a), reputational management (b), and potential crisis requiring immediate escalation (c).

**Exercise 15** *(Transfer Challenge)*

The chapter's "Still Puzzling" section identifies the authenticity problem: "The advice 'be genuine' is not useful to someone deciding what to type in the next twenty minutes." The chapter suggests this is a real unsolved problem in crisis communication practice.
